# Recommended Readings — ASCM CPIM (Planning & Inventory Management)

> A curated reading list for the student who wants to *master* the discipline behind the exam, not just pass it. Organized by canonical textbooks, seminal papers, industry resources, and free academic courses. Each entry includes a 1–2 sentence note on why it earns its place and *when* in your study plan to engage with it.

---

## 1. Canonical textbooks (read at least 2 of these in depth)

### *CPIM Learning System* — ASCM (latest edition)
Publisher: ASCM (Association for Supply Chain Management) | Format: Digital + workbooks | URL: ascm.org
**Why it matters:** The official ECM-aligned study system from the body that authors the exam. Every Process node, every example, every practice quiz aligns to the published Exam Content Manual. The single most important resource.
**When to use:** Throughout the entire 10-week study plan. Read each Process node in parallel with the corresponding course module here.

### *Manufacturing Planning and Control for Supply Chain Management*, 6th ed. — Vollmann, Berry, Whybark & Jacobs
McGraw-Hill, 2011 | ISBN 978-0073377827
**Why it matters:** The canonical MPC textbook used in operations programs at MIT, Wharton, Stanford GSB, Carnegie Mellon Tepper, and most MBA Operations electives. Covers the entire CPIM body of knowledge from a more rigorous academic angle than the ASCM materials. Chapter 1 (strategy), Chapter 2 (forecasting), Chapter 3 (S&OP), Chapters 5–6 (MPS/MRP), Chapter 7 (PAC), Chapter 9 (inventory) map almost one-to-one to this course.
**When to use:** As the deep-dive supplement after Modules 4–6. Best for students who want the *why* behind ASCM's rules.

### *The Goal: A Process of Ongoing Improvement*, 4th anniversary ed. — Goldratt, Eliyahu M. & Cox, Jeff
North River Press, 2014 (original 1984) | ISBN 978-0884271956
**Why it matters:** The TOC novel. One weekend read. Teaches the 5 Focusing Steps, Drum-Buffer-Rope, and the throughput-vs-inventory-vs-operating-expense triad in a story format that sticks better than any textbook chapter. Cited in every CPIM exam question about constraints.
**When to use:** During Module 5 (Capacity Planning). Read on a Saturday-Sunday; do nothing else study-related that weekend.

### *The Machine That Changed the World: The Story of Lean Production* — Womack, James P., Jones, Daniel T. & Roos, Daniel
Free Press, 1990 (reissued 2007) | ISBN 978-0743299794
**Why it matters:** The original MIT-IMVP study that named "Lean" as a Western movement distinct from Toyota's internal vocabulary. Every Lean concept on the CPIM exam — the 7 wastes, kanban, takt, jidoka, andon — traces to the principles articulated here.
**When to use:** During Module 8. Pair with the Liker book below for the management-philosophy layer.

### *Manufacturing Strategy*, 2nd ed. — Hill, Terence J.
Palgrave Macmillan, 2000 (1st ed. 1985) | ISBN 978-0333762219; or *Manufacturing Operations Strategy: Texts and Cases* (Hill, 2007)
**Why it matters:** Origin of the order-winner / order-qualifier framework that is the #1 most-tested vocabulary pair in Module 1. Hill's volume-variety / process-choice reasoning underpins the entire CPIM exam approach to manufacturing strategy.
**When to use:** Module 1, then revisit during the Module 3 S&OP work to align strategy and execution.

### *Sales and Operations Planning: The How-To Handbook*, 3rd ed. — Wallace, Thomas F. & Stahl, Robert A.
T.F. Wallace & Company, 2008 | ISBN 978-0967488400
**Why it matters:** The canonical 5-step Wallace model that ASCM teaches. Every CPIM question about S&OP cadence, demand vs supply review, pre-S&OP reconciliation, and executive ratification draws from this book. The format is consultant-practical, not academic.
**When to use:** Module 3.

## 2. Seminal papers & HBR articles (5–10 to engage with)

### *Manufacturing — Missing Link in Corporate Strategy* — Skinner, Wickham
*Harvard Business Review*, May–June 1969
**Why it matters:** The founding article that argued operations had been overlooked in corporate strategy. Created the conceptual space for Hayes & Wheelwright, Hill, and ultimately ASCM's modern teaching. Required reading in MBA Operations electives at Harvard, Wharton, MIT Sloan, Stanford GSB.
**When to use:** Module 1, before reading Hayes & Wheelwright.

### *Link Manufacturing Process and Product Life Cycles* — Hayes, Robert H. & Wheelwright, Steven C.
*Harvard Business Review*, January–February 1979 (pair with *The Dynamics of Process-Product Life Cycles*, HBR Mar–Apr 1979)
**Why it matters:** The product-process matrix in its original HBR form, before the 1984 book. Two short reads that crystallize the diagonal-alignment principle.
**When to use:** Module 1.

### *Industrial Dynamics: A Major Breakthrough for Decision Makers* — Forrester, Jay W.
*Harvard Business Review*, July–August 1958
**Why it matters:** The original demand-amplification insight that became the bullwhip effect. Forrester at MIT was decades ahead of his time; the Lee-Padmanabhan-Whang formalization (1997) is the modern restatement.
**When to use:** Module 2 (bullwhip section).

### *The Bullwhip Effect in Supply Chains* — Lee, Hau L., Padmanabhan, V. & Whang, Seungjin
*Sloan Management Review* 38(3), Spring 1997
(Companion: *Information Distortion in a Supply Chain*, *Management Science* 43(4), April 1997)
**Why it matters:** The most-cited supply chain paper of the last 30 years. Names the four causes of bullwhip (demand-signal distortion, order batching, price promotions, rationing/shortage gaming) that every CPIM exam tests in some form.
**When to use:** Module 2.

### *Rapid-Fire Fulfillment* — Ferdows, Kasra, Lewis, Michael A. & Machuca, Jose A.D.
*Harvard Business Review*, November 2004
**Why it matters:** The canonical Inditex/Zara case study in HBR's archives. Demonstrates the deliberate off-diagonal positioning on the Hayes-Wheelwright matrix and the alignment of operations strategy to fast-fashion order winners.
**When to use:** Module 1.

### *Hewlett-Packard: DeskJet Printer Supply Chain* — Lee, Hau L., Billington, Corey & Carter, Brent
*Interfaces* 23(4), 1993 (and *Sloan Management Review* / *HBR* follow-on coverage 1994)
**Why it matters:** The canonical postponement case. Every CPIM question about postponement, decoupling-point shifts, and mass customization traces back to this paper.
**When to use:** Module 1.

### *How Many Parts to Make at Once* — Harris, Ford W.
*Factory: The Magazine of Management* 10(2), 1913 (rediscovered and re-published in *Operations Research* 38(6), 1990, by Donald Erlenkotter)
**Why it matters:** The original EOQ paper, lost for decades before Erlenkotter found a copy in 1990. The 1913 derivation is identical to the modern textbook formula. Historical-significance read.
**When to use:** Module 6 (EOQ section).

### *Various Optimizers for Single-Stage Production* — Smith, Wayne E.
*Naval Research Logistics Quarterly* 3(1–2), 1956
**Why it matters:** The proof that SPT (Shortest Processing Time) minimizes mean flow time on a single machine. One of the few clean closed-form results in scheduling theory.
**When to use:** Module 7 (PAC).

### *Sales and Operations Planning Maturity: Where Are You, Where Should You Be?* — Lapide, Larry
MIT Center for Transportation & Logistics white paper, 2007 (updated 2014)
**Why it matters:** The canonical 4-stage S&OP maturity model (Marginal → Rudimentary → Classic → Ideal) that ASCM exam references and that Oliver Wight expanded into the Class A standard. Useful for diagnosing where any organization is on the ladder.
**When to use:** Module 3, and again during the Capstone Project.

### *Operationalizing the Triple Bottom Line* — Slaper, Timothy F. & Hall, Tanya J.
*Indiana Business Review*, Spring 2011
**Why it matters:** A practitioner-friendly elaboration of Elkington's TBL into measurable operations practice. Useful when the CPIM exam asks how a TBL commitment cascades into operations decisions.
**When to use:** Module 1 (sustainability section).

## 3. Industry resources to subscribe / monitor

### ASCM SCM Now magazine
ASCM membership benefit | ascm.org
**Why it matters:** The current practitioner case studies and ECM-aligned articles direct from the body that authors the exam. Issues 2023–2026 cover Connected Planning, post-COVID inventory rebuilds, post-Red-Sea routing, and AI-augmented S&OP — all material that may appear on the modern CPIM exam.

### Lean Enterprise Institute (lean.org)
LEI runs the leading practitioner content on Lean, founded by James Womack himself. The *Lean Post* (free) and *Lean Solutions* (membership) publish current cases on Lean in manufacturing, healthcare, software, and government.
**When to use:** During Module 8 and after.

### *IndustryWeek* magazine and IndustryWeek.com
The longest-running US trade publication for manufacturing leadership. Their annual "Best Plants" issue is a survey of high-performing operations from across North America.
**When to use:** Continuously. Read 2024–2026 archive selectively.

### *APICS Magazine* archives (pre-2018; ASCM SCM Now is the successor)
Historic content (2000–2018) is still valuable for the foundational concepts; not aligned to the latest ECM but excellent for the *why* behind the rules.

### Supply Chain Management Review (SCMR)
A practitioner journal covering S&OP, demand planning, inventory, and network design. Useful for case-study material beyond the ASCM perimeter.

### *Practical Wisdom from Toyota* (Lean Enterprise Institute video series)
Short-form content from senior Toyota production-system practitioners. Helpful supplement to the Ohno and Liker books.

## 4. Free academic courses (engage with at least one)

### MIT MicroMasters in Supply Chain Management (edX)
Five graduate-level MIT courses (SC0x Logistics, SC1x Demand Forecasting, SC2x Design, SC3x Dynamics, SC4x Tech & Systems). Free to audit; ~$1,200 total for verified certificate. Aligned to MIT's Center for Transportation & Logistics curriculum (Larry Lapide, Yossi Sheffi, Chris Caplice).
**When to use:** SC1x (Forecasting) overlaps Module 2; SC3x (Dynamics) overlaps Modules 2 + 3. The full MicroMasters extends well beyond CPIM into CSCP/CLTD territory.

### MIT 15.762J — Supply Chain Planning (OpenCourseWare)
MIT OCW free course materials, Stephen Graves and others | ocw.mit.edu
**Why it matters:** Lectures, problem sets, and exam-style problems from the MIT Sloan MBA Operations curriculum. Heavier on the math (queueing, stochastic inventory) than ASCM-CPIM but excellent for deepening forecasting and inventory mastery.
**When to use:** During Modules 2 and 6.

### Stanford OPS 363 — Supply Chain Operations (Stanford Online; selected sessions available free)
Hau Lee's flagship Stanford GSB Operations course. The Lee work on bullwhip and postponement underpins much of the CPIM curriculum.
**When to use:** Throughout, particularly Modules 1, 2, and 3.

### Wharton Coursera — Operations Analytics (free to audit) + Operations Management (Christian Terwiesch)
Foundational MBA-level operations content. Lighter than MIT MicroMasters but free to audit and well-paced.
**When to use:** Early in study, before or during Modules 1–3.

### Coursera — Supply Chain Operations (Rutgers / SUNY) and edX — Supply Chain Fundamentals (Georgia Tech)
Less prestigious than MIT but well-structured for the working professional. Free to audit.

## 5. Adjacent ASCM Credentials (for context, not exam material)

If you're considering follow-on certifications after CPIM:

- **ASCM CSCP** (Certified Supply Chain Professional) — the supply-chain-strategy credential, broader scope; this course's sibling at `10-ASCM-CSCP/`.
- **ASCM CLTD** (Certified in Logistics, Transportation, and Distribution) — deeper on logistics and warehousing; `12-ASCM-CLTD/`.
- **ASCM CDDP / CDDL** (Certified Demand Driven Planner / Leader) — the DDMRP-specific credential by ASCM in partnership with the Demand Driven Institute. Build on the DDMRP discussion in Module 4 of this course.
- **ISM CPSM** (Certified Professional in Supply Management) — the supply-side / procurement complement to CPIM; `13-ISM-CPSM/`.

## A reading sequence for the time-constrained learner

If you only have time for *one* book outside the ASCM Learning System, read **Vollmann, Berry, Whybark & Jacobs** (MPC, 6th ed.).
If you only have time for *one* paper, read **Lee, Padmanabhan & Whang (1997)** on the bullwhip effect.
If you only have time for *one* case, study **the HP DeskJet postponement story (Lee, Billington & Carter, 1993)**.
If you only have time for *one* novel-format read, read **Goldratt's *The Goal*** in a single weekend.
If you only have time for *one* free course, audit **MIT MicroMasters SC1x (Demand Forecasting)**.

The CPIM exam will test you on what ASCM published. The wider literature will make you *good at the job* — which is the bar a Cornell, Harvard, Stanford, or MIT graduate-level program would expect from you, and the bar this course is built to.
