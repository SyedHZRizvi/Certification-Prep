## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

### Q1: **D. Make-to-Stock**
Standardized product, forecast-driven, FG inventory held — classic MTS. The customer pulls from finished goods.

### Q2: **B. Assemble-to-Order**
Dell is the textbook ATO example. Modules are forecast-built; final assembly is order-triggered. Many casual sources call Dell "MTO" but CPIM language is **ATO** because the decoupling point is at the *sub-assembly* level, not raw materials.

### Q3: **B. The point in the supply chain where the customer order penetrates production**
Also called the "push-pull boundary." Upstream of this point, production is forecast-driven (push). Downstream, it is order-driven (pull).

### Q4: **B. The criterion that causes a customer to choose your product over a competitor's**
A *qualifier* is the minimum. A *winner* is the differentiator. (Source: Terry Hill, 1985.)

### Q5: **A. A winner to a qualifier**
The classic Hill principle: yesterday's winner becomes tomorrow's qualifier as competitors catch up. ABS, airbags, and Bluetooth all followed this path in autos.

### Q6: **B. Delaying final product differentiation until the customer order is received**
Postponement = pushing the decoupling point downstream. It is a structural strategy, not just a packaging tweak.

### Q7: **B. Built generic printers centrally and added country-specific power kits at regional DCs**
HP redesigned the printer so the chassis was country-neutral and the power module + manual + plug were added at the regional DC on receipt of the order. Inventory dropped ~25%.

### Q8: **C. Line/repetitive process producing high volume of low-variety product**
The matrix diagonal pairs *high volume + low variety* with *line/continuous* processes, and *low volume + high variety* with *job shop/project* processes.

### Q9: **B. Business strategy**
"How do we compete in this market?" is the business-strategy question. Corporate strategy decides *which markets we are in*; functional strategy decides *how Operations supports that*.

### Q10: **D. Headcount**
The five competitive priorities are Cost, Quality, Delivery, Flexibility, and Service/Innovation. Headcount is a resource, not a competitive priority.

### Q11: **B. ATO architecture combined with postponement**
Mass customization = high-volume economics + customer-specific output. ATO + postponement is the standard implementation pattern (Dell, BMW configure-to-order, etc.).

### Q12: **D. Engineer-to-Order**
Design happens *after* the contract, then sourcing and production. ETO has the longest customer lead time.

### Q13: **B. People, Planet, Profit**
Coined by John Elkington in 1994. ASCM emphasizes sustainability as a strategic constraint on the current exam.

### Q14: **C. Job shop**
High variety, low individual-product volume, functional layout (by machine type) — the job-shop signature.

### Q15: **B. ATO holds forecast-built sub-assemblies; MTO starts at raw materials**
The decoupling point is at *modules* for ATO and at *raw materials* for MTO. This directly affects how MPS is built (two-level for ATO; single-level at end-item for MTO).

### Q16: **D. MTS**
Standardized + lowest cost = high-volume MTS. Cost leadership and volume go together.

### Q17: **B. Trade-offs become impossible to manage and the operation excels at nothing**
This is the central "trade-off" lesson of manufacturing strategy. You must pick 1–2 priorities and align everything to them.

### Q18: **B. A working camera and a one-day battery**
Every modern smartphone has these — they are *qualifiers* (the minimum), not winners. The other three options are differentiators.

### Q19: **B. Bring previously offshored production back to the domestic market**
Reshoring is the reverse of offshoring. Drivers include tariffs, supply chain risk, energy cost shifts, and labor automation.

### Q20: **D. ETO**
Engineer-to-Order holds essentially zero finished-goods inventory because every product is unique and built to a specific contract. MTS holds the most FG.

### Q21: **B. Quality**
"Premium," "craftsmanship," "perceived quality" all point to the *Quality* competitive priority.

### Q22: **B. A structural mismatch between product variety/volume and process choice**
Off-diagonal = e.g., a job shop trying to deliver high-volume/low-variety output, or a continuous-flow plant trying to handle 500 custom variants. Either is structurally broken.

### Q23: **B. Corporate → Business → Functional**
Corporate decides which businesses you're in. Each business unit decides how it competes. Functional (Operations, Marketing, Finance) supports the business strategy.

### Q24: **A. MTS**
Forecast-driven daily production, products go into stock at corner stores, customer pulls from finished goods. Anna's bagels = MTS.
