# Module 9: Sustainability, Risk & CSR 🌱

> **Why this module matters:** Domain 1 (Environment & Strategy) is 25% of the exam, and modern editions of the CSCP Learning System lean heavily on sustainability, risk management, and CSR. Investors and regulators now require what the exam will test — ESG reporting, ISO frameworks, business continuity, scope-3 emissions.

---

## 🌍 A Story: The Apparel Brand That Found Its $7M Risk

Hassan is Chief Supply Chain Officer at **NorthSlope Outdoor**, a $480M apparel company. In late 2022 a customer audit asked a routine question: "Provide your Tier-2 supplier list for the down jackets." Hassan's team had Tier-1 (the cut-and-sew factories). They had no Tier-2 (the down processors). Or Tier-3 (the duck farms).

Six weeks of digging revealed three Tier-3 farms with no animal-welfare audits. Forced-labor allegations were emerging in the broader sector. If the press got hold of it, NorthSlope could lose its biggest retail partner — a $7M annual relationship — overnight.

Hassan launched a 6-month program: full multi-tier mapping, switch to Responsible Down Standard certified suppliers, ISO 14001 environmental management at owned facilities, scope-3 emissions accounting with CDP disclosure. Cost: $1.2M. Avoided risk: $7M+. Side benefit: 12 new B2B customers wanted exactly that transparency and chose NorthSlope as a result.

**Sustainability and risk used to be "nice to have."** They are now table stakes. This module gives you the frameworks.

## 🌿 The Triple Bottom Line (3 P's)

John Elkington coined "Triple Bottom Line" in 1994. The idea: measure success on three axes, not just profit.

| P | Dimension | Examples |
|---|-----------|----------|
| **People** | Social — labor, community, human rights | Living wage, no child labor, DEI |
| **Planet** | Environmental — carbon, water, waste, biodiversity | Net-zero plan, water reduction, circular flows |
| **Profit** | Economic — financial sustainability | Margin, cash, ROI |

🎯 **Exam tip:** TBL is the foundation of every modern sustainability question. If the answer mentions "balance environmental, social, and economic" — you're in TBL territory.

## ♻️ The Circular Economy

The Ellen MacArthur Foundation popularized circular economy as the alternative to "take-make-waste" linear flow.

### Linear vs Circular

```
   LINEAR:   Take → Make → Use → Dispose
   CIRCULAR: Take → Make → Use → Reuse / Refurbish / Recycle → back to Make
```

### The 9 R's (varies by source)

| # | R | Description |
|---|---|-------------|
| 0 | Refuse | Don't buy / make at all |
| 1 | Rethink | Redesign product / system |
| 2 | Reduce | Use less material |
| 3 | Reuse | Same product, multiple lives |
| 4 | Repair | Restore function |
| 5 | Refurbish | Restore to like-new |
| 6 | Remanufacture | Industrial restoration to OEM spec |
| 7 | Repurpose | Different use for the item |
| 8 | Recycle | Material recovery |
| 9 | Recover | Energy from waste |

🎯 **Exam tip:** Earlier "R's" are higher value (better for environment). Recycle is *near the end* — circular doesn't mean "recycle everything."

## 📐 Sustainability Metrics & Standards

| Standard / Framework | Focus |
|---------------------|-------|
| **ISO 14001** | Environmental management systems (EMS) — certifiable |
| **ISO 14064** | GHG accounting & verification |
| **ISO 50001** | Energy management |
| **ISO 26000** | Social responsibility (guidance, not certification) |
| **GRI** (Global Reporting Initiative) | Sustainability reporting standards |
| **SASB** (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board) | Industry-specific ESG metrics |
| **TCFD** (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) | Climate financial risk disclosure |
| **CDP** (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project) | Investor questionnaire on carbon |
| **SBTi** (Science-Based Targets initiative) | Net-zero target validation |
| **B Corp** | Third-party certification of social/environmental performance |
| **OECD Guidelines** | Multinational enterprise conduct |
| **UN Global Compact** | 10 principles on human rights, labor, environment, anti-corruption |
| **EU CSRD** | Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive — mandatory EU reporting |

### Scope 1, 2, 3 Emissions

| Scope | What | Example |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Scope 1** | Direct emissions from owned sources | Company trucks, on-site boilers |
| **Scope 2** | Indirect from purchased energy | Electricity, steam, heating |
| **Scope 3** | Other indirect, value chain | Supplier emissions, employee commute, product use, end-of-life |

🚨 **Trap on the exam:** Scope 3 is usually 70–90% of a company's total footprint but is hardest to measure. Most modern net-zero commitments include scope 3.

## ⚠️ Supply Chain Risk Management (SCRM)

ISO 31000 defines risk as "the effect of uncertainty on objectives." SCRM applies the risk management process to supply chain.

### The risk management process (ISO 31000)

```
   1. Establish context
   2. Identify risks
   3. Analyze risks (likelihood × impact)
   4. Evaluate / prioritize
   5. Treat risks (avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept)
   6. Monitor & review
   7. Communicate & consult (throughout)
```

### Categories of supply chain risk

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Demand risk** | Forecast volatility, sudden market shifts |
| **Supply risk** | Supplier failure, capacity loss |
| **Operational risk** | Equipment failure, IT outages |
| **Financial risk** | FX, supplier credit, payment default |
| **Geopolitical risk** | Sanctions, war, trade restrictions |
| **Climate / natural** | Hurricanes, floods, wildfires, drought |
| **Cyber** | Ransomware, data breach, IP theft |
| **Reputational** | Modern slavery exposure, ESG scandal |
| **Compliance / regulatory** | New laws, customs changes |
| **Pandemic / health** | COVID, supplier workforce illness |

### Risk treatment options (4 T's)

| Strategy | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Tolerate / Accept** | Live with low-impact risk |
| **Treat / Mitigate** | Reduce likelihood or impact (dual sourcing, safety stock) |
| **Transfer** | Insurance, contractual indemnification |
| **Terminate / Avoid** | Exit the activity entirely |

## 🛠️ Risk Tools You Should Know

### Risk register
A table of identified risks with: description, likelihood, impact, owner, treatment, status.

### Risk heatmap
2-axis chart (likelihood × impact) coloring high-risk items red, low green.

```
   Impact
     High │  M  │  H  │  H  │
   ───────┼─────┼─────┼─────┤
   Medium │  L  │  M  │  H  │
   ───────┼─────┼─────┼─────┤
     Low  │  L  │  L  │  M  │
          └─────┴─────┴─────┘
            Low   Med  High
                Likelihood
```

### FMEA (Failure Mode & Effects Analysis)
Systematic process where each potential failure mode is scored on:
- **Severity (S)** 1–10
- **Occurrence (O)** 1–10
- **Detection (D)** 1–10 (1 = easy to detect, 10 = hard)

RPN = S × O × D. Higher RPN = higher priority.

## 🛡️ Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery

### BCP (Business Continuity Plan) vs DRP (Disaster Recovery Plan)

| | BCP | DRP |
|---|------|------|
| Scope | All business functions | IT systems |
| Goal | Keep critical processes running | Restore IT after disaster |
| Standard | ISO 22301 | Often part of BCP |

### Key BCP metrics

| Metric | Definition |
|--------|------------|
| **MTPD** (Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption) | Longest the business can be down before unacceptable harm |
| **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective) | Target time to restore a function (≤ MTPD) |
| **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective) | Max acceptable data loss (e.g., 4 hours = restore from 4-hour-old backup) |
| **MAO** (Maximum Acceptable Outage) | Synonym for MTPD in some standards |

### Business Impact Analysis (BIA)
Quantifies the impact of disruption per function (financial, operational, reputational, regulatory) and informs RTO/RPO.

🎯 **Exam tip:** RTO is **time to restore**. RPO is **acceptable data loss**. Don't confuse them.

## 🌱 CSR — Corporate Social Responsibility

CSR is the discretionary commitment of a business to operate in an economically, socially, and environmentally responsible manner. Often framed around:

### CSR pyramid (Carroll, 1991)

```
       ┌──────────────┐
       │ Philanthropic│  Discretionary
       ├──────────────┤
       │   Ethical    │  Right to do
       ├──────────────┤
       │    Legal     │  Required
       ├──────────────┤
       │   Economic   │  Required
       └──────────────┘
```

### ESG — the investor-driven evolution of CSR

| Letter | Examples |
|--------|----------|
| **E** Environmental | Carbon, water, waste, biodiversity |
| **S** Social | Labor, human rights, diversity, community |
| **G** Governance | Board diversity, ethics, anti-corruption, exec pay |

ESG ratings come from MSCI, Sustainalytics, ISS, S&P Global. Bad ESG ratings increasingly affect cost of capital.

## 🚫 Modern Slavery & Forced Labor

| Regulation | Country | Scope |
|------------|---------|-------|
| **UK Modern Slavery Act 2015** | UK | Annual statement required > £36M revenue |
| **California Transparency in Supply Chains Act** | CA | Retailers + manufacturers > $100M |
| **EU CSDDD** (Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive) | EU | Major firms must conduct human rights & environmental due diligence |
| **Germany Lieferkettengesetz** | Germany | Supply chain due diligence law (2023) |
| **Uyghur Forced Labor Prevention Act (UFLPA)** | US | Rebuttable presumption against Xinjiang imports |

Implication: **multi-tier visibility** is no longer optional. Tier-2, Tier-3 supplier transparency is a compliance requirement.

## 🌐 Cyber & Information Risk in SC

### Key threats
- Ransomware on supplier or 3PL
- Compromised software updates (SolarWinds-style supply chain attacks)
- Data breach of customer/PII shared with suppliers
- IP theft, especially in JV / offshore manufacturing
- IoT device compromise on shop floor

### Standards / frameworks
- **NIST CSF** (Cybersecurity Framework) — Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover
- **ISO 27001** — Information security management
- **NIST 800-161** — Supply chain cybersecurity
- **CMMC** — US DoD cybersecurity maturity model

## 🔄 Resilience: Beyond Risk Management

A resilient supply chain absorbs shocks AND adapts. Levers:

| Lever | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Redundancy** | Multi-source, multi-site, safety stock |
| **Flexibility** | Cross-trained workforce, configurable lines |
| **Visibility** | Real-time tracking, multi-tier mapping |
| **Collaboration** | CPFR, shared planning, supplier intimacy |
| **Diversification** | Geographic spread, product mix |
| **Postponement** | Delay final differentiation |

🎯 **Exam tip:** Pure efficiency optimization (single source, no inventory) is fragile. Resilience is the antidote. Post-COVID exam questions favor resilience moves over pure-cost answers.

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---------------|---------|
| "Sustainability = recycling" | Reduce/reuse/refurbish come before recycle |
| "CSR is charity" | CSR includes economic and legal layers (Carroll) |
| "Scope 1 is the biggest emissions source" | Scope 3 usually is — 70-90% in many industries |
| "Risk = bad outcomes" | ISO defines risk as the effect of uncertainty — can be positive too |
| "BCP = DRP" | DRP is IT-focused; BCP is whole-business |
| "RTO = RPO" | RTO is time to restore; RPO is acceptable data loss |
| "Resilience = redundancy" | Redundancy is one lever; resilience also needs flexibility + visibility |
| "ISO 14001 is government law" | It's a voluntary international standard, not legislation |

## 🚨 Exam Traps

1. **Scope 1/2/3** — Know definitions cold; scope 3 is the supply chain piece.
2. **RTO vs RPO** — Time vs data loss.
3. **9 R's hierarchy** — Refuse/reduce/reuse beat recycle.
4. **ISO 14001 vs ISO 27001** — Environmental vs information security.
5. **CSR vs ESG** — CSR is the philosophy; ESG is the investor measurement set.
6. **Risk transfer = insurance, not elimination** — Risk is shared, not removed.
7. **Triple bottom line trade-offs** — Real firms balance all 3 P's, not just profit.
