## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

### Q1: **B. Material, information, and cash**
The three flows are the bedrock definition. Don't confuse this with three-letter acronyms or actor lists.

### Q2: **C. 6**
Plan, Source, Make, Deliver, Return, **Enable**. Older texts say 5 — they're outdated. Enable was added in SCOR 11.
🧠 Memory: "P-S-M-D-R-E."

### Q3: **B. Source**
Source covers procurement, supplier selection, and inbound receiving. Plan handles forecasting/balancing.

### Q4: **C. Efficient**
Stable, predictable = functional product (Fisher) → efficient, low-cost supply chain.

### Q5: **D. Agile**
High demand + high supply uncertainty = Hau Lee's "Agile" quadrant. Combines responsiveness with risk-hedging.

### Q6: **B. Forecast-driven to order-driven boundary**
CODP is the strategic point where push transitions to pull.

### Q7: **B. Postponement**
Delaying final differentiation reduces SKU-level inventory risk while still serving regions. The HP printer story is the canonical example.

### Q8: **C. Lower freight costs**
The five bullwhip causes are forecast updating, order batching, price fluctuations, rationing/shortage gaming, and long lead times.

### Q9: **B. RAM is the qualifier; battery life is the winner**
Qualifiers get you considered; winners earn the sale. RAM is the minimum bar; battery life differentiates.

### Q10: **C. Flexibility**
Cost, quality, delivery, flexibility, and innovation are the five priorities. Flexibility covers volume, mix, and new-product agility.

### Q11: **D. Engineer-to-Order**
A one-of-a-kind dam is designed per project — the CODP sits at the design phase. Longest customer wait, lowest inventory.

### Q12: **C. Spans multiple firms**
Value chain (Porter) is internal to one firm. Supply chain spans many firms.

### Q13: **A. Customer-facing reliability**
Perfect order is a reliability metric. Cycle time is responsiveness. Cash-to-cash is asset.

### Q14: **B. Days of working capital tied up**
Cash-to-cash = DIO + DSO − DPO. Lower (even negative, like Amazon) is better.

### Q15: **C. Risk-hedging**
Pooling capacity across suppliers to absorb supply shocks is the textbook risk-hedging behavior.

### Q16: **B. Demand and supply uncertainty**
Lee's two axes. Cost/quality describe priorities, not strategy choice.

### Q17: **B. Cost efficiency**
Functional products (salt, sugar, milk) demand low-cost efficient supply chains.

### Q18: **C. Risk-hedging**
Stable demand + uncertain supply = pool resources, hold strategic reserves.

### Q19: **B. Share POS data with upstream partners**
Information sharing (CPFR, VMI) attacks the root cause (independent re-forecasting). Safety stock and batching make bullwhip worse.

### Q20: **B. ATO**
Components are held; assembly is triggered by order. Dell's classic model.

### Q21: **A. Reshoring / nearshoring**
Bringing production closer (Mexico for the US market) is nearshoring; back to the home country is reshoring.

### Q22: **C. Enable**
Enable was added in SCOR 11 to cover supporting infrastructure (IT, governance, talent, risk).

### Q23: **B. Support overall competitive advantage**
Strategy alignment ensures the SC supports the company's chosen way of competing (speed, cost, differentiation, etc.).

### Q24: **C. Raw materials**
The CODP sits where the chain switches from forecast to order. With Make-to-Order, the only thing pre-positioned is raw material.

### Q25: **C. Push uses forecasts; pull uses actual demand**
Push = produce based on a plan/forecast. Pull = produce only when a customer signal arrives.

### Q26: **B. On-time, complete, undamaged, correct documentation**
The four conditions of a perfect order. Score is the product of the four percentages.
