# Module 10 Quiz — تسلط و روانی (Mastery & Fluency)

**25 Multiple Choice Questions | Accent Neutralization, Interference Patterns, Oratory, Storytelling, Debate, Newspapers, C2 Self-Assessment**

---

**1.** At C2 level, the primary goal of Persian language study shifts from:

- A) Learning new vocabulary to memorizing grammar rules
- B) Correctness to naturalness — thinking in Persian, not translating
- C) Formal writing to colloquial speech only
- D) Reading to only listening

**Answer: B** — The C2 milestone is when you stop translating and begin *thinking* in Persian — when proverbs surface before their English equivalents, when you laugh at jokes before processing them.

---

**2.** Which phonological feature do Urdu speakers need to REMOVE when speaking Persian?

- A) Long vowels (آ vs ا)
- B) The trilled 'r' sound
- C) Retroflex consonants (ٹ، ڈ، ڑ)
- D) The letter خ (kh)

**Answer: C** — Persian has NO retroflex consonants — a major feature of Urdu that must be consciously eliminated. The retroflex ٹ, ڈ, ڑ sounds of Urdu do not exist in Persian phonology.

---

**3.** In Persian phonology, the vowel length distinction **آ vs ا** is:

- A) Not meaningful — they sound the same
- B) Only relevant in classical poetry
- C) Meaningful — long vs. short vowel distinction changes words
- D) Only important in formal writing

**Answer: C** — Vowel length is meaningful in Persian. آ (long ā) vs ا (short a) can distinguish different words. Urdu speakers must master this distinction for accurate Persian.

---

**4.** The Persian sentence stress pattern is best described as:

- A) Stress-timed like English
- B) Syllable-timed — more even rhythm
- C) Final-syllable stress like French
- D) Tonal like Mandarin Chinese

**Answer: B** — Persian is syllable-timed (stress falls more predictably, typically penultimate syllable) — not stress-timed like English. This affects the overall rhythm of speech.

---

**5.** Which of the following is a grammatical calque (interference error) to AVOID in Persian?

- A) به نظرم این درست نیست
- B) من فکر می‌کنم که این درست نیست (translating "I think that...")
- C) به نظرم درست نیست
- D) تابستان به پارک رفتم

**Answer: B** — من فکر می‌کنم که این درست نیست is a calque of the English "I think that..." — native Persian speakers prefer the more compact به نظرم این درست نیست ("In my view, this isn't correct").

---

**6.** In natural Persian, the sentence **در تابستان من به پارک رفتم** can be improved by:

- A) Adding more adjectives
- B) Dropping redundant subject من: تابستان به پارک رفتم
- C) Using the passive voice
- D) Adding داشتم for emphasis

**Answer: B** — Persian frequently drops the subject pronoun (pro-drop language). تابستان به پارک رفتم = "In summer, [I] went to the park" — more natural without the explicit من.

---

**7.** A traditional Persian oration (خطابت) typically OPENS with:

- A) A list of agenda items
- B) An apology for speaking
- C) بسم الله / با نام خدا (Opening with God's name)
- D) A joke to warm up the audience

**Answer: C** — Persian formal oratory traditionally opens with بسم الله الرحمن الرحیم or با نام خدا — invoking God's name before beginning — this is a 1,200-year tradition.

---

**8.** In Persian oratory, **سؤال بلاغی** (*so'āl-e balāghi*) refers to:

- A) A question at the end for audience Q&A
- B) A rhetorical question that creates engagement without expecting an answer
- C) A question about the speaker's qualifications
- D) A survey of the audience's views

**Answer: B** — سؤال بلاغی = rhetorical question — a device in Persian oratory to create engagement. Example: "If one word could change the fate of a nation, what would that word be?"

---

**9.** A skilled Persian orator typically CLOSES with:

- A) A list of statistics
- B) An English phrase for international audiences
- C) A couplet from Hafiz or Rumi (استشهاد به شعر)
- D) A request for applause

**Answer: C** — استشهاد به شعر (quoting poetry) is the hallmark of an accomplished Persian speaker. Closing with a Hafiz or Rumi couplet is the highest mark of eloquence.

---

**10.** The traditional Persian storytelling opening **یکی بود یکی نبود** translates as:

- A) Once there was a king in ancient times
- B) In a land far away, long ago
- C) There was one, there was none (Once upon a time)
- D) Listen to this tale of wonder

**Answer: C** — یکی بود یکی نبود (*yaki bud yaki nabud*) = literally "There was one, there was none" — the Persian equivalent of "Once upon a time."

---

**11.** In Persian storytelling, the **historical present tense** (using present tense for past events) is used to:

- A) Indicate a habitual action in the past
- B) Create vivid re-enactment — bringing the story to life
- C) Express uncertainty about past events
- D) Show that the story is fictional

**Answer: B** — Using the present tense in Persian storytelling (می‌رود، می‌گوید instead of رفت، گفت) creates dramatic immediacy — the listener feels immersed in the unfolding action.

---

**12.** In the short C2 story **داستان: دانه‌ای از دانایی**, the wise man's answer about happiness consists of:

- A) Five pillars of Persian wisdom
- B) Three things: knowing you don't know, wishing to know, having patience
- C) A single word: صبر (patience)
- D) A quotation from Hafiz's Divan

**Answer: B** — سه چیز: بدانی که نمی‌دانی، بخواهی که بدانی، و صبر داشته باشی تا بدانی — "Three things: know that you don't know, wish to know, and have patience until you know."

---

**13.** In a Persian debate (مناظره), how do you express "I concede this point"?

- A) این استدلال قابل نقد است
- B) در نهایت شواهد نشان می‌دهد
- C) در این نکته حق با شماست
- D) موضع من این است که

**Answer: C** — در این نکته حق با شماست (*dar in nokte haqq bā shomāst*) = "On this point, you are right" — the formal Persian way to concede a point in debate.

---

**14.** When stating your position in a formal Persian debate, you would open with:

- A) شواهد نشان می‌دهد
- B) موضع من این است که
- C) درست است که ولی
- D) این استدلال قابل نقد است

**Answer: B** — موضع من این است که (*mozé'-e man in ast ke*) = "My position is that..." — the standard Persian formula for stating a debate position.

---

**15.** Persian newspaper convention: the verb in a headline typically appears:

- A) At the beginning of the sentence
- B) In the middle
- C) At the end of the sentence
- D) In a separate subheading

**Answer: C** — Persian headlines place the verb at the end (SOV word order), e.g., ایران توافق امضا کرد = "Iran agreement signed" (verb کرد at end). This mirrors standard Persian word order.

---

**16.** Which Persian newspaper is rated at the **C2 (complex)** register level?

- A) همشهری (Hamshahri)
- B) بی‌بی‌سی فارسی (BBC Persian)
- C) کیهان (Keyhan)
- D) اعتماد (Etemad)

**Answer: C** — کیهان (*Keyhan*) is rated C2 register level — it uses highly formal, complex Persian. BBC Persian and Etemad are rated C1; Hamshahri is B2/C1.

**17.** For C2 listening, which Iranian film is rated the MOST linguistically challenging?

- A) بچه‌های آسمان (Children of Heaven) — B2
- B) خانه‌ی دوست کجاست؟ (Where Is the Friend's House?) — B2
- C) جدایی نادر از سیمین (A Separation) — C1
- D) فروشنده (The Salesman) — C1/C2

**Answer: D** — فروشنده (*The Salesman*) by Farhadi is rated C1/C2 — it uses urban, rapid Persian with literary references, making it the most linguistically demanding of the recommended films.

**18.** Mohammad Reza Shajarian (محمدرضا شجریان) is recommended for Persian language learners because:

- A) He sings in Dari dialect
- B) He uses standard Persian with perfect diction in classical vocal music
- C) His songs contain modern slang and colloquial expressions
- D) He recites newspaper articles to music

**Answer: B** — Shajarian is the gold standard of Persian classical vocal music — standard Persian pronunciation, perfect diction, and classical texts (often Hafiz and Rumi) set to music.

**19.** The CEFR C2 Persian speaking benchmark includes the ability to:

- A) Read menus and follow basic conversations
- B) Discuss work and hobbies with some effort
- C) Give a prepared 10-minute formal Persian presentation fluently
- D) Understand simple Persian sentences

**Answer: C** — C2 speaking benchmark: giving a 10-minute prepared presentation in formal Persian fluently, switching registers (formal/informal) without thinking, using proverbs and idioms naturally.

**20.** Which word choice demonstrates more fluent, native-sounding Persian — the native word or the Arabic loan?

- A) جامعه (Arabic loan for "university") is preferred
- B) دانشگاه (native Persian compound "knowledge-place") is preferred
- C) Both are equally native-sounding
- D) یونیورسیتی (English loan) is the most prestigious

**Answer: B** — دانشگاه (دانش = knowledge + گاه = place) is the native Persian compound word for "university" — preferred by educated native speakers. جامعه = Arabic loan; یونیورسیتی = English loan.

**21.** In Persian, the native word **شادی** is preferred over the Arabic loan **فرح** because:

- A) شادی is easier to pronounce
- B) شادی carries warmer, more natural connotations in conversational Persian
- C) فرح is considered impolite
- D) شادی was borrowed from Turkish

**Answer: B** — شادی feels warmer and more natural in conversational Persian. فرح is correct but feels more formal/literary. Native speakers default to شادی for "happiness." Note: شادی/shādi is also used in Urdu!

**22.** **Shadowing** as an accent neutralization technique means:

- A) Reading Persian silently while listening
- B) Playing authentic Persian audio and repeating immediately after the speaker
- C) Recording yourself speaking and reviewing later
- D) Writing Persian while listening to pronunciation guides

**Answer: B** — Shadowing = playing authentic audio and repeating immediately after — this forces your mouth and brain to synchronize with native prosody, rhythm, and pronunciation patterns.

**23.** How many Persian speakers are there approximately in Iran?

- A) ~10 million
- B) ~35 million
- C) ~85 million
- D) ~200 million

**Answer: C** — Iran has approximately 85 million Persian speakers, making it the largest single Persian-speaking country. Total Persian speakers worldwide: 140+ million.

**24.** The phrase **تبادل زبانی** (*tabādol-e zabāni*) refers to:

- A) Translation services
- B) Language exchange — practicing with a native speaker
- C) Multilingual dictionary lookup
- D) Official Persian language certification

**Answer: B** — تبادل زبانی = language exchange — practiced on platforms like italki and Tandem. The recommended C2 practice: one hour Persian, one hour English/Urdu with a native partner.

**25.** Which statement BEST captures what C2 Persian mastery means for an Urdu speaker?

- A) Being able to write Persian emails with no spelling mistakes
- B) Recovering a linguistic birthright — the language of Iqbal, Ghalib, and the Mughal court that shaped Urdu civilization
- C) Passing a formal CEFR examination
- D) Understanding all BBC Persian news broadcasts

**Answer: B** — For Urdu speakers, C2 Persian is not just a language certificate — it is recovering the language of Iqbal's philosophy, Ghalib's self-critique, and the Mughal court that gave birth to Urdu itself. This is the deepest level of the Persian-Urdu connection.
