### Detailed Explanations

**1-C:** The passage states أنا من باكستان — "I am from Pakistan."

**2-C:** أدرس اللغة العربية — "I study the Arabic language."

**3-C:** أبي مدرّس — "My father is a teacher."

**4-B:** The text mentions أخي وأختي — "my brother and sister" — two siblings.

**5-B:** أسرة صغيرة = "a small family." صغيرة (ṣaghīra) = small/little.

**6-C:** ش (shīn) is a Sun letter. The Moon letters (where ل is pronounced) include ب، م، ق. Sun letters cause the ل to assimilate: الشمس = ash-shams, not al-shams.

**7-B:** Before a Sun letter, the ل assimilates: الشمس is pronounced ash-shams. The written form still shows ال but the ل sound disappears.

**8-B:** ض (ḍād) is considered uniquely Arabic — Arabic was historically called "the language of ḍād" (لغة الضاد) because no other language has this emphatic lateral sound.

**9-B:** The root of كتاب (kitāb = book) is ك-ت-ب (k-t-b = to write). The long ā vowel and the pattern kiCāC are secondary; the root letters are ك-ت-ب.

**10-C:** فتحة (fatḥa) is the short "a" vowel, written as a small diagonal line above the letter. It produces the "a" sound as in "bat" or Arabic كَتَبَ (kataba).

**11-B:** The standard response to السلام عليكم is وعليكم السلام (wa-ʻalaykum as-salām) — "And upon you peace."

**12-C:** صباح الخير (ṣabāḥ al-khayr) = Good morning. مساء الخير = Good evening/afternoon.

**13-B:** يسعدني (yusʻidunī) literally means "it makes me happy" or "I am pleased" — from the root س-ع-د (saʻada = to be happy/fortunate).

**14-D:** Both أنا اسمي فاطمة and اسمي فاطمة are correct and natural ways to say "My name is Fatima." The أنا is optional emphasis.

**15-B:** من فضلكَ is for a man; من فضلكِ (min faḍlaki, with kasra) is for a woman. Arabic pronouns and associated endings change by gender.

**16-C:** سبعة (sabʻa) = 7. خمسة = 5, ستة = 6, ثمانية = 8.

**17-D:** Both كم الساعة؟ and ما الوقت الآن؟ are correct ways to ask for the time. كم الساعة is more colloquial; ما الوقت الآن is slightly more formal.

**18-C:** الأربعاء (al-arbaʻāʼ) = Wednesday (literally "the four" — fourth day). الاثنين = Monday, الثلاثاء = Tuesday, الخميس = Thursday.

**19-B:** يناير (yanāyir) = January. This is a borrowed/Arabised form of the Latin/English "January." Other months use similar borrowed forms in MSA.

**20-C:** الثالثة والنصف = "the third and a half" = 3:30. والنصف (wa-an-niṣf) = "and a half."

**21-B:** بيت (bayt) = house/home. Note: البيت الأبيض = the White House; بيت الله = the House of God (Kaaba).

**22-D:** أحمر (aḥmar) = red. أزرق = blue, أصفر = yellow, أخضر = green.

**23-C:** طبيب (ṭabīb) = doctor/physician. Root: ط-ب-ب. طب (ṭibb) = medicine (the same root you know from الطب النبوي — Prophetic medicine).

**24-C:** ماء (māʼ) = water. حليب = milk, عصير = juice, شاي = tea.

**25-C:** مدرسة (madrasa) = school (primary/secondary). مكتبة = library, مستشفى = hospital, جامعة = university.

**26-A:** البيتُ كبيرٌ is the correct nominal sentence (جملة اسمية): subject (مبتدأ) in nominative case (ḍamma — ُ) + predicate (خبر) in nominative with tanwīn (ـٌ).

**27-C:** نحن (naḥnu) = We. هم = They (masculine), أنتم = You (plural masculine), هن = They (feminine).

**28-B:** هذا (hādhā) = this (for masculine nouns). هذه (hādhihi) = this (for feminine nouns). ذلك = that (masculine), تلك = that (feminine).

**29-B:** كان (kāna) is the past tense of "to be" — was/were. It is the most important Arabic verb and heads the group كان وأخواتها (kāna and its sisters).

**30-C:** أنا طالبٌ is a classic nominal sentence: subject أنا (I) + predicate طالبٌ (a student, nominative with tanwīn). Options A and B start with verbs or pronouns followed by verbs, making them verbal sentences. Option D is just a single verb with no sentence structure.
