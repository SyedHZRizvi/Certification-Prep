### Detailed Explanations

**1-B:** The passage opens by noting Arabic's unique status (مكانة فريدة), covers its historical contribution, then addresses modern challenges — a balanced argument, not alarmist or one-sided.

**2-B:** ازدواجية (izdiwājiyya) refers to diglossia — the coexistence of الفصحى (formal MSA) and العاميات (colloquial dialects). The passage calls this تعايش راسخ (a deeply rooted coexistence) and a rare phenomenon in linguistic history.

**3-C:** عبر الترجمات اللاتينية = "through Latin translations." This is the historical mechanism by which Greek-via-Arabic scholarship entered medieval Europe (the 12th-century Translation Movement).

**4-C:** بصمات (baṣamāt, sing. بصمة) = fingerprints / indelible traces/marks. The phrase بصماتٍ لا تُمحى = "traces that cannot be erased."

**5-B:** ظلام (ẓalām) = darkness; القرون الوسطى (al-qurūn al-wusṭā) = the Middle Ages. "The darkness of the Middle Ages" — the passage contrasts Arab scholarly flourishing with European stagnation in that period.

**6-B:** عزوف (ʻuzūf) from the root ع-ز-ف means "turning away from / avoiding / abstaining from." The phrase عزوف بعض الشباب عن الكتابة الرسمية = some young Arabs turning away from formal writing.

**7-B:** Three challenges: (1) انتشار العاميات = spread of dialects; (2) سيطرة الإنجليزية على المجالات التقنية = dominance of English in technical fields; (3) عزوف بعض الشباب عن الكتابة الرسمية = some Arab youth turning away from formal writing.

**8-B:** تُجدّد نفسها (tujaddid nafsahā) = "it renews/reinvents itself"; بهدوء (bi-hudūʼ) = "quietly/calmly." The Arabic uses Form II reflexive meaning via نفسها (its own self).

**9-B:** The examples algebra (الجبر), algorithm (الخوارزمي), alcohol (الكحول), zenith (السمت), nadir (النظير) are cited as بصماتٍ لا تُمحى في المفردات العلمية الغربية — "indelible traces in Western scientific vocabulary."

**10-C:** The author acknowledges Arabic's unique greatness AND its modern challenges (diglossia, English dominance, youth disengagement) while ending optimistically with الفصحى renewing itself. This is analytically nuanced, not alarmist or purely celebratory.

**11-B:** درساً has tanwīn fatḥa (ـاً), marking it as an indefinite noun in the accusative case (منصوب) — specifically the direct object (مفعول به) of the verb كتب.

**12-B:** إن + past verb (ذهبتَ) is the standard real conditional. لو would make it contrary-to-fact. Real conditional with إن: "If you go/went to the library, you will/would find the book."

**13-C:** Form X (استفعل) pattern: استعلم (istaʻlama) = to inquire / seek information / make inquiries. أعلم = Form IV (causative: to inform). عَلَّم = Form II (to teach).

**14-C:** ذهاب (dhahāb) is the maṣdar (verbal noun/gerund) of ذهب. Pattern: فَعَال (faʻāl) is a common maṣdar pattern. ذاهب = active participle (going); مذهوب = passive participle.

**15-C:** رغم ذلك (raghm dhālika) = "despite that / nevertheless" — a concessive conjunction. It acknowledges a preceding point before introducing a contrasting idea.

**16-B:** الحال (al-ḥāl) is the circumstantial qualifier — an indefinite accusative noun or adjective describing the state of the subject or object at the time of the action. Example: جاء الرجل راكباً = the man came riding (راكباً is the ḥāl).

**17-C:** After the preposition إلى (to/towards), nouns always take the genitive case (مجرور — kasra): إلى المدرسةِ. Prepositions in Arabic always govern the genitive.

**18-B:** مُعَلِّم (muʻallim) is the active participle (اسم فاعل) of Form II verb عَلَّم (ʻallama = to teach). The Form II active participle pattern is مُفَعِّل (mufaʻʻil).

**19-B:** The passive (بناء المجهول) is formed by changing the vowel pattern: فتحة on the first root letter becomes ضمة, and the vowel before the last root letter becomes كسرة. كَتَبَ (kataba) → كُتِبَ (kutiba = it was written).

**20-B:** النواسخ (an-nawāsikh = "abrogators") include كان وأخواتها (kāna and sisters — which put the predicate in accusative) and إن وأخواتها (inna and sisters — which put the subject in accusative). They "abrogate" (abrogate = to cancel) the normal nominative predicate.

**21-B:** لولا + nominative noun expresses "were it not for X...": لولا اللهُ لهلكنا = Were it not for God, we would have perished. The noun after لولا is nominative (subject of a suppressed verb).

**22-B:** A key Arabic grammar rule: non-human plurals (including broken plurals of things/animals) are treated grammatically as feminine singular. So الكتبُ (books) takes the feminine singular adjective الكبيرةُ (not الكبيرون or الكِبار for human males).

**23-B:** استعارة (istiʻāra) = metaphor — one of the central devices of Arabic rhetoric (علم البيان). In simile (تشبيه), you say "he is LIKE a lion" using كـ. In استعارة, you say "I saw a lion" (meaning a brave man) — the comparison word is absent.

**24-B:** رأيت بحراً من الكرم = "I saw a sea of generosity." The word بحراً (sea) is used metaphorically (استعارة تصريحية) to replace "a very generous person" — an explicit metaphor where the original meaning is named.

**25-B:** علم البيان (ʻilm al-bayān) = the science of clarity/expression — deals with simile (تشبيه), metaphor (استعارة), and metonymy (كناية). The other branches of بلاغة are علم المعاني (meaning/syntax) and علم البديع (ornamentation).

**26-B:** الطباق (al-ṭibāq) = antithesis — pairing opposites for rhetorical effect. Example: وجعلنا الليل والنهار آيتين (We made night and day two signs) — night and day are opposites united for rhetorical contrast.

**27-B:** بحر الطويل (baḥr al-ṭawīl) = the "long metre" — the most used of the 16 classical Arabic poetic metres. It uses the pattern: فعولن مفاعيلن فعولن مفاعيلن (per half-line). Used by Imru' al-Qays, al-Mutanabbī, and most classical poets.

**28-B:** قِيلَ وقالَ (qīla wa-qāla) is an idiomatic expression meaning hearsay, gossip, or rumour — things said without verified source. قِيلَ is the passive of قال = "it was said."

**29-B:** السجع (as-sajʻ) = rhymed prose — endings that rhyme or have the same rhythmic pattern in consecutive clauses. Used extensively in the Quran (e.g., the openings of many suras), classical Arabic letters, and the maqāmāt genre.

**30-A:** من صبر ظفر (man ṣabara ẓafira) = "Whoever is patient, wins/triumphs." صبر (ṣabara) = was patient; ظفر (ẓafira) = won/triumphed/achieved. One of the most famous Arabic proverbs.

**31-B:** الحمدُ لله رب العالمين is a nominal sentence (جملة اسمية): الحمدُ (praise — subject, nominative) + لله رب العالمين (belongs to/for God, Lord of the worlds — prepositional phrase as predicate). There is no verb "is" written — it is implied.

**32-C:** The preposition لـ (li- = for/to) governs the genitive case (kasra): لِلطالبِ (for the student), لِلكتابِ (for the book). All Arabic prepositions take the genitive.

**33-C:** إياك نعبد — placing إياك (You / the object) BEFORE the verb نعبد (we worship) is a grammatical fronting technique that creates exclusivity: "You ALONE we worship" (not others). This is the device of التخصيص أو الاختصاص بالتقديم — fronting the object for specifying "only you."

**34-B:** اسم الفاعل (ism al-fāʻil) = active participle — the doer of the action. Pattern for Form I: فاعل (fāʻil). Examples: كاتب (writer), ذاهب (goer), عالم (scholar/knower).

**35-B:** الرحمان (ar-Raḥmān) follows the فَعلان (faʻlān) pattern — an intensive/hyperbolic adjective indicating comprehensive, all-encompassing quality. رَحَم = to be merciful → رَحمان = the intensively/comprehensively merciful.

**36-A:** أفلا تعقلون = "Do you not reason/understand?" (rhetorical question of reproach). The أ prefix is the interrogative hamza; فلا = "then do not" (combined with لا negation); تعقلون = you (plural) reason. Root: ع-ق-ل.

**37-B:** لبيك (labbayk) = "Here I am at Your service" — the response of total presence and devotion. Used in the Hajj talbiyya: لبيك اللهم لبيك (Here I am, O God, here I am). Grammatically a dual form expressing repeated response.

**38-B:** الإعجاز القرآني rests on the linguistic argument that the Quran's Arabic is beyond human composition — occupying a unique space between poetry and prose, with unmatched precision, rhythm, and multilayered meaning. The Quran challenges (تحدي) humanity and jinn to produce even one sura like it (سورة البقرة 2:23).

**39-B:** الموضوع: (al-mawḍūʻ:) = "Subject:" — the standard field label for the subject line in formal Arabic email and letter formats.

**40-C:** رؤية 2030 = Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 — Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman's national transformation programme (launched 2016) to diversify the economy beyond oil, develop tourism, and modernise society.

**41-B:** واسطة (wāsiṭa) literally means "middle/intermediary" but culturally refers to the use of personal connections, nepotism, or influence (similar to the Spanish concept of "enchufismo"). It is a significant feature of Gulf and broader Arab business culture.

**42-A:** الحوكمة المؤسسية (al-ḥawkama al-muʼassasiyya) = corporate governance. حوكمة is a modern Arabic coinage from English "governance."

**43-B:** شركة ذات مسؤولية محدودة (sharika dhāt masʼūliyya maḥdūda) = LLC — Limited Liability Company. Literally: "a company with limited liability." This is the standard legal term across Arab jurisdictions.

**44-B:** في إطار (fī iṭār) = "within the framework of / in the context of." اطار = frame/framework. Commonly used in formal Arabic to contextualise a statement: في إطار الإصلاحات الاقتصادية = within the framework of economic reforms.

**45-B:** الناتج المحلي الإجمالي = GDP (Gross Domestic Product). الناتج = output/product; المحلي = local/domestic; الإجمالي = gross/total.

**46-A:** أوافقكم الرأي (uwāfiqukum ar-raʼy) = "I agree with you (pl.) in opinion." This is the formal register. أوافق = I agree (Form III of وفق); الرأي = the opinion/view.

**47-B:** اطلبوا العلم ولو في الصين = "Seek knowledge even if [you must go] to China." China represented the farthest reaches of the known world — the proverb urges seeking knowledge wherever it is found, no matter how distant.

**48-B:** الضيافة (aḍ-ḍiyāfa) = hospitality — one of the supreme virtues in Arab cultural tradition. A guest (ضيف) must be received generously; refusing to host or hosting poorly is considered deeply shameful. This cultural value is referenced in classical poetry and is central to understanding Arab business and social interactions.

**49-C:** لا حول ولا قوة إلا بالله (lā ḥawla wa-lā quwwata illā billāh) = "There is no power or strength except through God." Called الحوقلة (al-ḥawqala) — said when facing difficulty, hardship, or something overwhelming. حول = power/ability; قوة = strength.

**50-A:** تعقلون is from the root ع-ق-ل (ʻ-q-l) = to reason / have intellect. عقل (ʻaql) = reason/intellect is central to Quranic epistemology — the Quran repeatedly urges تعقّل (reasoning). The Form I present plural is تعقلون (you reason/understand).

### Short Answer Model Answers

**SA-1 Model Answer (Root System):**
The Arabic root ك-ت-ب (k-t-b) carries the meaning of "writing." From it derive: كَتَبَ (kataba) = he wrote; كِتَاب (kitāb) = book; كَاتِب (kātib) = writer/clerk; مَكْتَبَة (maktaba) = library/bookshop; مَكْتَب (maktab) = office/desk; كِتَابَة (kitāba) = writing (noun); مَكْتُوب (maktūb) = written/letter; مُكَاتَبَة (mukātaba) = correspondence; كُتَّاب (kuttāb) = traditional Quranic school; كَتَّبَ (kattaba) = to cause to write (Form II). This demonstrates how one root generates an entire semantic family.

**SA-2 Model Answer (Formal Email):**
الأستاذ الفاضل [الاسم]، تحية طيبة وبعد، أكتب إليك بشأن مشروع الميزانية السنوية الذي نحن بصدد مراجعته. أودّ اقتراح اجتماع خلال الأسبوع القادم لمناقشة البنود الرئيسية وتحديد الأولويات. أنا متاح يوم الاثنين أو الثلاثاء من الساعة العاشرة صباحاً. برجاء إعلامي بما يناسبك. مع خالص التحية والتقدير، [الاسم].

**SA-3 Model Answer (Rhetoric):**
العلمُ نورٌ والجهلُ ظلامٌ = "Knowledge is light and ignorance is darkness." This uses الطباق (al-ṭibāq) — antithesis: العلم (knowledge) is contrasted with الجهل (ignorance); نور (light) with ظلام (darkness). Additionally, it uses الاستعارة (metaphor) — knowledge is not literally light, nor ignorance literally darkness; light and darkness are metaphors for guidance and confusion respectively.

**SA-4 Model Answer (Conjugation):**
كَتَبتُ (katabt) = I wrote / كَتَبتَ (katabta) = you wrote (masc.) / كَتَبتِ (katabti) = you wrote (fem.) / كَتَبَ (kataba) = he wrote / كَتَبَتْ (katabat) = she wrote / كَتَبنَا (katabnā) = we wrote

**SA-5 Model Answer (Fusha vs. ʻAmmiyya):**
الفصحى (Fusha) is Modern Standard Arabic — used in newspapers, official documents, education, and formal broadcasting across all 22 Arab countries. It is mutually intelligible to all educated Arabs. العامية (ʻAmmiyya) is colloquial Arabic, varying significantly by country. Example: "I want water" in Fusha: أريد ماءً (urīdu māʼan). In Egyptian Arabic: أنا عايز ماية (ana ʻāyiz māya). In Moroccan Darija: بغيت لماء (bghīt l-māʼ). A Saudi and a Moroccan would use Fusha to communicate formally, as their dialects differ too much for casual comprehension.
