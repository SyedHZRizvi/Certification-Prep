## 🎯 What Is a Stakeholder?

Per PMI:
> *"An individual, group, or organization that may affect, be affected by, or perceive itself to be affected by a decision, activity, or outcome of a project."*

Translation: **anyone who cares about, is impacted by, or has influence over the project.**

### Examples of Stakeholders:
- 👔 Sponsor
- 👥 Team
- 🏢 Functional managers
- 💼 Customers / users
- 🤝 Vendors
- 🏛️ Regulators
- 🌍 Community
- 💰 Investors
- 📰 Media (in major projects)

---

## 🔄 The 4 Stakeholder Processes

### 1. Identify Stakeholders
- 📋 Find everyone who has a stake
- 🛠️ Tools: Stakeholder Analysis, Power/Interest Grid, Stakeholder Cube
- 📋 Output: **Stakeholder Register**

### 2. Plan Stakeholder Engagement
- 🎯 How to engage each stakeholder
- 📋 Output: **Stakeholder Engagement Plan**

### 3. Manage Stakeholder Engagement
- 💬 Communicate
- 🤝 Build trust
- 🚧 Address concerns

### 4. Monitor Stakeholder Engagement
- 👀 Track engagement levels
- 🔄 Adjust approach as needed

---

## 📋 Stakeholder Register

The central document. Includes:
- 🆔 Identification (name, role, contact)
- 📊 Assessment (requirements, expectations, influence, interest)
- 📂 Classification (internal/external, friend/foe, etc.)

🎯 **Continuously updated** — stakeholders change, expectations evolve.

---

## 📊 Stakeholder Analysis Tools

### Power-Interest Grid (most-tested)
A 2×2 matrix:

```
              INTEREST →
            LOW            HIGH
       ┌────────────────────────────┐
  HIGH │  Keep        │  Manage     │ ← (engage closely)
       │  Satisfied   │  Closely    │
POWER  ├──────────────┼─────────────┤
       │   Monitor    │  Keep       │
   LOW │  (min effort)│  Informed   │
       └────────────────────────────┘
```

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "High power, high interest stakeholder. How to engage?" → **Manage closely.**

### Power-Influence Grid
Similar but uses Power × Influence (active engagement) instead of interest.

### Salience Model
3 dimensions:
- **Power** — ability to enforce
- **Legitimacy** — appropriate involvement
- **Urgency** — time-pressure to act

7 categories of stakeholders based on which dimensions apply.

## 🎯 Engagement Levels (KEY!)

Per PMBOK, stakeholders fall into 5 levels:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know about project |
| **Resistant** | Knows + against it |
| **Neutral** | Knows + neutral |
| **Supportive** | Knows + supports |
| **Leading** | Actively driving success |

### Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix
For each stakeholder:
- C = Current level
- D = Desired level

🎯 **Goal:** Move stakeholders toward "Leading" (or at minimum, "Supportive").

---

## 🎯 Stakeholder Strategies

### For Resistant Stakeholders:
- Listen to concerns
- Address misunderstandings
- Show how project benefits them
- Engage them in decisions
- Be patient

### For Supportive Stakeholders:
- Keep informed
- Leverage their influence
- Show appreciation
- Don't take for granted

### For Neutral Stakeholders:
- Provide regular updates
- Identify what would move them to "supportive"

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "PM identifies a resistant stakeholder."
- ❌ Wrong: Ignore them; focus on supporters
- ❌ Wrong: Force compliance
- ✅ Right: **Engage to understand concerns + address them**

## 🎯 The Stakeholder Cube

3D version of Power-Interest:
- Power (high/low)
- Interest (high/low)
- Attitude (positive/negative)

= 8 categories

🎯 Less commonly tested than 2D grids.

## 🏢 Internal vs External Stakeholders

### Internal:
- Sponsor
- Team
- Functional managers
- Other employees

### External:
- Customers
- Vendors
- Regulators
- Community
- Competitors

🎯 Internal often have more direct influence; external may have legal or reputational power.

---

## 🎯 Sponsor's Role (Important!)

The **Sponsor** is a special stakeholder:
- 💰 Provides funding
- 📜 Approves Project Charter
- 🛡️ Champions the project
- ⚠️ Removes top-level impediments
- 🎯 Defines success criteria

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "Major obstacle outside the team's control. PM has tried everything."
- ✅ **Escalate to Sponsor.**

The PM should NOT escalate trivial things to the sponsor — only major impediments the team can't address.

---

## 🤝 Stakeholder Engagement in Agile

In agile, stakeholder engagement is **continuous**:
- 🎁 **Sprint Reviews** = stakeholder demos
- 💬 **Continuous feedback** loop
- 🔄 **Backlog refinement** with key stakeholders
- 📊 **Transparent metrics** (burndown, velocity)

🎯 Agile actively involves stakeholders; predictive may engage them more at gates.

---

## ⚠️ Common Stakeholder Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Identifying stakeholders only at the start
- ❌ Treating all stakeholders the same
- ❌ Communicating only with supportive stakeholders
- ❌ Ignoring "soft" stakeholders (community, public)
- ❌ Failing to update the register as new stakeholders emerge

## 📊 Case Study — Sydney Opera House (1957–1973)

**Situation.** The Sydney Opera House began with a design competition won in 1957 by Danish architect Jørn Utzon. The original cost estimate was **AUD 7 million** and the planned completion was 1963 — a 4-year build. The project's sponsor was the New South Wales government, with Premier Joseph Cahill as the political champion. Stakeholders included: NSW government (multiple, changing administrations), the Opera House Trust, Utzon and his Danish design firm, structural engineers (Ove Arup), the Sydney Symphony Orchestra and Opera Australia (end users), Sydney's harbor-using public, the construction unions, and the international architecture press.

**Decision.** Project leadership made several stakeholder-management decisions that became canonical examples of *how not to*: (1) the NSW government decided to start construction in 1959 — before the structural engineering for the iconic sail-shaped roof was solved (Utzon and Arup worked out the geometry only in 1961, two years into construction); (2) Premier Cahill died in office October 1959, and successive premiers (Heffron, Renshaw, Askin) each had different priorities, communication channels, and risk tolerances — but the project had no formal stakeholder-engagement plan to handle administration changes; (3) in 1965, the incoming Askin government's Public Works Minister Davis Hughes (replacing the Cahill-Heffron lineage) initiated direct cost interventions, restructured payment arrangements, and effectively pushed Utzon out (Utzon resigned February 1966 and never set foot in Australia again); (4) end-user stakeholders (the orchestra and opera company) were brought in late, leading to interior redesigns that mismatched the exterior's vision; (5) the construction unions were not formally engaged as stakeholders despite their sustained influence over schedule.

**Outcome.** Final cost: approximately **AUD 102 million** — about **1,357% over budget** in nominal terms. Final completion: 1973 — **10 years late**. Utzon's exterior design was completed, but the interior (which Utzon had envisioned with specific acoustic and aesthetic logic) was redesigned by Peter Hall and others under government direction; the resulting interior compromised the acoustic performance for which the Opera House was supposed to be celebrated. Utzon's exclusion was, in international architectural opinion, one of the great cultural-management failures of the 20th century. He was awarded the Pritzker Prize in 2003 (still alive then) but never received an apology in person. The building was UNESCO World Heritage listed in 2007 — a testament to the design — but the project's PM history is taught as a cautionary case in stakeholder, governance, and sponsor-change management at every major project-management school.

**Lesson for the exam / for practitioners.** The Sydney Opera House is the canonical example of **what happens when stakeholder identification stops at project kickoff**. PMBOK 7's Stakeholders principle (§3.3) and the Identify Stakeholders → Plan Engagement → Manage → Monitor cycle test this. On the exam, when you see scenarios like *"a new political sponsor enters mid-project"* or *"a previously unknown end-user community emerges"*, the answer is *not* "continue as planned." It is *"re-execute Identify Stakeholders, update the Stakeholder Register, re-plan engagement, and renegotiate the Charter terms if needed."* The Opera House also tests the limits of **Stewardship**: Utzon's resignation was a foreseeable outcome of weak stakeholder governance, and the project lost the technical authority needed to deliver to vision. **Stakeholder identification is continuous; the register is a living document; and the loss of a key stakeholder is a project risk, not a personnel matter.**

**Discussion (Socratic).**
- Q1: Utzon's resignation was triggered by political stakeholder change, not technical failure. What stakeholder-governance mechanisms (e.g., Charter clauses, steering-committee composition, mediation pathways) would have protected the design authority across multiple government administrations?
- Q2: End-users (orchestra, opera company) were engaged late. Whose responsibility was that — the PM's, the sponsor's, the architect's? Defend a position using PMBOK 7's Stewardship and Stakeholders principles.
- Q3: The unions exercised sustained influence but were not in the Stakeholder Register. Under what conditions should a PM include "informal-influence" stakeholders in the formal register, and what are the trade-offs?

## 💬 Discussion — Socratic prompts

1. **Mitchell-Agle-Wood Salience Model.** The Salience Model (1997) classifies stakeholders by Power, Legitimacy, and Urgency. For a controversial wind-farm project, classify the local fishing community, the federal regulator, the financiers, and the press. Where does each sit, and what engagement strategy follows?
2. **Stakeholder engagement matrix as a living tool.** Most teams build the matrix at kickoff and never revisit it. Design a quarterly ritual that keeps the matrix accurate without turning into bureaucracy. Cite PMBOK 7's Monitor Stakeholder Engagement process.
3. **Sponsor change mid-project.** A project loses its sponsor mid-execution (promoted out, fired, retires). What's your 30-day plan to re-anchor the project? Cite Module 02's communication frameworks.
4. **"Resistant" stakeholder = wrong stakeholder, or wrong engagement?** A resistant stakeholder may signal a project flaw, not stakeholder pathology. How do you tell the difference, and what's your obligation under PMI's Code of Ethics if the resistant stakeholder is right?
5. **Stakeholder ethics in AI projects.** A facial-recognition project for a city has affected residents who never consented to be stakeholders. Do they belong in the register? Defend an answer using the PMI Code of Ethics and the GDPR/AI Act framing.
