# PMP Module 1: Foundations 🏛️

> **Why this module matters:** Sets the mental model for everything else. Get this wrong, and the situational questions will trip you up across all 10 modules.

---

## 🏗️ A Story: Two Construction Sites

**Site A** is in 1985. The project manager has a Gantt chart. He commands. He controls. He has weekly status meetings where everyone reports up. He fights every scope change like a lawyer. The project takes 3 years and goes 40% over budget. He blames the architects.

**Site B** is in 2025. The project manager has a Kanban board on a wall. She walks the site daily, asking workers: *"What's blocking you?"* When a customer wants a design change, she discusses tradeoffs with the team and stakeholders openly. She protects the team's focus. The project finishes on time, with happy stakeholders.

**The PMP-2021 exam wants you to be Site B's PM.** Period.

## 📖 What Is a Project?

Per PMI:
> *"A temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result."*

3 keywords:

- 🕒 **Temporary** — has a beginning and end
- 🎁 **Unique** — produces something new
- 📦 **Result** — product, service, or outcome

### Project vs Operations
| Project | Operations |
|---------|------------|
| Temporary | Ongoing |
| Unique deliverable | Repetitive |
| Has end date | No end |
| Building a new app | Maintaining an existing one |
| Constructing a bridge | Charging tolls |

🎯 **Exam tip:** "Is X a project?" Look for: temporary + unique outcome.

---

## 🧩 What Is Project Management?

Per PMI:
> *"The application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet project requirements."*

In plain English: **getting the right people, doing the right things, in the right order, to deliver value.**

## 🎭 The PMP Triangle: Project Performance Domains

The new PMP (since 2021) is structured around **8 Project Performance Domains** (replacing the old "5 Process Groups + 10 Knowledge Areas" structure of PMBOK 6).

### The 8 Performance Domains:

1. 👥 **Stakeholders** — Identifying, engaging, managing
2. 🤝 **Team** — Building, leading, supporting
3. 🛠️ **Development Approach & Life Cycle** — Predictive/Agile/Hybrid choice
4. 📐 **Planning** — Coordinating activities
5. 📦 **Project Work** — Doing the work
6. 🎁 **Delivery** — Producing value/outcomes
7. 📊 **Measurement** — Performance tracking
8. ⚠️ **Uncertainty** — Risk, ambiguity, change

🎯 **Memorize these 8.** They're the spine of the new PMP.

---

## 🌈 The 12 Project Management Principles (PMBOK 7)

These replace the old "10 Knowledge Areas" — they're principles, not processes.

1. **Stewardship** — Be diligent, respectful, caring
2. **Team** — Create a collaborative team environment
3. **Stakeholders** — Effectively engage stakeholders
4. **Value** — Focus on value
5. **Systems Thinking** — Recognize, evaluate, respond to system interactions
6. **Leadership** — Demonstrate leadership behaviors
7. **Tailoring** — Tailor based on context
8. **Quality** — Build quality into processes & deliverables
9. **Complexity** — Navigate complexity
10. **Risk** — Optimize risk responses
11. **Adaptability & Resilience** — Embrace change
12. **Change** — Enable change to achieve envisioned future state

🧠 Memory aid: **STSV-SLT-QCRA-C** (Stewardship, Team, Stakeholders, Value, Systems thinking, Leadership, Tailoring, Quality, Complexity, Risk, Adaptability, Change)

You don't need to memorize the order. Just recognize the principles.

## 🎯 The Three Project Approaches

### Predictive (Waterfall)
- 📅 Plan everything upfront
- 🔄 Sequential phases
- 📋 Detailed scope/schedule/cost baselines
- ✅ Best for: stable requirements, well-known work
- 🏗️ Examples: Construction, manufacturing, regulated industries

### Agile (Iterative & Adaptive)
- 🌱 Plan as you learn
- 🔁 Iterations / sprints
- 🎯 Embrace change
- ✅ Best for: complex/uncertain work, evolving requirements
- 💻 Examples: Software, product development, R&D

### Hybrid (Mix)
- 🎭 Combines predictive + agile
- 📐 Some phases predictive, others agile
- ✅ Best for: complex projects with mixed certainty
- 🏥 Examples: Hospital construction (predictive) + IT system (agile)

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "What approach should the team use?" — depends on:

- Requirement stability (stable = predictive)
- Customer involvement (high = agile)
- Risk profile (high uncertainty = agile)
- Regulatory needs (regulated = predictive often)

## 📜 The PMP Code of Ethics & Professional Conduct

🎯 ~5-10 questions on this. **Memorize the 4 values:**

### 1. Responsibility
- Take ownership of your decisions
- Disclose conflicts of interest
- Don't engage in fraud or misuse

### 2. Respect
- Treat people with consideration
- Listen, understand
- Conduct yourself professionally
- Honor different cultures

### 3. Fairness
- Make impartial decisions
- Equal opportunity
- No favoritism
- Disclose conflicts of interest

### 4. Honesty
- Truth in communications
- No deception
- Take ownership of mistakes

🎯 **Common ethics traps:**
- A friend offers you a "gift" to influence a vendor decision → **Refuse + Disclose**
- You realize you reported wrong status → **Correct it immediately**
- You see a coworker behaving unethically → **Address it; report if necessary**
- You're asked to do something you believe is unethical → **Refuse + escalate**

## 👤 The Project Manager's Role

### Core Responsibilities:

- 🤝 Lead and support the team
- 👥 Manage stakeholder expectations
- 📋 Plan, execute, monitor, close projects
- 🎯 Deliver value
- 📊 Communicate (PMs spend 90% of time communicating!)
- 🛡️ Mitigate risks
- 🎓 Foster team development
- 🔄 Adapt approaches as needed

### What PMs Do (Time Allocation):

- 📞 Communication: ~90% of time
- 🤔 Decision-making: small but critical %
- 📅 Planning: continuous
- 🛠️ Execution: by team, monitored by PM

### What PMs DON'T Do:

- ❌ Do all the work themselves
- ❌ Tell every team member what to do
- ❌ Hide problems from stakeholders
- ❌ Make all decisions alone
- ❌ Stick rigidly to a 12-month-old plan

## 🎯 PMI Talent Triangle (3 skill areas)

Per PMI's PMP framework:

```
       Ways of Working
     (technical PM skills)
              ↑
              |
  Power Skills ←→ Business Acumen
  (leadership)    (strategic)
```

You need ALL three. The exam tests all three.

🎯 **Recent updates** — formerly "Technical, Leadership, Strategic" — now refreshed:

- **Ways of Working** — predictive, agile, hybrid PM skills
- **Power Skills** — leadership, communication, conflict resolution
- **Business Acumen** — strategy, finance, industry awareness

## 🌍 Project Environment & Influences

### Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEFs)
External or organizational factors that influence the project (mostly unchangeable):

- Organizational culture
- Government regulations
- Market conditions
- Available skills/staff
- Project management info systems

### Organizational Process Assets (OPAs)
Internal artifacts the team uses:

- Templates
- Historical records / lessons learned
- Policies and procedures
- Knowledge repositories

🎯 **Exam pattern:** Question references "templates and procedures" → OPAs. References "regulations and culture" → EEFs.

---

## 🏢 Organizational Structures

Affects how PMs operate:

### Functional Organization
- 🏛️ Traditional hierarchy
- 📊 Manager has authority
- 🤝 PM has LITTLE authority
- 🐢 Slow decision-making for projects

### Matrix Organization (3 sub-types)
- **Weak matrix:** PM has limited authority
- **Balanced matrix:** PM and functional manager share authority
- **Strong matrix:** PM has high authority

### Projectized Organization
- 🚀 Project-first culture
- 📊 PM has FULL authority
- ⏱️ Fast project decisions
- 🔄 Resources reallocated as projects start/end

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "PM has full authority over budget and team..." → Projectized. "Functional manager controls resources..." → Functional/weak matrix.

---

## 💼 PMO (Project Management Office)

A central office that supports project management:

### 3 Types of PMO:

1. **Supportive** — provides templates, training, mentoring (low control)
2. **Controlling** — requires compliance with frameworks (medium control)
3. **Directive** — directly manages projects (high control)

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "The PMO requires PMs to use specific templates..." → Controlling PMO.

## ⚠️ The 4 Most Common PMP Trap Patterns

### Trap 1: "Escalate" Reflex
- ❌ Wrong: "Escalate to sponsor"
- ✅ Right (usually): "Talk to the team / stakeholder first"

### Trap 2: "Change Request" Reflex
- ❌ Wrong: "Submit change request immediately"
- ✅ Right: "Analyze impact first, then if needed, change request"

### Trap 3: "Tell the Team" Reflex
- ❌ Wrong: "Tell the team to fix it"
- ✅ Right: "Coach the team to find a solution"

### Trap 4: "Reject Change" Reflex
- ❌ Wrong: "Reject the new requirement; we have a baseline"
- ✅ Right: "Discuss with stakeholders; analyze; consider value"

🎯 **The PMP-2021 mindset is COLLABORATIVE, not COMMANDING.**

## 📊 Case Study — Boeing 737 MAX (2019–2020)

**Situation.** In 2018 and 2019, two Boeing 737 MAX aircraft (Lion Air Flight 610, October 2018, and Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302, March 2019) crashed within five months, killing 346 people. The MAX program had been an enormous project — Boeing's response to Airbus's A320neo, with the first delivery in May 2017 after a ~$2.5 billion development effort and tight timeline pressure to defend the narrow-body market. The Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) — a piece of flight-control software added to handle the larger LEAP-1B engines mounted further forward on the airframe — was implicated in both crashes.

**Decision.** Boeing's project leadership made a sequence of choices: (1) treat the MAX as a "derivative" of the 737NG to qualify for streamlined FAA certification under the Organization Designation Authorization (ODA) program rather than re-certify the airframe; (2) minimize required pilot training (no simulator time) to preserve a contractual concession to Southwest Airlines; (3) tie MCAS to a single angle-of-attack sensor rather than fuse two sensors, with no cockpit annunciation by default; (4) under cost and schedule pressure, suppress or downgrade internal engineering concerns about MCAS authority and failure modes (later documented in the US House Transportation Committee's 2020 *Final Committee Report on the Design, Development, and Certification of the Boeing 737 MAX*).

**Outcome.** The global fleet was grounded for ~20 months (March 2019 to late 2020 in the US, later elsewhere). Boeing recorded an estimated $20+ billion in direct costs (compensation, halted production, certification rework), with broader market-cap and reputational damage exceeding $60 billion. CEO Dennis Muilenburg was fired (December 2019). Boeing paid a $2.5 billion settlement with the US Department of Justice in January 2021 (deferred-prosecution agreement). The FAA's certification process itself was reformed via the Aircraft Certification, Safety, and Accountability Act (December 2020). Internal documents released to Congress described project culture as "designed by clowns supervised by monkeys."

**Lesson for the exam / for practitioners.** This is a textbook failure of every PMBOK 7 principle this module teaches: **Stewardship** (ethical care for resources and outcomes), **Stakeholders** (pilots, regulators, traveling public, employees), **Quality** (built-in vs inspected-in), **Risk** (single-sensor design = single point of failure), **Systems Thinking** (a hardware change cascaded into software, training, regulatory, and commercial decisions), **Leadership** (suppressing dissent), and **Governance** (sponsor/PMO/regulator decision rights). On the PMP exam, when you see a situational question about "PM under pressure to skip a verification step / hide a defect / avoid escalation to keep the schedule," the right answer is *always* the one that aligns with Stewardship and the Ethics value of Responsibility — even when the wrong answer is operationally tempting. The MAX case is the canonical 21st-century example of why PMI tests this so heavily.

**Discussion (Socratic).**
- Q1: Boeing's project leadership defended the MCAS design by saying it was "within the certification envelope." Where in the PMBOK 7 principles framework does that defense fail, and what would a Stewardship-driven PM have done differently when first told MCAS would tie to a single sensor?
- Q2: The MAX was managed under a "derivative" classification rather than as a new airframe. From a Tailoring-principle perspective, when does choosing a lighter framework become a governance failure — and how would you, as PM, push back on a sponsor who wanted that classification for cost reasons?
- Q3: Several Boeing engineers raised internal concerns that were documented but not escalated. What organizational structures (PMO type, Talent-Triangle Power-Skills, Code of Ethics provisions) would you put in place from project kickoff to make sure those concerns reach a decision-maker who can act on them?

## 💬 Discussion — Socratic prompts

These are open-ended; defend your answer in writing (≥ 250 words) and revisit them after Module 10.

1. **The "escalate first" reflex.** Compare a regulated-industry PM (e.g., nuclear plant decommissioning) with a SaaS-startup PM. Both encounter a non-trivial scope change request from a stakeholder. The "PMP-2021 mindset" says default to *talk to the team first, then analyze, then change-control*. Is that mindset uniformly correct across both contexts, or is the SaaS PM right to default to "just absorb it into the next sprint"? Build the strongest case for both positions and pick which one you would defend at a portfolio review.
2. **PMO types and authority dilution.** A startup that just raised a Series C wants to install a Directive PMO to "professionalize delivery." The senior PMs argue this will choke their autonomy. At what stage of company maturity, and for what kinds of projects, is a Directive PMO net-positive — and where does it cross the line into bureaucracy? Cite PMBOK 7's Tailoring principle and Larry Spears's servant-leader behaviors in your answer.
3. **PMBOK 7 vs PMBOK 6 on a regulated project.** PMBOK 7 (PMI, 2021) emphasizes principles + performance domains; PMBOK 6 (PMI, 2017) emphasized process-groups + ITTOs. For a 5-year FDA-regulated medical-device project, which framework should you use for project documentation — and how would you handle a sponsor who insists on PMBOK 6 because "that's what the auditors recognize"?
4. **EEFs as a strategic constraint.** Identify an Enterprise Environmental Factor in your current (or most recent) workplace that you believe systematically degrades project outcomes. Using the Code of Ethics value of Responsibility as your justification, write the email you would send to your sponsor proposing a change to that EEF.
5. **The Talent Triangle and you.** Score yourself 1–5 on each of the three Talent Triangle pillars (Ways of Working, Power Skills, Business Acumen). Based on PMI's own 2024 *Pulse of the Profession* findings on which pillar most predicts project success, design a 90-day plan to lift your weakest pillar by one point.
