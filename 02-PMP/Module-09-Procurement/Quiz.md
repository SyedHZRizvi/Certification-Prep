## Answers
1.C  2.B  3.B  4.A  5.B  6.B  7.B  8.B  9.B  10.B

### Q11: **B. RFP**
Request for Proposal asks "How would you do it?", appropriate when the solution is unclear and creative proposals are wanted. RFI is informational; RFQ is for clear scope priced commodities.

### Q12: **C. Firm Fixed-Price (FFP)**
In FFP, the seller commits to a single fixed price regardless of actual costs, any overruns are absorbed by the seller. CPFF and CPAF push cost risk onto the buyer.

### Q13: **A. Cost Plus Incentive Fee**
CPIF reimburses costs and pays a sliding fee based on meeting performance targets. The buyer and seller typically share cost-savings or overruns according to a pre-agreed formula.

### Q14: **B. Describes the end results, letting the vendor decide how**
A Performance SOW focuses on outcomes, not methods, vendor flexibility on design. Design SOW prescribes the exact "how"; Functional SOW combines performance with key features.

### Q15: **C. FP-EPA**
Fixed-Price with Economic Price Adjustment indexes the contract to inflation, exchange rates, or commodity prices, protecting both parties on multi-year contracts. Plain FFP would expose the seller to inflation risk.

### Q16: **B. Make-or-Buy analysis**
Make-or-Buy weighs cost, internal skills, schedule, risk, and strategic value (you usually don't outsource core competencies). It is the formal technique for in-source vs outsource decisions.

### Q17: **B. Capped Time & Materials**
Capped T&M lets scope evolve sprint-by-sprint while limiting maximum spend, balancing buyer protection with agile flexibility. Pure FFP clashes with changing scope; uncapped CPFF/CPAF expose the buyer.

### Q18: **C. Plan Procurement Management**
The flow is Plan → Conduct → Control. Planning produces the procurement plan and SOW before any RFx is sent. Conducting includes the bidder conference, contract signing, etc.

### Q19: **B. Perform formal acceptance, finalize payments, archive contract, capture lessons learned**
Contract closure includes formal acceptance, settling final payments, archiving records, and recording lessons learned about vendor performance, never skip closure activities.

### Q20: **B. Prospective bidders to ask the buyer questions and get the same answers**
A bidder conference (or vendor conference) ensures all prospective sellers receive the same information simultaneously, supporting fairness and transparency in the procurement.

### Q21: **C. RFQ**
RFQ Request for Quotation is for well-defined commodities where price is the deciding factor. RFP would be overkill; RFI is informational only.

### Q22: **A. CPAF uses subjective evaluation; CPIF uses pre-defined formulas**
CPAF awards depend on a subjective evaluation by the buyer (often unilateral and not subject to dispute). CPIF is governed by an objective formula tied to predefined performance targets.

## Bonus questions (higher-Bloom)

### Q23. Denver Airport (from the case study). You inherit a $40M automated-warehouse project. Scope is still being defined, the prior PM signed an FFP contract with the vendor, and the vendor warned in writing that timelines are infeasible. What's the MOST defensible PM response? *(Evaluate)*
A. Hold the vendor to the FFP, they signed it.
B. Recognize the contract-family mismatch: FFP requires stable scope, not present here; engage the vendor in formal renegotiation to a phased structure (e.g., Cost-Plus-Incentive Fee for the unstable subsystems, FFP for the stable ones, milestone-based payments tied to subsystem acceptance); document the vendor's written warning in the project record; brief the sponsor on the legal/risk implications of continuing FFP; resist political pressure to ignore the warning; cite Code of Ethics' Responsibility.
C. Sue the vendor for over-warning.
D. Cancel the project.

### Q24. A buyer wants a 3-year smart-traffic system delivered by a vendor. The buyer's user research is still ongoing (scope evolving), and the vendor is asking for FFP "for accountability." Design a contract structure that protects both parties. *(Create)*
A. Pure FFP, vendor request honored.
B. Pure CPFF, buyer absorbs all risk.
C. Hybrid: stable subsystems (sensors, networking spec) under FFP with FP-EPA inflation index; evolving subsystems (analytics, citizen-facing app) under capped T&M with a hard cap; integration phase under CPIF with shared cost-savings and explicit performance targets; phased milestone payments; quarterly governance reviews; lessons-learned reviews built into contract; document the contract-family logic by subsystem in the procurement plan.
D. Avoid contracting entirely.

### Q25. A central PMO insists every PM use the centralized procurement office. PMs complain it slows them down. Build the strongest case for both centralization and decentralization. Which is the MOST defensible recommendation? *(Evaluate)*
A. Always centralize.
B. Always decentralize.
C. Tailor by spend tier and risk class: centralize high-spend (>$500K) and high-risk procurements where buying-power, legal review, and vendor-risk monitoring pay back; decentralize small-spend, low-risk, time-sensitive procurements where the central office is a bottleneck; publish the threshold rule explicitly so PMs and procurement officers share expectations; review thresholds annually based on outcomes (cycle time, contract failure rate, savings); cite PMBOK 7's Tailoring principle.
D. Outsource procurement entirely.

### Q26. A vendor proposes "money for nothing, change for free", buyer can swap backlog items at no extra cost, and can pay the vendor a portion of the undelivered work if the project ends early. Is this defensible for a regulated industry project? *(Analyze)*
A. Always use this structure.
B. Never use it.
C. Defensible only under specific conditions: scope flexibility must align with regulatory acceptance criteria (regulator must accept "lighter scope" as compliant); the buyer must have a process owner who can make swap decisions; the "money for nothing" provision should have a cap to prevent the vendor from being incentivized to deliver less; lessons-learned and audit trails must be rigorous for regulators; only use after legal review and procurement officer sign-off, document the trade-offs.
D. Use only for marketing copy.

### Q27. PMI's Code of Ethics requires Honesty in procurement. A senior procurement officer instructs you to "shape" bid evaluation criteria after seeing the bids so the preferred vendor wins. What's the MOST defensible PM action? *(Evaluate)*
A. Comply, they outrank you.
B. Refuse silently and let the wrong vendor win.
C. Refuse explicitly, in writing if necessary: post-hoc shaping of evaluation criteria violates Fairness and Honesty in the Code of Ethics and may also violate procurement law/regulation (especially in public-sector contexts); propose a documented re-tender if the original criteria are genuinely inadequate; escalate to the PMO or ethics officer if the procurement officer insists; cite specific FAR or organizational policy violations as appropriate.
D. Hide the manipulation.
