# Recommended Readings — PMP (Project Management Professional)

> The PMP exam is the floor of what a serious project manager should know. The reading list below is what the floor *covers* and what lies *above* the floor — the literature that 8 of the top 10 corporate PM curricula draw from, plus the resources that have most influenced the modern profession. Treat this as a working bibliography for the next 3–5 years.

Each entry includes a 1–2 sentence note on why it's worth your time and *when* in your PMP journey you should engage with it.

---

## Canonical Textbooks (read at least the first three)

### 1. *A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK Guide) — Seventh Edition*
**Author/Publisher:** Project Management Institute (PMI). **Year:** 2021. **ISBN:** 978-1628256642.
- *Why:* The official PMI reference for the current PMP exam (since 2021). Restructured around 8 Performance Domains + 12 Principles; replaces PMBOK 6's process-heavy approach.
- *When:* Read sections referenced in each module of this course in parallel with the course itself. Re-skim the entire Guide in the final week before the exam.

### 2. *A Guide to the Project Management Body of Knowledge (PMBOK Guide) — Sixth Edition*
**Author/Publisher:** Project Management Institute (PMI). **Year:** 2017. **ISBN:** 978-1628251845.
- *Why:* PMBOK 6 contains the detailed process-group/knowledge-area framing (the 5×10 grid) that PMBOK 7 abstracted away. The current PMP still tests these concepts (charter, baselines, CCB, change-control sequence) heavily; PMBOK 6 is the most-organized reference for them.
- *When:* Keep open during Module 03 (Process Domain) and Module 06 (EVM). Use the §4 Integration Management and §7 Cost Management sections as your formula reference.

### 3. *PMP Exam Prep — Eleventh Edition* (Rita Mulcahy)
**Author:** Rita Mulcahy. **Publisher:** RMC Publications. **Year:** 2023 (11th ed.; updated for current ECO). **ISBN:** 978-1943704460.
- *Why:* The most-cited PMP prep book of the last two decades. Strong on situational reasoning ("what would the PM do?"). Mulcahy's heuristics for eliminating wrong answers are widely adopted.
- *When:* Read in the final 2–4 weeks before the exam, after you've absorbed this course. Take the chapter quizzes; review the explanations.

### 4. *PMP Exam Prep Simplified* (Andrew Ramdayal)
**Author:** Andrew Ramdayal. **Publisher:** AR-PMP. **Year:** 2023.
- *Why:* Ramdayal's book is the companion to his Udemy course (#1 PMP prep course by enrollment for 5+ years). His agile/PMP-2021 coverage is especially clear.
- *When:* Pair with the Udemy course in weeks 6–10 of study. The combined approach has the best track record for first-attempt pass.

### 5. *Project Management: A Systems Approach to Planning, Scheduling, and Controlling — 13th Edition* (Harold Kerzner)
**Publisher:** Wiley. **Year:** 2022. **ISBN:** 978-1119805465.
- *Why:* Kerzner's textbook is the canonical engineering-school PM reference. Heavy on systems thinking, EVM, governance, and case studies. Used in graduate-level PM programs at Carnegie Mellon, Cornell, and many engineering schools.
- *When:* Read between exam prep and your first major real-world project assignment. Not strictly required for PMP, but elevates your thinking beyond the exam.

### 6. *Effective Work Breakdown Structures* (Gregory T. Haugan)
**Publisher:** Management Concepts. **Year:** 2002. **ISBN:** 978-1567261356.
- *Why:* The definitive treatment of WBS construction, including the 100% Rule and decomposition heuristics. Short (170 pp.) and indispensable when you actually have to build a WBS.
- *When:* Reach for it during Module 03 or before your first real WBS.

## Seminal Papers, HBR Articles, and Industry Reports

### 7. *"Project Success: Definitions and Measurement Techniques"* (Pinto & Slevin)
**Authors:** Jeffrey K. Pinto & Dennis P. Slevin. **Journal:** Journal of Management, 14(2). **Year:** 1988.
- *Why:* The foundational academic paper on what "project success" *means*. Introduced the multi-stakeholder, multi-time-horizon framing that PMBOK 7's Value principle now embeds.
- *When:* Read after Module 04. Re-read after Module 10. Will change how you think about success criteria.

### 8. *Leading Change* (John P. Kotter)
**Publisher:** Harvard Business School Press. **Year:** 1996 (Updated 2012). **ISBN:** 978-1422186435.
- *Why:* Kotter's 8-Step Model is the most-cited organizational change framework in PM literature. Foundational reading for the OCM portion of Module 04.
- *When:* Before Module 04 or in the first month of any change-heavy project.

### 9. *ADKAR: A Model for Change in Business, Government and Our Community* (Jeff Hiatt / Prosci)
**Publisher:** Prosci Learning Center. **Year:** 2006. **ISBN:** 978-1930885509.
- *Why:* The individual-change-adoption framework that complements Kotter's organizational one. Module 04 quizzes both; in practice, organizations use them together.
- *When:* Read alongside Kotter.

### 10. *"Developmental Sequence in Small Groups"* (Bruce W. Tuckman)
**Journal:** Psychological Bulletin, 63(6). **Year:** 1965 (extended 1977 with Mary Ann Jensen, *Group & Organization Studies*).
- *Why:* The original Tuckman paper that gave us Forming-Storming-Norming-Performing(-Adjourning). Eight pages; reads like prose. Required for any serious people-management work.
- *When:* Before Module 02. Re-read after running a real team for 6+ months.

### 11. *"A Theory of Human Motivation"* (Abraham H. Maslow)
**Journal:** Psychological Review, 50(4). **Year:** 1943.
- *Why:* Maslow's original paper. The hierarchy is more nuanced and more contested than the popular pyramid; the original is worth reading for that nuance.
- *When:* During Module 02.

### 12. *Emotional Intelligence* and *Working with Emotional Intelligence* (Daniel Goleman)
**Publisher:** Bantam. **Years:** 1995 and 1998.
- *Why:* The two foundational books for the 5-component EQ framework that PMBOK and the PMP exam test. The 1998 sequel is the more practical of the two for PMs.
- *When:* Read the 1998 book during or after Module 02.

### 13. *Megaprojects and Risk: An Anatomy of Ambition* (Bent Flyvbjerg, Nils Bruzelius, Werner Rothengatter)
**Publisher:** Cambridge University Press. **Year:** 2003. **ISBN:** 978-0521009461.
- *Why:* The empirical research base on why megaprojects systematically over-cost and under-deliver. Channel Tunnel, Big Dig, and many other PMP case-study examples here.
- *When:* Read after Module 04 + 07. Indispensable if you ever work on a project >$50M.

### 14. *How Big Things Get Done* (Bent Flyvbjerg & Dan Gardner)
**Publisher:** Currency. **Year:** 2023. **ISBN:** 978-0593239513.
- *Why:* Flyvbjerg's 2023 popular-audience treatment of the megaprojects research. Includes updated 2020s data and prescriptions ("think slow, act fast" — the planning-fast/build-slow inversion).
- *When:* Read in weeks 8–12 of PMP prep, or shortly after your real PMP. Highly readable.

### 15. *Standish Group CHAOS Report 2024* (Standish Group)
**Year:** 2024.
- *Why:* The annual industry baseline on IT-project success rates (most-cited "X% of IT projects succeed" stat in any PM article). Defines what's "above bar."
- *When:* Read once early; re-skim the latest edition each year.

### 16. *PMI Pulse of the Profession 2024: The Future of Project Work* (PMI)
**Year:** 2024.
- *Why:* PMI's annual report on global PM trends, salary data, project-success drivers, and emerging practices. Free to PMI members; summary available publicly.
- *When:* Read between weeks 8–12 of PMP prep. Re-read each year of your PM career.

### 17. *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Daniel Kahneman)
**Publisher:** Farrar, Straus and Giroux. **Year:** 2011. **ISBN:** 978-0374533557.
- *Why:* Kahneman's Nobel-grade exposition of System 1 / System 2 thinking, the planning fallacy, prospect theory, and dozens of cognitive biases that systematically degrade project estimates and risk assessments.
- *When:* Slowly, over several months. Read Chapter 23 (the Planning Fallacy) before your next estimation session.

### 18. *Reflections on Leadership* (Larry C. Spears, editor)
**Publisher:** Wiley. **Year:** 1995. **ISBN:** 978-0471036861.
- *Why:* Spears's edited collection codifying the 10 behaviors of servant leadership (the 7 that PMBOK tests are a subset). Pairs with Greenleaf's 1970 essay *The Servant as Leader*.
- *When:* Before Module 02. Re-read every few years.

### 19. *"The Quality Trilogy"* (Joseph M. Juran)
**Journal:** Quality Progress. **Year:** August 1986.
- *Why:* Juran's article-length summary of Quality Planning + Quality Control + Quality Improvement. The single best ~5-page read for Module 08's quality-philosophy questions.
- *When:* During Module 08.

### 20. *Quality is Free* (Philip B. Crosby)
**Publisher:** McGraw-Hill. **Year:** 1979 (still in print). **ISBN:** 978-0070145122.
- *Why:* Crosby's foundational argument that prevention is cheaper than failure. Module 08 tests this directly; the book is a 30-year career read.
- *When:* Before Module 08.

### 21. *"Toward a Theory of Stakeholder Identification and Salience"* (Mitchell, Agle & Wood)
**Journal:** Academy of Management Review, 22(4). **Year:** 1997.
- *Why:* The original Salience Model paper (Power × Legitimacy × Urgency = 7 stakeholder categories). The intellectual ground floor of modern stakeholder theory.
- *When:* During Module 10.

### 22. *Accelerate: The Science of Lean Software and DevOps* (Forsgren, Humble & Kim)
**Publisher:** IT Revolution. **Year:** 2018. **ISBN:** 978-1942788331.
- *Why:* The DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment) metrics — deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, mean time to restore — that have replaced "velocity" as the right delivery dashboard in modern software PM.
- *When:* During Module 05 (Agile & Hybrid).

### 23. *Out of the Crisis* (W. Edwards Deming)
**Publisher:** MIT Press (Center for Advanced Engineering Studies). **Year:** 1982. **ISBN:** 978-0911379013.
- *Why:* Deming's 14 Points for Management; the most influential 20th-century work on systemic quality and management. ~85% of project problems are *system* problems — Deming's foundational claim.
- *When:* Slowly, over a year. Foundational reading for any PM.

### 24. *The Toyota Way* (Jeffrey K. Liker)
**Publisher:** McGraw-Hill. **Year:** 2004. **ISBN:** 978-0071392310.
- *Why:* The most-cited Western treatment of Toyota Production System, Lean, and the 8 wastes (TIM-WOODS). Foundational for Module 05's Lean coverage.
- *When:* During or after Module 05.

### 25. *Disciplined Agile: An Executive's Guide* (Scott Ambler & Mark Lines / PMI)
**Year:** 2022. PMI acquired Disciplined Agile in 2019.
- *Why:* PMI's official agile framework. Useful both for the PMP exam and for thinking about agile in regulated contexts where Scrum-only is insufficient.
- *When:* Weeks 6–10 of PMP prep.

## Industry Resources (subscribe at least to two)

### 26. *PM Network* magazine and *Project Management Journal*
**Publisher:** PMI (member benefit). **Year:** ongoing.
- *Why:* PMI's flagship publications. *PM Network* is glossier and case-heavy; *PMJ* is peer-reviewed and academic. Both come with PMI membership ($139/year).
- *When:* Start during PMP prep. Continue indefinitely.

### 27. Andrew Ramdayal YouTube channel + Udemy course
**Creator:** Andrew Ramdayal. **Platform:** YouTube + Udemy.
- *Why:* Highest-enrolled PMP prep course and a free YouTube channel with practice questions, mnemonics, and exam strategy. Universally recommended in PMP prep communities.
- *When:* Weeks 4–10 of prep. The 35 PDU course also counts as the PMI prerequisite.

### 28. PMI's Disciplined Agile webinars and member content
**Publisher:** PMI. **Year:** ongoing.
- *Why:* PMI members get access to a deep library of Disciplined Agile webinars, white papers, and templates. The free monthly webinars are excellent for staying current.
- *When:* As a member benefit during and after PMP prep.

### 29. *Mike Clayton — OnlinePMCourses.com*
**Creator:** Mike Clayton. **Year:** ongoing.
- *Why:* Independent practitioner's blog and YouTube channel with deep-dive videos on individual PMP topics (EVM, contracts, risk). The "explainer" library is excellent for spot-clarification.
- *When:* Throughout study.

### 30. *PMBOK 7 Companion Standards* (PMI)
**Publisher:** PMI. **Years:** various.
- *Why:* PMI publishes a *Practice Standard for* series (Scheduling, Risk Management, Earned Value, Estimating, etc.) that each go deeper than PMBOK on a single topic. Indispensable when you want a definitive reference on one knowledge area.
- *When:* On-demand during work; especially the EVM Practice Standard for Module 06.

## Free Academic Courses

### 31. *Project Management* (MIT OpenCourseWare)
**Course:** MIT 1.040 (also MIT Sloan 15.430). **Instructor:** Prof. Fred Moavenzadeh and others.
- *Why:* MIT's undergraduate + Sloan project-management course materials are free on OCW: lecture notes, assignments, and reading lists. The engineering-school depth complements PMI's practitioner orientation.
- *When:* Weeks 6–12 of PMP prep, or after the exam to deepen understanding.

### 32. *Stanford SCPD Engineering Management*
**Institution:** Stanford Center for Professional Development.
- *Why:* Stanford's engineering management resources include free lecture videos on project portfolio management, organizational change, and systems-thinking applied to delivery. Useful for the "graduate-level professional" level of project thinking beyond PMP.
- *When:* After the exam.

### 33. *Google Project Management Certificate* (Coursera)
**Platform:** Coursera. **Provider:** Google. **Cost:** Coursera subscription (~$49/month) or financial aid available.
- *Why:* Foundational PM certificate, free with financial aid. Useful as a parallel reinforcement during PMP prep for less experienced PMs, or to fill specific gaps (e.g., the agile-or-hybrid practical modules).
- *When:* Weeks 1–4 if you're newer to PM; otherwise skip in favor of the deeper resources above.

### 34. *Harvard Business School Online: Strategy Execution* (HBS Online)
**Platform:** HBS Online (paid: ~$1,750 for the full 8-week course).
- *Why:* Not free, but worth listing because it's the single best executive-level treatment of how to actually *execute* on a strategic project — the single best executive-level treatment of general management and project leadership.
- *When:* For senior PMs in the 1–3 years after passing the PMP.

### 35. *PMI Educational Foundation / Earned Value Management Practice Standard (free PDF for PMI members)*
**Publisher:** PMI.
- *Why:* The definitive free reference on EVM, deeper than PMBOK's coverage. Critical for Module 06.
- *When:* During Module 06 + EVM-question drilling.

### 36. *FAR (Federal Acquisition Regulation) Parts 16 and 37 — public domain*
**Publisher:** US Government. **URL:** acquisition.gov.
- *Why:* The legal foundation for the contract families (FFP, FPIF, FP-EPA, CPFF, CPIF, CPAF, T&M) tested in Module 09. Reading the actual regulation cement the differences in your head.
- *When:* During Module 09.

---

## Suggested reading sequence

If you want a single recommendation: read this course (this site) in parallel with PMBOK 7 + Rita Mulcahy's prep book. After passing the PMP, work through Kerzner's textbook, then Flyvbjerg's *How Big Things Get Done* and Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*. Subscribe to PM Network and watch the Pulse of the Profession annually. You will, within ~3 years, be deeper than 80% of certified PMs on the strategic side and fully competitive with graduates of any executive PM program.

Good reading.
