# 🚶 Module 8: Walk Cycles & Action

## What a Walk Reveals

You can tell almost everything about a character from how they walk. The swagger of a confident person. The shuffle of someone exhausted. The tip-toeing stealth of someone trying not to be caught. A dragging foot that tells a story of injury. An enthusiastic bounce that says "I am happy and I have too much energy." An animation professor once said: "I don't need to see a character's face to know who they are. Show me them walking across a room."

The walk cycle is the most studied, most analyzed, most referenced exercise in the history of animation. Richard Williams spent decades refining his understanding of it. Preston Blair drew hundreds of variations. Every animation school assigns it in the first month. And yet, after all the analysis, the thing that makes a walk cycle feel alive is not the four-pose formula — it's the *personality* built into every aspect of the timing, the arcs, and the weight.

This module gives you the four-pose formula (because you need the structure before you can break it), then shows you how Cuphead, Gravity Falls, and Avatar: The Last Airbender break it beautifully.

---

## 🦶 The Four-Pose Walk Cycle

Every walk cycle — no matter the character, no matter the style — is built from four key poses:

### Contact Position
The heel of the lead foot touches the ground. The opposite foot is about to push off. The body is at a medium height. Arms are at opposite swing positions (left arm forward with right leg forward). The key tension point of the pose: the heel strike. This is the moment of maximum extension.

### Down Position
The character takes the impact of the step. The stepping leg bends as the knee absorbs the body's weight. The body drops to its lowest point. The opposite foot completes its push-off and begins the upswing. Secondary action: head bobs slightly forward and down.

### Passing Position
One foot passes the other. The stepping foot is now flat on the ground (or ball of foot if animated on toes). The swinging foot is at mid-air, passing the planted foot. The body is at its highest point. Arms continue their counter-swing.

### Up Position
The body has reached its highest point and is about to descend into the next contact. The leading foot begins to extend forward toward its heel strike. The rear foot has nearly completed its push-off arc. This is often where stylistic variation shows most: how high does the character lift their foot? How sharply does the body crest?

## 📐 Timing the Walk Cycle

A standard walk cycle at 24fps runs 12 frames — 6 frames per step.

| Frame | Pose |
|-------|------|
| 1 | Contact (left foot forward) |
| 3 | Down |
| 5 | Passing Position |
| 7 | Up |
| 9 | Contact (right foot forward) |
| 11 | Down |
| 13 | Back to frame 1 |

The cycle then loops. Note that a 12-frame cycle repeats 2 times per second at 24fps — a normal walking pace. Slower characters use 16–20 frame cycles; faster characters use 8–10 frame cycles.

### Animating on Twos

For a hand-drawn style walk cycle, many animators use 6 unique drawings total (one per pose) and hold each for 2 frames. This gives 12 frames total and creates the classic "on-twos" cartoon bounce.

## 🏃 Run Cycles

A run differs from a walk in fundamental ways:

| Walk | Run |
|------|-----|
| One foot always on the ground | Both feet can be off the ground simultaneously |
| 12 frames (standard) | 8 frames (standard) |
| Body height range: low-medium | Body height range: medium-high, with airborne pose |
| Arm swing: parallel to legs | Arm swing: exaggerated, often crossing centerline |
| Head relatively stable | Head pitches forward; strong forward lean |

The run's **airborne position** — where both feet are off the ground — is the defining moment. For a snappy, energetic run, the airborne pose is held for 2–3 frames. For a floaty, superhero-style run, the airborne phase is longer.

## ⚡ Anticipation Before Action

Anticipation is a preparatory motion before a major action. It prepares the audience for what's about to happen and gives the action contrast — the more the character winds up, the more impactful the release.

### Anticipation Formula

```
Hold/pose → Anticipation (opposite direction) → Main action
   8 frames       4–8 frames                      6–12 frames
```

For a jump:
- Character holds standing pose (audience reads the pose)
- Character crouches into anticipation: knees bend, arms drop back, head dips
- Character launches: legs extend, arms swing up, body stretches vertically

The anticipation must go in the **opposite direction** of the main action. A character jumping UP first goes DOWN. A character punching LEFT first moves RIGHT.

> 🚨 **Trap on the exam:** Anticipation without a hold before it reads as jerky and confusing. Always hold the primary pose long enough for the audience to register it before going into anticipation.

## 💥 Snappy vs. Fluid Action Styles

Different animation traditions approach action timing very differently:

| Style | Timing | Key Shows | Characteristics |
|-------|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Snappy** | Few frames, fast transitions, strong holds | Cuphead, Hazbin Hotel, classic Warner Bros. | Long holds → sudden fast motion → new hold; feels like a flip book |
| **Fluid** | Many in-betweens, gradual transitions | Spider-Verse (Miles Morales), Avatar, Gravity Falls | Smooth arcs, less abrupt; motion feels more like live action reference |
| **Anime** | Hybrid: holds + sudden fast smears | Demon Slayer, One Piece | Extended holds interrupted by burst speed and smear frames |

### Cuphead's Snappy Style

Cuphead (Studio MDHR) was animated frame-by-frame to deliberately recreate the 1930s Fleischer Brothers style: long holds on poses, sudden fast transitions of 2–3 frames, exaggerated squash and stretch, and rubber-hose limbs. The snappiness is intentional and defines the game's visual identity.

### Gravity Falls' Fluid Style

Gravity Falls uses more in-betweens per action and softer arc transitions, giving it a more naturalistic feel despite being a stylized cartoon. Characters feel like they have real weight, especially in the later, more emotionally intense episodes.

### Avatar: The Last Airbender's Reference-Based Style

Avatar's action sequences were designed by bending masters — each bending style (water, earth, fire, air) is based on a specific real-world martial art (Tai Chi, Hung Gar Kung Fu, Northern Shaolin, Ba Gua). The animators rotoscoped reference from martial arts practitioners to get the footwork right, then stylized it into the cartoon idiom.

## 🎯 Action Animation Principles

### The Four Key Principles Applied to Action

| Principle | Application in Action |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Anticipation** | Wind-up before every major movement |
| **Squash and Stretch** | Impact squashes; launch stretches |
| **Follow-Through** | Hair, clothing, secondary elements continue after the main motion stops |
| **Arcs** | Even fast punches follow arcs; straight-line motion feels mechanical |

### Impact Poses

Impact (the moment of collision, landing, or contact) deserves special attention:
- The frame just BEFORE impact should be the maximum stretch
- The frame AT impact should be the maximum squash
- The frames AFTER impact show the ripple effect through the body (bounce, jiggle, shock wave)

## 🔄 Walk Cycle Variations

Once the standard walk is mastered, stylistic variations become possible:

| Variation | Characteristics |
|-----------|----------------|
| Creep/Sneak | Very slow; exaggerated tip-toeing; long passing position hold |
| Strut | Chest out; each step hits a held accent pose |
| Exhausted Shuffle | Feet barely lift; heavy downward weight; short arcs |
| Bounce Walk | Exaggerated Up position; joyful, energetic; spring in each step |
| Heavy Walk (giant) | Long cycle duration; very low Down position; earth-shaking secondary |

---

## 📋 Summary

- Walk cycles are built from four key poses: Contact, Down, Passing, Up.
- Standard walk = 12 frames at 24fps (2 steps per second); runs = 8 frames with a 2-foot-off-ground airborne pose.
- Anticipation always moves in the opposite direction of the main action; hold before anticipation is essential.
- Snappy styles (Cuphead) use long holds + few fast transition frames; fluid styles (Gravity Falls, Avatar) use more in-betweens.
- Impact poses: maximum stretch just before impact; maximum squash at impact.
- Walk style expresses character personality — the structure is the starting point, not the finish line.
