## ✨ Why This Course Matters

### 🎯 The Promise

Animation is not a single trick. It is a stack of disciplines that build on each other in a very specific order: you need to understand the interface before you can time a tween, understand tweening before you can rig a character, and understand rigs before you can animate dialogue. Most YouTube tutorials teach you a trick. This course teaches you the **stack**.

The target credential — **Adobe Certified Professional in Animate (Adobe/Certiport)** — is the industry-recognized badge that signals to studios, agencies, and clients that you understand the tool at a production level, not just as a YouTube hobbyist. But the course is designed to be useful even if you never sit the exam: the skills are what open doors.

The animation industry is expanding rapidly. Streaming platforms commission more 2D content than they did in the broadcast era. Short-form video culture has created a permanent appetite for motion graphics and animated explainers. The indie creator economy means a solo animator with the right skill set can earn a full-time living without ever setting foot in a studio. And the studios themselves — from small boutiques to the major houses — are perpetually understaffed with people who can both *draw* and *animate in software*.

This course is the one you'd design if you were combining the best of CalArts' animation theory, the School of Motion's software depth, and the practical pipeline knowledge that only comes from working inside a real studio.

### 💼 Career Outcomes After Completing

- **Studio Animator / 2D Character Animator** ($65K–$100K) — the core production role at animation studios (major and boutique)

## 🎯 What You'll Master

By the end of this course, you'll be able to:

- Navigate Adobe Animate and After Effects at a production level — timeline, layers, library, panels, keyboard shortcuts, and all
- Draw directly in Animate using its vector brush tools with pressure sensitivity; understand when to draw in Photoshop, Illustrator, or on paper and import
- Animate frame-by-frame with proper onion skinning, rough-to-cleanup workflow, and arc tracking — the same discipline used in hand-drawn animation
- Build motion tweens with custom easing curves, understanding why "floaty" tweens fail and how to fix them with the Motion Editor
- Rig a cut-out character using symbol hierarchies and Animate's bone tools (IK/FK) — the same technique behind South Park and Hazbin Hotel
- Import Illustrator and Animate assets into After Effects, apply the Puppet Pin Tool, and use "Create Shapes from Vector Layers" for fully scalable rigs
- Build production-grade character rigs in After Effects using DUIK Bassel/Angela — walk cycles, bendy limbs, spring rigs
- Animate dialogue with phoneme breakdowns, the Preston Blair mouth chart, and auto + manual lip sync techniques
- Execute the full walk cycle (contact, down, pass, up) plus runs, anticipation, and snappy vs. fluid action styles inspired by Cuphead, Gravity Falls, and Avatar
- Export to any delivery format — H.264, ProRes, GIF, WebM, Lottie JSON — with correct codec, frame rate, and color space settings
- Describe the full 2D studio pipeline from pre-production through delivery, and know which tools (Toon Boom Harmony, TVPaint, Animate, After Effects) belong where

## ⚠️ The 7 Most Common Reasons Animators Plateau

1. ❌ **Skipping frame-by-frame for tweens only** — Tweens are a shortcut, not a foundation. If you can't animate by hand, you can't tell when a tween looks wrong. Module 2 is non-skippable.
2. ❌ **Never reading the 12 Principles** — Squash and stretch, anticipation, arc, follow-through, staging, timing — these are physics distilled. Without them, software knowledge alone makes stiff, lifeless animation.
3. ❌ **Ignoring audio** — The best animators cut to audio first. Every beat, every consonant, every breath is a keyframe opportunity. Module 7 teaches this; don't skip it.
4. ❌ **Over-rigging** — A rig with 200 bones and a joystick for every finger is not a great rig. It's a liability. A great rig is the *minimum* structure for the motion the character needs. Module 6 explains the calculus.
5. ❌ **Not watching enough reference** — Watch *everything*: live action reference for weight and timing, great cartoons for style and exaggeration, bad cartoons to understand what not to do. Your eye must outpace your hand.
6. ❌ **Wrong frame rate for the job** — 24fps for cinematic, 12fps for that hand-drawn anime feel, 30fps for broadcast TV, 60fps for gaming UI. Module 9 explains the tradeoffs; choosing wrong wastes render time and looks wrong.
7. ❌ **Shipping without color space correction** — sRGB in Animate vs. Rec.709 in After Effects vs. P3 in Premiere. One wrong toggle and your beautifully graded color explodes on someone else's monitor. Module 9 covers this.
