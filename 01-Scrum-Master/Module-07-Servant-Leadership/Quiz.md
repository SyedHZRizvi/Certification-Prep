## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

### Q1: **B. True leader who serves**
Per 2020 Scrum Guide.

### Q2: **C. Coach with questions**
Coaching > telling. Builds developer's own thinking.

### Q3: **C. Manager**
SM is NOT a manager. The 5: servant leader, coach, facilitator, teacher, change agent.

### Q4: **C. Teacher**
New teams need to learn Scrum first. Coaching can come later.

### Q5: **B. Educate the manager**
Scrum measures team-level outcomes, not individual performance.

### Q6: **C. Team, PO, and organization**
All three groups are explicitly listed in the Scrum Guide.

### Q7: **B. Open-ended questions**
"What do you think?" "Why?" "What else?"

### Q8: **C. Engage partners to remove it**
Escalate, partner, work the system. Don't let it sit.

### Q9: **D. Facilitator (and possibly Teacher)**
Running effective events = facilitation; explaining the timebox = teaching.

### Q10: **B. Being a doormat**
Servant leaders have courage to challenge.

### Q11: **B. Courage**
Avoiding hard truths = lack of courage. Openness is also relevant, but courage is the more direct fit.

### Q12: **B. Teach Scrum, build trust**
First duty: foundational understanding.

### Q13: **B. Servant Leader, Coach, Facilitator, Teacher, Change Agent**
The five Scrum.org stances. "Manager" is never one of them.

### Q14: **B. Level 2 (team-to-org bridge), work with peer SMs and managers to resolve**
Cross-team dependencies sit between the team and the org. The SM partners with peers to remove them.

### Q15: **C. Coach the team toward self-management**
The SM builds capability, not dependency. Asking questions develops the team's own decision-making muscle.

### Q16: **B. Teacher**
Explaining how a Scrum event works = direct instruction = teaching.

### Q17: **C. The team, PO, and the larger organization**
Per the Scrum Guide, the SM serves all three groups.

### Q18: **B. Telling Developers exactly which tasks to take**
Servant leaders coach and remove impediments, they do not assign tasks.

### Q19: **B. Private conversation with each, then facilitate resolution**
The SM addresses team-level conflict directly, with respect and confidentiality, before escalating.

### Q20: **B. Scrum Guide rule, safety issue, or true emergency**
Tell mode is reserved for non-negotiables. For "how" questions, coach.

### Q21: **B. Foster psychological safety and model courage**
Avoiding hard truths is a values gap (courage). The SM cultivates an environment where honesty is safe.

### Q22: **B. Change Agent / serving the organization**
Helping HR (or finance, leadership) adapt to Scrum is part of serving the organization.

### Q23: **B. "What options have you considered, and what are the trade-offs?"**
Coaching = open-ended questions that develop the person's own thinking. Telling, demonstrating, or abandoning are not coaching.

### Q24: **B. Make events meaningful with new formats and real feedback**
The fix for "Scrum theater" is bringing the WHY back into the events, not lecturing or scrapping them.

## 🎯 Answer key, Q25–Q30

### Q25: **B. Daily Scrum + team transparency**
The Scrum analog to the andon cord is collective rather than individual the Daily Scrum surfaces impediments and the team can collectively decide to pause/replan based on what's inspected. The Scrum Guide's emphasis on Transparency is the closer analog than any single-person veto power. (A is about Sprint Goal, not stopping work; C is about quality at output, not stopping in-progress work; D contradicts servant leadership the SM doesn't unilaterally stop work.)

### Q26: **B. Rule-cop is anti-servant-leader**
The Scrum Master's accountability is to *establish* Scrum which involves teaching, coaching, and modeling. Citing the Guide as a rule-cop signals distrust and prevents team learning. The mature SM teaches once, then asks questions that lead the team to discover the answer. Greenleaf's servant-leader does not "enforce" they enable.

### Q27: **B. Three-step coaching response**
Acknowledge → teach → offer alternatives. This is the canonical servant-leader pattern for handling org-level pressure that conflicts with Scrum values. (A undermines team; C is confrontational and unhelpful; D is unethical.)

### Q28: **B. Coach with questions**
Technology choice is a Developer self-management decision, the team should decide based on their context. The SM coaches by asking questions ("What are the team's skills?" "What does the architecture suggest?") rather than dictating. The Tell-mode is reserved for Scrum-Guide rules, safety, and emergencies. This is neither.

### Q29: **B. Calibration to prevent disempowerment**
The 2020 rephrasing addressed a documented misreading of "servant" as "subservient." The new phrasing preserves Greenleaf's intent while emphasizing leadership *is* the role, service is *how*, not *whether*. This calibration matters in practice because many Scrum Masters reported being disempowered by leaders who took "servant" too literally.

### Q30: **B. Doormat SM, failure of courage**
The over-serving SM fails the Scrum value of Courage. Symptoms: chronic unresolved impediments, team avoids hard conversations, SM's voice fades. Servant leadership is *both* service AND challenge, Greenleaf's essay explicitly emphasizes the leader's discernment and willingness to challenge.
