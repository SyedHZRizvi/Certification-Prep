## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

### Q1: **C. Developers (or organization)**
DoD is owned by the Developers, unless the org provides one.

### Q2: **B. NOT in Scrum Guide**
Definition of Ready is a practice, not a Scrum requirement.

### Q3: **C. Developers**
Always developers. Always.

### Q4: **C. Relative complexity, effort, uncertainty**
NOT time. NOT hours.

### Q5: **B. Forecast future Sprints (same team)**
Within the same team, for planning. Never for cross-team comparison.

### Q6: **C. Not comparable**
Story points are relative *to that team*. Team A might have larger reference items.

### Q7: **B. Anti-pattern**
"Quality does not decrease", Scrum Guide. Lowering DoD = lowering quality.

### Q8: **B. Releasable; PO decides**
Increment must be releasable; release timing is the PO's call.

### Q9: **B. Useful but optional**
Not required by the Scrum Guide.

### Q10: **B. Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable**
Mike Cohn's criteria for good user stories.

### Q11: **B. No, DoD must also be met**
Both AC + DoD are required.

### Q12: **B. Evolve to higher bar**
Adding criteria = good. Lowering = anti-pattern. Per-developer DoD = anti-pattern.

### Q13: **C. Nothing, velocity is not in the Scrum Guide**
Velocity is a useful planning tool, but it is not prescribed by Scrum.

### Q14: **B. Story points are team-relative, so velocities are not comparable**
Each team picks its own reference items; comparing velocities across teams is meaningless and incentivizes inflation.

### Q15: **C. Definition of Ready**
DoR is a common practice but is NOT defined in the Scrum Guide. DoD, Sprint Goal, and Product Goal all are.

### Q16: **C. Small enough to fit in a Sprint**
INVEST: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable. "S" = Small.

### Q17: **B. Go back to the Product Backlog for the PO to re-order**
Unfinished items return to the backlog. The PO decides whether to put them at the top of the next Sprint or re-prioritize.

### Q18: **B. Optional team practice; not in the Scrum Guide**
DoR is a useful refinement aid but is NOT a Scrum Guide concept.

### Q19: **C. Capture relative effort, complexity, and uncertainty**
Story points are deliberately not time. They enable comparison between items, not between teams.

### Q20: **B. Allowed, the Increment is releasable; PO decides when**
Done ≠ Released. The Increment must be releasable, but the PO decides if and when to actually release.

### Q21: **B. Avoid anchoring and surface assumptions**
Simultaneous reveal prevents the first speaker from biasing others; outliers spark discussion that surfaces hidden assumptions.

### Q22: **B. Misuse of velocity as a target metric**
Velocity is a planning tool, not a target. Targeting it inflates estimates and erodes quality.

### Q23: **B. Optional transparency tool**
Burndown is helpful but NOT required by the Scrum Guide.

### Q24: **B. Healthy evolution, DoD can grow as quality goals rise**
DoD can be raised over time. Lowering it is the anti-pattern; adding rigor is the goal.

## 🎯 Answer key, Q25–Q30

### Q25: **B. Consistent with Scrum**
The Scrum Guide says work that doesn't meet DoD is *not Done*. Cutting un-Done features means: "this work doesn't yet meet DoD, so it returns to the backlog for refinement and a future Sprint." The Increment ships with what *is* Done. This is canonical Scrum, not violation. Microsoft's discipline is what *enabled* faster release cadence, the opposite of how it looks at first glance.

### Q26: **B. Inflation, not improvement**
The classic velocity-targeting anti-pattern. The team can't fundamentally change throughput by changing the unit, so they inflate the estimates a 5 becomes an 8 to "hit 40." Reported velocity rises, real value doesn't, and quality regresses as the team takes shortcuts to "ship 40 points." Within 2–3 Sprints, the data is meaningless. This is the failure mode Cohn warned about in *Agile Estimating and Planning* (2005) and the lesson Microsoft learned in 2011 (Module 6 case study reference).

### Q27: **B. #NoEstimates is Scrum-compatible**
The Scrum Guide does NOT prescribe estimation method. It requires Developers to be responsible for estimates and PBIs at top of backlog to fit in a Sprint. A #NoEstimates team satisfies both, they count completed PBIs (which is itself a primitive estimate) and keep items small. Vasco Duarte's argument is that *the estimation effort itself* is waste; a Scrum team can decide that's true for their context. Compliance turns on outcome (items fit in Sprint), not method.

### Q28: **B. Gradual tightening over time**
DoD evolves *upward* adding rigor as the team's capability and the product's stakes grow. The progression in (B) is realistic: start with what's truly achievable, add rigor at points where the Retrospective exposes a quality gap the team is ready to close. (A) is rigid and unrealistic; (C) is the anti-pattern (lowering DoD); (D) is impossible you can't ship without a DoD.

### Q29: **B. Compliant and defensible**
The Scrum Guide is silent on technique. T-shirt sizes work for early-stage backlogs and reduce false-precision debates. They make "velocity" harder to compute, which may itself be valuable if the team is trying to escape velocity-as-target dysfunction. This is a legitimate, often-superior choice for the right context.

### Q30: **B. Empiricism reveals the asymmetry**
DoD gates *output* without it, the team has no shared definition of "done" and quality becomes lottery. DoR gates *input* making it mandatory would re-introduce Waterfall-style "all requirements complete before Sprint" thinking. The Scrum Guide trusts the team to refine emergent items and demands rigor at the output. This is empiricism: trust the upstream process, enforce the downstream quality.
