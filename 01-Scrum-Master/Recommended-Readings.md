# Recommended Readings, Scrum Master (PSM I)

> **The single most important resource is the Scrum Guide itself, read it three times before the exam.** Everything below supplements but does not replace it. Use this list as a curated map: each entry includes *why* it's worth your time and *when* in the course to engage with it.

---

## 📖 Canonical textbooks (3–5)

### 1. *The Scrum Guide* (2020 edition), Sutherland & Schwaber, scrumguides.org
- **Length:** ~13 pages. Free PDF.
- **Why:** This is the *authoritative* definition of Scrum. The PSM I exam is literally based on this document. Every quiz answer, every Practice Exam, every scenario in this course traces back to the Guide.
- **When:** Read it before Module 1. Re-read after Module 5. Re-read again the night before the exam.
- **Cost:** Free.
- **Edition note:** The 2020 edition replaced the 2017 edition with significant changes (Developers terminology, Product Goal commitment, lean thinking foundation, 3-topic Sprint Planning, "true leader who serves" phrasing). Older editions are obsolete for the exam.

### 2. *Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work in Half the Time*, Jeff Sutherland, 2014, Crown Business
- **ISBN:** 978-0385346450
- **Why:** Sutherland is one of Scrum's two co-creators. This is his trade book, accessible, story-driven, full of case studies (Salesforce, Microsoft Windows 7, FBI Sentinel). Reads in 2 evenings and gives you the *why* behind every rule in the Guide.
- **When:** Read after Module 2. The narrative reinforces what you've learned about the framework.
- **Cost:** ~$18 paperback.

### 3. *Agile Estimating and Planning*, Mike Cohn, 2005, Prentice Hall PTR
- **ISBN:** 978-0131479418
- **Why:** Cohn defined what we now call story-point estimation, planning poker, and velocity. This is the canonical reference for *everything* the Scrum Guide is deliberately silent about regarding estimation. Essential for Module 6 depth and for actual Scrum Master practice.
- **When:** Read after Module 6, especially if your team uses story points.
- **Cost:** ~$45 paperback or ebook.

### 4. *Coaching Agile Teams*, Lyssa Adkins, 2010, Addison-Wesley Professional
- **ISBN:** 978-0321637703
- **Why:** The definitive book on the *behavior* of an effective Scrum Master. Covers the SM stances (coach, teacher, facilitator) in depth, with real conversations from Adkins' practice. The book PSM I scenario questions are essentially built around.
- **When:** Read alongside Module 7 (Servant Leadership) and use as a reference when working on the Capstone Project.
- **Cost:** ~$40 paperback.

### 5. *The Lean Startup*, Eric Ries, 2011, Crown Business
- **ISBN:** 978-0307887894
- **Why:** Ries codified Build–Measure–Learn and the Minimum Viable Product. Scrum's empiricism and the lean thinking added to the 2020 Guide are deeply connected to Ries's framework. Reading this connects Scrum to the broader product-development discipline.
- **When:** Read after Module 5 or in parallel with the Capstone.
- **Cost:** ~$15 paperback.

### 6. *The Phoenix Project*, Gene Kim, Kevin Behr, George Spafford, 2013, IT Revolution Press
- **ISBN:** 978-0988262508
- **Why:** A *novel* about a struggling IT operations team adopting DevOps and Agile. Reads like a thriller. Demonstrates servant leadership, impediment removal, and continuous improvement in narrative form, what you'd actually live through as a Scrum Master.
- **When:** Any time. Many readers finish it in one weekend.
- **Cost:** ~$15 paperback.

## 📜 Seminal papers, articles, and HBR pieces (5–10)

### 7. The Agile Manifesto (Beck et al, 2001), agilemanifesto.org
- **What:** The 68-word document that launched the Agile movement. Co-signed by 17 software developers including Beck, Sutherland, Schwaber, Cockburn, Fowler, Highsmith, Martin.
- **Why:** The foundational text. Memorize the 4 values. Skim the 12 principles. Take the principles seriously.
- **When:** Before Module 1.
- **Cost:** Free.

### 8. "The New New Product Development Game", Hirotaka Takeuchi & Ikujiro Nonaka, 1986, *Harvard Business Review* (Jan–Feb 1986)
- **What:** The HBR article that gave Scrum its name. Takeuchi and Nonaka studied Honda, Canon, NEC, Epson, Brother and observed that fast-moving product teams used a "rugby scrum" approach, overlapping development phases, multi-disciplinary teams, learning-as-they-went.
- **Why:** Sutherland and Schwaber cite this paper directly as the conceptual ancestor of Scrum. Required reading to understand why Scrum looks the way it does.
- **When:** Before Module 2.
- **Cost:** HBR subscription or ~$9 for the article.

### 9. "SCRUM Development Process", Sutherland & Schwaber, 1995, *OOPSLA '95 Business Object Design and Implementation Workshop*
- **What:** The first published paper on Scrum. Defines the original framework, including the term "Scrum Master."
- **Why:** Historical foundational text. Read to see what was in Scrum from day 1 vs what was added later.
- **When:** Optional supplementary reading, anytime.
- **Cost:** Free PDF online.

### 10. "Developmental Sequence in Small Groups", Bruce Tuckman, 1965, *Psychological Bulletin* 63(6): 384–399
- **What:** The original "Forming–Storming–Norming–Performing" paper. Tuckman added "Adjourning" in 1977.
- **Why:** Every team you ever work with will go through these stages. Knowing the model helps you, as Scrum Master, recognize Storming for what it is (necessary, not failure) and Performing for what it is (rare, hard-won).
- **When:** Before or alongside Module 7.
- **Cost:** ~$30 institutional access or free via Google Scholar.

### 11. "A Leader's Framework for Decision Making", David Snowden, 2007, *Harvard Business Review* (November 2007)
- **What:** Introduces the Cynefin framework, the model that distinguishes Simple, Complicated, Complex, and Chaotic domains.
- **Why:** Scrum is the framework for *complex* problems. Cynefin gives you the language to defend that distinction when stakeholders push for predictive plans on complex work.
- **When:** Alongside Module 1.
- **Cost:** HBR subscription.

### 12. "ING's agile transformation", McKinsey Quarterly, January 2017
- **What:** The canonical case study of ING Netherlands restructuring into squads/tribes (Module 3 case study).
- **Why:** Read alongside the Module 3 case to see McKinsey's framing of the same events.
- **When:** Alongside Module 3.
- **Cost:** Free.

### 13. "Failed #SquadGoals", Jeremiah Lee, 2020, jeremiahlee.com
- **What:** A former Spotify engineer's public critique of the Spotify model and the companies that copied it literally.
- **Why:** Read alongside the Module 1 Spotify case to see the insider counterpoint. Essential for understanding why "copying scaling structures" fails.
- **When:** Alongside Module 1 or Module 8.
- **Cost:** Free.

### 14. "One Strategy", Steven Sinofsky, 2010, Wiley (also a chapter in *One Strategy: Organization, Planning, and Decision Making* by Sinofsky & Iansiti)
- **Why:** Sinofsky's account of how Windows 7 shipped, the Module 6 case study. Shows what strict DoD enforcement looks like at 7,000-engineer scale.
- **When:** Optional. Reference if interested in Module 6's case.
- **Cost:** ~$30 hardcover.

### 15. "Agile Retrospectives: Making Good Teams Great", Esther Derby & Diana Larsen, 2006, Pragmatic Bookshelf
- **ISBN:** 978-0977616640
- **What:** The canonical book on Retrospective formats. All the formats mentioned in Module 4 (Sailboat, 4Ls, Start-Stop-Continue, etc.) trace back here.
- **Why:** If your real-world retros feel stale, this book is the cure.
- **When:** Before running real Retrospectives, or as part of the Capstone Project (Deliverable 3).
- **Cost:** ~$25.

### 16. "Practices for Scaling Lean & Agile Development", Craig Larman & Bas Vodde, 2010, Addison-Wesley Professional
- **ISBN:** 978-0321636409
- **Why:** The canonical text on multi-team Scrum, feature teams vs component teams, and the LeSS framework. Larman is one of the field's most rigorous thinkers.
- **When:** Alongside Module 8.
- **Cost:** ~$50.

## 🏭 Industry resources (blogs, podcasts, newsletters)

### 17. Scrum.org Blog, scrum.org/resources/blog
- **Why:** The official Scrum.org blog, with content from PSTs (Professional Scrum Trainers) including Sutherland, Verheyen, and Doshi. Posts often clarify edge cases that come up on the PSM I.
- **When:** Subscribe and skim weekly. Reference when you have a specific exam question that you can't resolve from the Guide alone.
- **Cost:** Free.

### 18. Atlassian Agile Coach, atlassian.com/agile
- **Why:** Atlassian (the Jira/Confluence company) has built a free, well-edited library of Agile/Scrum/Kanban primers. Their "Spotify Model" page (linked from Module 1's case study) is especially useful as it gives Atlassian's own retrospective on why the model didn't transplant.
- **When:** Use as a quick-reference resource.
- **Cost:** Free.

### 19. Mountain Goat Software Blog (Mike Cohn), mountaingoatsoftware.com/blog
- **Why:** Cohn writes prolifically about story estimation, planning, and Agile coaching. His posts are evidence-based and pragmatic. Mountain Goat also offers free training videos.
- **When:** Reference when working on Module 6 (estimation) or anything Cohn-defined (story points, INVEST, velocity).
- **Cost:** Free.

### 20. Agile Alliance, agilealliance.org
- **Why:** The professional society for Agile practitioners. Their *Agile2024*, *Agile2025*, *Agile2026* conference proceedings are publicly archived and include presentations from Spotify, Salesforce, Bosch, John Deere, and many others.
- **When:** Reference for case studies or when researching scaling frameworks.
- **Cost:** Free for most content; paid for conference attendance.

### 21. *InfoQ* Agile Track infoq.com/agile
- **Why:** Long-form articles and conference talks from senior practitioners. Good for staying current on Agile/Scrum thinking after you pass the exam.
- **When:** Post-exam, ongoing.
- **Cost:** Free.

### 22. "Agile Uprising" podcast, agileuprising.com
- **Why:** Multi-host podcast that goes deep on Scrum, Kanban, and Agile transformations. Useful for hearing how working Scrum Masters talk about the work.
- **When:** Listen during commutes; especially helpful for the Module 7 (Servant Leadership) topic.
- **Cost:** Free.

### 23. Scrum Inc. Newsletter (Jeff Sutherland's company), scruminc.com
- **Why:** Sutherland's own writings, training schedules, and case studies. Less for exam prep, more for ongoing practice.
- **When:** Subscribe after passing the exam.
- **Cost:** Free.

## 🎓 Free academic / MOOC overlap (2–3)

### 24. MIT OpenCourseWare "Engineering Innovation and Design" MIT 16.842
- **What:** A free MIT course that covers iterative design, prototyping, and includes coverage of Scrum as one implementation of iterative engineering.
- **Why:** Connects Scrum to the broader engineering-design discipline; good context for why iterative approaches outperform sequential ones in complex environments.
- **When:** Optional supplementary material; takes ~20 hours to work through.
- **URL:** ocw.mit.edu
- **Cost:** Free.

### 25. Stanford Online, "Designing Lean Operations" (Stanford Continuing Studies / Stanford LEAD)
- **What:** A short course on lean operations principles, kanban, just-in-time, value stream mapping.
- **Why:** Helps you understand the *lean thinking* foundation that Scrum 2020 added. Lean is half of Scrum's stated foundation but most Scrum prep ignores it.
- **When:** Optional; reference after Module 2.
- **Cost:** Free audit on Coursera or Stanford Online; certificate available for fee.

### 26. Harvard Online (HBX / Harvard Business School Online), "Disruptive Strategy" with Clayton Christensen
- **What:** A 6-week course on Christensen's Jobs-to-be-Done and disruption frameworks.
- **Why:** Christensen's JTBD framework is the conceptual foundation for *why* the Product Owner accountability exists, to discover and prioritize the jobs the product is hired to do. Optional but enriching.
- **When:** Post-exam, when working on real product management.
- **Cost:** ~$1,750 for the certificate; some lectures available free on YouTube.

### 27. edX, "Agile Leadership Principles" (multiple Harvard / MIT-affiliated courses)
- **What:** Several edX courses cover Agile leadership broadly. Search "Agile Leadership" on edx.org.
- **Why:** Good supplementary material if you want a structured course alongside this self-study.
- **When:** Optional supplementary.
- **Cost:** Free audit; paid certificate.

### 28. Coursera, "Agile Development" Specialization (University of Virginia, Darden School)
- **What:** A 6-course specialization on Agile that pairs nicely with PSM I preparation.
- **Why:** University-credentialed alternative if you want a paid certificate alongside the PSM I credential.
- **When:** Optional supplementary.
- **Cost:** ~$49/month Coursera subscription.

## 📚 Going deeper after PSM I

If you pass the PSM I and want to continue:

- **Professional Scrum Master II (PSM II)**, Scrum.org's intermediate certification; harder, more scenario-based. Requires deeper understanding of Adkins' *Coaching Agile Teams*.
- **Scaled Professional Scrum (SPS)**, Scrum.org's scaling cert focused on the Nexus framework. Read the *Nexus Guide* (scrum.org/resources/nexus-guide) first.
- **Professional Scrum Product Owner (PSPO)**, If you want to shift toward the PO accountability.
- **The original *Scrum* book** *Agile Software Development with Scrum* by Schwaber & Beedle (2002, Prentice Hall) historical foundational text. Heavier read than Sutherland's 2014 book, but the original.

## ⚠️ Notes on what NOT to read for exam prep

- **Pre-2020 Scrum Guides**, The 2020 edition replaced the 2017 edition with significant changes. Older guides will mislead you.
- **"Scrum certifications" not from Scrum.org**, Several other bodies (e.g., Scrum Alliance's CSM) teach Scrum but their exam questions differ in subtle ways. Stick to Scrum.org materials for PSM I.
- **"Hybrid Agile" books that prescribe additional rules**, Books that say "Scrum needs a project manager" or "Scrum requires story points" are teaching against the Guide. Useful for context, dangerous for exam prep.
- **Generic Agile certification cram books** that mix PSM and CSM content, These often present "best practices" the Scrum Guide does *not* prescribe, leading to wrong answers on PSM I.

---

> **One final note.** The PSM I exam is open-book in the sense that you can have the Scrum Guide PDF open while taking it. But you will NOT have time to look up every answer, the exam is 60 minutes for 80 questions, ~45 seconds each. The reading list above is to *build understanding*, not to use as a lookup. Internalize the Scrum Guide; everything else supplements.

Good luck. The PSM I is one of the most respected entry-level Agile credentials in the industry. Earning it gives you both a credential and the start of a real professional discipline.
