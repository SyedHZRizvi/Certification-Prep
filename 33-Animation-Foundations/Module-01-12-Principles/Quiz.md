## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1, B. Squash and Stretch**
Thomas and Johnston placed Squash and Stretch first because it is the foundation of all other principles, without it, objects feel rigid and lifeless.

**Q2, C. The volume (mass) of the object must remain constant**
This is the core constraint. A squashed ball must widen; a stretched ball must narrow. Volume = mass conservation. Violating this makes the object feel like it is inflating or deflating, not being compressed.

**Q3, C. Anticipation**
The extended pause before Miles leaps is precisely anticipation, preparing the audience (and Miles himself) for the action. The anticipation earns the emotional payoff.

**Q4, B. The audience cannot immediately identify what to look at or what emotion to feel**
Staging is about communicating ideas clearly. Bad staging means the audience is confused about where to look or what to feel.

**Q5, B. Straight-Ahead**
Straight-Ahead animation proceeds frame by frame in sequence. Pose-to-Pose plans key poses first, then fills in the in-betweens.

**Q6, C. Follow-Through and Overlapping Action**
The coat continuing past the stopped body is follow-through. The slightly different timing of coat vs body is overlapping action. Both occur in the same scenario.

**Q7, B. An S-shaped curve (flat at beginning and end, steep in the middle)**
A slow-in/slow-out motion is slow at start (flat curve), fast in the middle (steep curve), slow at end (flat curve again). This S-shape is the visual signature of ease-in/ease-out.

**Q8, C. Living things move in arcs because limbs rotate around joints**
This is the physics reason. Joints rotate; rotation produces arcs. A hand does not slide along a straight line, it swings through space on the arc of the arm's rotation.

**Q9, B. Secondary Action**
Secondary action supports the primary action without competing with it. The primary action is Chihiro holding still; the secondary action (trembling lip, controlled blink) shows the internal emotional effort.

**Q10, C. 48 frames for the entire lift**
More frames = more time = more weight and effort. A 48-frame lift communicates that the boulder is genuinely difficult to move. A 6-frame lift would read as the boulder being lightweight.

**Q11, C. Solid Drawing**
Solid Drawing is the principle about three-dimensional form, weight, and balance in the 2D medium. It includes foreshortening, overlapping, contour lines, and shadow.

**Q12, B. It communicated the weight of objects and strain of effort more clearly than anatomically correct movement**
Brad Bird's argument was that squash and stretch should serve emotional truth, not anatomical accuracy. The exaggerated deformation communicates clearly to the audience.

**Q13 A. 4 frames because the speed makes the impact surprising and sudden**
Speed is fundamental to physical comedy. The same gag at 4 frames reads as a shock/pratfall; at 48 frames it reads as tragic or deliberate.

**Q14 B. Timing frame count communicates character traits and emotional state**
The different frame rates are a deliberate application of Timing, the number of frames (and therefore the visual "weight" of the character's presence) communicates something about who each character is.

**Q15, B. A preparatory movement that occurs before the main action to signal what is about to happen**
This is the correct, technical definition of Anticipation.

**Q16, B. The straight-line path violates the Arcs principle; hands naturally follow curved paths**
The most common beginner error in 3D animation is linear interpolation producing straight-line paths. Arcs must be manually enforced.

**Q17 B. Straight-Ahead because its spontaneous, sequential nature captures the organic unpredictability of effects**
Effects animators traditionally work straight-ahead because fire, water, smoke, and explosion do not have planned "key poses." They flow and surprise.

**Q18, B. Stage the character large in the foreground with the monster barely visible in the background shadows**
This staging communicates the character's vulnerability (they are the focus, unaware of danger) while staging the monster as a threat (barely visible, hidden in shadow, something to be revealed).

**Q19, C. It supports and enriches the primary action without competing with it for the audience's focus**
Secondary action is always subordinate to the primary action. If it competes for attention, it has been misapplied.

**Q20 B. Characters must have an engaging quality clarity of character, that makes audiences want to watch them, whether or not they are likeable**
This is the correct, full definition. Appeal is not cuteness. Villains can have enormous appeal.

**Q21, B. Movement appears robotic, mechanical, or uniform in speed**
Constant-speed movement is the symptom of missing slow-in/slow-out. Everything looks like it is driven by a motor rather than muscle.

**Q22, C. *The Illusion of Life: Disney Animation* (1981)**
Thomas and Johnston's book is the primary source for the 12 Principles. Required reading for every animation student.

**Q23, B. The upper arm moves first, followed by the forearm a few frames later, followed by the hand a few frames after that**
This is overlapping action: sequential timing of connected elements. Each part of the arm responds to the initiating force in order, with a slight delay.

**Q24, C. Rhythm**
Rhythm is not one of the 12 Principles. The 12 are: Squash & Stretch, Anticipation, Staging, Straight-Ahead/Pose-to-Pose, Follow-Through/Overlapping Action, Slow-In/Slow-Out, Arcs, Secondary Action, Timing, Exaggeration, Solid Drawing, Appeal.
