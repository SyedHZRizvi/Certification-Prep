# Module 4: Storyboarding & Animatics 🎞️

> **Why this module matters:** Every animated production — from a 30-second commercial to a two-hour feature — begins with boards. Storyboarding is where the director's vision becomes a visual plan. An animator who cannot read boards will struggle to execute shots correctly; one who can board effectively can communicate ideas to a production team without building a single frame of final animation.

---

## 📖 A Story: How Storyboarding Saved *Finding Nemo*

In 2001, Andrew Stanton's *Finding Nemo* was in production at Pixar. The story had been through multiple versions, and a rough cut of the first act was not working — the emotional stakes were not clear, and test audiences were not connecting with Marlin, the father.

The solution came from story artists, not from animators. A team of story artists spent weeks re-boarding the first act, changing the staging of the opening sequence, the pacing of the shark scene, and the reveal of the fish tank world. They presented these new boards in what Pixar calls a "story reel" — an animatic, essentially — before a single shot of final animation was changed.

The story reel showed the new version working. Production was redirected. The final film's first act is substantially different from what existed before that boarding session — and the result is one of the most emotionally effective opening sequences in animated film history.

The lesson: **storyboards and animatics are the cheapest place to fix a film.** A story artist can redraw a shot in an afternoon. A finished shot takes a team of animators, lighters, and compositors weeks. Problems found in the boarding stage cost $500; problems found in the final render stage cost $500,000.

## 🖼️ Shot Types in Storyboarding

A storyboard artist must be fluent in the vocabulary of shot types. This is the same vocabulary covered in Module 2's visual language section — but now applied to the specific craft of boarding a scene.

| Shot | Abbreviation | Storyboard Use |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| Extreme Wide Shot | EWS | Establishing shots; showing geography; opening a scene |
| Wide Shot | WS | Full character action; entering/exiting spaces |
| Medium Shot | MS | Dialogue; character interaction |
| Close-Up | CU | Emotional reaction; critical story information |
| Extreme Close-Up | ECU | Specific object or detail; maximum emotional intensity |
| Insert | INS | A specific object cut in for emphasis |
| Point of View | POV | What a character sees; establishes the character's physical or emotional position |

## 🔄 Transitions in Storyboarding

| Transition | How It Works | When to Use |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Cut** | Instant switch; no transition | Standard; continuous action; 95% of all transitions |
| **Dissolve** | Images blend from one to next | Time passage; memory; dream sequence |
| **Fade to Black** | Image fades to black then new scene fades in | Act break; end of sequence; significant time jump |
| **Wipe** | One image pushes off screen as another pushes on | Comic effect; stylized transition (Star Wars) |
| **Match Cut** | Visual element matches between two shots | Elegant scene transitions; thematic parallels |
| **J-Cut / L-Cut** | Audio from the next/previous scene overlaps the cut | Reality of sound; foreshadowing |

## ✏️ Thumbnailing: The First Draft of Vision

**Thumbnailing** is the process of making tiny, rough sketches (typically 1"–2" each) of potential compositions before committing to a full storyboard panel. The thumbnail is fast, cheap, and exploratory.

The professional goal of thumbnailing is to solve the *staging problem* — deciding which camera angle, which shot size, and which composition best tells the story beat — before investing time in a finished panel.

**The 3-thumbnail rule:** For any significant story beat, draw at least three different thumbnail compositions before choosing one. The first thumbnail is usually the most obvious choice; the third thumbnail is usually the most interesting one.

## 📋 Storyboard Format and Panel Elements

A standard storyboard panel contains:

1. **The drawing** — clear staging of the action and character positions
2. **Action description** — what happens in the shot (below the panel)
3. **Dialogue** — any spoken lines in the shot
4. **Camera direction** — arrows indicating camera moves (pan, tilt, push, pull)
5. **Shot number** — sequential numbering for production tracking
6. **Scene/sequence number** — organizational reference to the script

**Arrow conventions:**
- Arrow pointing right or left = camera **pans** (horizontal)
- Arrow pointing up or down = camera **tilts** (vertical)
- Arrow pointing toward the center = camera **pushes in** (zoom in effect)
- Arrow pointing outward = camera **pulls out** (zoom out effect)

## 🎬 Animatics: Boards in Motion

An **animatic** is a storyboard presented in video form, cut to timing, with placeholder sound (voice-over, music, or temp track). It is sometimes called a "story reel" or "animatic rough cut."

The animatic is the first time a production team can evaluate **whether the story works in time** — whether the scene is too long, too short, whether the pacing of the reveals is correct, whether the jokes land at the right moment.

**What an animatic proves (or disproves):**
- Pacing — does the scene breathe, or does it drag?
- Timing of emotional beats — does the audience have time to register the emotion?
- Clarity of action — can the audience follow what is happening?
- Comedy timing — does the gag land at the right frame?
- Music and sound sync — does the visual rhythm match the audio rhythm?

**Industry tool: Storyboard Pro (Toon Boom)**
The industry-standard application for professional storyboarding. Storyboard Pro integrates drawing, panel management, camera move simulation, audio import, and animatic export in one application. Used at Pixar, DreamWorks, and virtually every major animation studio.

**Alternative tool: Miro**
For collaborative boarding — especially in game development and pre-production — Miro provides an infinite canvas where multiple people can contribute to a board simultaneously. Not a dedicated storyboard application, but widely used for collaborative visual planning.

## 🎭 Pitching Boards to a Director

The board pitch is a fundamental professional skill. You stand in front of a room and *perform* the boards — describing the action, speaking the dialogue, conveying the emotion of each panel.

**The board pitch is not a reading.** It is a performance. The best board artists perform their pitches with energy, voice characterization, and physical gesture — because the pitch is the first test of whether the scene works emotionally. If you cannot make the room feel the scene while you are pitching, the scene probably does not work.

**Pitch structure:**
1. **Context** — "We're in the fish tank world. Gill has just told Nemo the escape plan."
2. **Walk through the boards** — describe action, voice dialogue, indicate key camera moves
3. **Justify significant choices** — "I chose a low angle here to make Gill look more menacing than we've seen him before"
4. **Invite notes** — "Does the pacing of the shark reveal feel too fast?"

**What directors look for in a pitch:**
- Clarity of story logic — does the scene make sense?
- Emotional truth — do the character reactions feel earned?
- Visual efficiency — is the staging doing the most work with the fewest panels?
- Solvable problems — does the board artist understand what problems exist and have ideas for solutions?

## 🔬 Continuity: The Rules That Keep Sequences Coherent

**Continuity** in animation is the consistency of character position, physical props, lighting, and staging across the cuts within a scene. A character whose coffee cup switches from their right hand to their left between shots has a continuity error.

More subtle and more common: **spatial continuity** — maintaining consistent understanding of where characters are positioned relative to each other and to the environment across a series of cuts.

The storyboard is where spatial continuity is planned. The board artist draws a **floor plan** of the scene — a bird's-eye diagram showing where each character stands, where the camera is placed for each shot, and how the camera moves between shots — to ensure that the shots cut together coherently.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Continuity, the 180-degree rule, and shot transitions are frequently tested together. Know the vocabulary; know the purpose of each.
