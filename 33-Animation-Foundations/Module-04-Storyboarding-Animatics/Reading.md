# Module 4: Storyboarding & Animatics 🎞️

> **Why this module matters:** Every animated production from a 30-second commercial to a two-hour feature begins with boards. Storyboarding is where the director's vision becomes a visual plan. An animator who cannot read boards will struggle to execute shots correctly; one who can board effectively can communicate ideas to a production team without building a single frame of final animation.

---

## 📖 A Story: How Storyboarding Saved *Finding Nemo*

In 2001, Andrew Stanton's *Finding Nemo* was in production at Pixar. The story had been through multiple versions, and a rough cut of the first act was not working, the emotional stakes were not clear, and test audiences were not connecting with Marlin, the father.

The solution came from story artists, not from animators. A team of story artists spent weeks re-boarding the first act, changing the staging of the opening sequence, the pacing of the shark scene, and the reveal of the fish tank world. They presented these new boards in what Pixar calls a "story reel" an animatic, essentially before a single shot of final animation was changed.

The story reel showed the new version working. Production was redirected. The final film's first act is substantially different from what existed before that boarding session, and the result is one of the most emotionally effective opening sequences in animated film history.

The lesson: **storyboards and animatics are the cheapest place to fix a film.** A story artist can redraw a shot in an afternoon. A finished shot takes a team of animators, lighters, and compositors weeks. Problems found in the boarding stage cost $500; problems found in the final render stage cost $500,000.

## 🎬 Case Study: The Storyboard Crisis of *Ratatouille* (2007)

Before Brad Bird directed *Ratatouille* (2007), the film had a different director and had been in production at Pixar for three years. When Bird was brought in as the new director, he saw a near-completed animatic and made a radical decision: he re-boarded approximately 70% of the film, including the climactic restaurant sequence.

The original boards staged the cooking and criticism sequences as conventional dramatic scenes characters in kitchens, close-ups on food, reaction shots. Bird's re-boarding introduced the famous Gusteau's kitchen-as-orchestra staging: the kitchen is a system, every chef is an instrument, and Remy is the conductor. The new storyboards established spatial relationships between stations in the kitchen that became the logic for all subsequent production decisions environment design, lighting, camera positions.

The re-boarding cost Pixar months of work and required revising modeled environments that had been nearly finalized. The financial cost was significant. The creative result was a film with one of the most coherent sequences of sustained spatial logic in animated cinema, the audience always knows exactly where Remy is relative to Linguini, the stove, the door, and the window, across dozens of shots.

**The production lesson:** No amount of animation, effects, or lighting can compensate for boards that do not solve the spatial logic of a scene. The storyboard stage is the correct place to solve spatial problems, not because it is creative tradition, but because it is the cheapest place in the pipeline to do so.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** The *Ratatouille* case illustrates that spatial continuity must be solved in the storyboard phase. Know that the storyboard is the definitive spatial document for every shot in a production.

## 🎬 Case Study: How *Coco* Used Storyboards to Solve an Unsolvable Problem

Pixar's *Coco* (2017) presents one of the hardest problems in animated filmmaking: the Land of the Dead is populated by millions of animated skeletons, each of whom is a distinct character with a distinct life story. The storyboard department faced a challenge that had no precedent: how do you establish emotional stakes for a world of faces that are, by definition, without flesh?

Director Lee Unkrich and co-director Adrian Molina worked with story artists for over a year on a single problem: which skeleton face designs communicate personality distinctly enough to be distinguishable at film speed, and how do you board scenes to ensure the audience always knows who they are looking at in a crowd?

The solution was storyboarded before it was modeled: every major skeleton character would have one or two distinctive visual hooks an extraordinary hat, a distinctive jaw width, a color accent in their clothing that read in silhouette at a distance. The storyboards established these design constraints visually, before any technical work began.

The lesson: storyboards are not just shot planning. They are the testing ground for every visual decision in a production, including character design, environment design, and color.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** The case of *Coco* illustrates that story artists must understand character design and staging simultaneously, the storyboard is where these disciplines first intersect.

## 🖼️ Shot Types in Storyboarding

A storyboard artist must be fluent in the vocabulary of shot types. This is the same vocabulary covered in Module 2's visual language section, but now applied to the specific craft of boarding a scene.

| Shot | Abbreviation | Storyboard Use |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| Extreme Wide Shot | EWS | Establishing shots; showing geography; opening a scene |
| Wide Shot | WS | Full character action; entering/exiting spaces |
| Medium Shot | MS | Dialogue; character interaction |
| Close-Up | CU | Emotional reaction; critical story information |
| Extreme Close-Up | ECU | Specific object or detail; maximum emotional intensity |
| Insert | INS | A specific object cut in for emphasis |
| Point of View | POV | What a character sees; establishes the character's physical or emotional position |

---

## 🔄 Transitions in Storyboarding

| Transition | How It Works | When to Use |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Cut** | Instant switch; no transition | Standard; continuous action; 95% of all transitions |
| **Dissolve** | Images blend from one to next | Time passage; memory; dream sequence |
| **Fade to Black** | Image fades to black then new scene fades in | Act break; end of sequence; significant time jump |
| **Wipe** | One image pushes off screen as another pushes on | Comic effect; stylized transition (Star Wars) |
| **Match Cut** | Visual element matches between two shots | Elegant scene transitions; thematic parallels |
| **J-Cut / L-Cut** | Audio from the next/previous scene overlaps the cut | Reality of sound; foreshadowing |

## ✏️ Thumbnailing: The First Draft of Vision

**Thumbnailing** is the process of making tiny, rough sketches (typically 1"–2" each) of potential compositions before committing to a full storyboard panel. The thumbnail is fast, cheap, and exploratory.

The professional goal of thumbnailing is to solve the *staging problem* deciding which camera angle, which shot size, and which composition best tells the story beat before investing time in a finished panel.

**The 3-thumbnail rule:** For any significant story beat, draw at least three different thumbnail compositions before choosing one. The first thumbnail is usually the most obvious choice; the third thumbnail is usually the most interesting one.

---

## 📋 Storyboard Format and Panel Elements

A standard storyboard panel contains:

1. **The drawing**, clear staging of the action and character positions
2. **Action description**, what happens in the shot (below the panel)
3. **Dialogue**, any spoken lines in the shot
4. **Camera direction**, arrows indicating camera moves (pan, tilt, push, pull)
5. **Shot number**, sequential numbering for production tracking
6. **Scene/sequence number**, organizational reference to the script

**Arrow conventions:**
- Arrow pointing right or left = camera **pans** (horizontal)
- Arrow pointing up or down = camera **tilts** (vertical)
- Arrow pointing toward the center = camera **pushes in** (zoom in effect)
- Arrow pointing outward = camera **pulls out** (zoom out effect)

## 🎬 Animatics: Boards in Motion

An **animatic** is a storyboard presented in video form, cut to timing, with placeholder sound (voice-over, music, or temp track). It is sometimes called a "story reel" or "animatic rough cut."

The animatic is the first time a production team can evaluate **whether the story works in time**, whether the scene is too long, too short, whether the pacing of the reveals is correct, whether the jokes land at the right moment.

**What an animatic proves (or disproves):**
- Pacing, does the scene breathe, or does it drag?
- Timing of emotional beats, does the audience have time to register the emotion?
- Clarity of action, can the audience follow what is happening?
- Comedy timing, does the gag land at the right frame?
- Music and sound sync, does the visual rhythm match the audio rhythm?

**Industry tool: Storyboard Pro (Toon Boom)**
The industry-standard application for professional storyboarding. Storyboard Pro integrates drawing, panel management, camera move simulation, audio import, and animatic export in one application. Used at Pixar, DreamWorks, and virtually every major animation studio.

**Alternative tool: Miro**
For collaborative boarding especially in game development and pre-production Miro provides an infinite canvas where multiple people can contribute to a board simultaneously. Not a dedicated storyboard application, but widely used for collaborative visual planning.

## 🎭 Pitching Boards to a Director

The board pitch is a fundamental professional skill. You stand in front of a room and *perform* the boards, describing the action, speaking the dialogue, conveying the emotion of each panel.

**The board pitch is not a reading.** It is a performance. The best board artists perform their pitches with energy, voice characterization, and physical gesture, because the pitch is the first test of whether the scene works emotionally. If you cannot make the room feel the scene while you are pitching, the scene probably does not work.

**Pitch structure:**
1. **Context**, "We're in the fish tank world. Gill has just told Nemo the escape plan."
2. **Walk through the boards**, describe action, voice dialogue, indicate key camera moves
3. **Justify significant choices**, "I chose a low angle here to make Gill look more menacing than we've seen him before"
4. **Invite notes**, "Does the pacing of the shark reveal feel too fast?"

**What directors look for in a pitch:**
- Clarity of story logic, does the scene make sense?
- Emotional truth, do the character reactions feel earned?
- Visual efficiency, is the staging doing the most work with the fewest panels?
- Solvable problems, does the board artist understand what problems exist and have ideas for solutions?

## 🧭 Storyboarding for Different Production Types

Storyboarding conventions vary significantly between production contexts. A board that is correct for a feature film may be wrong for a TV series or a game cinematic.

| Production Type | Board Style | Key Priorities | Typical Panel Count |
|----------------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Theatrical feature** | Detailed; shaded; high craft | Emotional clarity; complex staging | 2,000–8,000 panels per film |
| **TV animation** | Simpler; faster; often digital | Efficiency; clear action lines; layout reference | 300–600 panels per 22-min episode |
| **Game cinematic** | Detailed; perspective-accurate | Technical accuracy; camera lock-off often required | Varies; often pre-vis quality |
| **Commercial/spot** | Precise; tight timing | Shot-by-shot timing (frames matter); brand consistency | 15–40 panels per 30-second spot |
| **Music video** | Often impressionistic | Mood; rhythm; visual metaphor | 50–150 panels |

**The TV animation speed requirement:** A television story artist typically boards 30–50 panels per day. A feature film story artist may spend a week on 20 panels during complex sequences. The skill sets overlap but the pacing requirements are dramatically different.

### The Spider-Verse Storyboarding Approach

*Into the Spider-Verse* pushed the boundaries of what a storyboard could communicate. Because the film's visual style was unprecedented no one had boarded for "comic book panels within cinematic frames" before the story department had to invent new conventions:

- **Sound effects as visual elements**: the "THWIP" and "POW" text effects were planned in storyboard panels, not added in post
- **Panel border effects**: scenes where Miles crosses between panels in the Spider-Verse used frame-within-frame staging that had to be worked out at the board level
- **Multi-character staging**: scenes with multiple Spider-People from different visual styles required the boards to specify which character was rendered in which visual language

The film's story artists including Humberto Rosa, Alberto Mielgo (who later directed the "Witness" segment of *Love Death + Robots*), and others effectively invented a new visual language for storyboarding that has been influential in subsequent productions.

## 📊 The Animatic as Communication Tool: Studios vs Freelancers

The professional use of animatics differs between large studio productions and freelance/independent animation.

| Context | Animatic Purpose | Typical Tools |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Feature film studio** | Internal approval at each story review milestone | Toon Boom Storyboard Pro; Avid; internal story reel system |
| **TV animation studio** | Delivered to production pipeline as layout reference | Storyboard Pro; sometimes Premiere |
| **Independent short** | Self-approval; festival committee review | Storyboard Pro; Premiere; even iMovie |
| **Commercial production** | Client approval before animation begins | Often PDF of panels; sometimes simple video |
| **Game cinematic** | Technical pre-visualization before filming/capture | Storyboard Pro; Maya pre-vis; Unreal Engine |

**The client animatic:** In commercial animation, the animatic shown to a client before production is often the most critical communication tool in the entire project. It is the last point where changes are inexpensive. Clients who approve an animatic and then request changes after animation is complete pay significantly more, and animators who can produce compelling animatics give their clients the confidence to approve quickly.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Know that the animatic's primary function changes based on production context. In a feature, it is an internal development tool. In a commercial, it is the primary client approval document.

## 🔬 Continuity: The Rules That Keep Sequences Coherent

**Continuity** in animation is the consistency of character position, physical props, lighting, and staging across the cuts within a scene. A character whose coffee cup switches from their right hand to their left between shots has a continuity error.

More subtle and more common: **spatial continuity**, maintaining consistent understanding of where characters are positioned relative to each other and to the environment across a series of cuts.

The storyboard is where spatial continuity is planned. The board artist draws a **floor plan** of the scene a bird's-eye diagram showing where each character stands, where the camera is placed for each shot, and how the camera moves between shots to ensure that the shots cut together coherently.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Continuity, the 180-degree rule, and shot transitions are frequently tested together. Know the vocabulary; know the purpose of each.

## 🔬 Shot Progression: How a Scene is Built from Boards

Understanding how a storyboard sequence is constructed from first concept to final animatic illuminates why the storyboard is the most powerful problem-solving document in animation production.

### The Anatomy of a Boarded Scene

Using the opening sequence of *Finding Nemo* as a model, here is how a complex scene is typically broken into boards:

**Step 1, The establishing context:** The audience needs to know where they are before they care about who is there. An EWS (Extreme Wide Shot) or WS establishing the coral reef geography, the time of day, and the scale of the environment. This is always the first board in a new location.

**Step 2, The character introduction:** The protagonist (Marlin) appears in a medium shot that establishes him in the environment, his physical scale relative to the space, and his emotional state (excited, nervous, hopeful). The camera angle is chosen to communicate his power relationship to the environment.

**Step 3 The emotional close-up:** The detail shot that makes the audience invest for *Nemo*'s opening, this is the close-up of the single surviving egg that Marlin names Nemo. This is the shot the entire scene has been building toward, and the board holds it long enough for the audience to register the emotional weight.

**Step 4 The action and reaction:** The scene's inciting event (the barracuda attack) requires a different board strategy very fast cuts, low shot duration, kinetic camera moves that communicate violence and speed. The reaction shots (Marlin before and after) are boarded as wide/medium shots that show his vulnerability.

**Step 5 The hold:** After the action, a long beat Marlin alone with the surviving egg. The board holds on his stillness. The silence of this board choice does what no action could.

This sequence establishing → character → close-up → action/reaction → hold is not a formula but a pattern that experienced story artists recognize and use as a structural toolkit.

### Key Skills That Differentiate Storyboard Artists

The skills that separate an intermediate story artist from a senior one are not primarily drawing skills:

| Differentiating Skill | Description |
|----------------------|-------------|
| **Story structure instinct** | Recognizing when a beat is not working and understanding *why*, not just what looks wrong |
| **Director's language** | Translating a director's verbal note ("this feels slow") into a specific visual revision |
| **Economy** | Telling the same story with fewer panels; knowing which panels can be cut |
| **Pitching performance** | Making a room feel the scene during a board pitch |
| **Collaboration under pressure** | Taking strong notes and revising quickly without becoming defensive |

## ⚠️ Common Traps in Storyboarding

| Common Mistake | Correct Approach |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Boarding one shot per beat | A single story beat often requires 3–6 shots; board for coverage, not minimalism |
| All shots at eye level | Vary camera angle to communicate power relationships; eye level is neutral, not default |
| Arrows everywhere on the panel | Arrows indicate camera moves only, not character movement; use action notes for character action |
| Boarding before understanding the emotional beat | Identify the emotional purpose of the beat before deciding the camera angle |
| Matching shot sizes through an entire scene | Shot size variety (WS → CU → MS) creates visual rhythm; same shot size for every panel creates monotony |
| Forgetting the 180-degree rule | Draw a floor plan for any scene with multiple characters before boarding the individual shots |

🚨 **Trap on the exam:** Students frequently confuse "shot" and "scene." A *scene* is a unit of story (usually continuous in time and space). A *shot* is a single camera take within a scene. One scene contains many shots. One panel of storyboard typically represents one shot.

## 📊 Transition Type Reference: When to Use Each

The choice of transition between shots is a timing and meaning decision that the storyboard must specify. This reference table covers all standard transitions and their appropriate contexts.

| Transition | Visual Description | Typical Use | When to Avoid |
|-----------|------------------|------------|---------------|
| **Hard cut** | Instantaneous switch with no transition | Continuous action; standard dialogue cuts; 95%+ of all transitions | Never avoid, this is the default |
| **Dissolve / cross-dissolve** | Two images blend over 12–48 frames | Memory; time passing; dreamlike connection between scenes | Real-time action; strong emotional contrast moments |
| **Fade to black** | Image darkens to black; new scene fades in from black | End of act; significant time jump; death | Within a continuous scene; loss of momentum |
| **Fade to white** | Image brightens to white; new scene fades in from white | Memories; overexposure; transcendence | Same avoidances as fade to black |
| **Wipe** | One image pushes off as another pushes on | Stylized/comic productions; Star Wars; deliberate style choice | Realistic drama; emotional weight moments |
| **Match cut** | A visual element (shape, motion, direction) matches between two different scenes | Elegant transitions; thematic parallels; smart writing reward | Forced matches that have no thematic resonance |
| **J-Cut** | Audio from Scene B begins while Scene A's image is still showing | Building anticipation; reality of sound preceding vision | Very short scenes where audio lead has no payoff |
| **L-Cut** | Audio from Scene A continues while Scene B's image begins | Reality of lingering sound; character still processing previous scene | Same avoidances as J-Cut |
| **Smash cut** | Hard cut to a dramatically contrasting scene | Shock; comedy; ironic juxtaposition | Any moment where the contrast disrupts rather than serves |
| **Jump cut** | Two shots of the same subject from slightly different angles | Deliberate time compression; disorientation; video essay style | Traditional storytelling where continuity is valued |

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Know the J-cut vs L-cut distinction (J-cut: audio from next scene leads the visual cut; L-cut: audio from previous scene trails the visual cut). This is the most frequently confused pair.

## 🔬 Pacing Analysis: The *Spirited Away* Train Sequence

The sequence in *Spirited Away* where Chihiro and her companions travel by train across the flooded landscape approximately 5 minutes with almost no dialogue and only ambient sound is one of the most studied pacing choices in modern animation. It is useful as a case study precisely because it is so unusual.

**What happens in the sequence:** The characters board a train. They ride. They watch other passengers. They get off. Nothing else occurs.

**Why it exists (Miyazaki's stated reasons):** The sequence is about the experience of time passing, not clock time but experiential time. Chihiro is not actively solving her problem during this sequence. She is waiting. The sequence puts the audience in the same state: waiting, observing, moving through space without purpose. The pacing communicates interiority.

**The storyboard choices that produce this effect:**

| Board Choice | Effect |
|-------------|--------|
| Very long held shots on landscape (30–60 seconds each) | Audience cannot skip forward emotionally; must inhabit the time |
| Characters in profile or from behind, not facing camera | Audience cannot read expression; mystery is maintained |
| Other passengers who are ghostly, anonymous | No character introduction; no story obligation created |
| Ambient water/train sound, minimal score | No musical cue to tell audience how to feel |
| Cut only when camera has run out of journey | Every cut feels like a gentle necessity, not a story driver |

This sequence teaches, more than any explicit principle, that **the storyboard is where pace is created, not just planned**. The specific shot duration that Miyazaki chose for each board panel is the sequence's meaning. Change the held duration and you change what the audience experiences.

## ⚠️ The Floor Plan: Why Every Complex Scene Needs One

The floor plan a bird's-eye diagram showing character positions, camera positions, and camera movements for a multi-shot scene is the most underused tool in student storyboarding and the most relied-upon tool in professional storyboarding.

Without a floor plan, a complex scene with multiple characters and multiple camera angles frequently violates the 180-degree rule by accident. The story artist boards each shot individually without maintaining a consistent mental map of the scene's physical space, and the result is a sequence that cuts together with characters appearing to teleport or switch sides.

**The floor plan should show:**
- The location of every character in the scene
- The location and angle of the camera for every shot
- The direction the camera moves, if it moves
- The axis of action (the line between primary characters that all cameras must stay on one side of)
- Any objects or environmental features that affect spatial logic

**A practical rule:** If a scene has more than two characters or more than three camera angles, draw a floor plan before you board the shots. The 10 minutes spent on the floor plan save hours of revision when the sequence cuts together wrong.

## 🎯 Module 4 Quick Reference: The Storyboard Decision Framework

When boarding a scene, use this decision framework to determine the right shot choice for each moment.

| Story Moment | Camera Distance | Camera Angle | Transition To Next |
|-------------|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Opening a new location** | EWS or WS (establish geography) | Eye level or slight high angle (neutral, orienting) | Cut to character within that location |
| **Character making a decision** | CU or MCU (internal moment) | Eye level | Cut to what they decide to do |
| **Physical conflict / action** | Varies rapidly; use multiple shot sizes | Dynamic; low angle for power, high angle for vulnerability | Cut with action matches; fast pace |
| **Emotional revelation** | CU → ECU progression | Hold on character; let the realization land | Hold longer than comfortable; let the silence work |
| **Comedy beat** | Establish in WS; punch in for reaction | Often slightly low angle for comedic dignity | Cut on the best frame of the reaction; not too soon, not too late |
| **Villain's moment of power** | Low angle (make them large in frame) | Below eye line; character fills top of frame | Cut to high-angle shot of vulnerable protagonist |
| **Character connection / warmth** | Medium two-shot (both characters visible) | Eye level; comfortable | Dissolve is acceptable for time passing; cut for real-time dialogue |
