# Module 9: Your First Animated Short 🎬

> **Why this module matters:** Everything before this module was learning to read. This module is writing your first sentence. The bouncing ball, the walk cycle, the character performance, these are the three foundational exercises that every animation program in the world uses to teach animation, and for good reason. Each one is harder than it looks and teaches something essential.

---

## 📖 A Story: Richard Williams and the Ball

Richard Williams, director of *Who Framed Roger Rabbit* and author of *The Animator's Survival Kit*, taught master classes on animation throughout his career. He always began the same way.

He put a bouncing ball on screen. A simple circle. Bouncing.

Then he showed 40 different versions of a bouncing ball the same shape, the same trajectory, the same start and end position animated with 40 different timing and spacing choices. Each ball communicated something completely different: rubber, metal, water balloon, cotton ball, lead weight. No visual cue except the spacing of the frames.

"You think this is simple," he said. "This is everything."

He was right. The bouncing ball teaches squash and stretch, slow-in/slow-out, anticipation, follow-through, arcs, timing, and weight, the entire toolkit of animation, compressed into one exercise. Students who master the bouncing ball with genuine control over all its variables are students who understand animation. Students who rush through it to "get to the good stuff" carry that gap with them for years.

This module will not let you rush.

## 🎬 Case Study: *Luxo Jr.* (1986) and What Makes a Minimal Character Compelling (Pre-Exercise Context)

Before you attempt the bouncing ball, it is worth understanding *why* Pixar's very first theatrical short *Luxo Jr.* begins with exactly this premise: a ball bounced by two desk lamps. The choice was not arbitrary.

John Lasseter chose a ball as the central prop because it is the most explicit demonstration of the animator's control over physics. A ball has no internal structure that a viewer can misread. It has no character design that draws attention away from the animation. It is pure timing, pure physics, pure principle demonstration.

When Luxo Jr. (the baby lamp) bounces too hard and the ball deflates, the entire emotional response depends on one thing: did the animator's choices communicate the ball's mass and material before the impact? If the ball felt real before it was destroyed, the destruction has consequence. If it felt like a floating sphere, the deflation is just geometry changing.

The bouncing ball exercise exists to teach you the same thing Lasseter demonstrated in 1986: before a character can be believed, the objects in its world must be believed. Animation is world-building at the level of individual frames.

## ⚽ Exercise 1: The Bouncing Ball

### What the Bouncing Ball Teaches

| Principle | How the Ball Teaches It |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Squash & Stretch** | Ball squashes on impact; stretches on flight, volume constant |
| **Slow-In/Slow-Out** | Slow near the top of the arc; fast at the bottom |
| **Arcs** | The ball's path through space is a parabolic arc |
| **Timing** | Frame count = perceived weight and material |
| **Anticipation** | A ball dropped from height anticipates by appearing stationary then beginning to fall |
| **Follow-Through** | The ball bounces a second, third time, smaller each time |
| **Weight** | 4 frames = ping-pong ball; 24 frames = medicine ball |

### The Bouncing Ball Checklist

Before you call it done:

- [ ] The ball follows a clean parabolic arc, not a V-shape or angular path
- [ ] Squash at the moment of impact, the ball widens as it compresses
- [ ] Stretch on the way down to and up from the floor, the ball elongates
- [ ] Volume remains constant: squashed wide ball and stretched narrow ball are the same "mass"
- [ ] More frames near the top of each arc (slow-in/slow-out)
- [ ] Fewer frames at the bottom of each arc (fastest at impact)
- [ ] Each successive bounce is smaller and faster (energy dissipation)
- [ ] The final "settle" is convincing, the ball doesn't just stop, it diminishes

### Variations to Practice

Once the basic rubber ball works, redo it as:
1. A golf ball (small squash, hard, fast bounces that decay quickly)
2. A basketball (medium squash, predictable geometry)
3. A water balloon (extreme squash, breaks on final impact)
4. A bowling ball (minimal squash, slow, each bounce small and labored)
5. A soap bubble (floats, minimal gravity, pops on contact)

Each variation is the same exercise with different timing and squash-stretch ratios. Completing all five demonstrates control.

## ⚠️ Common Bouncing Ball Mistakes (and Why They Matter)

The bouncing ball is deceptively simple to describe and genuinely difficult to execute with control. Every mistake a student makes on the bouncing ball is a mistake they will also make on a character, a walk cycle, and a performance, just more hidden.

| Common Mistake | What It Reveals | Correction |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Ball follows V-shaped path** | Misunderstanding of arc physics; arcs are parabolic, not angular | Study parabolic arc; the ball's path curves, it does not corner |
| **Volume changes between squash and stretch** | Misunderstanding of volume conservation | Measure the ball area in each drawing: squashed = wider and shorter; stretched = narrower and taller |
| **Even spacing throughout** | Misunderstanding of slow-in/slow-out | Drawings must cluster at arc peak and spread at impact zone |
| **Squash on wrong frame** | Misunderstanding of contact timing | Squash happens at the exact frame of ground contact, not a frame before or after |
| **Ball just stops bouncing** | Misunderstanding of energy dissipation | Successive bounces decrease in height AND in duration; the final settle is a micro-bounce |
| **No stretch on fall** | Anticipation and follow-through not applied | The ball stretches slightly in the direction of travel on every fast movement, not just on impact |

🎯 **What the exam tests:** The bouncing ball is a direct test of understanding all seven principles simultaneously. Assessors look specifically at volume consistency and the relationship between speed and squash/stretch degree.

## 🚶 Exercise 2: The Walk Cycle

### Why Walk Cycles Are the Animation Industry's Standard Test

Every studio in the world every recruiter, every hiring animator uses walk cycles as the primary test of a junior animator's understanding. The walk cycle contains:

- **Weight shift**, the character's center of gravity shifts laterally with each step
- **Contact and passing positions**, the two key poses of a walk
- **Up and down movement**, the body rises on the passing position, falls on the contact
- **Arm swing**, opposite arm from leading leg swings forward
- **Head bob**, subtle up-and-down that communicates weight
- **Timing**, 12 frames per step (8 fps on 2s) for a typical walk; 8 frames for a snappy walk; 16 frames for a tired/heavy walk

### The Key Positions of a Walk

```
CONTACT POSITION: 
- Leading foot touches ground
- Body at its lowest point (just before weight is transferred)
- Opposite arm forward

PASSING POSITION:
- Free leg swings past the standing leg (passing)  
- Body at its highest point
- Arms at neutral center position

DOWN POSITION:
- Body drops as weight transfers to forward foot

UP POSITION (Recoil):
- Body rises as the standing leg straightens and pushes off
```

### Walk Cycle Timing Reference

| Type | Frames per Step | Impression |
|------|----------------|------------|
| Snappy/energetic | 8–10 | Young, energetic, excited |
| Normal | 12 | Standard adult walk |
| Tired/heavy | 16–20 | Exhausted, old, sad, carrying weight |
| Menacing/deliberate | 20–24 | Threatening, powerful, purposeful |

### The Walk Cycle Checklist

- [ ] Contact and passing positions are clearly defined key poses
- [ ] Center of gravity shifts laterally with each step (the body moves side to side slightly)
- [ ] Body rises on passing position, falls on contact/down
- [ ] Arm swing is opposite to leg (right arm forward when left leg leads)
- [ ] Head bob is subtle, not cartoon-exaggerated unless that is the style
- [ ] The cycle loops seamlessly, the last frame connects to the first without pop
- [ ] Timing communicates the character's personality and emotional state

## 📊 Walk Cycle Style Comparison: Four Studios

Understanding how different studios approach the same technical problem a character walking is one of the clearest ways to understand the range of the animator's expressive toolkit.

| Studio / Style | Walk Cycle Characteristics | Key Influences |
|----------------|--------------------------|---------------|
| **Disney Feature** | Full weight shift; pronounced hip sway; secondary action on hair, clothing; naturalistic | Life drawing; animal locomotion study |
| **Looney Tunes** | Exaggerated weight shift; legs may cycle faster than physically possible; full squash on plant foot | Vaudeville, rubber-hose physics, broad comedy |
| **Anime (TV)** | Minimal or simplified walk; often reduced to 4–6 drawings; dramatic arm swing | Budget limitations aestheticized; "limited animation" conventions |
| **Pixar/DreamWorks 3D** | Physically accurate baseline with character-specific variation; procedural secondary | Rig-based keyframe animation; reference footage |
| **Spider-Verse** | Highly stylized; Miles's walk is on 2s and includes graphic motion trails; no conventional smoothness | Comics aesthetics; explicit rejection of Pixar smoothness |
| **Studio Ghibli** | Often walking-to-running transitions with extended hang time; slightly floaty; atmospheric | Miyazaki's personal drawing style; emphasis on the environment the character moves through |

**The practical implication:** A "generic walk cycle" does not exist in professional animation. Every walk cycle is a character statement. The timing, the weight distribution, the amount of secondary action, and the degree of exaggeration all define the character's physical personality before they say a word.

## 🎭 Exercise 3: Character with Personality

### The Pantomime Test

The third and most important exercise is a pantomime performance: a character reacts to something finding an object, receiving surprising news, missing a bus *without dialogue*. The performance must tell the entire story through movement alone.

This exercise tests the full range of skills from all 10 modules:
- **Drawing/staging**: poses must read clearly
- **Timing**: emotional beats must land at the right frame
- **Secondary action**: supporting movement enriches the performance
- **Anticipation**: each major action is prepared
- **Appeal**: the audience must want to watch this character

### Production Checklist for the First Short

**Pre-production:**
- [ ] Story is clear and complete in one sentence ("A nervous frog tries to hop across a lily pad but keeps slipping")
- [ ] Storyboards exist for every shot
- [ ] Animatic with temp audio is complete and shows the scene working

**Production:**
- [ ] Reference footage was recorded and studied before animating
- [ ] Key poses were designed and approved before in-betweening
- [ ] Timing charts or graph editor work matches intended emotional beats

**Post-production:**
- [ ] Output codec is appropriate (see Module 8)
- [ ] Audio is mixed and synced
- [ ] Project files are backed up

## 🎬 Case Study: *Luxo Jr.* (1986) and What Makes a Minimal Character Compelling

Pixar's *Luxo Jr.* a 2-minute short that premiered at SIGGRAPH 1986 is the most studied minimal-character animation in history, and for good reason. It features two desk lamps. Neither has a face in the traditional sense: no visible eyes, no mouth, no expressive features. Both are expressed entirely through movement.

The short contains:
- **Squash and stretch:** Luxo Jr. bounces and squashes as it lands from jumps; the lamp shade tilts and flexes
- **Anticipation:** Before Luxo Jr. jumps, there is a preparatory lean and gathering of the spring base
- **Follow-through:** After landing, the shade oscillates briefly before settling
- **Secondary action:** The cord attached to Luxo's base drags with inertia through every move
- **Timing:** The small lamp's timing is fast and bouncy; the large lamp's timing is slower and more measured, establishing personality through frame count alone
- **Appeal:** Despite having no face, Luxo Jr. generates more emotional response in two minutes than many feature film characters sustain for two hours

The lesson for your first animated short: **you do not need a complex character to make a compelling performance.** The flour sack, the bouncing ball, a desk lamp, these are sufficient vehicles for genuine emotional storytelling if the performance is committed and the timing is honest.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** *Luxo Jr.* is frequently cited as evidence that the 12 Principles, not visual complexity, determine whether animation communicates. Know the specific principles it demonstrates and why minimalism can be more powerful than complexity.

## 💼 What Studios Look for in First-Year Student Work

Understanding what professional animators and hiring managers actually evaluate in student work helps you focus your practice on the right things.

### The Hierarchy of What Matters

| Priority | What Evaluators Look For | Why |
|----------|-------------------------|-----|
| **1st, Timing** | Does the motion feel real? Does the weight read? | Timing is the hardest skill to fake; it reveals genuine understanding immediately |
| **2nd, Poses** | Do the key poses read clearly and communicate emotion? | Strong poses show understanding of staging and appeal |
| **3rd, Arcs** | Do the movements follow arc paths? | Linear motion is the most obvious beginner error |
| **4th, Polish** | Are the in-betweens smooth? Does secondary action work? | Polish is achievable with iteration; fundamentals are not |
| **5th, Complexity** | How ambitious is the scene? | A simple scene done perfectly outranks a complex scene done poorly |

**The most common mistake in first-year student work:** Rushing to complexity (complex character, complex scene, complex dialogue) without having solid fundamentals. A bouncing ball with perfect timing and weight is more impressive to a professional evaluator than a full character performance with bad timing.

## 🔍 The Self-Critique Framework

Most animation students' first work fails because they cannot see what is wrong. The self-critique framework gives you a structured way to evaluate your own work.

**Step 1: Watch without sound**, Do the emotions and actions read purely from movement?
**Step 2: Cover the character and watch the background**, Is the staging clear from the negative space?
**Step 3: Play at half speed**, Do the in-betweens hold up? Are there pops or slides?
**Step 4: Show to someone who has not seen it**, What story do they see?
**Step 5: Wait 48 hours and watch again**, Fresh eyes reveal problems that familiarity hides.

## 🔬 Reference Gathering: How Professionals Use It for Pantomime

The pantomime exercise (a character reacting to something without dialogue) is the exercise where reference gathering is most often misused. Here is the professional approach.

### The Reference Gathering Process for a Pantomime Shot

**Step 1, Act it yourself first.** Before you search for any external reference, act out the scene yourself. Record yourself on your phone. Review the recording. Ask: where does your weight go? What do your hands do? What is the first thing that moves when you receive the news?

This step is not optional. Acting the scene yourself gives you immediate access to the emotional truth of the movement. External reference will be filtered through another actor's choices; your own performance is filtered through your understanding of the scene.

**Step 2, Study professional reference.** Look for reference that matches the specific emotional quality of your scene, not just the physical action. A character receiving devastating news requires different reference from a character receiving surprising news, even if the physical action (reading a letter) is the same. Study actors you admire for this specific emotional state, not just actors doing the same physical thing.

**Step 3, Identify the moments, not the movements.** From your reference, identify:
- The moment of realization (where does the character "get it"?)
- The first physical response (what moves first?)
- The emotional crescendo (where is the scene's peak?)
- The settle (how does the character come to rest after the peak?)

These four moments are your key poses. Everything between them is in-betweening.

**Step 4, Put the reference away.** Animate from your understanding of the four key moments, not from frame-by-frame copying of the reference. The reference gave you the emotional truth; the animation choices are yours.

## 📊 Student Short Film Assessment Rubric

Understanding how instructors and industry reviewers evaluate first animation work helps you prioritize your effort during production.

| Criteria | Weight | What Evaluators Look For |
|----------|--------|-------------------------|
| **Timing and weight** | 30% | Does the motion feel real? Does the weight read accurately? Are slow-in/slow-out applied correctly? |
| **Pose quality** | 25% | Do key poses read clearly? Are they interesting and specific? Do they communicate the character's state? |
| **Arcs and path of action** | 15% | Do movements follow arc paths? Is linear motion avoided? |
| **Secondary action** | 10% | Is there overlapping action? Does follow-through appear on clothing, hair, appendages? |
| **Technical execution** | 10% | Are there foot slides, geometry pops, or interpenetrations? Does the loop work (for walk cycles)? |
| **Completion and ambition** | 10% | Is the work complete? Does the scope match the time investment? |

**The 30%/25% concentration:** Over half of all evaluation weight is on timing and poses. These are the two skills that the bouncing ball and the character pantomime exercise most directly train. If you have limited time, ensure these are as strong as possible before polishing secondary action or fixing technical issues.

## 💬 Socratic Questions for Discussion

1. Richard Williams began every master class with a bouncing ball. He believed you could not understand animation without understanding the ball first. Do you think this is still true in an era where many students begin animation on 3D rigs that automate volume consistency and arc paths? What does the ball teach that the software cannot?
2. The self-critique framework asks you to show your work to someone who has not seen it and observe what story they see. What are the risks of relying on untrained viewer feedback for animation critique? What are the risks of relying only on your own evaluation?
3. *Luxo Jr.* communicates personality through movement alone, without facial features. Some contemporary animation relies heavily on facial performance (large eyes, expressive mouths) as the primary vehicle for emotion. Which approach demands more craft from the animator, and which produces stronger audience connection?
4. Every studio in the world uses walk cycles as the primary test of a junior animator's understanding. Is this still the right benchmark? What aspects of contemporary animation practice (game animation state machines, procedural secondary action, motion capture cleanup) does the traditional walk cycle test fail to measure?

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes in First Animated Shorts

This table collects the most frequently cited mistakes in student animation review sessions and explains both the specific error and the underlying misunderstanding it reveals.

| Mistake | What It Looks Like | Underlying Misunderstanding | Fix |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------------|-----|
| **Floating characters** | Foot slides during walk; no ground contact feeling | Weight and floor contact are not established through pose; timing at contact is wrong | Establish clear contact positions; squash slightly at ground contact; use timing to communicate weight |
| **Overly mechanical movement** | All movement starts and stops at the same time; no overlap | Overlapping action principle not applied; all parameters keyframed together | Offset the timing of body parts; secondary elements should lag primary by 2–4 frames |
| **Dead holds** | Character completely freezes between actions | "Rest" is understood as no animation; actually needs living hold with secondary | Add micro-movements: subtle breath expansion, slight weight shift, eye movement |
| **Poppy in-betweens** | Sudden jerks or slides between poses | In-betweens are mathematically even (constant speed) instead of eased | Adjust graph editor curves; drawings should cluster near key poses (slow-in/slow-out) |
| **Lost arcs** | Limbs move in straight lines between poses | Arc principle not applied; 3D default is linear interpolation | Manually add arc path to limb positions; use offset technique |
| **Too ambitious in scope** | Complex dialogue, multiple characters, complex environment; all underdeveloped | Scope exceeds available time; polish is impossible when breadth is too wide | Narrow scope; a simple scene done with control beats a complex scene done poorly |
| **Unreadable pantomime** | The action is unclear; test audiences cannot identify what the character is doing | Staging and pose design not strong enough to communicate without sound | Apply staging principles; use the silhouette test on every key pose before animating |

🎯 **What the exam tests:** The relationship between scope and quality is frequently tested: a simple scene done with full control is always more valuable than an ambitious scene done poorly. Know that the goal of the first short is demonstrating control over fundamentals, not demonstrating ambition.

## 🔬 The Story in One Sentence: Concept Discipline for Student Shorts

The single most important discipline for a first animated short is being able to describe the entire story in one clear sentence. This is not a marketing discipline, it is a creative discipline.

**The one-sentence test:**

If you cannot describe your short film's story in one sentence, you do not have a story yet. You have a premise, or a character idea, or a setting, but not a story with a beginning, middle, and end.

**Good one-sentence story descriptions:**
- "A nervous chef must prepare a perfect dish, but his own imagination keeps sabotaging him."
- "A tiny robot discovers a flower in a dead world and decides to protect it."
- "Two bouncing balls compete for the same landing spot and must negotiate who gets to bounce."
- "A ball rolls toward freedom but must decide whether to leave behind the only place it has known."

**Why this matters for production:**

Every production decision how many shots, how much time to allocate per shot, which principles to emphasize follows from the clarity of the story. An animator who knows their story can make every timing and staging choice in service of that story. An animator who is discovering the story during production makes inconsistent choices.

The one-sentence story also functions as a quality control tool: every shot should contribute something to that story. If you can remove a shot and the story sentence still makes sense, you should probably remove the shot.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** The concept discipline for short films: a clear story in one sentence, a complete animatic before any final animation begins, and scope appropriate to the time budget.

## 🎯 Module 9 Quick Reference: The First Short Production Checklist

Use this checklist as a gate for each phase of production. Do not move to the next phase until the current phase passes.

### Pre-Production Gate
- [ ] Story described in one sentence; someone else understood it correctly when you told them
- [ ] Storyboard drawn for every shot; boarding reviewed by at least one other person
- [ ] Animatic completed with temp audio; the scene works in time
- [ ] Reference footage recorded and studied for all key actions
- [ ] Project settings locked (frame rate, resolution, aspect ratio, render settings)
- [ ] File naming convention established; folder structure created

### Production Gate
- [ ] Key poses designed and staged; silhouette test passed for all key poses
- [ ] Timing charts or graph editor work planned; not using software defaults
- [ ] Blocking pass reviewed before adding in-betweens
- [ ] Secondary action planned; not added as afterthought

### Post-Production Gate
- [ ] Watch without sound, story still reads
- [ ] Watch at half-speed, no pops, slides, or timing errors
- [ ] Show to someone who has not seen it, they describe the story correctly
- [ ] Wait 48 hours; review with fresh eyes
- [ ] Output codec correct (not H.264 as only copy)
- [ ] Project files backed up (3-2-1 rule)
