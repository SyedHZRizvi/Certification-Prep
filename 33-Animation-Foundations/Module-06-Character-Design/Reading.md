# Module 6: Character Design 🎨

> **Why this module matters:** Character design is where visual art meets storytelling. A well-designed character communicates personality before they say a word, before they move. Bad character design undermines every other element of a production. This module covers the professional tools silhouette, shape language, color personality that make character design function.

---

## 📖 A Story: Why Scar Looks Like a Triangle

*The Lion King* (1994) has four major characters. Look at their basic shapes:

- **Simba** (young): round, soft, large eyes, curved forms, approachable, young, vulnerable
- **Mufasa**: large rectangle-influenced torso, broad shoulders, square mane, stable, powerful, trustworthy
- **Timon and Pumbaa**: contrasting organic shapes Timon is angular and small, Pumbaa is round and large comic through contrast
- **Scar**: angular, elongated, triangular sharp jaw, angular cheekbones, pointed mane dangerous, unstable, threatening

Every major character in *The Lion King* is built from **shape language**, the idea that basic geometric shapes carry inherent psychological associations for human viewers. Andreas Deja, the animator who brought Scar to life, did not choose those angles arbitrarily. He worked from character design that understood that triangles read as threatening, that sharp edges read as dangerous, that darkness reads as concealment.

This is the fundamental tool of character design: you can communicate who a character is, and what role they play in the story, through the shapes that compose their visual design, before animation, before dialogue, before color.

## 🎬 Case Study: Milt Kahl and the Problem of *The Rescuers* (1977)

Milt Kahl widely considered the greatest technical animator among Disney's Nine Old Men was the character designer and supervising animator for the protagonists of *The Rescuers* (1977): Bernard and Miss Bianca. The mice presented a specific design problem that Kahl documented extensively.

**The problem:** Anthropomorphic mice are inherently cute, large heads, large eyes, small bodies. The design almost forces a "cute" read that undermines the film's dramatic intentions (the film involves child kidnapping). Kahl needed characters who were simultaneously believably mouse-like AND capable of carrying genuine dramatic weight.

**Kahl's solution:** He pushed the proportions in the direction of naturalism for key emotional moments. Bernard's proportions are slightly more realistic than a "cute" mouse design would dictate, his snout is longer, his body less round, his limbs more articulated. This makes him less immediately adorable but more capable of registering nuanced reaction.

Miss Bianca's design uses a different approach: her proportions are conventionally cute, but her expressions are deliberately adult, she does not use the wide-eyed look that reads as childlike surprise; she raises one eyebrow in an expression of sophisticated amusement that reads across cultures as "intelligent adult woman."

**The lesson:** Appeal is not one-dimensional. The question "how appealing should this character be?" is always subsidiary to "what is this character's function in this story?" Kahl's mice are less immediately cute than they could be because they need to carry dramatic weight that conventional cute design would undermine.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Appeal calibration, understanding that the appropriate level of cuteness/appeal in a character design depends on the character's narrative function, not on maximum adorability.

## 🔬 Case Study: The Design of *Arcane*'s Jinx and Vi

Netflix's *Arcane* (2021) contains what many critics and designers consider the most sophisticated use of character design as narrative tool in the streaming era. The two central characters Jinx (Powder) and Vi are designed to be visual opposites that gradually, over the course of the series, exchange design elements as their relationship fractures and transforms.

**As children (Act 1):**
- **Vi** is dominant shapes: square jaw, wide shoulders, solid triangular forms in her fighting stance, warm muted colors (pink and brown that read as dependable)
- **Powder/Jinx** is recessive shapes: narrow, fragile, large eyes relative to face width, cold blues and purples that read as vulnerable and isolated

**As adults (Act 2–3):**
- **Vi** retains her solid forms but her color palette shifts to include harder contrasts, the prison goggles are harsh geometric circles, the tattoos sharp-edged
- **Jinx** has absorbed triangular menace into her design, her twin pigtails are styled to echo horns, her eye makeup creates artificial triangular shapes, her color palette remains blue-purple but becomes electric rather than soft

The design shift communicates the series' core tragedy (the inversion of their relationship) without requiring dialogue to explain it. A viewer who knew nothing about the story could look at Act 1 and Act 3 designs side by side and understand that something has been fundamentally exchanged between the two characters.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** *Arcane* is increasingly cited in character design assessments because it demonstrates how design evolves with character arc. Know that character design is not static, it should reflect the character's internal journey.

## 🔷 Shape Language: The Foundation

Every character design is fundamentally composed of geometric shapes. The psychological associations these shapes carry are remarkably consistent across cultures:

| Shape | Psychological Association | Best For |
|-------|--------------------------|---------|
| **Circle / Sphere** | Friendly, approachable, safe, youthful, innocent | Heroes, children, comic characters, sidekicks |
| **Square / Rectangle** | Stable, reliable, dependable, stubborn, powerful | Authority figures, mentors, reliable friends |
| **Triangle / Point** | Dangerous, unpredictable, energetic, menacing, dynamic | Villains, tricksters, fast characters, antagonists |

**The principle in practice:** Mickey Mouse circles everywhere. Built for approachability. Maleficent elongated triangles in her headdress, her silhouette, her dress. Built for menace. The design communicates before the character acts.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Shape language questions often present a scenario ("You are designing a villain for a children's film") and ask which shapes should dominate. The answer is triangles/angular shapes, not because they are inherently evil, but because they communicate the psychological danger that the villain role requires.

## 👤 Silhouette Readability: The Black Shape Test

**The Silhouette Test:** Fill a character design with solid black. If the pose is still readable if the audience can identify who it is and what they are doing from the black shape alone the character passes the silhouette test.

Characters that fail the silhouette test cause production problems at scale:
- Audiences cannot distinguish them from other characters at a distance
- Action poses are unreadable in compressed video or small screen sizes
- Secondary/background crowd characters all look identical

**Disney's test:** The Disney character design department has traditionally applied the silhouette test to every design before approving it for production. A design that only works with full color and detail is not a strong design.

**For pose design:** Every key pose in animation should also pass the silhouette test. If you cannot read what a character is doing from the silhouette alone, the pose needs to be redesigned for clarity.

## 🎭 Designing for Movement

Characters designed without considering how they will animate create production nightmares. The character designer and the animation department must collaborate, but the designer must understand animation basics.

### Design Rules for Animatability

| Problem | Design Principle |
|---------|-----------------|
| Too much surface detail | Detail should be concentrated on areas that don't deform much in animation (face, hands) |
| No clear joint location | Joints must be visually obvious in the design so in-betweening is consistent |
| Overly complex silhouette | Simpler overall silhouette = faster to animate + stronger reading |
| Hair that obscures the face | Hair design must allow face to remain readable in extreme poses |
| Symmetrical design | Slight asymmetry in design allows more natural, less robotic animation |

### The Character Turnaround

A **character turnaround** (also called a model sheet or rotation) shows the character in standardized views: front, 3/4 view, side (profile), back, and 3/4 back. The turnaround ensures that:
- All animators are drawing/rigging the same character with consistent proportions
- Joint locations are clearly defined from all angles
- Volume and proportion are consistent across views (this is the construction method applied at a design level)

## 🎨 Color for Personality

Color in character design is not decorative, it is communicative. The color palette of a character tells the audience about their personality, their role in the story, and their relationship to other characters.

### Color Personality Reference

| Color | Common Personality Association | Example |
|-------|------------------------------|---------|
| **Red** | Energy, passion, danger, confidence, love | Moana's dress (energy), Syndrome's suit (danger) |
| **Blue** | Calm, trustworthy, melancholy, cold | Elsa (isolation/cold), Megara (mysterious/controlled) |
| **Yellow/Gold** | Warmth, wisdom, royalty, optimism | Belle's dress (warmth), Mufasa's mane (authority) |
| **Green** | Nature, envy, unnatural, growth | Maleficent (supernatural threat), Fern Gully |
| **Purple** | Power, mysticism, royalty, villainy | Ursula (dark power), Thanos |
| **Orange** | Playful, adventurous, youthful | Nemo, Tigger |
| **Black/Dark** | Mystery, villainy, sophistication, concealment | Scar, Maleficent |

🚨 **Trap:** These are conventions, not rules. *Arcane*'s Jinx (Powder) is a major protagonist whose color palette includes blues and purples and pinks, colors that read differently in context. Color associations are culturally influenced and can be subverted intentionally.

### Color Harmony in Character Design

A character's color palette should:
1. **Read clearly against typical backgrounds**, high enough contrast with the production's background palette
2. **Distinguish them from other characters**, especially in ensemble casts where characters are often on screen together
3. **Reflect their emotional arc**, characters who change through a story sometimes have their costumes change color to reflect internal change (Moana's costume evolution, Rapunzel's)

## 🌍 Character Design Across Cultural Contexts

Western and Japanese animation traditions developed different conventions for what makes a character design "work." Understanding these conventions is essential both for cross-cultural production awareness and for designing characters intended for global audiences.

### Design Convention Comparison

| Element | Western Animation (Disney/Pixar lineage) | Japanese Animation (Anime lineage) |
|---------|----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Eyes** | Proportional; communicate through shape variation | Disproportionately large; iris color and catchlights are primary emotion communicators |
| **Noses** | Simplified but present; size communicates age/personality | Often reduced to a line or dot; almost invisible in profile |
| **Mouth size** | Moderate; used for expression range | Often small in rest; expressive primarily in extreme states |
| **Body proportions** | Varies by character type; generally recognizable anatomy | Head-to-body ratio varies greatly by genre; chibi and bishojo extremes |
| **Hair** | Usually follows gravity and physics | Stylized; often defies gravity; color communicates personality |
| **Age cues** | Soft features = youth; sharp features = age | Large eyes and round face = youth regardless of biological age |
| **Villains** | Angular shapes; saturated "wrong" colors (green, purple) | Often beautiful; indistinguishable from heroes; villainy revealed through context |

**The Ghibli exception:** Studio Ghibli characters are notable for *not* following standard anime design conventions. Miyazaki's heroines (Nausicaä, San, Chihiro, Sophie) have relatively small eyes, naturalistic hair, and proportions closer to the Disney tradition than to contemporary anime. This is intentional, Miyazaki has explicitly criticized the large-eye convention as a form of infantilization that prevents characters from communicating genuine adult emotion.

### Designing for Global Audiences

Productions intended for global distribution face specific design challenges:
- **Skin tone representation**: default design choices communicate unexamined cultural assumptions; deliberate inclusive design requires research into actual demographic representation
- **Clothing and cultural markers**: what reads as "neutral" in one culture reads as highly specific in another (business casual Western dress is culturally marked outside Western contexts)
- **Gesture and expression**: thumbs-up, OK sign, and other gestural conventions mean different things in different cultures; character design must account for expression-in-motion being culturally legible

## 🖊️ The Professional Character Design Process

Understanding how professional character design is developed from first concept to approved model sheet helps situate the craft within the larger production context.

### Typical Feature Film Character Design Pipeline

| Stage | Activity | Deliverable |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Concept** | Reference gathering; mood boards; initial thumbnail sketches | 20–50 rough thumbnail concepts |
| **Exploration** | Refine 3–5 directions; explore shape variations; color studies | 5–10 refined concept sheets |
| **Character director review** | Internal critique; director selects and gives notes | Selected direction + revision notes |
| **Development** | Refine selected design; address notes; expression range | Approved design + 12+ expressions |
| **Model sheet** | Turnaround (5 views); proportion guides; special poses | Approved model package for production |
| **Rig consultation** | Work with rigger to ensure design is animatable | Technical notes integrated into design |
| **Style guide** | Document design rules for assistants and outsource studios | Full style bible |

**Timeline reality:** At a major studio, the character design process for a lead character typically takes 6–18 months. At a television studio, it may take 4–8 weeks. Freelance commercial character design is often 1–4 weeks. The quality and depth of each stage varies accordingly.

## 🔬 Expression Sheets: Communicating the Range of a Character

A character design is not complete with a single model sheet and turnaround. The **expression sheet** also called a facial expression reference sheet or emotional range reference is the document that demonstrates what the character can communicate emotionally.

### Standard Expression Sheet Contents

| Expression | Purpose in Design | Common Design Test |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Neutral** | The resting face; defines the default emotional baseline | Is the neutral face interesting? Does it suggest personality? |
| **Happy/Joy** | The primary positive state | Does the face read as genuinely happy, or just as a smile? |
| **Sad** | Grief, disappointment, longing | Does the brow shape do the heavy lifting, or does it rely too much on mouth shape? |
| **Angry** | Frustration, rage | Are the eyebrows sufficiently differentiated from the sad expression? |
| **Surprised** | Shock, wonder | Does it read as positive surprise or negative shock, or is it ambiguous? |
| **Disgusted** | Revulsion, contempt | A subtle expression; often confused with "squinting" |
| **Fearful** | Terror, anxiety, worry | Wide eyes and raised brows; test this against "surprised" |
| **Concentrated/Thinking** | Cognitive effort | Often underdesigned; reduced to a generic squint |
| **Playful/Mischievous** | Not in Ekman's basic list but important for animation | Asymmetrical expression; one eyebrow up |

**The 7 basic expressions (after Paul Ekman):** Psychologist Paul Ekman's research identified seven expressions cross-culturally universal: happiness, sadness, anger, surprise, fear, disgust, and contempt. Animation programs teach these as the foundation of expression design because they are reliable communication tools across cultures and ages.

**The animation consideration:** Expression sheets also serve a technical function, they establish the *range* of the rig's expressive capability. If the expression sheet shows a wide-open mouth, the rigger knows the jaw deformer must accommodate that range without geometry errors.

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Know the 7 basic expressions and that expression sheets function as both creative reference and technical specification.

## 💼 IP Considerations in Character Design

**Intellectual Property (IP)** considerations affect character design in professional practice:

- **Original designs cannot infringe on existing IP**, a character design that is too similar to an existing trademarked character creates legal risk
- **Style can be referenced; specific designs cannot**, you can design in the "style" of Pixar without infringing; copying a specific Pixar character design is infringement
- **Character designs are themselves IP**, in a professional context, the employer owns the designs you create on their work time

This becomes practically relevant when freelancing: ensure your contract specifies ownership of designs created for a client.

## 📊 Shape Language in Major Productions: A Case Study Matrix

This table applies shape language analysis to iconic character designs across multiple studios and eras, providing a reference for exam scenarios.

| Character | Dominant Shapes | Color Reading | Personality Communicated | Studio |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|------------------------|--------|
| **Bambi (fawn)** | Circles, ovals, soft curves | Warm brown, white spots | Innocent, vulnerable, young | Disney, 1942 |
| **Scar** | Triangles, elongated forms, sharp lines | Dark; green eyes (unnatural) | Menacing, intelligent, deceptive | Disney, 1994 |
| **Bob Parr (Mr. Incredible)** | Rectangles, squares, massive volume | Red (confidence, power) | Reliable strength; hidden frustration | Pixar, 2004 |
| **Violet Parr** | Rounded but slightly contained | Blue-black hair (concealment) | Withdrawn, self-hiding power | Pixar, 2004 |
| **Miles Morales** | Rounded hero form; contemporary casual | Red/black (Spider-Man lineage); blue accent | Approachable, urban, authentic | Sony, 2018 |
| **Jinx (child)** | Narrow, fragile, large eyes | Blues, purples (vulnerable) | Isolated, frightened, desperate for love | Riot/Fortiche, 2021 |
| **Jinx (adult)** | Added triangular elements; sharpened | Electric blue, pink accent | Dangerous, chaotic, unmoored | Riot/Fortiche, 2021 |
| **Howl (Howl's Moving Castle)** | Elongated, angular, conventionally "handsome villain" shapes | Dark blues, silver | Defies shape language expectations; appears villainous, is protagonist | Ghibli, 2004 |
| **Chihiro (Spirited Away)** | Ordinary; neither heroic nor round | Muted greens, natural colors | Intentionally unremarkable; ordinary child in extraordinary world | Ghibli, 2001 |

**The Howl paradox:** Miyazaki deliberately designed Howl to look like a standard Western villain (angular, dark, intimidatingly beautiful) and made him the protagonist. This works because the film's visual language establishes Chihiro as the moral center through her actions rather than her design. Design communicates initial impression; narrative overrides it.

## ⚠️ Common Character Design Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Same body type for all characters** | Vary proportions meaningfully; contrast is what makes each character readable against the others |
| **Design that only works in the "hero" pose** | Every design must work in extreme action poses, tired/slouched poses, and profile; test with the turnaround |
| **Color palette chosen for appeal, not communication** | Color choices must be justified by character function; a villain in warm orange works if the rest of the cast is cool |
| **Ignoring how the design will be animated** | Overly complex surface detail is nightmare to animate; concentrate detail in areas that matter (face, hands) |
| **Neglecting negative space in the silhouette** | The holes in a character's silhouette (between arm and body, between legs) are as important as the filled forms |
| **Designing characters in isolation** | Every character exists within an ensemble; test all main characters together in the same frame |

🎯 **What the exam tests:** Character design questions often ask you to identify design problems from descriptions or to apply shape language principles to a described scenario. Know the relationship between shape, color, and character function.

## 🔬 The Ensemble Cast Problem: Designing Characters That Work Together

The hardest design problem in character-ensemble productions is not designing any single character well, it is designing all the main characters so they work together as a visual system.

**The problem:** If every main character has an interesting design, there is no visual hierarchy. The audience cannot read the importance of characters from their design alone. Conversely, if one character is overwhelmingly visually dominant, every other character reads as secondary regardless of their narrative role.

### Ensemble Design Principles

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| **Size contrast** | The ensemble should include significantly different scales. Small vs large characters create immediate visual contrast and hierarchy. (*Finding Nemo*: Marlin is small; sharks are enormous) |
| **Shape contrast** | If the lead character is round, the antagonist should be angular. Contrast between character shapes makes each one's identity clearer in shared scenes. |
| **Color contrast** | Each character should have a dominant color that does not appear as the dominant color of any other main character. Test: can you identify each character from their color silhouette alone? |
| **Silhouette differentiation** | In a crowd scene with all main characters present, can you identify each character from their black silhouette? If not, two characters are too similar. |
| **Proportion contrast** | Vary the head-to-body ratio across the ensemble. Not every character needs the same proportion system. (*The Incredibles*: Mr. Incredible's proportion is very different from Jack-Jack's) |

**The *Incredibles* ensemble design test:** Freeze-frame any scene in *The Incredibles* where multiple main characters appear together. Fill them all with black. Can you identify Violet, Dash, Jack-Jack, Mr. Incredible, and Elastigirl from their silhouettes alone? Yes. This is the result of intentional ensemble design.

## 🎯 Module 6 Quick Reference: Shape Language at a Glance

| Shape | Primary Associations | Classic Example | Use For |
|-------|---------------------|----------------|---------|
| **Circle** | Safe, friendly, approachable, young, comic | Mickey Mouse, Olaf, Luca | Heroes, children, loveable sidekicks |
| **Square/Rectangle** | Reliable, strong, stable, dependable | Mufasa, Buzz Lightyear | Authority figures, reliable support characters |
| **Triangle (pointing up)** | Energetic, dynamic, ambitious | Heroes in action poses | Action moments; dynamic character poses |
| **Triangle (pointing down / inverted)** | Menacing, dangerous, unstable | Scar, Maleficent | Villains; antagonists; characters to be feared |
| **Organic irregular** | Natural, wild, unpredictable | Forest spirits, Totoro | Nature characters; ancient beings; comic relief |
| **Mechanical/geometric** | Artificial, orderly, cold | HAL 9000, Syndrome's suit | Technology; systems; characters who have replaced humanity with order |

**The combination principle:** Most well-designed characters use a combination of shapes. Scar's body incorporates angular dominant shapes but his voice and movement introduce sinuous curves, the combination makes him more dangerous than pure angles because the curves suggest deceptive flexibility.

## 📊 Character Design in Different Formats: Constraints and Freedoms

The format of the final product theatrical feature, streaming series, video game, mobile app imposes specific constraints on character design that must be understood before design begins.

| Format | Constraint | Design Implication |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Theatrical feature (cinema)** | Will be viewed at 4K on 80-foot screens; full animation budget | Maximum detail and complexity; every texture visible; complex expression range |
| **Broadcast TV animation** | Compressed video delivery; viewed on ~55-inch screen; limited animation budget | Simplified design; fewer complex shapes; reduced surface detail |
| **Streaming (Netflix, Apple TV+)** | 4K delivery possible; variable screen sizes; variable animation budget | Design between feature and TV complexity; tested at multiple scales |
| **Video game (AAA)** | Real-time rendering on player's GPU; must work at all angles including unexpected camera positions | Topology matters; no "bad angles" allowed; textures optimized for real-time performance |
| **Mobile game** | Low-resolution screens; minimal texture memory | Simplified silhouettes; bold graphic shapes; very few surface details |
| **Web / social media** | Viewed at 1080p on phone screens; often without sound | Even simpler than mobile game; extreme clarity at thumbnail size |

**The mobile game design challenge:** A character designed for mobile must read at the size of a postage stamp. The silhouette test is not just recommended, it is mandatory. A character that requires 4K to be identified cannot work at mobile resolution.

**The "never a bad angle" game design constraint:** In a cinematic production, every shot is planned and the character is viewed from controlled angles. In a game, the player can move the camera to any position. This means game characters cannot have "bad angles", no politely unexplored back-of-head where the hair meets the neck in a messy topology. Every surface must be designed.

## 💼 The Model Sheet: Industry Standard Document Structure

The model sheet (also called a character sheet or character pack) is the document that the animation department uses as the definitive reference for every drawing of a character. Understanding its standard structure is essential for anyone entering a production environment.

### Standard Model Sheet Contents

| Document | Contents | Purpose |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Turnaround** | 5 standardized views (front, 3/4, side, 3/4 back, back) | Proportion and volume reference from all angles |
| **Height comparison** | Character standing next to other main characters with measurement guides | Consistent proportion across the production |
| **Expression sheet** | All 7 basic expressions + production-specific expressions | Expression range reference for animators |
| **Hand study** | Key hand poses (open, fist, pointing, holding object) | Hand reference for recurring poses |
| **Costume detail sheet** | Close-ups of costume elements that are complex or easily misdrawing | Buttons, insignia, pattern details that need specific reference |
| **Color model** | Character with official production colors labeled | Prevents color drift across departments and outsource studios |
| **Action poses** | 5–10 poses showing the character in characteristic poses | Appeal and personality reference; not anatomy reference |

**The difference between a student character design and a production character design:** A student design is usually one or two drawings. A production-ready design is a complete model sheet package with all the above elements. The package exists because dozens of animators will draw this character consistently over hundreds of shots, the model sheet is the shared reference that makes consistency possible.

## 🎯 Module 6 Quick Reference: The Character Design Checklist

Before presenting a character design for production approval, run through this checklist. Any item answered "no" indicates a revision is needed.

| Checklist Item | Why It Matters |
|----------------|---------------|
| **Does the character pass the silhouette test?** | The design must read as a recognizable black shape from any expected angle and pose |
| **Does the shape language match the character's role?** | Hero/protagonist: avoid excessive triangular forms; villain: angular; comedy: contrast |
| **Does the color palette distinguish this character from every other main character?** | No two main characters should share a dominant color |
| **Is the character's personality readable in the neutral pose?** | The resting design should hint at personality; it should not require an expression to read |
| **Does the design work at the production's minimum viewing size?** | If for mobile: silhouette at 50px. If for theatrical: silhouette at 100px on a phone screen |
| **Are joints clearly located?** | Animators and riggers need to read joint positions from the design |
| **Is the design consistent across the turnaround?** | Volume must be the same from all five views (front, 3/4, side, 3/4 back, back) |
| **Is the expression range achievable with this design?** | All 7 basic expressions (happiness, sadness, anger, surprise, fear, disgust, contempt) should be drawable |
| **Does the design work in the production's color palette?** | Test the character against major background palettes from the color script |
| **Is the design original and legally clear?** | No elements that could constitute trademark infringement |
