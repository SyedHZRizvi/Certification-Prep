## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B** — The Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) confirmed to Abu ibn Ka'b that Ayatul Kursi is the greatest verse in the Quran (Sahih Muslim). He described it as containing the most comprehensive description of Allah's attributes of any single verse.

**Q2 — C** — Al-Qayyum (القَيُّوم) comes from the root qaama (قَامَ — to stand/subsist). It means the one who stands by Himself and upon whom all others depend. He sustains all creation at every moment.

**Q3 — B** — "لَا تَأخُذُهُ سِنَةٌ وَلَا نَوم" — Neither drowsiness (sinah = light drowsiness before sleep) nor sleep (nawm = full sleep) affects Allah. This affirms His perfect, uninterrupted awareness and oversight of all creation at all times.

**Q4 — B** — Tabatabai's insight: Al-Qayyum means Allah is not a distant creator who made the universe and stepped back. He is continuously, actively sustaining every particle of existence at every moment. If He withdrew His attention for an instant, everything would cease. This is the deepest meaning of the verse.

**Q5 — B** — A narration states: "Whoever recites Ayatul Kursi after every obligatory prayer, nothing prevents him from entering paradise except death." (Al-Nasai with good chain). A separate narration says reciting it before sleep brings a guardian until morning (Bukhari).

**Q6 — B** — The Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) said: "Everything has a heart, and the heart of the Quran is Yasin. Whoever reads Yasin, Allah will write for him the reward of reading the Quran ten times" (Tirmidhi). The designation "heart" suggests it contains the essential pulse of the entire Quran.

**Q7 — C** — Surah Yasin has 83 verses. It is divided thematically across five sections covering prophethood, historical narrative, cosmic signs, resurrection, and divine power.

**Q8 — C** — كُن فَيَكُونُ means "Be! — and it is." This is Allah's creative command — when He intends something, He simply wills it into existence. The verse (36:82) represents the Islamic concept of divine omnipotence in its most concise form.

**Q9 — B** — Surah Yasin is traditionally recited: (1) for the ill and dying — the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) encouraged reciting it over those who are ill; (2) on Thursday nights and Friday mornings for seeking blessing and forgiveness.

**Q10 — C** — Tabaarak (تَبَارَك) is derived from baraka (blessing) and means "blessed in a way that is inexhaustible and beyond measure." It is used exclusively for Allah in the Quran. The verb form implies Allah is the source of all blessing.

**Q11 — B** — Surah Al-Mulk has 30 verses. This is why a hadith refers to it as "a surah of thirty verses that interceded for its companion until he was forgiven."

**Q12 — B** — Multiple narrations (Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Ibn Masud) indicate that Surah Al-Mulk intercedes on behalf of its regular reciter in the grave. It is described as "Al-Waqiya" (the Preventer) — protecting its reciter from the punishment of the grave.

**Q13 — C** — Ibn Masud (ra) narrated that the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) never went to sleep without reciting Surah Al-Mulk. This is why it is called one of the nightly sunnah recitations, along with Al-Ikhlas, Al-Falaq, An-Naas, and Ayatul Kursi.

**Q14 — C** — Surah Al-Kahf contains four major stories: (1) People of the Cave (9-26), (2) The Two Gardens (32-44), (3) Musa and Khidr (60-82), and (4) Dhul-Qarnayn (83-98).

**Q15 — B** — The People of the Cave (Ashab al-Kahf) were young men of faith who were persecuted for their belief and sought refuge in a cave. Their story represents the fitnah of religion — how to maintain faith under persecution and external pressure.

**Q16 — C** — The story of Prophet Musa (as) and Khidr (as) represents the fitnah of knowledge. Even a prophet who was given divine knowledge had to acknowledge that another servant of Allah (Khidr) possessed knowledge he did not. The trial of knowledge is assuming one knows everything — or that human knowledge is supreme.

**Q17 — C** — A narration in Sahih Muslim states: "Whoever memorises ten verses from the beginning of Surah Al-Kahf will be protected from the trial of the Dajjal." These ten opening verses deal with the test of faith under trial — the core of the Dajjal's challenge.

**Q18 — B** — "Whoever recites Surah Al-Kahf on Friday, a light will illuminate him between this Friday and the next" (Al-Bayhaqi, authenticated by Al-Albani). This "light" (noor) is understood as spiritual clarity, guidance, and divine proximity.

**Q19 — B** — The recommended approach for longer surahs: begin with the opening verses and the key thematic verses, understand the structure and meaning thoroughly, then extend memorisation gradually. Complete memorisation is a long-term goal; understanding and regular engagement with portions is the starting point.

**Q20 — B** — Islamic scholars have generally understood "Kursi" in this verse as representing Allah's knowledge and sovereign authority over all creation — not a literal physical object. The Kursi "extending over the heavens and earth" means His awareness, power, and governance encompass everything.

**Q21 — B** — The verse establishes that intercession is real (Islamic theology affirms intercession) but conditional on Allah's permission. No prophet, angel, or saint can intercede independently or contrary to Allah's will. This verse refutes the idea of independent intermediaries.

**Q22 — B** — The final verses of Surah Al-Mulk (verses 28-30) build to a rhetorical question: Who can help you if Allah withdraws His mercy, and who can withhold His provision if He wills to give? The answer implied: no one. The surah ends on a note of profound dependence on Allah alone.

**Q23 — D** — Dhul-Qarnayn's story (verses 83-98) represents the fitnah of power. He was given extraordinary power and authority — he could travel the length of the earth. His test was whether he would use this power justly. He did — establishing barriers to protect the weak from the powerful (Gog and Magog).

**Q24 — B** — Scholars like Shaykh Muhammad al-Ghazali noted that the four stories of Al-Kahf address the four great trials that face every generation: persecution for faith (religion), seduction by material wealth, the arrogance of human knowledge, and the corruption that comes with power. Every century finds itself in these trials — making Al-Kahf perennially relevant.
