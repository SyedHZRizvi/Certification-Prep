## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B** · Qira'at are authenticated recitation styles, each with a complete Isnad chain back to the Prophet ﷺ. They are not translations or regional dialects.

**Q2 — C** · Classical scholarship recognizes 10 Qira'at, though 7 are considered Mutawatir (mass-transmitted). All 10 are authentic.

**Q3 — C** · Hafs 'an 'Asim is used by approximately 95% of the world's Muslims. Hafs was a student of 'Asim al-Kufi.

**Q4 — C** · Warsh 'an Nafi' is the standard Qira'ah in Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Libya, and most of West Africa.

**Q5 — B** · In Hafs: مَلِكِ (short Alif, 2 beats). In Warsh: مَالِكِ (long Alif Madd, 2+ beats). This is one of the most visible differences in Al-Fatiha.

**Q6 — B** · Lahn al-Jali is a gross error that changes the pronunciation of letters, alters vowels, or distorts the meaning.

**Q7 — C** · Lahn al-Jali committed intentionally is considered haram. Committed out of inability or ignorance by a learner, it must be corrected as the student progresses.

**Q8 — C** · Substituting ق for ك changes the letter — this is Lahn al-Jali. Options A and B and D are Lahn al-Khafi (Tajweed violations, not letter changes).

**Q9 — C** · Omitting Qalqalah is a Tajweed rule violation but does not change the letters — it is Lahn al-Khafi. The other options all change letters or meaning (Lahn al-Jali).

**Q10 — B** · Hamzat al-Qat' is always pronounced. The name "Qat'" (cutting) means it always "cuts through" and sounds in speech.

**Q11 — C** · Hamzat al-Wasl is dropped/silent when connected from a previous word in the same breath. It only sounds when starting fresh.

**Q12 — B** · The ال definite article always has Hamzat al-Wasl. This is why in بِسْمِ اللَّهِ, the Alif of اللَّهِ is silent.

**Q13 — B** · The Alif in اللَّهِ has Hamzat al-Wasl. When preceded by بِسْمِ in the same breath, the Wasl Hamza drops and you get bismillah, not bismi-il-lah.

**Q14 — B** · An Ijazah is a formal authorization from a certified teacher with a named chain of transmission back to the Prophet ﷺ.

**Q15 — B** · The Silsilah is the chain of names: student → teacher → their teacher → ... → the Prophet ﷺ. This documented lineage is the heart of the Ijazah system.

**Q16 — B** · The 'Ardh is the formal recitation of the complete Quran to the authorizing sheikh. It can take many sessions and the sheikh corrects errors throughout.

**Q17 — D** · Ijazah fi al-Qira'at al-Sab' (authorization in all 7 Mutawatir Qira'at) is the highest credential. It requires mastery of all 7 recitation traditions.

**Q18 — B** · Ijazah is for formally joining and passing on the authenticated chain. Any Muslim can recite and lead prayers without one.

**Q19 — B** · Mutawatir refers to a transmission passed by so many independent narrators simultaneously that fabrication is rationally impossible. All 10 Qira'at are Mutawatir.

**Q20 — B** · Lahn means error or deviation in recitation. It is divided into Jali (gross) and Khafi (subtle).

**Q21 — B** · Full Hifz is the non-negotiable foundation for any Ijazah. No sheikh will grant Ijazah to someone who has not memorized the complete Quran.

**Q22 — B** · Substituting ع for أ changes the letter — this is Lahn al-Jali. These are distinct letters with different Makhaarij; confusing them can change meaning.

**Q23 — B** · Shortening Madd al-Muttasil violates the Tajweed rule but does not change the letter or its meaning. This is Lahn al-Khafi.

**Q24 — C** · The seven Ahruf are seven authentic modes of revelation that accommodated the linguistic diversity and dialects of different Arab tribes, making the Quran accessible to all.

**Q25 — B** · When starting on a Hamzat al-Wasl from silence, you give it the prescribed vowel. For ال it gets a Kasra; for certain verb forms it gets Damma; for others Kasra.

**Q26 — B** · Knowing the 10 Qira'at allows you to recognize other authentic recitations when you hear them (especially Warsh) rather than mistaking them for errors — an important mark of Quranic literacy.
