## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B** — Surah Al-Ikhlas has 4 verses. Despite being extremely short, it covers the complete theology of tawhid (monotheism).

**Q2 — C** — The Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) said Al-Ikhlas equals one-third of the Quran (Sahih Bukhari and Muslim). The Quran's three major themes are: Islamic law, historical narratives, and the knowledge of Allah's attributes. Al-Ikhlas covers the third theme entirely.

**Q3 — B** — Ahad (أَحَد) describes an absolute uniqueness. Allāmah Tabātabāī explains in Al-Mizan that Ahad cannot be placed in any category or sequence — it is not "the first one" but "the only one of its kind." This is deeper than Wahid.

**Q4 — C** — As-Samad (الصَّمَد) means the one all creation turns to and depends upon, while He depends on nothing external. Everything else needs sustenance, support, or continuation from outside itself — Allah needs nothing.

**Q5 — B** — The three verses (2, 3, 4) of Al-Ikhlas address the three historical distortions: partnership (verse 4 — nothing equals Him), divine parentage (verse 3 — He did not beget/was not begotten), and absolute dependency (verse 2 — He is the only self-sufficient being).

**Q6 — B** — Al-Falaq (113) and An-Naas (114) are called Al-Mu'awwidhatān (المُعَوِّذَتَان — the two surahs of refuge). They are recited together frequently, especially for protection before sleeping.

**Q7 — C** — Al-Falaq (الفَلَق) means the breaking of dawn or daybreak. It refers to the splitting/opening of darkness by light. The Lord of the dawn is the Lord who controls the transition from darkness to light.

**Q8 — B** — Al-Falaq asks for refuge from: (a) evil of all creation generally, (b) darkness when it settles, (c) those who blow on knots (harmful practices), and (d) an envier when they envy. All four categories.

**Q9 — B** — The three invocations are: Rabb an-Naas (Lord of mankind), Malik an-Naas (King of mankind), Ilaah an-Naas (God of mankind). This triple invocation creates a complete appeal to Allah's authority as Nurturer, Ruler, and Deity.

**Q10 — B** — Al-Waswas al-Khannaas (الوَسوَاس الخَنَّاس) is the retreating whisperer — Shaytan (or evil influence) who whispers negative thoughts into the human heart, and who retreats/hides when the person remembers Allah. This is why dhikr is the prescribed remedy.

**Q11 — C** — Verse 6 specifically says "from jinn AND from people" (مِنَ الجِنَّةِ وَالنَّاسِ). This explicitly includes human sources of evil whispering — bad company, toxic influences, manipulative people.

**Q12 — B** — When the Prophet's (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) sons died (Al-Qasim and Abdullah), the enemies of Islam mocked him as "al-abtar" (without male heirs — "cut off"). This surah was Allah's response: "We have given you abundance — and your enemy is the one truly cut off."

**Q13 — C** — Al-Kawthar means "abundance" or "boundless good." Scholars include multiple meanings: the river Al-Kawthar in paradise, the Prophet's (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) countless followers, the blessing of prophethood, the Quran, and all divine gifts.

**Q14 — B** — "إِنَّ شَانِئَكَ هُوَ الأَبتَر" means "Your enemy/hater is the one who is abtar (cut off, without continuation/legacy)." The prophecy proved true — those who mocked the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) are forgotten; his legacy continues with 1.8 billion followers.

**Q15 — C** — Al-Asr (العَصر) means both "time" and "the afternoon." The oath sworn by time emphasizes time's preciousness — it is constantly passing, and what we do with it defines success or loss.

**Q16 — C** — Surah Al-Asr states that humans are "in loss" (لَفِي خُسر) — this is the default state. Time is always passing; unless one actively does something about it (the four conditions), loss is inevitable.

**Q17 — D** — Four conditions: (1) Iman, (2) Amal Salih, (3) Tawasi bil-Haqq, (4) Tawasi bis-Sabr. All four are required — omitting any one is insufficient.

**Q18 — C** — Hajj is not mentioned in Surah Al-Asr. The four conditions are: faith, righteous deeds, mutual counsel in truth, mutual counsel in patience. These are broader than any single pillar of practice.

**Q19 — B** — Imam Al-Shafi'i famously said: "If people only reflected carefully on this surah, it would be sufficient guidance for all of them." This is a remarkable statement about the completeness of its guidance in just three verses.

**Q20 — B** — The verb تَوَاصَوۡاْ (tawasaw) means to counsel each other — it is reciprocal. This means individual faith and deeds are not enough; the fourth condition requires a community dimension. You counsel others and are counselled — together.

**Q21 — B** — Tabatabai's insight from Al-Mizan: Wahid (وَاحِد) means "one" as in the first of a possible sequence — "one apple out of many apples." Ahad (أَحَد) is different — it refers to a reality so unique that no sequence applies to it. Allah is not "the first God" — He is in a category of uniqueness that excludes comparison entirely.

**Q22 — C** — Madd Lazim requires 6 beats when a Madd letter (long vowel) is immediately followed by a Shaddah. In الضَّآلِّين, the long Alif after Dhaad is followed by the Shaddah on Lam — making this a Madd Lazim (6 beats).

**Q23 — B** — The Sunnah narrated in Sahih Bukhari is to recite Al-Ikhlas, Al-Falaq, and An-Naas every night before sleeping: blow on the hands, rub them together, and wipe them over the body three times.

**Q24 — B** — Surah Al-Kawthar (108) is the shortest surah in the Quran, with only 3 verses. Al-Asr also has 3 verses, but Al-Kawthar's verses are shorter. (Note: Al-Kawthar is standardly recognized as the shortest surah.)
