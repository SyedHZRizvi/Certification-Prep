## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — A** — Surah Al-Fatiha is called Umm al-Quran (أُمُّ القُرآن — Mother of the Quran) and Umm al-Kitab (Mother of the Book). It is also called Al-Sab' al-Mathani (The Seven Repeated) because of its 7 verses recited repeatedly in prayer.

**Q2 — C** — The 5 daily prayers have 2+4+4+3+4 = 17 rak'at, each containing a recitation of Al-Fatiha. That's a minimum of 17 times daily.

**Q3 — C** — Surah Al-Fatiha has exactly 7 verses (ayat). This is why it is also called Al-Sab' al-Mathani — the seven often-repeated.

**Q4 — B** — In بِسمِ اللَّهِ, the Meem of بِسمِ carries a Kasra. The rule is: the Lam of اللَّه is thin (Tarqeeq) when preceded by a Kasra. So despite اللَّه having a generally "thick" Lam in most contexts, here it is thin.

**Q5 — B** — Ar-Rahmān (الرَّحمَٰن) is the vast, encompassing mercy that covers all of creation — believers, disbelievers, animals, everything in existence. Ar-Rahīm (الرَّحِيم) is the special mercy specific to believers and comes to its fullness in the Hereafter.

**Q6 — B** — The Arabic word Rabb (رَبّ) encompasses three meanings: Creator (who brought into existence), Owner/Master (who has authority), and Nurturer (who continuously sustains and brings to perfection). "Lord" in English captures only the second dimension.

**Q7 — B** — الحَمد (hamd) in Arabic is specifically the praise of a being for their essential qualities — their wisdom, beauty, kindness — not merely thanks for benefits received. You can praise a poet even if their poem didn't help you personally. Hamd is this disinterested recognition of excellence.

**Q8 — C** — العَٰلَمِينَ (all the worlds) in the Quranic context refers to all planes of existence: the physical universe, the world of humans, of jinn, of angels, the barzakh (purgatory), and the Hereafter. Allah's lordship encompasses all dimensions.

**Q9 — B** — الدِّينِ has a Shamsiyyah Lam. The Dal (د) is a "sun letter" (شمسية), so the Lam is absorbed into it, and the Dal receives a Shaddah: pronounced "ad-dīni" not "al-dīni."

**Q10 — B** — The two transmitted readings are: مَٰلِكِ (Māliki) meaning "Master/Owner/Sovereign" (used in Hafs an Asim — the most widely used qiraat) and مَلِكِ (Maliki) meaning "King" (used in Warsh an Nafi and some other qira'at). Both are authentic transmissions from the Prophet ﷺ.

**Q11 — B** — In Arabic, placing the object before the verb creates a specific emphasis. إِيَّاكَ (You alone) before نَعبُدُ (we worship) means: "It is specifically and exclusively You — not anyone else — that we worship." The normal word order would be نَعبُدُكَ; fronting إِيَّاكَ adds the "alone/exclusively" meaning.

**Q12 — A** — Verses 1-4 describe Allah in the third person: "He" — the worshipper describes Allah's attributes. At verse 5, the prayer shifts to direct address: "You." This is the spiritual turning point — the servant moves from contemplating Allah to speaking directly to Him in living dialogue.

**Q13 — B** — الصِّرَٰطَ has a Shamsiyyah Lam. Sod (ص) is a sun letter, so the Lam is absorbed into the Sod, and the Sod gets a Shaddah: pronounced "aṣ-ṣirāṭa" not "al-ṣirāṭa."

**Q14 — B** — Makarem Shirazi (Namoona) describes the straight path as the path of moderation and balance — avoiding extremes of rigidity (those who earned anger through knowingly refusing truth) and of deviation (those who went astray through confused beliefs). It is the path of the prophets and righteous scholars who lived with clarity and fairness.

**Q15 — B** — الضَّآلِّينَ contains a Madd Lazim (مَدّ لَازِم) — mandatory elongation. When a Madd letter (here, the long Alif after Dhaad) is followed immediately by a Shaddah (here, on the Lam), the Madd must be held for 6 beats. This is one of the strongest Madd types.

**Q16 — B** — Tabatabai's insight in Al-Mizan: all the praiseworthy qualities we see in creation — beauty, wisdom, kindness — are divine gifts. The one being praised for their wisdom is really reflecting Allah's gift of wisdom. So all real hamd (praise) ultimately flows back to Allah, the ultimate source of all praiseworthy attributes.

**Q17 — B** — Jawadi Amoli distinguishes: (1) tashri'i guidance — the revealed law and Quran; (2) takwini guidance — the natural compass (fitrah) inside every human; (3) specific divine guidance — the ongoing spiritual illumination that comes to the sincere worshipper. The du'a in verse 6 is asking for this third, deepest level of guidance.

**Q18 — B** — The "those You blessed" refers to those who received divine favor — primarily the Prophets, the truthful (siddiqeen), the martyrs, and the righteous (Quran 4:69). The path being requested in verse 6 is their path — the path of spiritual success and divine proximity.

**Q19 — B** — Shirazi emphasizes that إِيَّاكَ نَعبُدُ is a total declaration of freedom. In Islamic theology, worshipping Allah exclusively means rejecting all other forms of servitude — to money, to worldly power, to oppressive rulers, to one's own ego. Every Muslim who says this verse is renewing their commitment to spiritual and social liberation.

**Q20 — B** — Jawadi Amoli makes a careful distinction: the Quran does not condemn those who never received guidance. The category الضَّالِّين refers to those who had access to truth but turned away — not out of ignorance, but out of choice. This makes verse 7 an important prayer: "Don't let me become someone who knew and still chose wrongly."

**Q21 — C** — Surah Al-Fatiha is called the "Mother of the Quran" because it contains in summary form the core themes of the entire Quran: tawhid (monotheism — Allah's attributes in verses 1-4), ma'ad (resurrection — verse 4), nubuwwah (guidance through prophets — verse 7), and the human covenant of worship (verse 5). All Quranic themes branch from these seeds.

**Q22 — B** — Tabatabai explains that the repetition of الرَّحمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ in verse 3 is not redundancy. Verse 1 introduces these names in the context of the Bismillah (beginning with Allah's name). Verse 3 places them as an attribute of Rabb: "The Lord of all worlds" is specifically described as "the Merciful one." The message: Allah's lordship is defined by mercy, not by power or fear.

**Q23 — C** — There are many Madd Tabee'i instances throughout Al-Fatiha: the long Alif in الرَّحمَٰن, the long Ya in الرَّحِيم, the long Alif in العَٰلَمِين, and many more long vowels. The surah is rich with Madd Tabee'i — this gives it its characteristic melodic quality.

**Q24 — C** — Minimum 17 times: Fajr (2) + Dhuhr (4) + Asr (4) + Maghrib (3) + Isha (4) = 17 rak'at. Each rak'at requires Al-Fatiha, so the minimum is 17 times daily. If one prays voluntary prayers, this number increases significantly.
