## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B** — Arabic has exactly 28 letters. English has 26, Arabic has 28. The extra letters cover sounds unique to Arabic (like ع Ayn, ح heavy Ha, خ Kha).

**Q2 — B** — Arabic is written and read right to left, the same direction as Urdu, Persian, and Hebrew. This is a major advantage for Urdu speakers learning Arabic.

**Q3 — C** — Every Arabic letter has 4 forms: isolated (used alone), initial (beginning of a word), medial (middle), and final (end). This is why the same letter can look very different in different positions.

**Q4 — C** — Makhraj (plural: Makhaarij) means the "point of articulation" — the specific location in the vocal tract where a sound is produced. Understanding Makhaarij is essential for correct Quranic recitation.

**Q5 — C** — There are 17 primary Makhaarij, grouped into 5 major regions: Al-Jawf (cavity), Al-Halq (throat), Al-Lisan (tongue), Al-Shafatan (lips), and Al-Khayshum (nasal passage).

**Q6 — C** — ص (Sad) is one of the 4 emphatic letters (ص ض ط ظ). Emphatic letters are produced with the tongue body raised toward the back of the mouth, giving them a "heavy" or deep quality.

**Q7 — B** — ء (Hamza) and ه (Ha) are produced deepest in the throat, in the region called Al-Aqsa of the throat. Ayn and Ha-heavy are in the middle of the throat; Ghayn and Kha are at the top.

**Q8 — B** — Fa (ف) is produced by the upper front teeth touching the inner surface of the lower lip. This is called "dentolabial" articulation.

**Q9 — C** — ر (Ra) is one of the 6 non-connecting letters: ا (Alif), د (Dal), ذ (Dhal), ر (Ra), ز (Zay), و (Waw). These never connect to the letter following them.

**Q10 — C** — There are exactly 6 non-connecting letters in Arabic: Alif, Dal, Dhal, Ra, Zay, and Waw. Any other letter connects to the following letter.

**Q11 — C** — Qaf (ق) is produced at the very back of the tongue pressing against the soft palate (uvular area). It differs from Kaf (ك) which is produced slightly more forward.

**Q12 — A** — The four lip letters are ب (Ba — both lips), م (Meem — both lips + nasal), و (Waw — both lips rounded), and ف (Fa — upper teeth + lower lip).

**Q13 — A** — Ayn (ع) is produced by constricting the middle of the throat, while Hamza (ء) is produced at the very bottom/deepest part of the throat (a glottal stop). Ayn requires more muscular engagement.

**Q14 — B** — Ba (ب) and Ta (ت) have the same body shape. The only difference is dots: Ba has one dot below, Ta has two dots above. This "dot-pattern" system is a key feature of Arabic script.

**Q15 — B** — In the Quran, all short vowels are written as small marks called harakat (زبر، زیر، پیش). This makes the Quran the most readable form of Arabic for learners. In everyday non-Quranic Arabic text, these marks are usually omitted.

**Q16 — B** — Al-Jawf (the empty throat/mouth cavity) produces the three long vowel letters: Alif (ا), Waw (و), and Ya (ي). These are sometimes called "air letters" because they are produced in open space.

**Q17 — B** — Both ش (Sheen) and ج (Jeem) — along with ي (Ya as a consonant) — are produced at the middle of the tongue pressed against the middle of the hard palate.

**Q18 — C** — The advantage for Urdu speakers is twofold: (1) Urdu uses almost all the same letters as Arabic, just in a different calligraphic style (Nastaleeq vs Naskh), and (2) Urdu is also written right to left, so the reading direction requires no adjustment.

**Q19 — B** — Ghunnah is a nasal resonance produced through the nasal passage (Al-Khayshum). It appears with Noon (ن) and Meem (م) in certain Tajweed rules, giving those sounds a prolonged nasalised quality.

**Q20 — C** — The letter ض (Dad) produces a sound considered unique to Arabic — a lateralised emphatic consonant. Classical Arab grammarians called Arabic "لغة الضاد" (the language of the Dad) for this reason.

**Q21 — B** — There are 5 regions of articulation: Al-Jawf (cavity), Al-Halq (throat), Al-Lisan (tongue), Al-Shafatan (lips), Al-Khayshum (nasal passage). Within these, there are 17 specific articulation points.

**Q22 — D** — In اللَّهِ, the Alif at the beginning does not connect to the following Lam because Alif (ا) is one of the 6 non-connecting letters. The small gap you see in the middle of Allah's name follows this rule.

**Q23 — B** — Tha (ث), Dhal (ذ), and Dha-heavy (ظ) are all produced with the tip of the tongue between or near the upper and lower front teeth — an interdental or dental articulation point.

**Q24 — C** — All 28 Arabic letters have exactly 4 forms based on their position: isolated, initial, medial, and final. This is a universal rule with no exceptions (though some letters' forms look very similar to each other).

**Q25 — C** — Al-Shafatan means "the two lips" (the dual form of شفة meaning "lip"). The lip letters are Ba, Meem, Waw, and Fa.

**Q26 — B** — The four emphatic letters share "pharyngealisation" — the tongue body is raised toward the back of the mouth (not just the tip), depressing the middle of the tongue. This gives the sound a "dark" or "heavy" quality that affects surrounding vowels too.
