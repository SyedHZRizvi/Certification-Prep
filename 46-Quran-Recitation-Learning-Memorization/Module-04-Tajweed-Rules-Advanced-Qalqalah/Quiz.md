## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B** — Qalqalah (قَلقَلَة) means "trembling" or "shaking." The term describes the characteristic bounce/echo quality when these 5 letters appear with Sukoon.

**Q2 — C** — Ba (ب) is one of the 5 Qalqalah letters: ق ط ب ج د (quṭbujad). It has a medium level of Qalqalah.

**Q3 — C** — There are exactly 5 Qalqalah letters: Qaf (ق), Ta-heavy (ط), Ba (ب), Jeem (ج), Dal (د).

**Q4 — B** — The mnemonic قُطُب جَد (quṭbujad) contains all 5 Qalqalah letters: ق، ط، ب، ج، د. This two-word mnemonic is used universally in Tajweed instruction.

**Q5 — B** — Qalqalah Kubra (major) occurs when you make Waqf (stopping) on a Qalqalah letter at the end of a verse. The echo is stronger and more prolonged than in the minor version.

**Q6 — B** — Ikhfa Shafawi is the rule for Meem Sakin (مْ) before Ba (ب). The Meem is concealed (hidden, not clearly pronounced) with a Ghunnah of 2 beats. "Shafawi" means lip, referring to the shared lip articulation of Meem and Ba.

**Q7 — C** — Idgham Shafawi applies when Meem Sakin is followed by another Meem. The first Meem merges into the second, with a Ghunnah of 2 beats, producing a lengthened Meem.

**Q8 — C** — Izhar Shafawi is the default rule for Meem Sakin before any letter except Ba and Meem. The Meem is pronounced clearly without any Ghunnah. Pay special attention before Waw and Fa.

**Q9 — C** — The Lam of اللَّه is thick (Tafkheem) when preceded by a Fatha or Damma. This gives the name of Allah its characteristic weighty quality in phrases like قَالَ اللَّهُ.

**Q10 — B** — In بِسمِ اللَّهِ, the Meem of بِسمِ carries a Kasra (زِير). Because the Lam of اللَّه is preceded by this Kasra, it becomes thin (Tarqeeq). This is the exception to the general rule of thick Lam for Allah's name.

**Q11 — C** — The م (Meem) Waqf symbol stands for لَازِم (Lazim) — "necessary" or "obligatory." You must stop here; continuing would alter or harm the meaning.

**Q12 — B** — لا means "do not stop here." Stopping at this mark would distort the meaning or break the grammatical/semantic flow. This is a prohibition on stopping.

**Q13 — C** — When stopping on Fathatan (ـً) at the end of a word, the Tanween is replaced by a long ā sound. Example: هُدًى when stopped becomes "hudā." The Alif that represents the Fathatan is pronounced.

**Q14 — B** — Ra is thick (Tafkheem) when it carries a Fatha or Damma. Ra is thin (Tarqeeq) when it carries a Kasra or follows a Kasra without another vowel intervening.

**Q15 — B** — Qalqalah Sughra (minor/small) is the Qalqalah that occurs within a word when the Qalqalah letter carries a Sukoon in the middle of recitation (not at a verse-ending stop).

**Q16 — C** — Shafawi (شَفوِي) derives from شَفَة (shafah) meaning "lip." The term indicates that these Meem rules involve lip letters specifically — Meem and Ba are both lip letters.

**Q17 — B** — Both Ikhfa Shafawi and Idgham Shafawi require Ghunnah (2 beats). Only Izhar Shafawi has no Ghunnah.

**Q18 — C** — The ج (Jaiz) symbol means "permitted" — you may stop or continue. It indicates a natural pause point where stopping is allowable but not required.

**Q19 — B** — Wasla means "connection" — continuing the recitation without stopping. When you make Wasla, you pronounce the final harakat of the previous word and connect smoothly to the next.

**Q20 — C** — In نَخلُقكُم, if the Qaf (ق) carried a Sukoon, it would trigger Qalqalah. However, in this word the Qaf has a vowel, so Qalqalah applies only at a stopping point (Qalqalah Kubra).

**Q21 — A** — The strength order from strongest to lightest: ق (Qaf) > ط (Ta-heavy) > ب (Ba) > ج (Jeem) > د (Dal). Qaf has the strongest echo because of its deep back-of-throat origin point.

**Q22 — C** — When Meem Sakin precedes Fa (ف), Izhar Shafawi applies — the Meem is pronounced clearly. Although Fa is a lip letter, it uses the upper teeth + lower lip (not both lips like Meem), so there is insufficient proximity to trigger Ikhfa.

**Q23 — B** — Ba and Meem share the same articulation point — both lips pressed together. This phonetic proximity naturally causes the Meem to be slightly concealed/hidden before Ba. It's a phonological rule grounded in how these sounds are physically produced.

**Q24 — B** — Stopping at the end of a verse ﴾ is Sunnah — it was the practice of the Prophet ﷺ. It is recommended and beautiful, though not always obligatory (you may continue into the next verse in some recitation styles).
