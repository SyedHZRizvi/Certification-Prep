## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B** — An-Nasr was the last complete surah revealed. When companions heard it, scholars like Ibn Abbas understood it meant the Prophet's mission was complete and his time of departure near. Individual scattered verses were revealed after, but no complete surah.

**Q2 — B** — Al-Fath (الفَتح) in verse 1 refers specifically to Fath Makkah — the peaceful conquest of Mecca in 8 AH (630 CE), when the Prophet ﷺ entered Mecca and most of its people accepted Islam.

**Q3 — B** — Ibn Abbas narrated: when Umar asked him why he wept, he explained that this surah is not merely about victory — it is a subtle announcement that the mission is complete, meaning the Prophet's departure is near. Umar confirmed: "I too understood no other than what you understood."

**Q4 — B** — The three responses commanded are: سَبِّح (glorify — tasbih), بِحَمدِ رَبِّك (with praise of your Lord — hamd), and استَغفِر (seek forgiveness — istighfar). Not pride or self-celebration, but humble gratitude.

**Q5 — B** — The Quraysh offered the compromise: you worship our gods for one year and we'll worship yours for one year. This surah (Al-Kafirun) is the definitive response: no such deal is acceptable. Worship is not a matter of compromise.

**Q6 — B** — Verses 2-3 address present practice: "I do not worship what you worship." Verses 4-5 address future commitment: "I will never be a worshipper of what you worship." The shift from present to future removes any chance of "maybe later" interpretations.

**Q7 — B** — "For you is your religion, for me is mine" (لَكُمۡ دِينُكُمۡ وَلِيَ دِينِ) is a declaration of absolute distinction — not indifference, and not an invitation to compromise in the object of worship. It is a statement of principled clarity about the impossibility of merging incompatible objects of worship.

**Q8 — B** — The Quran redefines "denial of the Day of Judgment" behaviorally: the denier is identified not by their creed statement but by their treatment of orphans and the poor. Functionally living as if there is no accountability = denial in practice.

**Q9 — B** — يَدُعُّ (yaduu'u) means to push away harshly, to repel, to shove. It describes not passive neglect but active, harsh pushing away of the orphan who came seeking help.

**Q10 — B** — The warning in verses 4-6 specifically targets: (a) those who are heedless/mindless in prayer (going through motions without presence/awareness) and (b) those who pray for show (riyā — performing for an audience). It does not condemn all formal prayer.

**Q11 — B** — Al-Ma'un (المَاعُون) refers to small household items that neighbors lend each other — a bucket, a tool, a cooking pot. The fact that the surah ends on this smallest possible act of generosity is deliberate: even this most basic neighborly kindness is refused by the person being described.

**Q12 — C** — Those who pray to be seen are described by the Arabic يُرَآءُون — from the root riyā (رِيَاء) meaning performing for an audience/showing off. This is a form of shirk (associating others with Allah) in intention, if not in creed.

**Q13 — B** — The two annual Quraysh caravans: (1) Rihla al-Shita (winter journey) to Yemen for trade, and (2) Rihla al-Sayf (summer journey) to Syria/Levant. These journeys were protected by their status as guardians of the Kaaba.

**Q14 — B** — The argument is: Allah secured your trade routes (and protected you from Abraha in the preceding surah Al-Fil) and fed you from hunger and protected you from fear. The response: worship the Lord of this House.

**Q15 — B** — Al-Fil + Al-Quraysh = one continuous divine argument: First, "We saved you from the elephant army" (Al-Fil). Then, "We blessed your trade and gave you security" (Al-Quraysh). Conclusion: "So worship the Lord of this House."

**Q16 — C** — The event is dated to approximately 570 CE, the same year the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was born. This is why that year is called "The Year of the Elephant" (ʿĀm al-Fīl) — one of the most famous markers in pre-Islamic Arabian history.

**Q17 — B** — Abraha was the Abyssinian (Ethiopian) governor of Yemen. His army included war elephants — a powerful military weapon in the 6th century. The most famous elephant was named Mahmud.

**Q18 — B** — The birds (طَيراً أَبَابِيل) were sent by Allah in a miraculous intervention. They pelted the army with stones of hard clay (سِجِّيل). Classical scholars debate the exact nature of the event; what is clear is that the Quran presents it as direct divine action.

**Q19 — B** — كَعَصۡفٖ مَّأۡكُولِ means "like eaten/consumed straw" — hollow, brittle, completely destroyed from within. The metaphor conveys total and complete destruction; the army was left like grass eaten through by insects — nothing but hollow shells.

**Q20 — C** — Surah An-Nasr teaches that success is not a reason for pride but for humility. The first response to victory should be: glorify Allah (He gave this to us), praise Allah, and seek forgiveness (even in success we may have fallen short). This is the opposite of the ego's natural response to achievement.

**Q21 — B** — Verse 5 repeats verse 3 word for word: "Nor do you worship what I worship" (وَلَآ أَنتُمۡ عَٰبِدُونَ مَآ أَعۡبُدُ). The structure is: Present (v2-3) → Future (v4-5, mirroring 2-3). Verse 4 mirrors verse 2, verse 5 mirrors verse 3.

**Q22 — B** — The surah makes a profound theological connection: belief in the Day of Judgment necessarily produces behavior that cares for the vulnerable. Someone who truly believes they will be accountable for their treatment of orphans and the poor would not mistreat them. Behavioral denial is functional denial, regardless of verbal profession.

**Q23 — B** — Allah swears by time because time is humanity's most irreplaceable resource — it can never be recovered once lost. The oath underlines the urgency of the warning: every moment without the four conditions is a moment of loss. The surah's warning is not hypothetical — it is happening right now.

**Q24 — A** — The three protective surahs — Al-Ikhlas (112), Al-Falaq (113), and An-Naas (114) — form the daily routine narrated in Sahih Bukhari: recited three times each in the morning and evening, and once before sleep, with each recitation followed by blowing on the hands and wiping over the body.
