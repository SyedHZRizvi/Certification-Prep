## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B** — Harakat (حَرَكات, singular: حَرَكة) are the vowel diacritical marks. The word itself means "movements" — each mark "moves" the consonant into a specific sound.

**Q2 — C** — Fatha produces a short "a" sound, similar to the English vowels in "cat," "sun," or "mat." It is the most common harakat in Arabic.

**Q3 — B** — Kasra is written below the letter. Its name means "a break" or "fracture," alluding to the downward position.

**Q4 — D** — Damma is called پِیش (Pesh) in Urdu. Note the shape of the Damma — it looks like a small Waw (و), which is the letter that produces the long "ū" sound.

**Q5 — C** — Sukoon means "rest" or "stillness" in Arabic. The consonant is pronounced but no vowel follows it. The letter "rests" without a vowel.

**Q6 — C** — Shaddah doubles the consonant. The original Arabic term comes from the root meaning "to strengthen" or "intensify." The letter is effectively written once but represents two consonants: the first with Sukoon, the second with the following vowel.

**Q7 — B** — Tanween adds an "n" (nun) sound. The three forms correspond to the three cases in Arabic grammar: accusative (-an), genitive (-in), and nominative (-un).

**Q8 — B** — In بِسْمِ, the Seen (س) carries a Sukoon (سْ). This is why the pronunciation is "bismi" not "bisami" — there's no vowel between the "s" and "m."

**Q9 — C** — بَا = Ba (Fatha) + Alif = "bā" — a long "a" held for 2 beats. The Alif after a Fatha always lengthens the vowel.

**Q10 — D** — There are 8 harakat: Fatha, Kasra, Damma, Sukoon, Shaddah, Fathatan (ـً), Kasratan (ـٍ), Dammatan (ـٌ).

**Q11 — B** — The double Lam in اللَّهِ is written as a single Lam with a Shaddah (لَّ). The word "Allah" in Arabic script is: Alif + Lam + Lam(Shaddah) + Ha.

**Q12 — B** — كِتَابٌ = Ka (Fatha) + Ta (Fatha) + Ba (Fatha) + Lam + Alif (long ā) + Bun (Dammatan at end). The structure is: ki (Kasra) + tā (long a from Fatha+Alif) + bun (Dammatan).

**Q13 — B** — Long vowels in Arabic are formed by a short vowel + its corresponding semi-vowel letter: Fatha + Alif = ā, Kasra + Ya = ī, Damma + Waw = ū.

**Q14 — C** — Zabar (زَبَر) is the Urdu/Persian name for the Fatha. The word means "above" in Persian — reflecting its position above the letter.

**Q15 — B** — When Fatha precedes Alif, the "a" sound is lengthened to "ā" (held twice as long). This is the Madd al-Tabee'i (natural prolongation), the most basic Madd rule.

**Q16 — C** — Tanween appears only on nouns and adjectives (the "declinable" parts of speech in Arabic). Verbs, particles, and pronouns do not take Tanween.

**Q17 — C** — A Shaddah combines two identical consonants: the first carries an implied Sukoon and the second carries the following vowel. So الرَّحْمَٰنِ = Al + Ra(Sukoon) + Ra(Fatha) = "al-Rrahmān" where the R is held slightly longer.

**Q18 — B** — The three Tanween marks are double versions of the three basic harakat: Fathatan (ـً), Kasratan (ـٍ), Dammatan (ـٌ). The double mark signals the added "n" sound.

**Q19 — B** — رَبِّ (rabbi) contains a Shaddah on the Ba. This doubles the Ba: "rab-bi" where both B sounds are heard. رَبِّ means "my Lord."

**Q20 — B** — Kasra + Ya(Sukoon) = long "ī" sound, held 2 beats. This is called Madd al-Leen or Madd al-Tabee'i with Ya.

**Q21 — B** — The complete Quran has harakat on every letter — this is called "mushaf mutasharrik" (fully vowelled manuscript). This distinguishes Quranic text from everyday Arabic writing.

**Q22 — C** — Jazm (جَزم) in Urdu/Persian corresponds to the Arabic Sukoon. The Urdu term Jazm means "to cut off" — reflecting how the vowel is "cut off" from the consonant.

**Q23 — B** — Arabic text without harakat is called "unvoweled" or informally "dry." Newspapers, novels, and most modern Arabic print omit harakat, assuming readers know the words from context.

**Q24 — B** — Damma (ـُ) + Waw with Sukoon (وْ) = long "ū" held 2 beats. This is the Madd al-Tabee'i with Waw.

**Q25 — B** — The complete tashkeel (vowel marking) in the Quran is one of its greatest gifts to learners. Unlike any other Arabic text, every sound is explicitly written — you never have to guess how a word is pronounced.
