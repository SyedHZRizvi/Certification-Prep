## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — C.** Seyyed Ali Quli Qarai published his translation in 2005. It is phrase-by-phrase, deeply informed by classical tafseer literature, and published by ICAS Press, London.

**Q2 — C.** Qarai translates Al-Rahman as "All-beneficent" — a carefully chosen phrase that conveys the universal, all-encompassing nature of this mercy, distinct from Al-Rahīm ("All-merciful") which he uses for the special mercy toward believers.

**Q3 — B.** Maulana Zeeshan Haider Jawadi (1934–2010) is the Urdu translator. He was a renowned South Asian Quran scholar known for precision and fidelity to classical Arabic in his Urdu rendering.

**Q4 — C.** Al-Samad is the being to whom all things turn for all their needs, and who is entirely self-sufficient — hollow of need. Tabatabai explains this as the being who is the endpoint of all dependence.

**Q5 — B.** Jawadi Amoli teaches that "na'budu" (we worship) in the plural signifies that true worship is a communal journey — the believer does not stand before God alone but as part of the ummah.

**Q6 — B.** Ayatul Kursi is Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:255 — the 255th verse of the second and longest surah of the Quran.

**Q7 — B.** Al-Hayy = the Living One in an absolute, unrestricted sense; Al-Qayyum = He who subsists by Himself and is the subsistence of all others. Together they describe total divine self-sufficiency and the complete dependence of everything else on Him.

**Q8 — C.** Makarem Shirazi explains that Ayatul Kursi is called "Sayyid al-Qur'an" because it concentrates the most fundamental principles of pure monotheism (tawhid) in a single verse — including divine life, sovereignty, knowledge, intercession, and supremacy.

**Q9 — B.** Surah Al-Ikhlas contains 4 ayahs: "Qul Huwa Allahu Ahad / Allahu al-Samad / Lam yalid wa lam yulad / Wa lam yakun lahu kufuwan ahad."

**Q10 — B.** The four categories in Surah Al-Falaq are: (1) the evil of all created things in general, (2) the evil of dark night when it falls, (3) the evil of those who blow on knots (sorcery), and (4) the evil of an envier when he envies.

**Q11 — C.** Surah An-Naas is surah number 114 — the final surah of the Quran.

**Q12 — B.** Jawadi Amoli emphasizes that human whisperers — those who corrupt others through speech, media, and culture — can be as dangerous or more dangerous than unseen whisperers (jinn), because their methods are visible, normalized, and persistent.

**Q13 — C.** Al-Abtar means "the one who is cut off" — without lasting posterity, legacy, or continuation. The enemy who called the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) "abtar" proved himself to be the truly cut-off one.

**Q14 — B.** Tabatabai explains that Al-Kawthar in this world refers to the Quran, prophethood, and the continuation of prophetic legacy. The river in Paradise is a further dimension of this abundance.

**Q15 — C.** Surah Al-Asr's four conditions are: Iman (faith), Amal Salih (righteous deeds), Tawasi bil-Haqq (enjoining one another to truth), and Tawasi bil-Sabr (enjoining one another to patience). All four are required together.

**Q16 — C.** Tafseer-e-Namoona was written by Allāmah Nāsir Makārem Shīrāzī. It spans 27 volumes and is known for its practical, thematic, and accessible approach.

**Q17 — D.** Tafsir Tasnim by Jawadi Amoli spans 40+ volumes and is the most extensive modern commentary on the Quran, covering the Quran's mystical, philosophical, and spiritual dimensions in extraordinary depth.

**Q18 — B.** The defining methodology of Tafsir al-Mizan is interpreting the Quran through the Quran — allowing verses to explain other verses, rather than relying primarily on external hadith or rational argumentation as the primary method.

**Q19 — B.** According to Tabatabai, the shift in ayah 5 from third-person description of Allah (ayahs 1–4) to direct second-person address ("You alone we worship") represents a spiritual leap — the act of praising brings the worshipper into direct proximity with God.

**Q20 — B.** Surah Al-Kawthar is surah number 108 — one of the shortest surahs in the Quran, containing only 3 ayahs.

**Q21 — C.** The Huruf al-Muqatta'at opening Surah Yasin are "Ya Sin" — two disconnected Arabic letters whose full meaning belongs to divine knowledge.

**Q22 — B.** Surah Al-Mulk 67:2 explicitly states that Allah "created death and life that He may test you — which of you is better in conduct." The purpose is moral testing, not a demonstration of power as an end in itself.

**Q23 — B.** The Mu'awwidhatayn ("the two seeking-refuge") refers to Surah Al-Falaq (113) and Surah An-Naas (114) together — they form a paired protective shield, Falaq against external threats and Naas against internal corruption.

**Q24 — B.** Makarem Shirazi clarifies that "To you your religion, to me my religion" is a declaration of theological independence — the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa aalihi wasallam) refuses to compromise his monotheism under any social or political pressure. It is not a pluralistic statement but a firm boundary.

**Q25 — B.** Tabatabai explains that "Ahad" negates not merely multiplicity (having partners) but also the very possibility of a second — it is a more absolute negation than "Wahid" which simply means numerically one.

**Q26 — C.** The Quran contains 114 surahs, organized from the longest to the shortest (with the exception of Surah Al-Fatiha at the beginning).

**Q27 — C.** Juz 30 (the final thirtieth of the Quran) contains all the short surahs from Surah An-Naba (78) through Surah An-Naas (114), including Al-Kawthar (108), Al-Asr (103), Al-Ikhlas (112), Al-Falaq (113), and An-Naas (114).

**Q28 — B.** Jawadi Amoli teaches that the Siraat al-Mustaqeem is not an external road but an internal alignment with divine will — it runs through the heart of every soul. The du'a is a prayer for steadfastness on a path already illuminated by revelation.
