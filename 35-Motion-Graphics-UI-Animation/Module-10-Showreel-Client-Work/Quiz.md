## 🎯 Answer Key (No Cheating!)

```
Q1:  B, 60 seconds is the absolute maximum. 45 seconds is better. Never let a showreel run longer than 60 seconds.

Q2:  C, The Hook is your strongest single piece. It must stop the viewer from skipping. Not a title card, not a montage, your best shot.

Q3:  B, Primacy and recency effect: viewers remember what they see first and last most vividly. Lead with strength; close with strength.

Q4:  B, Spec work means creating professional work without payment to enter a competition or pitch. It is not about working to a "spec."

Q5:  C, Industry consensus: do not do spec work. It devalues the profession, normalizes unpaid creative labor, and primarily benefits clients, not designers.

Q6:  C, 2 rounds of revisions is the professional standard. State this explicitly in every proposal. Additional rounds are charged separately.

Q7:  B, Day rate: paid per day regardless of time (protects you when scope is unclear). Project rate: paid per deliverable (protects you when scope is well-defined but creates scope creep risk).

Q8:  B, The reference call is the most important conversation. It surfaces aesthetic preferences that clients cannot articulate in writing, preventing misalignment before work begins.

Q9:  C, Not adjacent. Separate similar styles to maintain variety and pacing. Two kinetic type pieces back-to-back make the reel feel narrow.

Q10: C, Acknowledge and offer to quote separately. Don't refuse, don't do it for free, don't surprise the client with a hidden charge.

Q11: B, Mid-level (2–5 years) in major US market: $600–$1,200/day is the 2026 benchmark. This is not excessive, it reflects overhead, non-billable time, and market value.

Q12: C, Ruthless edit: if you hesitate, cut it. The standard is absolute: every second of a weak piece costs you a second of a strong one.

Q13: B, Client work can generally be shown if publicly released, no NDA applies, and you credit the client. Always check before assuming.

Q14: B, Raw .aep files are the designer's intellectual property by default. Clients receive the rendered final. Raw files are an additional deliverable charged separately.

Q15: B, Revision Policy must define: how many rounds (typically 2), what constitutes a round, and cost per additional round. Without this, disputes are inevitable.

Q16: C, The only valid reason to cut is quality doubt. Age, resolution, and format are irrelevant if the piece is excellent.

Q17: B, The Specificity Test: "Why is this piece in the reel? What specific skill does it prove?" If the answer is vague, cut it.

Q18: C, The Close zone: name, contact info, website. Professional, clean, memorable. Not another complex animation, the viewer needs contact info, not more to watch.

Q19: B, Portfolio rights: designer retains the right to show completed work in portfolio, showreel, and promotional materials. State this explicitly in all contracts.

Q20: B, 45 seconds is more disciplined. The constraint forces better editing decisions. It also respects the creative director's time, which creates a positive impression.

Q21: B, Build a 60-second showreel and pass the Final Mock Exam at 85%+. Both are required. One without the other is insufficient.

Q22: C, Motionographer (industry showcase) and Vimeo (portfolio platform) are the primary discovery channels for motion designers. LinkedIn is secondary.

Q23: B, Scope creep: additional work that was not in the original brief. The client perceives it as "just a small change"; the designer experiences it as unbilled work.

Q24: B, Respect the client's wishes. Client relationship longevity is worth more than one showreel piece. You can ask the client to reconsider, but ultimately it's their call.
```
