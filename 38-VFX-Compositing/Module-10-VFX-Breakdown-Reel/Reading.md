# 🎬 Module 10: VFX Breakdown Reel & Career

## The Work You'll Never Show the Audience

Marcus Flacks has been a compositor at DNEG for six years. He has worked on *Tenet*, *Dune*, and *Oppenheimer*. When he sends his résumé to a new studio, he doesn't send the films — the audience has already seen those. He sends a 90-second reel that shows the supervisor of each shot in three or four frames: the raw plate, the tracked elements, the composited result, and sometimes a wireframe of the 3D elements. In 90 seconds, an experienced VFX supervisor at ILM knows exactly what Marcus can do.

Your reel is your practice exam. It is the one deliverable in your career that you have 100% control over — no client notes, no budget constraints, no pipeline limitations. Every second of it is a deliberate choice.

---

## 🎯 What VFX Studios Look For in a Reel

### The First 15 Seconds Rule

The supervisor reviewing your reel will make their initial assessment within the first 15 seconds. This is not cynicism — it is efficiency. A person reviewing 200 reels per week must triage quickly.

**Rules for the first 15 seconds:**
1. **Your absolute best shot is first.** Not your first shot chronologically. Not your most complex shot. Your best shot.
2. **No logos, no titles, no music intro.** Cut directly to the work.
3. **No slow fades in or moody establishing shots.** The reel is a technical portfolio, not a film.

### The Before/After Breakdown Structure

Every shot in your reel should be presented in one of these formats:

| Format | Duration | Best For |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Split-screen (live/plate left, comp right)** | 5–8 seconds | Keying, sky replacement, set extension |
| **Wipe left-to-right** | 5–8 seconds | Before/after across the frame |
| **Side-by-side: plate / elements / final** | 8–12 seconds | Complex comp, multi-element integration |
| **Breakdown sequence** | 10–20 seconds | Explosion FX, destruction sequence |

> 🚨 **Trap:** A reel that shows only the finished composite — with no breakdown — tells a supervisor nothing. They can see the finished film in theaters. They want to see the plate, the matte, the individual elements, and your decision-making in building the final shot.

### What Supervisors Are Actually Evaluating

| They Look For | They Will Reject For |
|--------------|---------------------|
| Clean edge quality on keys and roto | Edge fringing, visible mattes |
| Consistent film grain across the composite | Mismatched grain/noise |
| Correct color matching (element matches plate) | Color-cast elements that "float" |
| Believable integration (light wrap, interactive light) | "Pasted-on" CG / greenscreen |
| Shot variety (different VFX categories) | Single-technique-only reel |
| Evidence of Nuke work (for film positions) | Only After Effects in reel for a film job |
| Own work clearly labeled | Unclear what you specifically did in a team shot |

## 🏢 The VFX Artist vs Compositor Career Path

Two distinct career tracks emerge from this course:

### Compositor Track

A compositor's primary identity is **shot finishing** — integrating all elements into the final deliverable. The compositor works in 2D (or 2.5D) and focuses on integration, color, edge quality, and visual cohesion.

**Career progression:**
- **Junior Compositor** → **Mid Compositor** → **Senior Compositor** → **Lead Compositor** → **CG Supervisor / VFX Supervisor**

**Skills prioritized**: Nuke proficiency, color science, keying, roto management, shot matching, light wrap technique

**Typical employers**: ILM, Weta FX, Framestore, MPC, Dneg, Digital Domain, Rodeo FX, Blur Studio

### VFX Artist Track

A VFX artist's identity is **creating elements** — building the particle systems, simulations, and motion graphics that the compositor integrates. The VFX artist works in both 2D (AE, Nuke) and 3D (Houdini, Blender) and may specialize in FX simulation.

**Career progression:**
- **Junior VFX Artist** → **FX Artist** → **Senior FX Artist** → **FX Supervisor** → **CG Supervisor**

**Skills prioritized**: Houdini FX (fluid, destruction, particle), Blender simulation, Trapcode Particular, integration compositing

## 🏗️ Working at ILM vs Small Boutique

### Industrial Light & Magic (ILM)

- Founded 1975 by George Lucas for *Star Wars*; now a Disney company
- Global offices: San Francisco, London, Sydney, Vancouver, Mumbai, Singapore
- Work: every major Marvel, Star Wars, Indiana Jones production; IMAX documentary; commercial work
- Culture: **deep specialization** — a junior compositor at ILM may spend months on a single character or sequence
- Technology: proprietary pipeline tools + Nuke; increasingly VR and real-time (ILM XR for StageCraft)
- Career path: slower advancement due to scale; very high technical bar; extraordinary learning environment
- Salary: ILM pays IATSE VES-standard rates; SF/LA market; $55K–$75K junior, $90K–$130K mid, $130K–$200K senior

### Boutique VFX Studios (20–100 artists)

- Examples: Crafty Apes, Luma Pictures, Zoic Studios, Barnstorm VFX, Deluxe (TV division)
- Work: TV series VFX, mid-budget feature films, broadcast
- Culture: **generalist work** — a compositor at a boutique may key, roto, track, and finish shots in the same week
- Technology: Nuke/After Effects mix; pipeline is lighter; more direct director access
- Career path: faster advancement (can become lead/supervisor in 3–5 years); less job security (project-based)
- Salary: $50K–$80K junior; $75K–$110K mid; $100K–$150K senior; less overhead than major studios

| Attribute | ILM / Weta / Framestore | Boutique (Crafty Apes, Luma) |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| Specialization | Deep | Generalist |
| Shot complexity | Hero/Tier 4 | Tier 2–3 |
| Learning | Extraordinary | Rapid generalist |
| Advancement | Slower | Faster |
| Job security | More stable (owned by IP holders) | Project-dependent |
| Salary | Higher | Lower on average |

## 💻 Freelance VFX: Rates and Market

Freelance VFX work is abundant — music videos, commercials, branded content, YouTube creators, independent films, and streaming productions all hire VFX artists on a project basis.

### Freelance Rate Tiers (2025–2026)

| Experience | Hourly Rate | Day Rate | Notes |
|------------|-------------|---------|-------|
| Entry (0–2 years) | $35–$60/hr | $280–$480/day | Short-term projects, small studios |
| Mid (2–5 years) | $65–$100/hr | $520–$800/day | Music videos, commercials |
| Senior (5–10 years) | $100–$175/hr | $800–$1400/day | Broadcast, streaming |

## 🎭 The IATSE Union

**IATSE** (International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees) is the trade union for entertainment industry workers in North America, including VFX artists.

**IATSE Local 839** (The Animation Guild) covers:
- Animators, visual effects artists, concept artists, storyboard artists
- Works with Disney Animation, Pixar, DreamWorks, and increasingly streaming platforms (Netflix, Apple TV+)
- Minimum rates: negotiated periodically; current contract (2024) sets junior VFX floor at ~$55K/year

**Why Union Matters for VFX Artists:**
- Guaranteed minimum wages and overtime rates
- Health insurance and pension contributions from the employer
- Grievance procedures if studios violate contract terms
- Collective bargaining: the 2023 IATSE contract was the most significant wage increase in 15 years

> 🎯 **Industry Reality:** Many major VFX facilities are not unionized in 2026, particularly those outside Los Angeles. London's facilities (Framestore, MPC, DNEG's London office) operate under BECTU (the UK equivalent). Vancouver facilities operate without industry-wide collective agreements. IATSE coverage is growing but not universal.

## 🔧 Assembling Your First Professional Reel

### Reel Length

Professional reels are **60–120 seconds**. No more. A 3-minute reel tells a supervisor you don't know your best work. A 60-second reel of exceptional shots beats a 2-minute reel of mediocre shots every time.

### Shot Count

4–8 shots is ideal. Each shot should demonstrate a distinct skill:
1. Clean key (greenscreen integration)
2. Roto/matte work (clean edge, complex subject)
3. 3D element integration (tracked, correctly lit)
4. Particle FX (fire, smoke, or magic)
5. Invisible effect (set extension, cleanup, sky replacement)
6. Destruction/action FX

### Technical Specs

| Specification | Standard |
|---------------|---------|
| Resolution | 1920×1080 minimum; 3840×2160 preferred |
| Frame rate | 24fps (film standard) or match source |
| Format | H.264/H.265 MP4 or ProRes 422 |
| Audio | Music licensed or original (no copyrighted music) |
| Watermark | Your name and contact; subtle, corner |
| File size | Under 500MB for online submission |
