# Module 6: International Supply 🌍

> **Why this module matters:** CPSM is global. Roughly 10% of the exam tests Incoterms, FX, trade compliance, and cross-cultural negotiation. Get Incoterms 2020 cold — they're free points if memorized and certain misses if not.

---

## 🚢 A Story: The Containers That Never Arrived

A Chicago retailer ordered $1.8M of consumer electronics from a manufacturer in Shenzhen. The PO listed "FOB Shenzhen." The Chicago buyer assumed the goods were the supplier's problem until they docked in Long Beach.

The ship sank in a typhoon in the South China Sea. The supplier had bought insurance only up to the rail of the ship. The retailer had bought no insurance because "the supplier handled it."

The result: the retailer ate the $1.8M loss, plus the lost holiday sales. **Why?** Under Incoterms 2020 FOB, **risk transfers to the buyer when goods are loaded on the vessel at the named origin port**. The buyer was responsible from Shenzhen onward.

The buyer's category manager didn't know that. He thought "FOB" meant "Free On Board, the supplier handles everything until destination." It doesn't. Incoterms 2020 are precise — and CPSM tests every one of them.

## 🗺️ Why International Supply Is Different

Buying domestically:
- One currency
- One legal system
- One language and culture
- Short, predictable lead times
- Mostly familiar tax / customs

Buying internationally adds:
- Currency conversion + volatility
- Multiple legal systems (CISG, local laws)
- Cultural and language gaps
- Long, less-predictable lead times (4–10+ weeks for ocean freight)
- Customs, tariffs, duties, sanctions
- Country / geopolitical risk
- Different labor and environmental standards
- Anti-corruption laws (FCPA, UK Bribery Act)

The CPSM-trained leader anticipates every one of these.

## 📦 Incoterms 2020 — MEMORIZE THESE

Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) are an **ICC** (International Chamber of Commerce) standardized set of 11 terms that specify:
- Who arranges and pays for shipping
- Who insures
- Where **risk** transfers from seller to buyer
- Who clears customs (export and import)

The 2020 update is the current version. There are **11 terms** in **2 groups**:

### Group 1 — ANY MODE of transport (7 terms)

| Term | Full | Meaning (in plain English) |
|---|---|---|
| **EXW** | Ex Works | Buyer picks up at seller's door. Seller does the least. |
| **FCA** | Free Carrier | Seller delivers to a named place / first carrier; risk transfers there. |
| **CPT** | Carriage Paid To | Seller pays carriage to named destination; risk transfers at origin handover. |
| **CIP** | Carriage and Insurance Paid To | Like CPT + seller buys ALL-RISK insurance for buyer's benefit. |
| **DAP** | Delivered At Place | Seller delivers to named destination, unloaded NOT required. Buyer clears import. |
| **DPU** | Delivered at Place Unloaded | Seller delivers AND unloads at destination. (Replaced DAT in 2020.) Buyer clears import. |
| **DDP** | Delivered Duty Paid | Seller does EVERYTHING including import customs. Seller does the most. |

### Group 2 — SEA AND INLAND WATERWAY ONLY (4 terms)

| Term | Full | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **FAS** | Free Alongside Ship | Seller delivers alongside vessel at port of shipment. |
| **FOB** | Free On Board | Seller delivers ON BOARD vessel. Risk transfers when on board. |
| **CFR** | Cost and Freight | Seller pays freight to destination port; risk transfers when on board at origin. |
| **CIF** | Cost, Insurance, Freight | Like CFR + seller buys minimum insurance for buyer. |

## 🎯 Incoterms 2020 — Responsibility Matrix

This is the single most-tested table in CPSM. **Burn it into memory.**

| Incoterm | Mode | Loading at origin | Export customs | Main carriage | Insurance | Import customs | Unloading at destination |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **EXW** | Any | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer |
| **FCA** | Any | Seller* | Seller | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer |
| **CPT** | Any | Seller | Seller | Seller | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer |
| **CIP** | Any | Seller | Seller | Seller | **Seller (all-risk)** | Buyer | Buyer |
| **DAP** | Any | Seller | Seller | Seller | Seller | Buyer | Buyer |
| **DPU** | Any | Seller | Seller | Seller | Seller | Buyer | **Seller** |
| **DDP** | Any | Seller | Seller | Seller | Seller | **Seller** | Buyer |
| **FAS** | Sea | Seller | Seller | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer |
| **FOB** | Sea | Seller (on board) | Seller | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer |
| **CFR** | Sea | Seller (on board) | Seller | Seller | Buyer | Buyer | Buyer |
| **CIF** | Sea | Seller (on board) | Seller | Seller | **Seller (minimum)** | Buyer | Buyer |

*FCA: at seller's premises buyer loads; at any other named place, seller delivers to the carrier.

🎯 **MEMORIZE the trend:** Seller's responsibility grows as you go from EXW → DDP. Buyer's risk shrinks correspondingly.

🚨 **Trap on the exam:** "FOB" is commonly misused in US domestic shipping to mean "FOB Origin" or "FOB Destination" — these are NOT Incoterms (they're US UCC terms). Incoterms FOB applies to **sea/waterway only**, at the named port. Don't confuse them.

🚨 **2020 changes to know:**
- DAT (Delivered At Terminal) renamed → **DPU** (Delivered at Place Unloaded). Broader than terminals only.
- CIP requires **all-risk** insurance (was minimum in 2010); CIF still requires minimum only.
- FCA may now require a Bill of Lading "on board" notation for letter-of-credit purposes.

## 💱 Currency Risk and Hedging

When you buy in a foreign currency, you take on **FX risk** between contract signing and payment.

### Types of FX Exposure
| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Transaction** | Specific contract — price moves between contracting and paying |
| **Translation** | Foreign-subsidiary financials converted to home currency for reporting |
| **Economic / operating** | Long-term competitiveness as currencies move |

### FX Hedging Tools (Module 5 reinforces)
- **Forward contract** — lock in a rate for a future payment
- **FX futures** — exchange-traded standard contracts
- **FX option** — right but not obligation
- **Natural hedge** — match foreign-currency revenues to foreign-currency costs
- **Currency clause** in contract — share burden if FX moves > X%
- **Invoice in your own currency** — push FX risk to supplier (they'll price it in)

🎯 **Exam tip:** "Pay supplier in our home currency" doesn't eliminate FX risk — the supplier prices it in. The risk is shifted, not removed.

### Currency Clause Example
> Base price = €100/unit at EUR/USD = 1.10. If EUR/USD moves outside 1.05–1.15 at payment date, price adjusts by 50% of the excess.

## 📜 Trade Compliance — The Non-Negotiables

### US Export / Re-export Controls
- **EAR** (Export Administration Regulations) — Commerce Department, dual-use goods
- **ITAR** (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) — State Department, defense articles
- **OFAC** (Office of Foreign Assets Control) — Treasury, economic sanctions on countries / entities / individuals

🚨 **Penalties:** Heavy fines, denial of export privileges, criminal prosecution. Procurement teams must screen suppliers and end-users against denied/sanctioned lists.

### Anti-Corruption Laws
- **FCPA** (US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act) — prohibits bribery of foreign officials; applies to US-issuer companies AND their foreign agents worldwide
- **UK Bribery Act 2010** — broader than FCPA: includes private-sector bribery, has "failure to prevent" corporate offense, applies to any company doing business in UK
- **Sapin II** (France) and similar laws globally

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "A foreign agent suggests a 'facilitation payment' to clear customs faster." → Under FCPA, narrow facilitation-payment exception EXISTS but is risky and removed under UK Bribery Act. Best practice: REFUSE, document, escalate. Module 7 reinforces.

### Tariffs and Duties
- **Tariff** = tax on imports (e.g., 25% on imported steel)
- **Duty** = same thing, often used interchangeably
- **HTS code** = Harmonized Tariff Schedule classification that determines duty rate
- **MFN** (Most-Favored Nation) = standard duty rate under WTO
- **Anti-dumping / countervailing duties** = additional duties when foreign goods are "dumped" below cost or subsidized

🎯 **Tariffs change politically.** A 2018–2019 US-China tariff escalation added 25% on many product categories. CPSM expects you to monitor and plan for this risk.

## 🤝 Free Trade Agreements (FTAs)

FTAs reduce or eliminate tariffs between member countries. Major ones:

| Agreement | Members | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **USMCA** | US, Mexico, Canada | Replaced NAFTA in 2020; stricter auto rules of origin |
| **RCEP** | 15 Asia-Pacific (ASEAN + China, Japan, S Korea, Australia, NZ) | World's largest FTA by GDP |
| **CPTPP** | 11 Pacific Rim (Japan, Canada, Australia, UK joining…) | Successor to TPP after US withdrew |
| **EU Single Market** | 27 EU members | Customs union + free movement |
| **AfCFTA** | African Continental Free Trade Area | Active 2021 |
| **MERCOSUR** | Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay (+ associate members) | South American customs union |

🎯 **Exam tip:** Using FTA preferential rates requires PROOF — typically a **Certificate of Origin** stating the goods qualify under the FTA's rules of origin (e.g., USMCA: regional value content ≥ 75% for autos).

## 🌏 Country Risk

Country risk = the political, economic, legal, and operational risk of doing business in a specific country.

| Dimension | What To Assess |
|---|---|
| **Political** | Stability, regime change risk, expropriation, civil unrest |
| **Economic** | GDP, inflation, currency stability, debt rating |
| **Legal** | Contract enforceability, IP protection, courts |
| **Operational** | Infrastructure, ports, labor, logistics |
| **Compliance** | Corruption index (TI CPI), sanctions, ESG performance |
| **Climate** | Natural disaster exposure, water scarcity |

Tools: **Coface country risk ratings**, **Economist Intelligence Unit (EIU)**, **Transparency International CPI**, **OFAC sanctions lists**, **Moody's / S&P sovereign ratings**.

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "We're considering sourcing from Country X with high corruption index." → Mitigate with: contractual anti-corruption flow-downs, third-party due diligence, audits, training, escrow / payment controls.

## 🌐 Cross-Cultural Negotiation

Cultural differences profoundly affect negotiation. Key models:

### Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions
| Dimension | Examples |
|---|---|
| Power distance | High: India, China · Low: Denmark, NZ |
| Individualism vs collectivism | Indiv.: US · Collective: Japan, China |
| Masculinity vs femininity | Masc.: Japan · Fem.: Sweden |
| Uncertainty avoidance | High: Germany, Greece · Low: Singapore |
| Long-term orientation | High: China · Low: US |
| Indulgence vs restraint | High: Mexico · Low: Russia |

### Hall's High-Context vs Low-Context
- **High-context** (much meaning implicit, relationships matter): Japan, China, Arab cultures, much of Latin America
- **Low-context** (meaning explicit, words dominate): US, Germany, Scandinavia

### Practical Implications
- High-context: invest in relationship before deal; expect indirect "no"; gift-giving culture; longer negotiation cycles
- Low-context: contracts dominate, expect fast pace, direct yes/no
- Polychronic cultures (LATAM, Middle East): time is flexible, multiple agenda items in parallel
- Monochronic cultures (US, Germany, Switzerland): time is rigid, agenda items in sequence

🎯 **Exam pattern:** Cross-cultural scenarios — the right answer usually emphasizes **listen, learn, adapt** rather than imposing your home approach.

## 🛃 Documentation in International Trade

| Document | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Commercial invoice** | Pricing record for customs |
| **Packing list** | Carton-by-carton contents |
| **Bill of lading** | Title document + carrier receipt (ocean) |
| **Airway bill** | Air-cargo equivalent of B/L |

| **Insurance certificate** | Marine / cargo coverage |

| **HTS classification** | Tariff code |
| **Dangerous goods declaration** | Hazmat shipping |

## 💳 Methods of International Payment

| Method | Buyer Risk | Seller Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Cash in advance** | Highest | Lowest |
| **Letter of Credit (L/C)** | Medium | Medium-low (bank backs) |
| **Documentary collection** | Medium | Medium |
| **Open account (Net 30/60)** | Lowest | Highest |
| **Consignment** | Lowest | Highest |

🎯 **Exam tip:** New international suppliers often start with L/C or cash in advance; mature relationships move to open account.

## 🚨 Common International Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Misuse of Incoterms (e.g., FOB on air freight) | Disputes over insurance, customs, risk |
| Ignoring FX risk | Margin destruction on currency moves |
| No sanctions screening | Heavy fines (millions+), criminal exposure |
| Brand-named specs blocking local alternatives | Higher cost, longer lead times |
| Single-source in a high-risk country | Disruption when geopolitics shift |
| Cultural insensitivity in negotiation | Lost deals, damaged relationships |
| Skipping certificate of origin | Lose FTA preferential rates |
| Ignoring lead-time variability | Stockouts or excess safety stock |
