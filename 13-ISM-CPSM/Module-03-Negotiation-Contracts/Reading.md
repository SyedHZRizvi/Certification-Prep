## 🥶 A Story: The $12 Million Misunderstanding

In 2019, a mid-size electronics manufacturer awarded a 3-year contract to a contract manufacturer in Asia. The negotiation was friendly. Volumes, lead times, and unit prices were locked. Both sides shook hands. Champagne.

Six months later, demand crashed. The buyer wanted to cut order quantities by 60%. The supplier said no — the contract had a *take-or-pay* clause they had glossed over. The buyer had to pay for product they didn't need: **$12 million** sitting in a warehouse.

The category manager who negotiated the deal was experienced and respected. So how did this happen?

- She let the supplier draft the contract.
- She didn't bring legal in until the eleventh hour.
- She negotiated on price for 6 weeks, on terms for 30 minutes.
- She didn't understand *take-or-pay* meant "you pay even if you don't order."

CPSM teaches you both halves of the deal: how to negotiate well *and* how to write contracts that survive ugly downturns. Neither half works without the other.

## 🎯 What Negotiation Actually Is

Negotiation is a **process of two or more parties seeking mutually acceptable agreement on terms**. It's not a fight, and it's not a sales pitch. It's a structured exchange of value.

### The Two Classic Styles

| Style | Mindset | Outcome | When To Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Distributive (positional)** | Fixed pie — my gain = your loss | Win-lose | One-time deals, commodity buys, leverage-quadrant deals |
| **Integrative (interest-based)** | Expandable pie — we both win | Win-win | Long-term relationships, strategic suppliers, complex deals |

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "Your firm is forming a 5-year strategic partnership with a key supplier. What negotiation style is BEST?" → Integrative. "You're buying a one-off shipment of bulk steel from a commodity broker?" → Distributive may work.

🚨 **Trap:** "Integrative is always better." Wrong. Commodity buys don't need integrative dance.

## 🧭 Core Concepts (MEMORIZE THESE)

### BATNA — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement

Coined by Roger Fisher and William Ury of the Harvard Negotiation Project in *Getting to Yes: Negotiating Agreement Without Giving In* (Fisher, Ury & Patton, 1981; 3rd ed. Penguin, 2011). Your BATNA is what you'll do **if the negotiation fails**.

- Strong BATNA = leverage. You can walk away credibly.
- Weak BATNA = pressure. The other side senses it.
- Always know your BATNA *before* the negotiation starts.

**Example:** You're negotiating with Supplier A. Your BATNA is "buy from Supplier B at $11.50 with a 6-week lead time." Now you know your walk-away price.

### ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement

The range where buyer and seller's acceptable terms overlap.

```
Buyer's reservation: $12   (won't pay more)
Seller's reservation: $9   (won't go below)

      $9 ────────── $12   ← ZOPA
```

If ZOPA exists, a deal is possible. If not (buyer max $8, seller min $10), no agreement is possible without expanding the pie (longer term, larger volume, different scope).

### Reservation Price (Walk-Away Price)
The number beyond which you'd rather take your BATNA than agree. Each party has one.

### Target Price
What you want / what you'll celebrate getting. Optimistic but defensible.

### Anchor
The first number put on the table. Research shows it strongly biases the final outcome.

🎯 **Exam tip:** A common scenario asks "Should you anchor first?" — Generally YES if you have solid market data; the anchor pulls the deal toward you. But anchor outside the ZOPA = the other side walks. Tactical use.

## 🪜 The Negotiation Process (5 Phases)

Most frameworks (Lewicki et al., *Negotiation*, 8th ed., 2020, McGraw-Hill; Camp, *Start with No*, 2002, Crown; Fisher & Ury, *Getting to Yes*, 1981) describe roughly:

1. **Prepare** — Know your needs, BATNA, ZOPA, target, walk-away; research the supplier; assemble cross-functional team.
2. **Open** — Establish rapport, agree on agenda, exchange information.
3. **Explore / Bargain** — Trade across multiple variables (price, volume, terms, payment, lead time).
4. **Close** — Confirm agreement in writing; restate terms.
5. **Implement** — Execute the contract; manage performance.

🎯 **MEMORIZE THIS.** Up to 80% of negotiation success is preparation.

## 🎭 Negotiation Tactics — Recognize and Counter

| Tactic | What It Looks Like | Counter |
|---|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | First number sets the range | Counter with your own data-backed anchor |
| **Good cop / bad cop** | One negotiator is friendly, one is harsh | Address it openly; refuse to negotiate against yourself |
| **Nibbling** | Small concessions requested at the end | "We can do that — but here's what we need in return" |
| **Take it or leave it** | Ultimatum | Test it with silence; verify if it's real or theater |
| **Bogey** | Pretending one issue is more important than it is to trade away | Probe deeper questions |
| **Salami** | One small concession at a time | Bundle issues; trade packages, not slices |
| **Deadline pressure** | "Decide today or the offer's gone" | Walk away if BATNA permits; usually a bluff |
| **Higher authority** | "I have to check with my boss" | Mirror it — bring your own decision constraint |
| **Silence** | Long pauses to make you fill the gap | Sit with it; don't talk to fill space |

🚨 **Ethics line:** Misrepresenting facts, bribing, or threatening illegal action crosses into unethical/illegal territory. The ISM Principles of Sustainability and Social Responsibility (Module 7) ban these.

## 🌍 Cross-Cultural Negotiation (Module 6 expands)

CPSM tests heavily on global negotiation differences. Cultures vary in:
- **High-context vs. low-context communication** (Japan, China = high-context; US, Germany = low-context)
- **Time orientation** (monochronic vs. polychronic)
- **Relationship vs. transaction emphasis** (LATAM, Middle East = relationship; US = transaction)
- **Negotiation pace** (US fast; Japan slow)
- **Use of silence**, group decision-making, deference to seniority

🎯 **Exam tip:** When a scenario crosses borders, ask: "What would the more relationship-driven approach look like?" — it's usually the correct answer.

## 📜 Contracts: The Fundamentals

A **contract** is a legally enforceable agreement. For a contract to exist (under US common law and most jurisdictions):

| Element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Offer** | One party proposes terms |
| **Acceptance** | The other agrees to those terms |
| **Consideration** | Each side gives something of value |
| **Mutual assent** | Both parties intend to be bound |
| **Capacity** | Parties are legally capable to contract |
| **Legality** | Subject matter is legal |

🎯 **Exam fact:** Verbal contracts CAN be enforceable, but for high-dollar goods (>$500 under the UCC) or certain transactions, the **Statute of Frauds** requires a writing.

---

## 📑 Major US Commercial Law Frameworks

| Law | Applies To | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **UCC (Uniform Commercial Code)** | Sale of GOODS in the US | Article 2 governs goods; gap-fillers; warranties (express, implied) |
| **Common law** | SERVICES contracts | Stricter than UCC on mirror-image acceptance |
| **CISG (Convention on International Sale of Goods)** | International sale of goods between signatory countries | Applies by default unless contract opts out |
| **FAR (Federal Acquisition Regulation)** | US federal government contracts | Highly prescriptive; cost-plus rules; flow-downs |

🎯 **Exam triggers:**
- "Sale of widgets domestically" → UCC
- "Consulting agreement" → common law
- "Buyer in US, seller in Germany, goods crossing" → CISG (unless opted out)
- "US federal government as buyer" → FAR

## 💰 Contract Types — Risk Allocation

The contract type determines **who bears cost risk** — buyer or seller.

| Type | Description | Risk Borne By | When To Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fixed-Price (Lump Sum)** | One price for defined scope | Seller | Well-defined scope, low uncertainty |
| **Fixed-Price with Incentive Fee (FPIF)** | Fixed + bonus for outperformance | Mostly seller, some shared | Scope clear; reward efficiency |
| **Fixed-Price with Economic Price Adjustment (FP-EPA)** | Fixed but indexed to a commodity / inflation | Shared via index | Long-term + volatile inputs |
| **Cost-Plus Fixed Fee (CPFF)** | Buyer pays actual cost + fixed fee | Buyer | R&D, uncertain scope |
| **Cost-Plus Incentive Fee (CPIF)** | Cost reimbursed + incentive on performance | Mostly buyer | Some risk shared |
| **Cost-Plus Award Fee (CPAF)** | Cost + subjective award | Mostly buyer | Hard-to-measure outputs |
| **Time & Materials (T&M)** | Hourly + materials at actual cost | Buyer | Short engagements, undefined scope |

🎯 **MEMORIZE the risk allocation.** Fixed = supplier risk. Cost-plus = buyer risk. T&M = mostly buyer.

🚨 **Trap on the exam:** "Scope is undefined; the supplier is unwilling to take fixed-price risk. What's BEST?" → Cost-plus or T&M. Forcing fixed-price on undefined scope guarantees disputes or supplier failure.

## 📋 Essential Contract Clauses

| Clause | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Scope of Work / SOW** | What the supplier delivers |
| **Pricing / Payment Terms** | How much, when paid, currency |
| **Delivery / Schedule** | When, where (Incoterms — Module 6) |
| **Quality / Acceptance** | Standards, rejection rights |
| **Warranties** | Express + implied |
| **Indemnification** | Who pays if a third party sues |
| **Limitation of Liability** | Caps on damages |
| **Confidentiality / NDA** | Protect IP and trade secrets |
| **Intellectual Property** | Who owns work product |
| **Termination** | For convenience and for cause |
| **Force Majeure** | Excused performance during unforeseen events |
| **Dispute Resolution** | Mediation → arbitration → litigation; governing law; venue |
| **SLA (Service Level Agreement)** | Performance metrics + remedies |
| **Insurance** | Required coverage (general liability, workers' comp, cyber) |
| **Change Control** | How scope changes are approved |
| **Audit Rights** | Buyer's right to inspect supplier records |
| **Compliance / Code of Conduct** | Ethics, anti-bribery, labor |

## 🌪️ Force Majeure — The Pandemic Clause

A **force majeure** clause excuses performance when an *unforeseeable, unavoidable* event prevents it (war, earthquake, pandemic, government act).

Post-COVID, force majeure clauses are scrutinized harder:
- Was the pandemic foreseeable in a contract signed in 2022?
- Does the clause specifically list pandemics?
- Did the supplier exercise reasonable mitigation?

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "Supplier invokes force majeure due to a hurricane." → Generally enforceable IF clause covers it AND supplier mitigated.

---

## 🛑 Termination Clauses

| Type | Trigger | Compensation to Supplier |
|---|---|---|
| **Termination for Convenience** | Buyer chooses to end early, no cause | Pay for work done + reasonable wind-down costs |
| **Termination for Default / Cause** | Supplier breached materially | Usually nothing beyond accrued amounts; possible damages owed to buyer |
| **Termination for Insolvency** | Supplier goes bankrupt | Per applicable law; often automatic |
| **Mutual Termination** | Both agree | Negotiated wind-down |

🚨 **Trap:** "Just terminate for default to avoid wind-down payments." Wrong if there's no actual material breach — could trigger wrongful-termination litigation.

## 📊 Service Level Agreements (SLAs)

SLAs convert performance into measurable, contractual obligations. Each SLA should be:

- **Specific** — exact metric (e.g., 99.5% uptime)
- **Measurable** — how it's measured and reported
- **Time-bound** — measurement period (monthly, quarterly)
- **Consequence-defined** — credits or penalties for misses
- **Mutually agreed escalation** path

Common SLA examples:
- IT services: uptime, response time, mean time to repair (MTTR)
- Logistics: on-time delivery %, damage rate
- BPO: handle time, first-call resolution, accuracy

🎯 **Exam tip:** SLAs without **consequences** (credits/penalties) are just wishes. CPSM expects measurable + enforceable.

## ⚖️ Dispute Resolution Hierarchy

Most contracts mandate steps before litigation:

```
1. Direct negotiation between PMs
       ↓
2. Executive escalation
       ↓
3. Mediation (non-binding 3rd party)
       ↓
4. Arbitration (binding 3rd party)
       ↓
5. Litigation (court — last resort)
```

| Option | Binding? | Cost | Time | Confidential? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mediation | No | Low | Weeks | Usually yes |
| Arbitration | Yes | Medium | Months | Yes |
| Litigation | Yes | High | Years | No (public) |

🚨 **Trap:** "Just sue immediately." Almost always wrong on the exam. Negotiation → mediation → arbitration → litigation, in that order.

## 📊 Case Study — ASML's EUV Lithography Monopoly and How TSMC, Intel, Samsung Negotiate (2020-2024)

**Situation.** ASML Holding N.V. (Veldhoven, Netherlands) is the **sole producer worldwide** of Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines — the only equipment capable of patterning chips at 7nm and below. Each machine sells for **€150-220M** and weighs ~180 tonnes. ASML's order book at the start of 2024 stood at over €38B, and the company shipped just 53 EUV machines in 2023. The three buyers that matter — TSMC, Samsung, and Intel — are negotiating against a supplier with **no realistic BATNA on the buy side**: no other company on Earth makes a competing product.

**Decision.** Each buyer ran a different negotiation playbook with the same supplier:
1. **TSMC** (largest buyer): negotiated *priority allocation slots* in exchange for prepayments, long-term volume commitments (10+ year roadmap visibility), and joint R&D investment. TSMC accepted that they could not negotiate unit price down meaningfully — instead they negotiated for *time-to-receive*, getting first delivery of the next-generation High-NA EUV in 2024.
2. **Intel** (rebuilding fab capacity): paid a reported €350M+ for the **first** High-NA EUV machine in early 2024 — a publicity-driven move signaling its return to leading-edge manufacturing. Intel traded headline price for first-mover positioning.
3. **Samsung**: negotiated multi-year capacity reservations and pursued process improvements (Korean officials publicly pressed ASML on access during 2022-2024 export-control discussions).

The US, Dutch, and Japanese governments simultaneously imposed export controls (2023-2024) blocking sales of advanced EUV to China — narrowing ASML's customer base but *not* changing the supplier-power asymmetry for non-restricted buyers.

**Outcome.** ASML's gross margin held above 51% throughout 2024. The company was able to set delivery slots years in advance. Buyers competed for time-of-arrival rather than price. TSMC's strategy paid off most cleanly: first-mover on High-NA gave them an 18-month process lead. Intel's bet was riskier — the company has since slowed its fab buildout amid demand softness, but secured strategic positioning.

**Lesson for the exam / for practitioners.** This is the textbook **no-BATNA negotiation**. When the supplier holds the only product, conventional negotiation theory (BATNA, ZOPA, walk-away pricing) requires translation:
- **You can't negotiate unit price meaningfully.** Don't waste leverage trying.
- **You can negotiate everything else.** Slot priority, payment terms, joint R&D, capability transfer, future-product first-look rights, support terms — these are the real ZOPA.
- **Long-term integrative posture is forced.** Distributive tactics (Module 3) burn the relationship, and the supplier has zero incentive to absorb the burn.
- **Strategic patience matters.** TSMC's willingness to lock in 10-year commitments was the substantive concession that bought slot priority.

When a CPSM scenario describes a *true* sole-source negotiation, the right answer is rarely "negotiate harder on price" — it's "redirect the negotiation toward non-price terms where ZOPA exists" plus a parallel Module 2 effort to develop alternatives over the medium term.

**Discussion (Socratic).**
- Q1: Intel paid a reported €350M+ for the *first* High-NA EUV machine — well above the unit price others paid later. Defend the decision from the buyer's TCO perspective. Then attack it. Which would you defend at an Intel board review?
- Q2: ASML's CEO Peter Wennink (until April 2024, succeeded by Christophe Fouquet) repeatedly emphasized "we partner with our customers." Cynics call this PR; supporters say ASML can't ship without buyer R&D. What evidence would convince you which is right?
- Q3: If you were leading supply at a fourth-tier chip fab (e.g., a Chinese SMIC after sanctions, or an Israeli Tower Semiconductor), how would you re-architect your strategy knowing you cannot buy EUV? What's the trade-off you'd implicitly accept?

## 🧪 Warranties — Express vs Implied

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Express warranty** | Specifically stated (in writing, brochure, marketing) |
| **Implied warranty of merchantability** | UCC: goods fit for ordinary purpose |
| **Implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose** | UCC: buyer relied on seller's skill for a specific use |

Implied warranties can be disclaimed ("AS IS"), but with specific language. Don't assume default protection.

## 🚨 Exam Traps

| Trap | Right Lens |
|---|---|
| "Always anchor first" | Anchor only with solid data; respect ZOPA |
| "Integrative is always best" | Match style to relationship type |
| "Sue immediately" | Negotiate → mediate → arbitrate → litigate |
| "Fixed-price on undefined scope" | Force a dispute; use cost-plus or T&M |
| "Verbal contracts aren't enforceable" | Often are — but Statute of Frauds requires writing for $500+ goods |
| "Force majeure covers everything" | Only enumerated events + reasonable mitigation |
| "Termination for default is easier" | Only when actual material breach exists |
| "Lowest price = best deal" | Ignores total cost / risk allocation |

## 💬 Discussion — Socratic Prompts

1. **Anchoring with weak data.** Conventional wisdom says "anchor first if you have solid market data." Build the strongest case for anchoring *aggressively* with weak data. Then build the case against. When does each approach fail catastrophically?
2. **Fixed-price on R&D — when is it defensible?** Module 3 says fixed-price on undefined scope is a trap. Yet defense contractors regularly bid fixed-price on novel work (sometimes successfully). Under what conditions is fixed-price on R&D *not* a guaranteed dispute?
3. **The post-2024 force majeure question.** After COVID, climate disasters, the Red Sea Houthi attacks (2023-2024), and the Suez/Ever Given blockage, courts have grown more skeptical of force-majeure invocations. Argue whether the standard force-majeure clause should be *narrowed* (events must be enumerated specifically) or *broadened* (any disruption with material impact). Which would you sign as a buyer? As a seller?
4. **The two-tier ethics question.** ISM's code requires you to "decline gifts of significant value during active business." In high-context cultures (Module 6) where gift-giving is part of relationship-building, this can offend. How do you reconcile the absolute ethics rule with the practical risk of insulting a strategic partner?
5. **ASML's pricing power and antitrust.** The ASML case in this module shows extreme supplier power. At what point does monopolistic pricing power trigger antitrust action — and would a 2025-2030 antitrust split of ASML actually serve buyers, or hurt them?
