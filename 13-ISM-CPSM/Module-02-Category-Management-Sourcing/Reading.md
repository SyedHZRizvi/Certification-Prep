# Module 2: Category Management & Sourcing 🎯

> **Why this module matters:** This is the operational heart of CPSM. Spend analysis, Kraljic, and the strategic sourcing process appear in every practice exam — and they're the day-to-day toolkit of every senior supply professional. Skip nothing here.

---

## 🏪 A Story: The $4M Coffee Habit

A global insurance company had 22,000 employees in 38 offices. Each office bought its own coffee, milk, snacks, and break-room supplies — usually from the local grocery store, paid by P-card. Nobody talked to anyone about it.

A new procurement director arrived. She ran a **spend analysis**. The coffee-and-snacks category was **$4.1 million per year**, spread across 600+ vendors. The biggest single vendor got $38,000. The smallest got $11.

She put one category manager on it. Three months later: one national contract, two regional snack suppliers, two coffee suppliers (for choice), standardized assortment, online ordering. Spend dropped to $2.7M (a 34% saving) and admin time fell to near zero. The category manager went on to do the same with **office supplies**, then **temp labor**, then **MRO** (maintenance, repair, operations).

By year three, she'd unlocked **$48 million in annual savings** — without firing a single supplier or squeezing on quality. That is category management.

The lesson: **you can't manage what you can't see.** Step one of every sourcing journey is **spend visibility**.

## 💸 Spend Analysis: The Foundation of Everything

Spend analysis = systematically gathering, classifying, and analyzing **historical spend data** to drive decisions.

### What you need (data sources)
- ERP / AP system (invoices, POs)
- P-card transactions
- Travel & expense system
- Master vendor file

### What you classify by
- Category (a logical grouping like "IT hardware" or "marketing services")
- Supplier
- Business unit / cost center
- Geography
- Spend type (direct vs. indirect)

### Direct vs. Indirect Spend

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct** | Goes INTO the product you sell | Raw materials, components, packaging |
| **Indirect** | Supports operations but NOT in the product | IT, marketing, facilities, travel, MRO |

🎯 **Exam tip:** Direct spend is usually 60–80% of cost of goods sold in manufacturers, and gets more attention historically. **Indirect spend is often underdeveloped** — it's the hidden treasure for new category managers.

### Spend Analysis Outputs
1. Total addressable spend
2. Spend per category and supplier
3. Pareto: usually 80/20 (80% of spend with 20% of suppliers)
4. Maverick spend (off-contract buying) %
5. Savings opportunities

## 🗺️ Category Management — The Discipline

A **category** is a set of related goods or services bought from a similar supply market.

Examples:
- IT hardware (laptops, servers, monitors)
- Professional services (consulting, legal)
- Logistics (freight, warehousing, parcel)
- MRO (lubricants, cleaning supplies, tools)
- Marketing services (agencies, print, events)

**Category management** = treating each category as a strategic mini-business with its own multi-year strategy, dedicated owner, supplier portfolio, and KPIs.

### A Category Strategy Document Typically Contains
1. Category definition and scope
2. Spend profile (current and forecast)
3. Internal demand analysis (who buys, why, how)
4. Supply market analysis (suppliers, capacity, trends, Porter's 5 Forces)
5. Kraljic quadrant placement
6. Sourcing strategy (single / multi / dual / consortium)
7. Risk assessment
8. KPIs and savings targets
9. Implementation roadmap (1–3 years)

## 🎲 The Kraljic Matrix — THE Most-Tested Tool on CPSM

Developed by Peter Kraljic (1983, Harvard Business Review). Plots each category on two axes:

- **Profit impact** (x-axis): financial impact on the company
- **Supply risk** (y-axis): difficulty in obtaining the item

Four quadrants result:

```
                        PROFIT IMPACT
                  LOW                 HIGH
SUPPLY    ┌────────────────┬────────────────┐
RISK      │   BOTTLENECK   │   STRATEGIC    │
HIGH      │                │                │
          │  (custom parts,│ (key components│
          │   single src)  │  driving rev)  │
          ├────────────────┼────────────────┤
SUPPLY    │  NON-CRITICAL  │   LEVERAGE     │
RISK      │                │                │
LOW       │ (office stuff, │ (commodities,  │
          │  many suppl.)  │  many suppl.)  │
          └────────────────┴────────────────┘
```

| Quadrant | Profit Impact | Supply Risk | Strategy | Tactic |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Non-critical** | Low | Low | Efficient processing | Catalogs, P-cards, e-procurement |
| **Leverage** | High | Low | Exploit buying power | Competitive bidding, e-auctions, consolidation |
| **Bottleneck** | Low | High | Ensure supply | Inventory buffers, alternate sources, contracts |
| **Strategic** | High | High | Partnership | Long-term collaboration, joint development |

🎯 **MEMORIZE THIS.** You will see Kraljic on every practice exam. Be able to:
- Place a category in the right quadrant from a scenario
- Recommend the right sourcing approach
- Recognize when a category should MOVE quadrants (e.g., a bottleneck item that you've now dual-sourced becomes leverage)

🚨 **Trap on the exam:** "The category is leverage; what sourcing approach should you take?" Wrong answer: "Build a strategic partnership." Right answer: "Competitive bidding or e-auction." Don't confuse leverage with strategic.

## 🧮 Sourcing Strategy Choices

Within each Kraljic quadrant, you still pick a sourcing structure.

| Approach | When To Use | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Single source** | Only one supplier capable; we chose them | High supplier power risk |
| **Sole source** | Only one supplier exists in the market | Forced dependency |
| **Multi-source** | Use 2+ suppliers concurrently | Higher admin; lower risk |
| **Dual source** | 2 suppliers, primary + backup, often 70/30 or 80/20 split | Standard risk-mitigation pattern |
| **Consortium** | Multiple buyers pool demand (e.g., GPOs) | Lose direct relationship |
| **Insourcing** | Bring back in-house | Capacity investment required |

🎯 **Exam clarity:** "Single source" = you chose to use one (could have used more). "Sole source" = only one exists. Different concepts; the exam will test you.

## 🛠️ The Strategic Sourcing Process (7 Steps)

A.T. Kearney (now Kearney) popularized this. Memorize the sequence.

```
1. Profile category & internal demand
       ↓
2. Analyze supply market (Porter's 5 Forces)
       ↓
3. Develop sourcing strategy (Kraljic + structure)
       ↓
4. Solicit suppliers (RFI / RFP / RFQ / e-auction)
       ↓
5. Evaluate and select
       ↓
6. Negotiate and contract
       ↓
7. Implement and manage performance
```

## 📄 RFI, RFP, RFQ — Know the Difference

CPSM will test you on this distinction in every exam.

| Document | Purpose | When To Use | Output |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RFI** (Request for Information) | Learn about the market and capable suppliers | Early — when you don't know the supply landscape | Pre-qualified shortlist |
| **RFP** (Request for Proposal) | Evaluate suppliers on solution, capability, and price for a complex / undefined need | When the solution isn't standard (e.g., consulting, IT services, custom equipment) | Best-value proposal awarded |
| **RFQ** (Request for Quotation) | Get firm prices on a defined spec | When spec is clear and you only need price/terms | Lowest-qualified-price awarded |

🎯 **Exam pattern:** "We need a new ERP system" → RFP (complex, custom solution). "We need 50,000 widgets to drawing X" → RFQ (clear spec, price competition). "We're entering a new commodity market" → RFI first.

## 💻 E-Sourcing and Reverse Auctions

E-sourcing platforms (e.g., Coupa, SAP Ariba, Jaggaer, GEP) digitize the sourcing process: e-RFx, e-auctions, contract repositories, supplier portals.

### Reverse Auction Mechanics

In a **forward auction** (eBay), buyers bid UP. In a **reverse auction**, suppliers bid DOWN against each other to win the buyer's business.

**When reverse auctions WORK:**
- Clearly specified products or services
- Multiple qualified, willing suppliers
- Low switching costs
- Leverage quadrant categories (commodity-ish)

**When reverse auctions FAIL (or harm):**
- Strategic / innovation-critical categories (destroys collaboration)
- Bottleneck categories (few suppliers, they won't play)
- Services with subjective quality
- New relationships requiring trust

🚨 **Trap:** "Run a reverse auction on every category to maximize savings." Wrong. Wrong quadrant = supplier disengagement or damaged relationships. The right answer is **selective use in the leverage quadrant only**.

## 📊 Evaluating Supplier Proposals — Weighted Scoring

Don't pick on price alone (TCO matters — Module 5). A weighted scoring model uses multiple criteria.

Example for an RFP:

| Criterion | Weight | Supplier A score (0-5) | Weighted A | Supplier B score | Weighted B |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total cost | 35% | 4 | 1.40 | 3 | 1.05 |
| Technical capability | 25% | 3 | 0.75 | 5 | 1.25 |
| Quality (past data) | 15% | 4 | 0.60 | 4 | 0.60 |
| Delivery / lead time | 10% | 5 | 0.50 | 3 | 0.30 |
| Financial stability | 5% | 5 | 0.25 | 4 | 0.20 |
| ESG / diversity | 5% | 3 | 0.15 | 5 | 0.25 |
| Innovation contribution | 5% | 2 | 0.10 | 5 | 0.25 |
| **Total** | **100%** | | **3.75** | | **3.90** |

Supplier B wins on weighted total even though Supplier A is cheaper.

🎯 **Exam tip:** A "lowest total weighted score" or "highest weighted score wins" rubric is best practice. Always set weights and criteria **BEFORE** seeing proposals to avoid bias.

## 📋 Specifications — The Foundation

Bad specs = bad sourcing. Three types:

| Type | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Performance** | What the item must DO (output, throughput, accuracy) | When you want supplier innovation |
| **Functional** | What function it serves | Services |
| **Design / technical** | Exact materials, dimensions, drawings | When form must match (assemblies) |
| **Brand-specific** | Name a brand | Last resort (limits competition) |

🎯 **Exam:** "Brand-name specs" are usually wrong on the exam. They limit competition and lock you in.

## 🚦 The Bullwhip Effect (Demand Distortion)

Forecast errors amplify upstream. End customer demand wobbles 5% → distributors see 10% → manufacturers see 20% → raw material suppliers see 40%.

### Causes
1. Demand forecast updating (each tier re-forecasts)
2. Order batching (suppliers don't see continuous demand)
3. Price fluctuations (forward-buying on promos)
4. Rationing / shortage gaming

### Mitigations
- Share point-of-sale data with suppliers (VMI, CPFR)
- Smaller, more frequent orders
- Stable pricing (no promo-driven spikes)
- Visibility platforms (control towers)

---

## 🧠 Specification Standardization & Rationalization

A favourite CPSM theme:
- **Standardization** = reduce variety (one fastener spec instead of 12)
- **Rationalization** = reduce the supplier list (50 widget suppliers → 5)

Both lift leverage, reduce admin, simplify quality. Both require cross-functional sign-off (engineering, operations) — supply alone can't decide.

## ⚠️ Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It Hurts |
|---|---|
| No spend analysis before sourcing | You optimize blind |
| Treating every category the same | Wastes effort and damages strategic relationships |
| Using RFQ when you needed an RFP | Locks you into lowest-price-on-incomplete-spec |
| Reverse-auctioning strategic suppliers | Burns trust, kills future innovation |
| Setting weights AFTER seeing bids | Manipulation, audit risk |
| Brand-name specs by default | Kills competition |
| Ignoring indirect spend | Leaves 30%+ savings on the table |

## 🚨 Exam Traps

| Trap | Correct Lens |
|---|---|
| "Always run a reverse auction" | Only in leverage quadrant; never on strategic / bottleneck |
| "Single source = sole source" | They're different; see definitions |

| "RFP and RFQ are interchangeable" | RFP for complex; RFQ for defined |
| "Define weights after bids" | Audit & ethics violation |
| "Indirect spend isn't worth our time" | Often the biggest untapped savings |
