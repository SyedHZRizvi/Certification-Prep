# Module 1: Campaign Strategy & Brief Writing 📝

> **Why this module matters:** Every campaign that goes off the rails — and most do — was missing one thing on day zero: a written brief. This module fixes that. By the end, you'll be able to write a brief that survives first contact with reality.

---

## 🎯 A Real Story: How Liquid Death Briefed Their Way to $700M

In 2017, Mike Cessario walked into investor meetings selling **canned water**. Investors laughed. By 2024, Liquid Death was valued at **$1.4 billion** and selling more water than Smartwater on Amazon. Their secret wasn't the product (it's literally just water). It was their *brief*.

Every Liquid Death ad opens with the same one-line answer to the same question — written in their brand brief: **"Why would a 23-year-old at a concert reach for our can instead of a beer?"** Not "let's sell water." Not "let's target Gen Z." A single, specific, behavior-anchored question. Every brief, every script, every creative starts there.

Compare that to the average campaign brief: "Drive awareness for the brand, target millennials 25–44, use Instagram and TikTok, KPIs to be determined." That brief is going nowhere. It hasn't decided what success looks like. It hasn't picked one customer. It hasn't named one behavior change. Within 48 hours of launch, that team will be optimizing for vanity metrics because no one wrote down what real metrics were.

The brief isn't paperwork. **The brief is the campaign.** If the brief is sharp, mediocre creative will still work. If the brief is fuzzy, brilliant creative will fail. We're going to learn how to write the sharp kind.

## 📋 What A Campaign Brief Actually Is

A campaign brief is a **2-page contract between you, the team, and reality**. It commits to five things in writing:

1. **Who** you're talking to (one person, not a segment)
2. **What** you want them to do (one behavior, not "awareness")
3. **Why** they'd do it (the insight, not the feature list)
4. **Where** the conversation happens (channels, not "all of them")
5. **How** you'll know it worked (KPIs with numbers, not adjectives)

That's it. If your brief is longer than 2 pages, you haven't decided yet — you've just written down all the options.

🧠 **MEMORIZE THIS** — the 5 W's of a brief: Who · What · Why · Where · How. Skip any of the five and your campaign drifts.

## 🆚 Creative Brief vs Media Brief — Don't Confuse These

In a properly-staffed agency, there are TWO briefs and they go to two different teams. When you're a solo marketer (which most of you are), you write both — but you still need to think of them as separate documents.

| Dimension | Creative Brief | Media Brief |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Primary audience | Designers, copywriters, video editors | Media buyers, ad ops, analysts |
| Goal | Inspire great creative | Drive efficient delivery |
| Key output | Insight + tone + key message | Channel mix + budget + KPIs |
| Length | 1–2 pages | 2–3 pages |
| Lives forever? | Yes (defines the brand) | No (refreshed per campaign) |
| Frequency | Once per campaign or refresh | Once per quarter or campaign |

**The mistake 90% of solo marketers make:** they write *only* the media brief. They have a budget, a channel mix, KPIs — but they forgot to write down the insight. Then they ask Claude to generate ad copy and it's flat, because there was no creative brief feeding it.

🚨 **Trap:** Confusing "campaign brief" (umbrella term) with creative or media brief specifically. In conversations with clients or stakeholders, always ask: *"Do you mean the creative brief or the media brief?"*

## ✍️ The Creative Brief Template (Use Verbatim)

Here's the exact template I want you to copy. Paste it into a Google Doc, fill the blanks, and you have a usable creative brief in 30 minutes.

```
CAMPAIGN: [Campaign name]
DATES: [Start date — End date]
BUDGET: $[Total media + production]

THE ONE-LINE BRIEF:
[One sentence. What is this campaign trying to do, for whom, by when?]
Example: "Get 500 Phoenix-area Pilates instructors to book a free trial of
Mind Body Studio Plus by July 31."

THE SINGLE CUSTOMER:
Name: [Pick one. Give them a name.]
Age, location, role: [Specific. "Sarah, 34, Tempe AZ, owns a 12-mat studio"]
Current behavior: [What do they do today?]
Desired behavior: [What do you want them to do?]
Why they don't already: [The barrier — friction, awareness, cost, trust?]

THE INSIGHT:
[The non-obvious truth about this customer that makes the campaign work.
NOT a feature. NOT a benefit. An observation about life.]
Example for the Mind Body case: "Studio owners spend Sunday evenings
panic-Googling 'how to track payroll' because their bookkeeping system
doesn't connect to their class scheduler."

THE PROPOSITION (max 12 words):
[The single message we will deliver.]
Example: "Mind Body Studio Plus connects classes, payroll, and payments
in one Sunday-proof tool."

TONE & VOICE:
[3 adjectives + 1 anti-adjective.]
Example: "Calm, competent, candid — NOT salesy."

KEY MESSAGE PILLARS (3 max):
1.
2.
3.

MANDATORIES:
[Logo placement, legal disclaimers, accessibility requirements, brand
colors, etc.]

OUT OF SCOPE:
[What this campaign is explicitly NOT trying to do.]
```

That last line — "OUT OF SCOPE" — saves you hours of scope-creep arguments. Write it.

## 📊 The Media Brief Template

```
CAMPAIGN: [Same as creative brief]
DATES: [Same]
TOTAL MEDIA BUDGET: $[Number]

PRIMARY KPI:
[ONE metric. Conversion volume, CPA, ROAS, or qualified leads.]
Example: "200 free-trial signups at a CPA <= $40"

SECONDARY KPI:
[Health metric — usually CTR, CPC, or CPM benchmark.]
Example: "Maintain CTR > 1.5% across Meta Reels placements"

GUARDRAIL KPI:
[The metric that, if breached, triggers a pause.]
Example: "Stop scaling if CPA > $60 over 3 consecutive days"

GEOGRAPHY:
[Specific markets. NOT "USA". Use MSAs, postal codes, or DMAs.]
Example: "Phoenix-Mesa-Scottsdale MSA + Tucson + Las Vegas DMAs"

CHANNEL MIX (with budget split):
Channel              %     $       Why
Google Search        40%   $800    Bottom-funnel intent
Meta Reels           35%   $700    Mid-funnel video discovery
TikTok Spark Ads     15%   $300    UGC social proof
LinkedIn TLA         10%   $200    Owner/decision-maker layer

CONVERSION SETUP:
- Tracking: [Pixel, GA4, CAPI, server-side, etc.]
- Conversion events: [List each one + value]
- Attribution window: [7-day click / 1-day view, etc.]

FLIGHT PATTERN:
Week 1: Test phase — broad audiences, $30/day each channel
Week 2: Read learnings, kill bottom 20% of creatives
Week 3-4: Scale winners by 20% daily until CAC drifts
Week 5: Defend and harvest

REPORTING CADENCE:
- Daily: spend + conversions + CPA (Slack at 10am)
- Weekly: full pivot report (Friday EOD)
- End-of-campaign: post-mortem doc + recommendations
```

🎯 **Exam tip:** A real Google Ads Search Cert question asks about *naming KPIs by funnel stage*. Top of funnel = impressions / reach / video views. Mid funnel = CTR / engagement. Bottom funnel = conversions / CPA / ROAS. Don't mix levels.

## 🧪 ICP Validation Before You Spend a Dollar

**ICP = Ideal Customer Profile.** Most campaigns die because the ICP was wishful thinking. Here's how to validate before spending.

### The 3-Test ICP Validation Protocol

**Test 1 — The 20-Conversation Test.** Before launching, talk to 20 actual humans who match your ICP. Not survey responses. Real 15-minute calls. Ask 3 questions: (1) What did you do the last time you faced this problem? (2) What did you wish was different? (3) Would you have paid for [our solution]?

If 17 out of 20 say "I'd have paid for that," your ICP is real. If 6 out of 20, you don't have an ICP — you have a hypothesis. Re-segment and re-test.

**Test 2 — The Search Volume Test.** Use Google Keyword Planner or [Glimpse](https://meetglimpse.com/) (free trend tool). Plug in 10 keywords your ICP would search. If the combined monthly search volume is under 1,000, your ICP is too narrow OR doesn't search online for this problem. Either way: do not launch a search campaign. Use awareness channels instead.

**Test 3 — The Look-Alike Affordability Test.** Take your top 50 best existing customers. Upload to Meta as a Custom Audience. Build a 1% lookalike. If Meta returns an audience size < 100,000 in your target geo, you cannot scale on Meta. Either widen the geo or change channels.

⚠️ **Common mistake:** Marketers skip ICP validation because it feels slow. Then they spend $5,000 in two weeks on an ICP that doesn't exist online, and call it "Meta's algorithm fault." It wasn't.

## 🤖 AI-Assisted Brief Writing

This is where 2026 marketing changes the game. You can draft a usable brief in 20 minutes with Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini. Here's the prompt pattern that actually works.

### The 4-Layer Briefing Prompt

```
LAYER 1 — CONTEXT (paste at the top):

You are a senior brand strategist with 15 years of experience writing
campaign briefs for DTC brands. You write tight, specific briefs. You
refuse to write vague briefs. If I give you a vague request, ask me
clarifying questions until you have enough to write a specific brief.

LAYER 2 — INPUTS (give it real data):

Brand: [Name]
Product: [What it does, in 1 sentence]
Best existing customer: [Real description — paste a real review or case study]
Top 3 competitors: [Names + your read on each]
Budget: $[Number] over [Time period]
Available channels: [List]
Constraints: [Legal, brand, anything to avoid]
Past campaigns that worked: [If any]
Past campaigns that flopped: [Be honest]

LAYER 3 — TASK:

Draft a creative brief using this template: [paste the creative brief
template from above]

LAYER 4 — VALIDATION:

After drafting, list 3 assumptions you made that I should verify.
For each assumption, explain how I could verify it cheaply.
```

That last layer — "list 3 assumptions" — is what separates a usable AI brief from generated fluff. Without it, the LLM will confidently invent things.

### Which Model For What

| Model (2026) | Best at | Worst at |
|--------------|---------|----------|
| Claude Sonnet 4.7 | Long-form briefs with nuance, brand voice, complex constraints | Real-time data |
| ChatGPT GPT-5 | Quick iteration, structured outputs, web search integration | Subtle brand voice |
| Gemini 2.5 Pro | Multimodal inputs (paste a competitor's ad image), long context | Tone control |

**My current workflow:** Draft in Claude → fact-check live data in ChatGPT → review competitor visuals in Gemini → final polish in Claude. Three models, one brief, ~30 minutes total.

## 📐 The Solo Marketer's "Brief Yourself" Method

You're alone. There's no creative director to brief you. There's no PM to ask you for one. So how do you write a brief that would normally come from a senior? You **brief yourself**, formally.

### Step 1 — Block 90 minutes on your calendar
Not "soon." A specific 90-minute block with no Slack. Solo briefing requires writing, not Zooming.

### Step 2 — Do a "wall conversation"
Tape three pieces of paper to your wall. Write at the top of each:

- Page 1: **WHO** is the one person?
- Page 2: **WHAT** is the one behavior change?
- Page 3: **WHY** would they actually do it?

Stand up. Write under each. Cross things out. Rewrite. Do not type. The hand-and-wall workflow forces you to commit. Typing lets you wiggle.

### Step 3 — Translate to the template
Once each of the three pages has one clear answer, paste it into the creative brief template above. Fill the rest in 20 minutes.

### Step 4 — Sleep on it
A brief written at 5pm and reviewed at 9am the next morning catches its own holes. Always.

### Step 5 — Run the "stranger test"
Show your brief to someone outside the project (a partner, a fellow marketer in a Slack group). Ask: *"After reading this, what's the ONE thing this campaign is trying to do?"* If they can't answer in one sentence, rewrite.

## 🚨 The 6 Brief Anti-Patterns (Common Mistakes)

| Anti-pattern | What it sounds like | The fix |
|--------------|---------------------|---------|
| Multi-audience brief | "Target millennials AND Gen Z AND boomers" | Pick ONE. Write a separate brief for each segment if needed. |
| KPI-free brief | "Drive brand awareness and engagement" | Add numbers. "Reach 100K unique users at CPM <= $12" |
| Channel-first brief | "We're going to run TikTok ads because TikTok is hot" | Channel comes AFTER who/what/why. |
| Feature-list brief | "Highlight our 17 features" | One proposition. The rest goes in the landing page. |
| Inspiration-by-imitation | "Make it look like Liquid Death" | Reference for STRUCTURE, not for vibe-copying. |
| Eternal brief | "Refresh whenever we want" | A brief is for ONE campaign. New campaign = new brief. |

⚠️ **Common mistake:** "We don't have time to write a brief." If you don't have 90 minutes to write a brief, you don't have 4 weeks to run the campaign. The brief is the cheapest hour of the campaign — it's also the highest-leverage.

## 💼 Real Case Study: Notion's PLG Brief

Notion's growth team became famous in 2023 for their internal brief-writing discipline. Every paid campaign at Notion gets a brief that fits on one page and starts with a single sentence under "Why this campaign exists." Real example (publicly shared by Camille Ricketts, ex-Head of Marketing at Notion):

> *"Convince teams of 5–15 already using Notion for docs to add the Projects template, so they start tracking work in Notion instead of Asana — measured by Projects-template-installs per workspace within 30 days of campaign exposure."*

Notice what's in that one sentence:

- **Who:** teams of 5–15 already on Notion (not "all Notion users")
- **What:** install the Projects template (not "use more Notion")
- **Why:** so they switch from Asana (the competitive insight)
- **How measured:** template-installs per workspace, 30-day window

That's the entire brief boiled to 35 words. The actual brief was longer. But the first sentence does 80% of the work.

🎯 The exam-style takeaway: A brief without measurable behavior change is not a brief. It's an aspiration document.

## 🛠️ The $200 Campaign Brief — Worked Example

We'll be running this campaign for real in Module 10. Here's the brief we'll write together at the end of this course.

```
CAMPAIGN: Sunday Studio
DATES: Week 1–4 of the capstone
BUDGET: $200 ($100 Google Search + $100 Meta Reels)

THE ONE-LINE BRIEF:
Get 25 Phoenix-area Pilates studio owners (5+ mats) to download
"The Sunday-Proof Studio Checklist" PDF by [end date], from $200 split
50/50 across Google Search + Meta Reels.

THE SINGLE CUSTOMER:
Name: Sarah
Age, location, role: 34, Scottsdale AZ, owns a 12-mat Pilates studio
Current behavior: Spends Sunday evenings reconciling QuickBooks against
class bookings, manually.
Desired behavior: Downloads the checklist; opts into 5-email sequence;
books a 15-min consultation in week 4.
Why they don't already: Doesn't believe any tool is "studio-specific
enough"; tired of demos that don't apply.

THE INSIGHT:
Studio owners aren't searching for "studio management software." They're
searching for "how to fix QuickBooks payroll" on Sundays at 8pm. We have
to be there.

THE PROPOSITION:
"The 7-step Sunday checklist that makes payroll Monday-proof — free PDF."

PRIMARY KPI: 25 PDF downloads at CPA <= $8
SECONDARY KPI: Email open rate > 35% on welcome sequence
GUARDRAIL: Pause channel if CPA > $15 for 2 consecutive days

CHANNEL MIX:
Google Search   50%   $100   Bottom-funnel; Sunday-evening searches
Meta Reels      50%   $100   Top-mid; visual demo of the chaos -> calm

CONVERSION SETUP:
- GA4 event: pdf_download
- Meta Pixel: Lead event w/ value=8
- GTM container for both
- 7-day click, 1-day view attribution
```

You'll see this exact brief reused in Module 2 (we'll build the Google Search campaign for it) and Module 10 (capstone). Notice how concrete every line is — that's the bar.

## 🎓 Key Terms To Know (Add to Anki!)

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Brief** | A 1–2 page written contract for a campaign covering Who/What/Why/Where/How |
| **Creative brief** | The brief for the team making the assets (designers, writers) |
| **Media brief** | The brief for the team buying the placements (media buyers, analysts) |
| **ICP** | Ideal Customer Profile — the one person you're building for |
| **Insight** | A non-obvious truth about your customer that makes the message land |
| **Proposition** | The single 12-word-or-less message the campaign delivers |
| **Primary KPI** | The ONE metric that defines success for this campaign |
| **Guardrail KPI** | The metric that, if breached, triggers a pause |
| **Attribution window** | The time period after an ad interaction during which a conversion is credited |
| **PLG** | Product-Led Growth — growth driven by product usage rather than sales |
| **MSA** | Metropolitan Statistical Area — preferred geo unit for US targeting |
| **DMA** | Designated Market Area — Nielsen's TV market regions; common in media buys |
