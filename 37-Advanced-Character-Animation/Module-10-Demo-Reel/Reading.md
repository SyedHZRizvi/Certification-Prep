# Module 10: The Demo Reel — 90 Seconds That Determine Your Career 🎥

> **A story to open.** In 2018, a Pixar recruiter was reviewing demo reels at the rate of 400 per day during peak hiring season. Four hundred. A typical reel runs 90 seconds. At that rate, she spent approximately 2–3 seconds deciding whether to continue watching. She told a group of animation students: *"I have seen 90-second reels where I wanted to stop after frame 1 and 90-second reels where I called the person before the reel ended. The difference is almost always the first shot — and almost never the number of shots."* This module is about building the reel that gets the call before it ends.

---

## 🎯 What You'll Master

- Select the right shots for an advanced character animation reel
- Order shots for maximum impact
- Choose music that supports rather than competes with the work
- Calibrate reel length for the 60–90 second professional standard
- Self-critique your reel honestly and incorporate peer review
- Understand the specific submission requirements for Pixar, DreamWorks, Netflix, and Riot Games

---

## 📚 Part 1 — Shot Selection

### The Three Laws of Shot Selection

**Law 1: Your reel is only as strong as its weakest shot.**
Every shot on the reel will be watched. There is no "padding with average work to add length." An average shot that follows your best shot makes your best shot look worse. Include only shots you are proud to defend in an interview.

**Law 2: Show what you want to get hired to do.**
If you want to work on character animation at Pixar, your reel must be dominated by character performance work. If it is 60% body mechanics and 40% creature work, you will be evaluated as a body mechanics and creature animator. Know your target studio's specialization and curate for it.

**Law 3: Variety within the discipline.**
A reel with 6 shots of a similar character doing similar things reads as a portfolio of one competency. A reel with 6 shots showing dialogue, physical comedy, weight/mechanics, emotion, creature work, and crowd/complex shot interaction reads as a versatile professional.

### Shot Selection Matrix

| Shot Type | Pixar | DreamWorks | Riot Games | Framestore |
|-----------|-------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Emotional dialogue | Critical | Important | Rare need | Important |
| Body mechanics | Important | Critical | Critical | Important |
| Creature animation | Important | Critical | Rare | Critical |
| Physical comedy | Important | Important | Less critical | Less critical |
| Facial animation | Critical | Important | Important | Critical |
| Subtle performance | Critical | Important | Less critical | Important |

## 📚 Part 2 — Shot Ordering

The ordering of your reel is an editing exercise, not a retrospective. You are building a narrative arc from the opening frame to the final shot.

### The Proven Ordering Formula

| Position | Shot Type | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| 1st | Your single best shot | Recruiter's attention is highest; hook immediately |
| 2nd | Strong secondary shot of a different type | Proves versatility; maintains the hook |
| 3rd–5th (mid reel) | Range of competencies | Can be slower or subtler; recruiter is now invested |
| 2nd to last | Strong physical or high-energy shot | Re-energizes attention before close |
| Last | A technically or emotionally strong closer | Leaves a lasting final impression |

### What NOT to Do

- **Never** put your weakest shot first — even if chronologically it was first
- **Never** front-load all your best shots and taper — the reel dies in the middle
- **Never** put two similar shots back-to-back — variety maintains attention
- **Never** end on a weak shot — the final impression anchors the memory

## 📚 Part 3 — Music Choice

Music is the most misunderstood element of demo reel construction. The wrong music will damage a technically excellent reel.

### The Four Rules of Reel Music

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| 1. Music supports; it doesn't perform | The music should not be so catchy or distinctive that it draws attention from the work |
| 2. Tempo matches the reel's pacing | Fast-cut action reels need driving music; character reels can afford slower, more atmospheric tracks |
| 3. No licensed commercial hits | Using a recognizable song raises copyright flags at major studios and distracts reviewers |
| 4. Cut to musical structure | Shot transitions that align with musical beats or transitions feel more intentional |

### Recommended Music Approaches

- **Orchestral / cinematic scores** from royalty-free libraries (Epidemic Sound, Artlist)
- **Custom tracks** commissioned from music students if you have the relationship
- **No music for silent reels** — silence is valid for a reel with no audio track; it focuses attention on the work

> ⚠️ **The "Bohemian Rhapsody" problem:** Using a famous song on a reel is memorable — but primarily for the song, not the work. Recruiters will associate your reel with the song's existing emotional associations rather than your performance choices.

## 📚 Part 4 — Length Standards

### The 60–90 Second Rule

The professional standard for character animation demo reels at major studios is 60–90 seconds. This is not arbitrary:

- Under 60 seconds suggests limited output or lack of range
- Over 90 seconds tests recruiter patience; the recruiter has 399 other reels to watch today
- The sweet spot of 75–85 seconds accommodates 5–7 shots with adequate screen time per shot

### Time-Per-Shot Guidelines

| Shot Duration | Appropriate For |
|--------------|----------------|
| 3–5 seconds | Simple action shots; secondary motion showcases |
| 5–8 seconds | Body mechanics shots; clear physical action |
| 8–12 seconds | Dialogue/performance shots; emotional beats |
| 12–15 seconds | Complex performance; creature work; multi-character interaction |
| 15+ seconds | Only one shot per reel maximum, and only if every second is extraordinary |

### The Self-Editing Test

Play your reel with the audio off. Watch only the animation. For each shot, ask: *"Does this shot earn its time on screen?"* If you find yourself thinking "this part is slow" or "this part doesn't show anything new" — cut that shot or cut it shorter.

## 📚 Part 5 — Self-Critique vs. Peer Review

### Why Self-Critique Fails

Animators who self-critique alone before submitting frequently miss:
- Weak transitions between shots (you've seen it so many times, the transition is invisible to you)
- Timing issues that feel "right" because you placed every key yourself
- Shots that are technically impressive but don't communicate to a non-animator

### The Structured Peer Review Process

1. **Find two peers** who are not close friends (friends will be kind; you need honesty)
2. **Show the reel once without comment** — watch their faces, not the screen
3. **Ask two questions only:**
   - "Which shot made you most engaged?"
   - "Was there a moment you wanted to look away?"
4. **Watch the reel a second time** and ask them to call out any moment where they were confused or unimpressed
5. **Don't defend** — write everything down

If both reviewers identify the same weak shot: cut it.
If one reviewer is unsure about a shot: leave it in but monitor in further reviews.

### The "First 5 Seconds" Test

Before submitting, play your reel for someone who has never seen it. Watch their eyes in the first 5 seconds. Are they leaning in or leaning back? Leaning back means your opening shot is not strong enough.

## 📚 Part 6 — Studio-Specific Submission Requirements

### Pixar Animation Studios

- **Format:** H.264 MP4, 1080p minimum
- **Accepted method:** Online submission via Pixar's career portal; Vimeo/YouTube links accepted
- **What they look for:** Emotional authenticity, subtle performance, FACS-level facial work, story clarity in every shot
- **What kills a reel:** Technically impressive but emotionally hollow shots; over-animated performances
- **Submission timing:** Open call cycles + specific job postings; internship deadline typically February

### DreamWorks Animation

- **Format:** H.264 MP4; Vimeo/YouTube acceptable
- **What they look for:** Character personality, weight and body mechanics, creature work
- **What kills a reel:** Generic "animation student" shots with no character-specific identity
- **Submission timing:** Rolling applications; recruitment peaks in fall

### Netflix Animation

- **Format:** H.264 MP4, 1080p
- **What they look for:** Style range (Netflix produces in multiple styles), performance quality, collaboration history
- **What kills a reel:** Single-style reel submitted for a multi-style job

### Riot Games

- **Format:** MP4 preferred; portfolio link acceptable
- **What they look for:** Weight, physical impact, responsiveness (game animation principles), clear readability at 60fps
- **What kills a reel:** Film-only animation that doesn't translate to interactive requirements
- **Additional requirement:** Many Riot positions require a game-specific portfolio showing understanding of state machines, cycles, and 60fps optimization

## 📚 Part 7 — The Reel Checklist

Before submitting to any studio, run through this checklist:

- [ ] Opens with your single strongest shot
- [ ] No shot weaker than the one before it in the critical first 30 seconds
- [ ] 60–90 seconds total length
- [ ] Music volume is lower than the dialogue in dialogue shots (if applicable)
- [ ] Music does not end abruptly or loop obviously
- [ ] Title card shows name, contact email, and website/portfolio link (first 2 seconds)
- [ ] Title card does not contain an address (do not share your home address)
- [ ] All shots are original and created by you (no collaborative work without clear credit labeling)
- [ ] All dialogue shots have clearly audible audio
- [ ] Exported at 1080p minimum
- [ ] Tested on multiple screens (including a small laptop screen)
- [ ] Two peers have reviewed and you've addressed their key feedback
