# Module 2: Body Mechanics and Weight — the Physics of Believable Motion ⚖️

> **A story to open.** In 2001, a new animator at DreamWorks handed in a shot of Shrek lifting a heavy log. The supervisor looked at it and said: *"I believe the log is heavy. I don't believe Shrek is lifting it."* The animator had animated the log perfectly — slow to start, arcs through the air — but Shrek's legs were planted with zero anticipation, his center of gravity never shifted over his base of support, and his torso pivoted freely as if gravity had taken the day off. The log knew physics. The character did not. That note, replicated in a thousand forms across a thousand studios, is the subject of this module.

## 🎯 What You'll Master

- Locate and maintain a character's center of gravity through any action
- Distinguish real-world physics from animated physics and apply each intentionally
- Animate weight distribution in walking, lifting, pushing, and receiving impact
- Apply overlapping action correctly for heavy vs. light characters
- Break down the mechanics of a punch, jump, and fall with studio-grade precision
- Understand why animated physics must exaggerate and when not to

## 📚 Part 1 — Center of Gravity (COG): the Invisible Character

Every physical action begins and ends with the center of gravity. For a bipedal humanoid, the COG sits approximately at the pelvis — roughly between the hip joints, at about 55% of total body height.

### Why COG Controls Everything

| Action | What COG Does | What Animators Miss |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| Walking | Traces an inverted arc — rises at mid-stride, falls at contact | Translating the hips laterally without the vertical arc |
| Lifting | Shifts forward and down as the character bends; rises as they stand | Pelvis stays level when it should tilt and compress |
| Pushing | Inclines forward — COG moves toward the push direction | Character pushes from the arms without weight behind it |
| Receiving impact | Drops and displaces in the impact direction | Character barely moves; no COG response to force |
| Jump take-off | Sinks first (anticipation compresses COG), then launches | Skip the sink; launch from neutral |

**MEMORIZE THIS:** The COG must always be traceable to the base of support. If a character is standing on two feet, the COG projects to the ground between the feet. Shift it outside that base and the character is about to fall — or leap.

## 📚 Part 2 — Weight Distribution: Heavy vs. Light Characters

Weight is one of the most discussed and least-understood topics in animation education. The difference between a 5 kg character and a 500 kg character is not just timing — it is the *relationship between all body parts* over time.

### The Core Mechanics of Heavy vs. Light

| Property | Light Character | Heavy Character |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| Anticipation | Short, quick wind-up | Long, deep anticipation |
| Follow-through | Overshoots far past the stopping point | Barely passes the stopping point |
| Drag on secondary parts | Hair, clothing whip and linger | Jowls, belly settle slowly and firmly |
| Recovery from impact | Springs back quickly | Absorbs and settles; no spring |
| Stride tempo | Fast, staccato | Slow, deliberate; ground impact is heavy |
| Silhouette squash | Extreme squash on contact | Minimal squash; mass is incompressible |

### Overlapping Action for Heavy Characters

The 12 principles include overlapping action, but the principle operates differently for heavy vs. light characters. For a light character (a cat, a fairy), the secondary parts lead and follow wildly because they have little inertia. For a heavy character (an ogre, a T-Rex), secondary parts resist change: the belly does not bounce so much as *shudder* and *settle*.

> 🎯 **Exam tip:** "Drag" and "follow-through" are two sides of the same coin — drag resists the onset of motion; follow-through resists the cessation of motion. Heavy characters have more of both.

## 📚 Part 3 — Real Physics vs. Animated Physics

Real-world physics produces accurate motion. Animated physics produces *believable* motion. They are not the same thing.

**Why real physics fails on screen:**

Real physics is designed to be experienced in three-dimensional space, with peripheral vision, proprioception, depth cues, and continuous time. Film is a 24fps, 2D projection of 3D space viewed from a fixed position. To make the audience *feel* the physics rather than observe it, animated physics must:

| Real Physics | Animated Physics |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Exactly accurate timing | Slightly slower for emotional beats, slightly faster for comedy |
| Linear spacing in freefall | Slow-in / slow-out even in gravity (eases the feel of weight) |
| Exact arc of a parabola | Slightly flattened at the peak for readability |
| Instant impact | 2–4 frames of "squash" on contact even in rigid objects |
| Simultaneous multi-part motion | Overlapping stagger — lead body, then appendages, then tips |

**Where to use real physics:** science-visualization, motion capture cleanup, product demos.
**Where to use animated physics:** character animation in film, games, television — always.

## 📚 Part 4 — The Mechanics of a Punch

The punch is the fundamental test of weight understanding in animation. Every recruiter at Pixar, DreamWorks, and Riot Games has seen 10,000 punch animations. The ones that stand out are the ones where physics and character merge.

### The Seven-Phase Punch

| Phase | Frames (approx.) | What Happens |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1. Anticipation | 4–8 frames | COG drops and pulls back (chambering); shoulder rotates back |
| 2. Weight transfer | 2–4 frames | Weight shifts forward onto the lead foot |
| 3. Hip drive | 2–3 frames | Hips rotate first — the power comes from the ground up |
| 4. Torso rotation | 1–2 frames | Torso follows hips with a 2-frame delay |
| 5. Shoulder release | 1 frame | Shoulder follows torso with 1-frame delay |
| 6. Arm extension | 2–4 frames | Fist travels in an arc to the target |
| 7. Impact and settle | 4–10 frames | Squash on contact; the non-punching arm comes back; COG stabilizes |

**The most common punch error:** Animating the arm independently of the COG. The arm does not throw the punch — the ground throws the punch. The arm is the last link in a chain that starts at the foot.

## 📚 Part 5 — The Mechanics of a Jump

The jump reveals whether an animator understands energy — how it is stored, released, and conserved.

### Energy Storage: the Anticipation

A jump's power comes from the compression of the anticipation. The deeper and faster the compression, the more potential energy — and the higher the jump. This is *not* arbitrary: it is the animator telling the audience's visual system "a lot of energy was stored here."

### The Five Phases of a Jump

| Phase | Key Body Position | COG Behavior |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1. Anticipation / Crouch | Knees bent deeply; spine compresses; head drops | COG drops significantly |
| 2. Launch | Legs straighten; arms drive upward | COG accelerates upward |
| 3. Apex | Body stretches at peak — classic "stretch" principle | COG at maximum height; brief slow-in |
| 4. Descent | Body begins to curl for landing | COG accelerates downward |
| 5. Landing / Recovery | Knees absorb impact; follow-through in hair/clothing | COG drops then stabilizes |

> ⚠️ **Common error:** The stretch at apex is frequently over-applied in comedy and under-applied in action. Know your genre.

## 📚 Part 6 — The Mechanics of a Fall

Falls are one of the most technical actions in animation because real falls happen faster than the human eye can track — and faster than looks good on screen.

### Staged Fall vs. Real Fall

| Aspect | Real Fall | Staged (Animated) Fall |
|--------|----------|----------------------|
| Duration | 200–400ms for a standing fall | 12–30 frames (500ms–1.25s) |
| Head position | Drops immediately with the body | Trails the body by 4–6 frames (leading with hips) |
| Arms | Flail randomly for balance | Windmill symmetrically (reads better) |
| Impact | Sharp, instantaneous contact | 2–4 frames of approach slow-in, then snap contact |
| Settle | One rapid, final position | Secondary settle: head, then hair, then clothing |

### The "Pancake" Error

The most common beginner fall error: the character falls as a rigid unit, maintaining the same pose as they drop. Real and animated falls require the body to lead with the hips and COG while the head and extremities trail — the "noodle" effect that reads as genuine loss of control.

## 📚 Part 7 — Applied Case Study: Shrek Lifting the Log

Returning to our opening story — here is the correct analysis:

**What went wrong:**
- Shrek's COG never shifted forward and down before the lift
- His legs were straight (no hip-hinge) — the lift was all upper body
- The pelvis didn't tilt (anterior tilt when loading, posterior tilt at top of lift)
- No ground reaction — the feet didn't press harder into the ground at lift initiation

**The corrected shot (frame-by-frame):**
1. *Frames 1–6:* Shrek bends toward the log — COG drops and moves forward, knees unlock
2. *Frames 7–10:* Weight transfer to the front foot; grip tightens (finger animation)
3. *Frames 11–14:* Leg drive begins — hips push toward vertical
4. *Frames 15–20:* Torso follows hips upward; arms are just the final link
5. *Frames 21–30:* Log clears the ground; COG shifts back to balance the load
6. *Frames 31–40:* Settle — wobble in the log, secondary in clothing, deep breath

## 🔬 The Animator's Weight Checklist

Before submitting any shot involving weight:

- [ ] Can I trace the COG through every frame?
- [ ] Does the COG move to the base of support before the action begins?
- [ ] Does the action originate from the ground up (feet → hips → torso → extremities)?
- [ ] Is the anticipation proportional to the weight/force?
- [ ] Do secondary parts (hair, clothing, jowls) follow with appropriate drag?
- [ ] Does the settle read as the character's specific mass?
- [ ] Would I believe this character weighs what they are supposed to weigh?

## 🎯 Summary

| Concept | One-Line Takeaway |
|---------|-------------------|
| Center of Gravity | Lives at the pelvis; must stay over base of support |
| Weight Distribution | COG shifts before every major action |
| Heavy vs. Light | Heavy = long anticipation, firm settle, no spring; Light = quick, extreme follow-through |
| Animated Physics | Exaggerates real physics to produce emotional believability |
| Punch | Ground → foot → hip → torso → shoulder → arm; arm last |
| Jump | Store energy in crouch; release through stretch; land with settle |
| Fall | Lead with hips; head trails 4–6 frames; staged 2–3x slower than real |
