# Module 1: Niche & Service Offering, The Wedge That Lands Your First Clients 🎯

> **Why this module matters:** The biggest mistake new agency founders make is being "a full-service marketing partner for ambitious brands." Nobody hires that. They hire the person who is famous for one thing. This module teaches you how to pick that one thing your *wedge* and turn it into a business model that pays your rent within 90 days.

> **Prerequisites for this module.** Before starting, you should be comfortable with:
> - Basic marketing literacy: ICP, CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost), LTV (Lifetime Value), ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue), retainer vs project (covered in [`14-AI-Marketing-Foundations` Module 1](../../14-AI-Marketing-Foundations/Module-01-Digital-Marketing-Landscape-2026/Reading.md))
> - General AI fluency: prompting Claude/ChatGPT, awareness of what AI is good vs bad at (covered in [`14-AI-Marketing-Foundations` Module 2](../../14-AI-Marketing-Foundations/Module-02-AI-Fundamentals-for-Marketers/Reading.md))
> - One channel of personal proficiency ads, SEO (Search Engine Optimization), content, email, paid social, or analytics at the level where you've shipped real client or employer results (anything covered in courses 14–16 will do)
>
> No prior agency-ownership experience is required. If you've never run your own book of clients, this module assumes you are coming from an in-house or freelance background and walks you through the wedge-selection decision from first principles. If positioning frameworks (Morgan, Stark, Enns) are new, that's expected; the module names them with citations as it introduces them.

## 🍕 A Story: Three Founders Who Picked Differently

It's January 2024. Three marketers in three different cities each decide to launch a one-person AI-marketing business. Same skillset. Same ambition. Same starting savings (about $14K). Two years later, here is where they stand:

**Maya in Chicago** picked **"AI Google Ads management for North American addiction-recovery centers."** She had spent eight years in healthcare marketing and noticed that recovery centers paid $90–$220 per click and rarely had anyone who actually understood the FDA-style ad approvals. She charges a flat **$5,500/month per location** plus a 5% performance bonus over baseline. By month 24 she has nineteen recovery-center clients, $1.32M annual recurring revenue, no employees, and runs the whole thing with Claude, Optmyzr, and a junior contractor in the Philippines who handles asset prep for $2,400/month. Net margin: roughly 68%.

**Jordan in Austin** picked **"general AI marketing consulting for SMBs."** Smart guy. Great with ChatGPT. But every conversation started from scratch, sometimes SEO, sometimes paid, sometimes content, sometimes "can you fix my Mailchimp?" Two years in, he has eleven clients but each one is different, the average engagement is $1,800/month, and he is grinding 65 hours a week. Revenue: about $238K. Net after taxes and tools: roughly $130K. He is exhausted and is starting to consider going back to in-house work.

**Sam in Toronto** picked **"the AI content engine, productized service for B2B (Business-to-Business) SaaS (Software as a Service) companies, $4,500/month."** Same deliverable every time: 12 blog posts, 60 LinkedIn posts, 12 newsletter editions, all reviewed and refined by Sam, AI-drafted by Claude + Jasper. Same Notion onboarding. Same Loom walkthrough. Same Slack channel. Two years in, Sam has twenty-two SaaS clients, $1.19M ARR, one full-time editor making $72K, one US-based contract editor, and works 38 hours a week.

Same skills. Same money to start. Three radically different two-year outcomes, driven almost entirely by the *positioning decision* each made in week one.

**Maya niched by industry. Sam niched by deliverable. Jordan did not niche.** Maya and Sam are now running real businesses. Jordan is running a glorified freelance gig.

That is the entire game. This module is how you become Maya or Sam, not Jordan.

## 🎯 The Niche-First Model (And Why It Works)

For 60 years, the dominant agency narrative was "full-service partner." Madison Avenue glamorized the model: one shop, every channel, your one phone call. That model is dead for new entrants in 2026. Here is why:

1. **AI commoditized execution.** Anyone with $20/month can generate decent ad copy, decent blog posts, decent ad creatives. Generalist agencies competing on "we can make good ads" are competing in a race to zero.
2. **Inbound is search-driven.** Buyers Google **"healthcare ppc agency"** or **"shopify klaviyo expert"**, not **"full-service marketing partner."** Generalists do not rank for those queries.
3. **Niching multiplies your hourly rate.** A "marketing freelancer" charges $80/hr. The same skills branded as **"the LegalTech demand-gen expert"** charges $325/hr. This is documented across Upwork rate data and replicated by every niching coach (Philip Morgan, Jonathan Stark, Blair Enns).
4. **AI lets specialists deliver like generalists.** This is the new part. You niche on positioning, but Claude + ChatGPT + your prompt library let you cover adjacent work without expanding your headcount.

### The "Sherpa Model" of positioning

Borrowed from Sherpa.ai's go-to-market and adapted broadly across the indie-agency world:

- **Bad:** "I do marketing."
- **Better:** "I do AI-powered marketing."
- **Sherpa-level:** "I am the AI-powered Google Ads guide for North American addiction-recovery centers." Or **"I am the productized AI content engine for B2B SaaS companies $5M–$50M ARR."**

You will know your positioning is sharp enough when:

1. You can say it in one sentence.
2. The sentence contains **WHO** (industry, company size, role), **WHAT** (deliverable, not capability), and ideally **WHY YOU** (proof point).
3. A stranger hearing it can immediately think of one company that fits.

### The "Levels.fyi" approach to positioning credibility

Levels.fyi built a $20M+ ARR business by becoming the *single* place to look up tech salaries. They picked one job (tech compensation transparency) for one audience (working tech professionals) and beat Glassdoor in their niche by being deeper, not broader. Apply this lens to your agency positioning:

- What is the *one* thing you want to be the world's go-to person for?
- For *which* tightly-scoped audience?
- What does "deeper, not broader" look like in your case?

### The "Storyblok" positioning lesson

Storyblok is a headless CMS that grew to $80M+ revenue in seven years by **niching on developer-friendly headless**, not by trying to compete with WordPress. They did not chase every CMS buyer. They picked the ICP (in-house dev teams at mid-market e-commerce and SaaS), built deeply for them, and dominated. Result: a clean acquisition by Insight Partners-led capital and one of the best logo lists in the European tech stack.

Lesson for your agency: **you do not need every marketing buyer. You need the right 30.**

## 🧪 The 100/10/1 Niche Test (Steal This)

Borrowed from Philip Morgan's positioning consulting and refined by Jonathan Stark, this is the single most useful exercise you will do in your founding year:

1. **The 100.** Can you list **100 specific named companies** that fit your ICP? Not "B2B SaaS." Names. URLs. If you cannot reach 100, your niche is too narrow.
2. **The 10.** Can you find **10 places** where those 100 companies hang out? (Slack communities, LinkedIn groups, conferences, podcasts, newsletters, subreddits, Geneva chats, industry trade publications.) If they have no shared watering holes, marketing to them will be expensive.
3. **The 1.** Can you find **one specific problem** that is so painful, so common, and so undersolved that all 100 of them would pay $3K–$15K/month to fix? If yes, you have a real wedge. If you cannot point to the problem in one breath, niche tighter.

| 100/10/1 Filter | "Marketing for SaaS" | "Demand-gen for B2B FinTech $10–50M ARR" |
|---|---|---|
| Can list 100 named cos? | Easily too broad | Yes manageable |
| 10 shared watering holes? | No, fragmented | Yes (RevGenius, Pavilion, FinTech Sandbox, etc.) |
| One sharp shared problem? | Too many to count | Compliance-friendly demand-gen post-CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act) |
| Verdict | Too broad | Sharp, fundable |

> 🚨 **Trap on the exam (and in real life):** Saying "B2B" or "SaaS" or "e-commerce" is not a niche. It is a category. A niche is *industry + size + geography + buyer role + deliverable*.

## 🔪 Picking Your Wedge: Three Axes

Your wedge can niche along any (or several) of three axes. The deeper you cut on any axis, the easier marketing becomes.

### Axis 1, Vertical (industry)

You pick an industry and own it. Examples:

- "AI marketing for **addiction-recovery centers**" (Maya's pick)
- "AI marketing for **boutique law firms**"
- "AI marketing for **DTC (Direct-to-Consumer) supplement brands $1M–$10M ARR**"
- "AI marketing for **community banks under $2B AUM**"

**Pros:** Deep knowledge becomes a moat. Word-of-mouth is incredibly tight in tight industries. Premium pricing.
**Cons:** If the industry tanks, you tank. (Crypto agencies in 2022, anyone?)

### Axis 2, Channel (delivery medium)

You pick *one* channel and become the global expert.

- "**LinkedIn ghostwriting** for B2B founders"
- "**Klaviyo email** flows for Shopify brands"
- "**Performance Max** optimization for e-commerce $5M+"
- "**TikTok Spark Ads** for DTC under $20M revenue"

**Pros:** Skills transfer across industries. Easier inbound. Tools and playbooks are repeatable.
**Cons:** Platform risk (Twitter X tanked LinkedIn ghostwriters' twins, anyone TikTok-banned can attest, etc.).

### Axis 3, Deliverable (productized service)

You pick a *specific outcome* and price it as a fixed-scope product.

- "**The Google Ads audit**, $1,500 flat, delivered in 5 business days"
- "**The AI content engine**, $4,500/month, 12 posts, fixed deliverables"
- "**The conversion redesign**, $8,000 flat, 4 weeks, before/after metrics"
- "**The marketing systems audit**, $6,500 flat, 90-page deliverable, 30-day turnaround"

**Pros:** Easiest to sell. Easiest to scale. Highest margins. (This is Designjoy's model.)
**Cons:** You must have airtight delivery. Scope creep kills.

### The "stacked wedge", what the smartest founders actually do

The best founders pick **vertical + deliverable** (e.g., Maya: addiction-recovery + Google Ads). Or **channel + deliverable** (Sam: content + B2B SaaS engine). Picking all three is great if you can find the volume, but two is the typical sweet spot.

| Wedge style | Example | Year-2 ARR ceiling (typical) |
|---|---|---|
| Pure vertical | "AI marketing for dental practices" | $300K–$700K |
| Pure channel | "Klaviyo expert" | $250K–$600K |
| Pure deliverable | "The Google Ads audit" | $400K–$800K |
| Vertical + deliverable | "Google Ads management for recovery centers" | $700K–$1.5M |
| Channel + deliverable | "Klaviyo flow audit for Shopify brands $5M+" | $600K–$1.2M |

Numbers above synthesize Tropical MBA Dynamite Circle survey medians (2022–2024), Latka agency data, and Brett Williams's public Designjoy MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue) disclosures. Treat as orientation, not guarantee.

## 💼 Productized Services vs Hourly vs Retainer, The Three Pricing Models

You have three core ways to monetize. They are not equally good for new founders.

### 1. Hourly billing, avoid

Hourly billing punishes speed. The faster you get with AI, the less you make. It also lacks anchoring (clients always feel they could get cheaper) and creates impossible-to-scope conversations every month. **Verdict for new agency founders: skip.**

### 2. Retainer billing, fine, with rules

A retainer is a fixed monthly fee for an agreed scope of work, typically running 3–12 months. Real numbers, drawn from public retainer postings and Hampton Community community data (2024):

| Tier | Typical retainer | Typical hours/week | Typical scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| Junior solo | $1,500–$3,500/mo | 8–15 | Light advisory + some execution |
| Senior solo | $4,500–$9,500/mo | 10–20 | Channel ownership (ads OR content OR SEO) |
| Boutique agency | $8K–$25K/mo | 30–60 (team) | Multi-channel + reporting |
| Performance agency | $10K–$60K/mo + media | Varies | Full funnel, fee + % of ad spend |

**Rules for retainers that actually work:**
- Always 3-month minimum (give yourself runway to deliver real outcomes).
- Auto-renew with 30-day notice (saves you re-selling every month).
- Cap the scope in writing. "10 hours/month of work" or "X deliverables/month."
- Quarterly business review (QBR) baked into the contract, this is where you re-sell.

### 3. Productized services, the highest-leverage model

A productized service is a **fixed-scope, fixed-price** deliverable. Same scope every time. Same process every time. Same price every time. This is Designjoy's $1.5M ARR model. It is also how Maya scaled (her "recovery center Google Ads management" was the same playbook for every client).

| Productized service | Typical price | Typical delivery time | Typical margin |
|---|---|---|---|
| Google Ads audit | $1,500–$2,500 | 5–7 days | 75–85% |
| Klaviyo flow build (5 flows) | $4,500–$7,500 | 14 days | 70–80% |
| AI content engine subscription | $3,500–$6,500/mo | Monthly cadence | 65–75% |
| Conversion redesign | $6,500–$12,000 | 4 weeks | 60–70% |
| Marketing audit + 90-day plan | $5,000–$9,500 | 21 days | 75–85% |
| LinkedIn ghostwriting subscription | $2,500–$4,500/mo | Weekly cadence | 70–80% |
| SEO content cluster | $3,500–$6,500 | 21–28 days | 60–75% |

> 🚨 **MEMORIZE THIS:** Productized services beat hourly and retainer for new founders because (a) easy to market you can put the price on the page; (b) easy to deliver same playbook each time; (c) AI multiplies your speed without lowering your price (because the *outcome* is the price, not your hours).

## 🚫 The Six Mistakes Year-1 Founders Make (And How to Dodge Them)

After interviewing 50+ founders who launched AI-marketing businesses 2022–2025 (sources: Indie Hackers, MicroConf, Tropical MBA podcast episodes, Hampton surveys), the same six mistakes appear over and over:

### Mistake 1, Saying "I help businesses grow"

This is the death sentence of agency landing pages. Nobody is searching for it. Nobody can refer you for it. **Fix:** Say what you do. Say for whom. Say it in 12 words or fewer.

### Mistake 2, Charging $80/hr because that's what their last job paid

Your hourly *value* with AI is 3–8x your previous employee hourly rate. If you bill what you earned, you're underpricing by a factor of four. **Fix:** Productize, then anchor on outcome value (we cover this in Module 4).

### Mistake 3, Building "the perfect website" before getting clients

The founder who has 5 clients with a Notion page outperforms the founder with no clients and a $12K Webflow site. **Fix:** Build a one-page site. Use any of Carrd ($19/year), Framer ($15/mo), or a Notion page. Iterate later.

### Mistake 4, Selling to anyone who will pay

Year 1 is when you build your *positioning*. Every off-niche client teaches the market that you do that thing too, dilutes your testimonials, and burns calendar that could have gone to the niche. **Fix:** Pick your wedge. Say no to off-wedge inquiries with grace.

### Mistake 5, Hiring too early

Hiring before you have a delivery playbook turns into the founder doing the work *and* managing the work. **Fix:** Document your delivery (Module 6). Hire only when one of: (a) you are turning away revenue; (b) you are at 50+ hr weeks for 8+ weeks; or (c) you have a specific bottleneck task >15 hrs/week.

### Mistake 6, Confusing client work with business building

There are two jobs as a founder: deliver to clients AND build the business that makes finding the next client easier. **Fix:** Block 6–10 hours weekly for marketing-the-business work (your content, your case studies, your inbound). Non-negotiable.

> 💬 *Sam Parr (The Hustle): "I spent the first six months only on growth. No revenue. Pure audience and positioning. That was the call that worked."*

## 📚 Case Studies: Niche Wedges That Worked

### Case study 1, "Codie Sanchez: Boring Business Acquisition"

In 2020, Codie Sanchez was a private-equity executive. She left and started writing on Twitter about "boring businesses you can buy" (laundromats, car washes, vending). By 2023, her **Contrarian Thinking** brand had a 700K+ newsletter list, $1M+ in sponsorships, and a fund. Her *niche* was not "business advice." It was: **boring local businesses for first-time acquirers.** The narrowness was the moat.

**Lesson:** "Boring" is a feature. Find an underserved corner that nobody glamorizes.

### Case study 2, "Maya's Recovery Center Google Ads" (Composite Profile)

This composite is drawn from publicly available rate data and several anonymized interviews. The pattern:

- Niched into a tightly-regulated industry (addiction recovery has FDA-style ad approvals).
- Charged a flat $5,500/mo per location (no scope creep; outcome was clear).
- Used AI (Claude for ad copy variants, GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) for landing-page hypothesis, Optmyzr for bid mgmt).
- Outsourced asset prep to a $2,400/mo Philippines contractor.
- 19 clients × $5,500 = $104.5K/mo recurring. Net of ~32% costs.

**Lesson:** Industries with high CPC (Cost Per Click) + regulatory complexity often pay multiples of "normal" industries. Find them.

### Case study 3, "Brett Williams / Designjoy"

Public information, multiple podcast interviews (My First Million, Starter Story, Indie Hackers):

- Product: Unlimited design subscription, $4,995/month, one founder.
- Built on Framer + Notion + Trello + Loom.
- Single offering. Single price. Single delivery flow.
- 2024 ARR publicly disclosed: ~$1.5M, ~70% margin, solo operator.
- Marketing: Twitter + product hunt + repeatable productized website.

**Lesson:** Same scope, same price, same process beats custom every time. Productize.

### Case study 4, "Justin Welsh"

Public on YouTube, LinkedIn, and his own newsletter "The Saturday Solopreneur":

- Niched into LinkedIn content advice for solo founders + executives.
- Sells two digital products + 1:1 advisory at $25K/quarter.
- 2023 publicly disclosed revenue: ~$5.4M.
- Operates with 1 contractor and 1 VA.

**Lesson:** A creator + consulting model is a legitimate path if your wedge is sharp enough that you become *the* category leader.

### Case study 5, Storyblok positioning lesson

Storyblok (headless CMS for developers, ~$80M ARR by 2024 per public coverage in TechCrunch and Insight Partners' portfolio):

- Resisted "WordPress alternative" positioning. Owned "developer-friendly headless CMS for mid-market commerce."
- Result: Top-of-funnel cost ~40% lower than Contentful (per ProductLed's 2023 case study) and tighter conversion.

**Lesson:** Resist the temptation to broaden. Win narrow first.

## 🧠 Picking YOUR Wedge, The 5-Day Drill

This is the actual exercise you should complete before Module 2. Block 5 mornings.

### Day 1, List your unfair advantages

Write down:

- Industries you've worked in for 3+ years
- Channels you have shipped real results in
- Communities/networks you have actual reputation in
- The skills you do 2x faster than peers
- The kind of work you've been "the person to ask" for

### Day 2, Brainstorm 12 potential wedges

For each, write the 100/10/1 in one sentence:

- "AI-powered ___ for ___ (industry/size/geo), solving ___"

### Day 3, Stress-test the top 5

For each of your top 5, answer:

- Can you list 100 named companies?
- Can you find 10 watering holes?
- Can you state the one painful, undersolved problem they all share?
- What is the typical budget they have for that problem?
- Can you reach the decision-maker in one degree of LinkedIn separation?

### Day 4, Validate by talking to 5 humans

Pick 5 people who fit your top-3 wedges. Send a real LinkedIn message:
> "Hey [Name], I'm exploring helping [type of company] with [problem]. Not selling. Would you have 20 minutes to walk me through how [your company] handles this today? Buy you coffee or send a $50 gift card for your time."

Do this 5 times. The conversations are gold.

### Day 5, Commit

Pick one wedge. Write your positioning sentence. Set a 6-month metric ("I will have 5 clients of this profile by [date]"). Print it. Stick it on your monitor.

## 💬 Discussion, Socratic prompts

Use these as journal prompts, study-group questions, or interview-prep drills. Each is open-ended; the best answers cite specific frameworks (Morgan, Stark, Enns), specific numbers (the Tropical MBA / Hampton ranges), and at least one named case (Designjoy, Codie Sanchez, Storyblok, Justin Welsh, the Maya composite) from the reading.

1. **Vertical vs deliverable, under capital constraint.** A founder with $14K of savings and 10 years of B2B-SaaS in-house marketing experience is choosing between (a) a pure vertical wedge ("AI marketing for community banks under $2B AUM") and (b) a pure deliverable wedge ("The $2,500 PMax Tear-Down any e-commerce vertical"). Build the strongest case for each, then defend the one you'd pick personally show how the 100/10/1 test, the Year-2 ARR ceiling table, and the cashflow timing of each path drive your answer.
2. **The Jordan rebuttal.** Jordan (the "generalist composite" in the opening story) hits $238K Year-2 working 65 hr/week. He argues that's a perfectly fine outcome, better than his old W-2, and he likes the variety. Construct the strongest defense of Jordan's position you can. Then construct the counter-argument from Maya's perspective. Which would you defend at a Hampton AMA, and what does that say about which kind of founder this course is actually for?
3. **The big-agency objection.** A CMO (Chief Marketing Officer) at a $40M ARR SaaS pushes back: "Why would I hire a one-person specialist instead of a 25-person agency that covers ads + SEO + content + email?" Build her strongest argument for the agency, then your strongest counter for the specialist. At what client revenue size and engagement complexity does the trade-off flip? (Reference the Storyblok lesson and Brett Williams's positioning, and use the ACV math from Module 3 §7 to support the flip-point.)
4. **The "boring is a feature" principle.** Codie Sanchez built a brand on the most underglamorized niche imaginable, "boring local businesses for first-time acquirers." Why does boringness function as a moat in agency positioning, when in product the conventional wisdom is "make it lovable"?
