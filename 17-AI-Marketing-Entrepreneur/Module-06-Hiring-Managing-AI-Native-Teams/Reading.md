## 👤 A Story: The Hire Who Made Maya $200K More

It's October 2024. Maya (from Module 1's recovery-center Google Ads story) has 11 clients. She's working 58 hours a week. Two clients have just been told her "next available onboarding" is 5 weeks out, which probably loses her one of them.

She knows she needs to hire. But she's nervous: every founder she knows who hired prematurely got destroyed.

She decides to test the simplest, cheapest hire first: **a part-time Filipino contractor for asset prep + reporting** at $14/hr, 25 hr/wk = $1,400/mo. She finds Diana through OnlineJobs.ph (a $69 one-time fee), interviews 7 candidates, runs a paid 3-hour work-sample test ($42), and signs Diana for a 30-day trial.

By month 2:

- Diana handles weekly ad performance reports (was 6 hrs of Maya's time/wk = freed 24 hrs/mo)
- Diana preps creative assets in Figma + ChatGPT (was 4 hrs/wk = freed 16 hrs/mo)
- Diana handles client onboarding doc prep (was 3 hrs per new client)
- **Diana frees ~50 hours of Maya's time per month**

Maya uses those 50 freed hours to:

- Sign 4 more clients ($5,500/mo each = $22K/mo new ARR)
- Build a more polished website (drives more inbound)
- Take Friday afternoons off

By month 6, Diana has been promoted to **$2,200/mo full-time** with raises. Maya has 19 clients. New monthly ARR added since hiring Diana: ~$50K/mo. Diana's all-in cost: ~$2,500/mo. **ROI (Return on Investment) on Maya's first hire: ~20x.**

The right hire at the right time is the single biggest leverage move a solo agency founder can make. This module is how you make it.

## ⏰ When To Hire Your First Person (Not Before)

There are exactly **three legitimate reasons** to make your first hire. If none apply, you are hiring too early.

### Trigger 1, Sustained capacity overflow

You have logged **8+ consecutive weeks at 50+ hours**, with another 2–4 prospects in the pipeline that you cannot serve.

### Trigger 2, A specific bottleneck task

There is ONE specific task type (asset prep, reporting, content production, transcript editing) that consumes **15+ hours/week** and can be cleanly delegated to someone earning $14–$40/hr.

### Trigger 3, Active revenue you're turning away

You have at least **2 inbound prospects per month you are saying no to** because you cannot deliver. The cost of saying no exceeds the cost of hiring.

> 🚨 **Trap:** Founders hire because they "want a team" or feel they "should look bigger." Both kill profitability. Hire only when math forces you to.

### The 1.5x rule

A common rule of thumb in productized service businesses: a new hire should generate **1.5–2x their cost in freed capacity or new revenue within 90 days**. If it takes longer, the hire is either too senior, in the wrong role, or hired too early.

| Hire | Cost (US) | Required impact in 90 days |
|---|---|---|
| US VA / coordinator | $25/hr × 20 hr/wk = $2,000/mo | Free 30+ hr/mo or unlock $4K/mo new revenue |
| Filipino VA (full-time) | $1,400–$2,400/mo | Free 80+ hr/mo or unlock $3–4K/mo |
| Junior US employee | $5K–$7K/mo | Unlock $10–14K/mo new revenue |
| Mid-level US employee | $7K–$11K/mo | Unlock $14–22K/mo |
| Senior US employee | $11K–$18K/mo | Unlock $22–36K/mo |

## 🧬 The AI-Native Employee Profile (What To Hire For)

In 2026, hiring is not about hiring "marketers", it's about hiring **AI-native operators**. The profile:

### The 5 traits to screen for

1. **Daily AI usage.** Uses Claude/ChatGPT/Gemini for at least 50% of their writing/analysis work today, in their current job. Asks them: *"Walk me through how you used AI yesterday."* If they freeze, they're not AI-native.
2. **Loves shipping fast.** "Done is better than perfect." Indie hackers, ship-30-for-30 alumni, "build in public" people score high here.
3. **Self-teaching.** Can pick up a new tool in 3–5 hours without a course. Test with an unfamiliar tool in the work-sample.
4. **Async-first communicator.** Comfortable with Loom, written briefs, and not needing 8 hr/day of synchronous calls.
5. **Process-oriented.** Doesn't reinvent wheels. Will use the SOP you wrote, then suggest improvements.

### The 3 anti-traits (red flags)

- ❌ Says "I work better in person" without flexibility
- ❌ Refuses to use AI ("I'm a real writer / designer")
- ❌ Brittle ego, can't accept rapid feedback

### Job titles that work for AI-native operators

| Title | What they do | Comp band (US, FT, 2026) |
|---|---|---|
| **Marketing Coordinator** | Assets, scheduling, reporting | $48K–$72K |
| **Marketing Operator** | End-to-end campaign execution | $72K–$110K |
| **Content Producer** | AI-assisted long-form + social | $58K–$95K |
| **Performance Marketing Analyst** | Paid ads, audit work | $65K–$120K |
| **AI Marketing Engineer** | Automation, custom AI builds | $90K–$180K |
| **Growth Marketer** | Funnel optimization | $80K–$160K |
| **Senior Strategist** | Client-facing strategy lead | $110K–$220K |

Numbers above are aggregated from Glassdoor 2024–2025, Levels.fyi, Pavilion Compensation Report 2024, and Built In's 2024 marketing salary survey. Adjust for cost of living. Verify current data, pricing/comp shifts annually.

## 🎯 The Interview Framework (Work-Sample First)

**MEMORIZE THIS:** Work-sample tests beat behavioral interviews by 2–3x in predicting on-the-job performance, per the research of Schmidt & Hunter (1998) and replicated by Google, IDEO, and Stripe. Use a work sample.

### The 4-stage interview funnel

| Stage | What happens | Time |
|---|---|---|
| 1, Application screen | Review portfolio + custom application questions | 15 min |
| 2, 30-min intro call | Mutual fit, role overview, comp confirmation | 30 min |
| 3, Paid work-sample test (the deciding step) | Real task, 2–4 hours, paid at hourly rate | 2–4 hr |
| 4, Final call + offer | Reference check + offer | 30 min |

> 🎯 **The work sample is the entire interview.** Behavioral questions can be faked; work output cannot.

### Sample work-sample tests by role

#### Marketing Coordinator

> *"Here is a 25-page Google Ads audit. Build me a 1-page executive summary in our brand template (attached). Then write a 200-word client-facing email summarizing the top 3 recommendations. You have 2.5 hours. We will pay you $90 regardless of outcome."*

#### Content Producer

> *"Pick one of these 3 client briefs. Produce a 1,200-word blog post using Claude/ChatGPT as a draft assistant. Then build a 5-post LinkedIn series repurposing the article. Show your prompts. 4 hours. Paid $150."*

#### Performance Marketing Analyst

> *"Here is a 90-day Google Ads account export (Excel, anonymized). Identify the 5 highest-ROI optimizations and write a 1-page client memo. 3 hours. Paid $120."*

### Scoring the work sample

Use a rubric:

| Dimension | Weight | What to look for |
|---|---|---|
| Output quality | 35% | Did the deliverable solve the brief? |
| AI usage | 20% | Did they leverage AI smartly? Show prompts. |
| Speed | 15% | Done in the time given? |
| Communication | 15% | Clear written output, good documentation |
| Process | 15% | Logical, repeatable approach |

Score 1–10 in each. Hire on 7.5+ aggregate.

### Reference checks

Two reference calls, 15 min each. Ask:

1. "What was [Candidate] specifically responsible for?"
2. "What would [Candidate] need help with to be great?"
3. "Would you re-hire them tomorrow?" (Listen for hesitation.)
4. "How do they use AI in their work?"
5. "What's one project where they ran the show?"

## 📜 The Onboarding Playbook

The first 30 days determine whether a hire becomes a force multiplier or a drag. Use this exact playbook.

### Week 1, Context

| Day | Goal | Activities |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Setup | Tools access, Notion onboarding doc, payment forms, NDA |
| 2 | Business context | 90-min recorded Loom: ICP, wedge, services, clients, brand voice |
| 3 | Client tour | Loom tour of every active client + their context |
| 4 | Tool deep-dive | Hands-on tour of Apollo / Make / Notion / etc. |
| 5 | First task | Small real task with a partner / your supervision |

### Week 2, Shadowing

Pair them on real client work. They observe, take notes, ask questions. End-of-week 1-hour debrief.

### Week 3, Driving

They lead one workflow end-to-end with your review. Increase autonomy daily.

### Week 4, Solo

They run independent client work. You do daily 15-min check-ins.

### The 30/60/90 review

Document explicit performance milestones. Share these on Day 1.

```
By Day 30: Can execute [X workflow] independently with 2 review touchpoints
By Day 60: Owns [Y workflow] end-to-end; quality matches Maya's
By Day 90: Has identified at least 1 process improvement; is freeing 30+ hr/mo
```

### The "SOP library", your most important asset

Every recurring task needs a written SOP (Standard Operating Procedure). Build them in Notion. Each SOP has:

1. **Trigger**, when this SOP fires
2. **Inputs**, what's needed before starting
3. **Steps**, numbered, specific
4. **Tools**, links to specific software
5. **Output**, what "done" looks like
6. **QA checklist**, review items before delivery
7. **Owner**, who is responsible
8. **Notes**, gotchas, exceptions

A team of 5 running off a solid SOP library outproduces a team of 15 reinventing wheels.

## 💵 Compensation: What To Actually Pay

The compensation table in the AI-Native Employee Profile is your guide. Some additional notes:

### Salary vs hourly vs project for first hires

| Engagement type | When to use |
|---|---|
| **W-2 employee (US)** | Long-term, full-time, want commitment + IP clarity |
| **1099 contractor (US)** | Part-time, project-based; needs proper classification |
| **International EOR (Deel/Remote.com)** | Full-time international hires; legal protection |
| **Direct contractor (OnlineJobs.ph)** | Part-time international; you pay them directly |
| **Hourly retainer** | Established freelancer with track record |

> ⚖️ **Disclaimer:** US 1099 vs W-2 classification has serious tax + legal implications. The IRS has specific tests (control, financial relationship, type of work). California's AB-5 and similar state laws make this even stricter. **Always consult a tax attorney or CPA before hiring contractors in roles that look like employees.** This is general operational guidance, not legal advice.

### The variable comp structure

For senior hires, consider tying part of comp to outcomes:

```
Base: 85% of market salary
Bonus: 15–25% of base tied to:
  - Client retention (60% weighting)
  - New revenue generated or supported (30%)
  - Internal initiatives completed (10%)
```

This rewards the right behaviors without straining cash flow.

### Equity / profit-share

For agencies (not VC-funded), equity is rare. **Profit-sharing pools** are common: 5–15% of annual net profit, allocated by tenure × performance. Document it in writing.

## 🏗️ Managing AI-Native Teams, The Cadence

### Weekly cadence

| Day | Activity | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | All-hands weekly kickoff (30 min) | 30 min |
| Daily | Async stand-up via Notion / Slack | 5 min/person |
| Friday | "Wins of the week" share + reading list | 30 min |

### Monthly cadence

- Monthly 1:1 with each direct report (45 min)
- Monthly retro on processes (60 min for the team)
- Monthly performance reflection in a Notion doc

### Quarterly cadence

- Quarterly OKRs / objectives review
- Quarterly comp review (or annual, depending on growth)
- Quarterly client retro

### The "AI buddy" model

Pair every new hire with a structured AI workflow guide. Examples:

- Their daily Claude prompt template for writing tasks
- A Notion library of approved prompts
- Access to your "house style" guide that you use to prompt-condition AI

This means the hire is producing in your voice/style from week 2.

## 💼 Case Study, Buffer's Open-Salaries, Remote-First Hiring Model (2013–2024)

**Situation.** In 2010, Joel Gascoigne founded Buffer as a social-media scheduling SaaS. By December 2013, Buffer had grown to ~15 employees and $1M ARR but faced two acute hiring problems: (1) US-only hiring meant they couldn't compete with Twitter, Facebook, and HubSpot on cash comp; (2) interview processes were producing inconsistent hire quality, with ~30% of new hires not surviving year one. Gascoigne and co-founder Leo Widrich made an unconventional bet: instead of trying to compete on cash with SF/NYC firms, they would compete on *transparency + remote-first culture + structured async work*. They were the first VC-backed startup to publish every employee's salary publicly with the exact formula used to compute it. The decision was documented in real-time on the Buffer Open blog (open.buffer.com), starting with Gascoigne's December 19, 2013 post "Introducing Open Salaries at Buffer."

**Decision.** Buffer published, for every employee from CEO (Chief Executive Officer) to support: (1) the *exact* salary formula a public spreadsheet where comp = role base × experience multiplier × location multiplier × seniority + dependents allowance; (2) every individual employee's current salary with name attached; (3) every raise event, every promotion, every dismissal explained on the blog (with the employee's consent). They committed to fully remote no offices, no HQ, no "remote-friendly with US-Pacific-only" compromise. Hiring switched to a 5-stage funnel: (1) written application with structured questions, (2) values screen call, (3) *paid* skills test (45-day "Buffer Bootcamp" trial at full pay, ~$8,000 per candidate, with no obligation either side), (4) team interviews, (5) final offer with 1-year buyback clause. The Bootcamp specifically replicates what Schmidt & Hunter (1998, cited in this module) call work-sample testing, extended to 6 weeks rather than 4 hours. By 2017, Buffer had ~85 employees across 18 time zones.

**Outcome.** Verified from Buffer's publicly maintained transparency dashboard (open.buffer.com/salaries, last verified by the *Built In* salary survey 2024, and replicated by GitLab's *Remote Manifesto* citing Buffer as the foundational case): voluntary attrition dropped to 7.2% by 2019 (industry benchmark for SaaS: ~13–18%); time-to-hire compressed from 84 days → 47 days because the salary transparency eliminated the negotiation back-and-forth (every candidate knew the exact offer before applying); Bootcamp-to-permanent conversion stabilized at 78% (where industry first-year survival for traditionally-hired employees was ~70% per LinkedIn 2022 data). Open-salary critics predicted internal envy and resignations; the actual measured outcome over a decade was the opposite internal mobility (lateral moves + promotions) accelerated because employees could see exactly what role/skill earned what. Buffer scaled to ~95 employees, $20M+ ARR, and was profitable from 2019 onward without raising additional capital a rarity among 2013-cohort SaaS startups. As of 2024, Buffer still publishes every salary.

**Lesson for the practitioner.** Buffer's model directly maps the work-sample-first interview framework in §"The Interview Framework", but extended from 4 hours to 6 weeks at full pay. For a $1M+ agency operator, the full 6-week Bootcamp is overkill, but the principle (the work sample IS the deciding step) is identical. The salary-transparency play is the rarer lesson: if your wedge depends on attracting AI-native operators who are also operators-at-heart (i.e., they could be founders themselves), traditional opacity around comp filters them out. AI-native operators tend to want stake clarity, the same way they want SOP clarity. Buffer's outcome (78% Bootcamp conversion, 7.2% attrition) is what happens when a hiring process is structurally honest. The §"AI-Native Employee Profile" 5-trait screen plus a Buffer-style paid extended trial produces the highest-quality hires per dollar that publicly documented data captures.

**Discussion (Socratic).**
- Q1: Buffer pays each candidate ~$8K for a 6-week Bootcamp regardless of outcome. At a 22% rejection rate, that's ~$1,760 of "wasted" Bootcamp pay per filled seat. - Q2: Buffer publishes every salary. A founder argues that this is feasible at a values-first SaaS but actively destructive for a services agency where senior strategists and junior coordinators sit on the same Slack and one resents the other's number. Build her argument. Then construct the counter using Buffer's measured outcome (envy went *down*, not up, because the formula was visible and gameable). - Q3: The hidden cost of Buffer's remote-first model is *async management overhead*, every decision documented in writing, every meeting recorded, every onboarding turned into a 90-min Loom. For a 2-person agency, the overhead is invisible (you ARE the company). ## 🚫 The 8 Hiring Mistakes Founders Make

1. **Hiring before the workload exists.** "We want to look established." This drains cashflow.
2. **Hiring "marketing managers" who can't actually do the work.** They can manage; they can't ship.
3. **Hiring people who hate AI.** "I'm a real writer." Hard pass.
4. **Skipping the paid work-sample test.** Behavioral interviews fail.
5. **No SOPs ready.** The hire spends weeks asking questions instead of producing.
6. **No 30/60/90 plan.** No clear definition of "successful hire."
7. **Compensating like a fortune 500.** Going $115K when market is $85K wrecks margins.
8. **Hiring people in your image.** A founder + 4 founder-clones = chaos. Hire complements.

> 💬 *Greg Isenberg's documented advice on agency hiring: "The first hire is usually the wrong hire. Plan for it. Make the rest right."*

## 💬 Discussion, Socratic prompts

Use these as journal prompts, study-group questions, or interview-prep drills. Each is open-ended; the best answers cite the 3 hiring triggers, the 1.5x rule, the Schmidt & Hunter (1998) work-sample research, and at least one named case (Maya's Filipino contractor, Designjoy's solo-by-choice, FletchPMM's $90K+ hires, the Hampton + Pavilion comp data).

1. **Solo-by-choice vs scale-by-default.** Brett Williams has remained solo at $1.5M ARR; FletchPMM hires Marketing Strategists at $90K–$110K. Both are documented Year-3+ outcomes. A founder asks which path is "right." Construct the strongest argument for each archetype, what client profile, what founder personality, what financial goal does each fit? Then defend the path you'd choose, citing at least three concrete trade-offs (founder hours, P&L volatility, exit optionality).
2. **Work-sample tests at scale, fair or extractive?** The reading endorses paid 2–4 hour work-sample tests at the candidate's hourly rate (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998).
