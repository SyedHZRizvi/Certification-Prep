# Module 2: Productizing AI Marketing Services 📦

> **Why this module matters:** A productized service is the difference between selling your time and selling an *outcome*. The first model caps your income at hours-worked. The second model lets a solo operator hit $1M ARR. This module is the playbook for turning your skills into productized SKUs, scoping them so they cannot bleed, and using AI to deliver 5x faster than your costs imply.

## 🛠️ A Story: The Audit That Built an Agency

It's 2022. Hannah is a freelance Google Ads consultant in Manchester, UK. She's billing £85/hr to seven clients on retainers. She's burning out. The retainers always scope-creep. Her revenue is £180K/yr but it took 55 hours a week.

In November she takes a weekend to study Brett Williams's Designjoy. She does something radical: she emails her seven retainer clients and says **"I'm closing my retainer practice in 90 days. But I'm launching a new product: a Google Ads audit for £1,750, delivered in 5 business days, with a 21-day implementation plan you can run yourself or hire anyone to implement."** Three clients book audits. Four leave.

She launches the audit as **The Google Ads Tear-Down™** on Twitter, LinkedIn, and through a single Carrd landing page (£19/year). Same scope every time. Same Notion template. Same Loom walkthrough video at the end. Same 14-page PDF deliverable. AI generates the first-draft analysis from a GAQL data export; Hannah refines.

The first month she sells 11 audits at £1,750. That's £19,250 — more than half her old monthly average — and it took her 22 hours of *delivery* work plus another 14 hours of marketing. Two months later she's doing 18 audits/month. By month 6 she's hired one part-time analyst at £24/hr to run the data pulls.

Year 2: 280 audits/year × £1,750 = **£490K revenue**, ~75% margin, ~32 hrs/week. She added "The Performance Max Tear-Down" at £2,250 — same template, different channel.

That's the productized service in one story. **You sell an outcome with a clear edge — not your hours.**

## 🎯 What Makes a Service "Productized"

A productized service has **five non-negotiable characteristics**. Get any one wrong and you're back to consulting hell.

| Characteristic | What it means | What kills it |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed scope** | Same deliverables every time | Scope creep, client "while you're at it" |
| **Fixed price** | Same number, on the website | Quoted bespoke each time |
| **Fixed process** | Same steps + tools + templates | One-off processes per client |
| **Fixed time** | Same delivery window (e.g., 14 days) | Open-ended timelines |
| **Outcome-anchored** | Sold by result, not labor | Sold by hours or "things we'll do" |

**MEMORIZE THIS** — these are the **5 Fixed Anchors**. If a deliverable cannot be made to fit all five, it is a retainer or a project, not a productized service.

## 🤖 The AI Leverage Question: Where Does AI Cut Your Cost 5x?

A productized service is only profitable if AI gives you meaningful leverage. The right question is not *"can AI do this?"* but *"where in this service does AI replace 80% of my labor without reducing the perceived value?"*

### Leverage analysis: a Google Ads Audit, before & after AI

| Step | Before AI (hours) | With AI (hours) | Leverage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pull data exports from Google Ads API | 1.5 | 0.5 | 3x |
| Analyze search-term report | 4 | 0.75 | 5x |
| Identify quality-score issues | 2 | 0.5 | 4x |
| Diagnose conversion-tracking gaps | 2 | 1 | 2x |
| Write the 14-page audit deliverable | 10 | 2 | 5x |
| Record the 30-minute Loom walkthrough | 1 | 1 | 1x |
| Build implementation checklist | 3 | 0.5 | 6x |
| Client onboarding + kickoff call | 1 | 1 | 1x |
| QA + final review | 2 | 1.5 | 1.3x |
| **Total** | **26.5 hr** | **8.75 hr** | **~3x overall** |

At $1,750 per audit, that's:
- Before AI: $66/hr "effective" rate
- With AI: $200/hr effective rate

This is **AI leverage**. When you productize, you build the playbook *around the AI step* — not as an afterthought. The audit is designed so the AI-doable parts are 70%+ of the labor.

### Where AI cuts cost 5x+ (your highest-leverage productized services)

| Service type | AI leverage | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Audit + analysis | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 5–8x | AI is excellent at structured pattern-spotting in data |
| Long-form content drafting | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 5–7x | AI drafts; human refines + adds POV |
| Ad copy variants at scale | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 4–6x | 50 variants in 10 minutes vs. 4 hours |
| Brief & spec writing | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 4–5x | Templates + AI fill |
| Landing page + ad creative briefs | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 3–5x | Same as above |
| Email flow logic + copy | ⭐⭐⭐ 3–4x | Logic still needs human; copy is leveraged |
| Strategic positioning work | ⭐⭐ 2x | Human judgment still dominates |
| Discovery / sales calls | ⭐ 1–1.5x | Mostly human |
| Production filming / shooting | ⭐ 1x | Physical work |

**Design your productized services around the top 3 leverage rows.** That is where solo operators run economics that look like agencies of 5.

## ⚙️ The Anatomy of a Productized Service (Use This Template)

Every productized service needs the following components documented in your SOP library. This is the blueprint Hannah used and that you should build for each SKU.

### Component 1 — The promise (one sentence)

> *"In 7 business days, we will deliver a 14-page Google Ads audit identifying the 5 highest-ROI changes for your account, with implementation steps you can execute yourself or hand to any agency."*

This sentence goes on the sales page. It is your contract.

### Component 2 — The deliverables (list)

- Specific document(s)
- Specific format(s)
- Specific page/quantity count
- Specific timeline

### Component 3 — The process (steps)

Each step should specify:
- Who does it (you, AI, contractor)
- What tools are used
- What the output is
- How long it takes

### Component 4 — The intake form

A 5–10 question form that collects everything you need *before* you start. Examples:
- Google Ads account ID and admin access
- Current monthly spend
- Top 3 conversion actions
- Your last 3 audits or attempts
- Anything off-limits

### Component 5 — The kickoff (15-min Loom or call)

You record a Loom walking the client through what happens next, what they need to send, and when to expect the deliverable. This kills 80% of the "where are we?" emails.

### Component 6 — The deliverable template

Your audit / build / report should use the same template every time. Notion or Google Docs are fine. Same headings. Same chart placements. Same recommendation format.

### Component 7 — The walkthrough (30-min Loom)

Record a Loom walking through the deliverable. Better than a live call (which clients reschedule). Async + skimmable.

### Component 8 — The implementation handoff

What the client does next. Often a checklist. Often a Notion duplicatable template.

### Component 9 — The upsell

After delivery: an automated email at +14 days with "Want me to implement this for you?" or "Want a monthly subscription that maintains this?"

### Component 10 — The testimonial ask

Automated email at +21 days: "Quick favor — would you record a 60-second Loom describing what changed?" This is your marketing fuel.

> 🎯 **MEMORIZE THIS:** The 10 components apply to EVERY productized service. Build them once per SKU, reuse forever.

## 🛡️ Scope Control: The Five Rules That Keep You Profitable

Scope creep is the productized service's natural enemy. If you don't enforce scope from day one, you will end up doing custom consulting at a fixed price. Here are the five rules.

### Rule 1 — Define out-of-scope in the SOW

For every productized service, name what is NOT included. Example: "This audit covers Google Ads. It does NOT cover Microsoft Ads, Meta Ads, TikTok Ads, or organic SEO. If you need those, we offer separate audits at the same flat price."

### Rule 2 — Use a kickoff Loom, not an open-ended call

Open-ended calls invite "while you're at it" requests. A Loom is a one-way communication. Clients can respond by email — and you can decide if it's in scope.

### Rule 3 — Templatize the deliverable

The deliverable should look the same every time. Same template = same effort = same price. If a client wants a different format, that's a new product.

### Rule 4 — Have a "scope-change menu"

If the client asks for something outside scope mid-engagement, respond with: "Great idea — that's outside the audit scope. I have a separate product for [X] at $[Y]. Want me to set that up to start after delivery?"

### Rule 5 — Build in a single revision round (and only one)

Productized services should allow ONE revision round. Beyond that, additional revisions are billed at a flat $250–$500 each. State this in the SOW from day one.

> 💬 *Brett Williams (Designjoy) on scope: "I never give 'free changes.' I have a queue. You can request as many as you want. We do one at a time. That's the product."*

## 📜 Service-Level Agreements (SLAs) for Productized Services

An SLA is the explicit contract about timing, quality, and remedies. For productized work, your SLA should cover:

| SLA Element | Productized Audit example | Productized Subscription example |
|---|---|---|
| **Response time** | First reply within 1 business day | Same |
| **Delivery window** | 7 business days from intake completion | 14-day cadence (next batch by 14 days from prior) |
| **Revisions** | 1 round included | 2 rounds per deliverable |
| **Office hours** | M–F 9am–6pm local | Same |
| **Channels** | Email + 1 Slack channel | Same |
| **Out-of-scope** | Listed in SOW; quoted separately | Listed in SOW |
| **Refund / kill fee** | 25% refund within 48 hours of intake; 0% after | 30-day cancellation notice |
| **Late delivery remedy** | 10% credit per business day late, up to 50% | Pro-rated credit |

> 🚨 **Trap:** Founders skip SLAs because "it's just a friendly engagement." Two weeks in, the client demands daily Slack replies on weekends. Without an SLA in writing, you have no leg to stand on.

## 🧪 Three Real-World Productized Service Case Studies

### Case study 1 — Designjoy ($1.5M ARR, 1 person)

Brett Williams's now-famous productized design subscription:
- **Promise:** "Unlimited design requests. One at a time. $4,995/month. Pause anytime."
- **Scope:** Brand, web, ad creative, social. Same submission system (Trello). Same reply window (1–2 business days).
- **AI leverage:** Williams uses Figma + Midjourney + AI tools to draft and iterate, then refines.
- **Margin:** ~70% per public interviews.
- **What's not in scope:** Coding, motion graphics beyond simple, custom illustration. Each "no" preserves scope.

Lesson: **One product, one price, one process. The "one at a time" rule is the entire scope-control mechanism.**

### Case study 2 — Workshop Digital's "Search Audit" productized service

Workshop Digital (US, ~25 person agency, public on their blog and case studies) productized a Google Ads + SEO audit at $4,500 flat with a 14-day delivery. They describe it as their "wedge" product — clients buy the audit, then upgrade to retainer. The audit-to-retainer conversion is ~40% per their stated case studies. The audit alone is breakeven; the retainer pipeline it builds is the business.

Lesson: **Audit-as-wedge is a proven pattern. Sell the cheap thing; the relationship sells the recurring thing.**

### Case study 3 — Carry the Load (B2B content subscription)

A small B2B content agency (public case study on Indie Hackers, ~$640K ARR, 3-person team) productized a content subscription at $4,800/month: 8 blog posts + 30 social posts + 4 newsletters per month, AI-drafted and human-edited. Their process:
1. Quarterly content strategy intake.
2. Monthly content calendar approved by client.
3. Weekly batches drafted by AI, edited by team.
4. Bi-weekly client review call (30 min).
5. Monthly performance report.

They publish their gross margin at ~62%.

Lesson: **Subscription content is productizable IF the cadence is fixed, the formats are fixed, and the review touch points are scheduled — not on-demand.**

### Case study 4 — Ghost Productions (LinkedIn ghostwriting, public)

A solo ghostwriter (Justin Welsh wrote about a similar operator publicly) charging $4,000/month for a LinkedIn subscription: 4 posts/week + 1 monthly newsletter, ghostwritten end-to-end. Workflow:
1. 60-min monthly "voice capture" call with client.
2. AI drafts based on transcript + brand-voice memo.
3. Weekly batch sent for approval Friday.
4. Posted by VA on schedule.

At 15 clients × $4,000 = $60K/mo, with one VA at $1,800/mo and tools at ~$300/mo, this is roughly 70% net margin.

## 🧮 Pricing Productized Services — Quick Math

A productized service should target a **gross margin of 60–80%** at scale. Here's how to back into your price.

### Step 1 — Estimate the time

Time the SOP end-to-end three times before you publish a price. Most founders underestimate by 40%.

### Step 2 — Calculate your "effective hourly target"

For Year-1 founders, target **$150–$250/hr effective**. For Year-2+ operators with proof, target **$300–$500/hr effective**.

### Step 3 — Multiply

```
Price = (hours of work × effective hourly target) ÷ (1 - overhead %)
```

Example: A 9-hour Google Ads audit, $200/hr target, 25% overhead:

```
Price = (9 × $200) ÷ (1 - 0.25)
      = $1,800 ÷ 0.75
      = $2,400
```

Round to a clean number ($1,950, $2,250, $2,500). Test it.

### Step 4 — The "tripled price" exercise

Once you have your "calculated" price, **try selling it at 3x.** Most productized services that succeed have launched at 2–3x their original price. The market often pays more than the founder expects.

| Original price (founder's gut) | 3x test price | Actual result |
|---|---|---|
| $500 audit | $1,500 audit | Often sells at $1,500 |
| $2,000 build | $6,000 build | Often sells at $5,500 |
| $1,500/mo retainer | $4,500/mo | Often sells at $3,500 |

> 🎯 **MEMORIZE THIS:** The single largest mistake in productized pricing is **underpricing by 2–3x**.

## 📦 Stacking SKUs: The Path to $1M+

Year 1: 1 SKU. Year 2: 2 SKUs. Year 3: 3 SKUs. Beyond that, you don't need more SKUs — you need to deliver them better.

Hannah's catalog by Year 3:
1. The Google Ads Tear-Down — £1,750
2. The Performance Max Tear-Down — £2,250
3. The Google Ads Management Subscription — £4,500/mo

That's it. Three SKUs. £490K → £820K → £1.1M revenue arc.

**Resist the urge to add SKUs.** Each new SKU is a new sales narrative, a new delivery playbook, a new troubleshooting space. More leverage comes from selling more of your best SKU than from adding new ones.

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Productized services don't fit my work — it's too bespoke" | Almost any deliverable can be productized if you templatize the inputs and outputs |
| "Clients will reject fixed pricing" | Clients prefer fixed pricing. It's the agency that fears it. |
| "I need 10 products to look credible" | 1 SKU with proof beats 10 SKUs with no proof |
| "Productized = lower quality" | Productized = higher consistency. Bespoke = variable quality. |
| "AI replaces all the value" | AI replaces 60–80% of the labor. The judgment + framing is your value. |
| "Scope creep is the client's fault" | Scope creep is the agency's failure to define scope upfront |

## 💬 Discussion — Socratic prompts

Use these as journal prompts, study-group questions, or interview-prep drills. Each is open-ended; the best answers cite the 5 Fixed Anchors, the 10 components of a productized service, the AI-leverage table, and at least one named case (Designjoy, Hannah's Tear-Down, Workshop Digital, Carry the Load).

1. **The "bespoke client" carve-out.** A senior CMO loves your productized $6,500 Klaviyo flow build but asks for "a small custom dashboard at the end — should only take you a couple hours with AI, right?" Walk through how each of the 5 Fixed Anchors gets violated if you say yes once, and design a response that preserves the relationship without breaking the SKU. Then construct the counter-argument: when *should* an experienced operator accept a one-off bespoke addition? (Reference the scope-change menu and Brett Williams's "one at a time" rule.)
2. **Audit-as-wedge vs subscription-first.** Workshop Digital uses the cheap audit ($4,500) as the entry to a higher-ticket retainer (~40% conversion). Brett Williams skips the audit entirely and sells the $4,995/mo Designjoy subscription day-one. Which model fits a brand-new Year-1 founder with no proof, and which fits a Year-2 operator with case studies? Defend the right wedge for each stage and name the conditions that flip the answer.
3. **Where AI leverage breaks down.** The reading claims audit/analysis is the highest-leverage row (5–8x) and strategic positioning is low-leverage (~2x). A founder argues that with frontier models in 2026 the positioning row will also become 5–8x within 18 months. Build the strongest case for that argument and the strongest case against. If they're right, how should you re-architect the AI Leverage column of your SKU catalog? If they're wrong, why is positioning structurally different?
4. **The "12 SKU" temptation.** A Year-2 founder with one successful SKU at $4,500/mo is tempted to launch 8 more SKUs because every prospect call surfaces a different ask. Use the 1 SKU → 2 SKU → 3 SKU expansion logic to push back. Then construct the strongest case *for* expansion — under what specific signals (retention curve, deliverable overlap, sales-cycle data) would adding the second SKU actually be the right move at Year 2 rather than Year 3?
5. **Pricing the irreducibly bespoke.** A founder argues their wedge ("crisis-comms response for healthcare brands after FDA enforcement actions") is too unpredictable to productize — every engagement has different facts, different regulators, different windows. Construct the strongest argument that even *this* work can be productized (look at the 10 components for inspiration — maybe the SKU is the response framework, not the per-incident output). Then defend the opposite view: some work genuinely cannot be productized and should stay project-based. Which way do you lean, and why?

There are no "official" answers — defend your reasoning with specifics. Strong responses cite the 5 Fixed Anchors, at least one row of the AI-leverage table, and at least one named case from the reading.
