✅ Tajweed self-check — listen back and tick honestly

  • Makhaarij — each letter came from its correct point (throat, tongue, lips); the heavy letters خ ص ض ط ظ غ ق sound full, the light letters stay thin.
  • Ghunnah — a clear ~2-count nasal hum on noon and meem mushaddad (نّ مّ) and in ikhfaa.
  • Madd lengths — natural madd held 2 counts; connected/obligatory madd held 4–6 counts, consistently.
  • Qalqalah — a distinct echo-bounce on ق ط ب ج د when they carry sukoon or you stop on them.
  • Waqf & ibtida — you stopped and resumed at sensible places, never breaking a word mid-flow.
  • Tarteel — a measured, unhurried pace (Quran 73:4), not rushed.
  • Adab — you began with Ta'awwudh + Basmala and recited with presence of heart.
  • The model test — played side-by-side with the reciter, the two sound the same to your ear.

Automated speech-grading is intentionally not used for Quran recitation: general speech recognition cannot judge Tajweed (madd length, ghunnah, makhraj) and must not be relied on for the sacred text. Verify your Tajweed with the model recitation and a qualified teacher.

✅ Speaking self-check — listen back and tick honestly

  • Sounds — the tricky {{ include.language }} sounds are clear (not substituted with the nearest sound from your own language).
  • Word stress — the stressed syllable in each word matches the model.
  • Intonation — your sentence rises and falls like the model's (questions vs. statements sound different).
  • Pace & flow — you spoke in connected phrases, without long mid-sentence pauses or word-by-word stops.
  • Full sentences — you recorded a complete sentence or answer, not just isolated words.
  • The understandability test — a native speaker would understand you on the first try, without you repeating.
  • The model test — played right after the model, your version sounds close enough to pass for the same phrase.
