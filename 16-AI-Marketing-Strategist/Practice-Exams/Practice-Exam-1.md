## Detailed answer rationales

**Q1. Answer: C, Business and marketing strategy.**
- **Why C is correct.** STP (Smith 1956; Kotler) is positioned at the business-strategy and marketing-strategy levels in Module 1's strategy-level table, it answers "how do we win in this market" and "what audiences, positioning, mix."
- **Why the other options are wrong.**
  - A (corporate strategy): Corporate strategy uses Ansoff and BCG, not STP.
  - B (tactical only): Tactics are channel/creative-level decisions, not segmentation.
  - D (campaign only): Campaign strategy is downstream of STP and operates on shorter horizons.
- **Exam-takeaway.** Match the framework to the strategy level; STP spans business → marketing.

**Q2. Answer: D, Inexpensive.**
- **Why D is correct.** ISADA is Identifiable, Substantial, Accessible, Differentiable, Actionable. Cost is not part of segment-validity.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A, B, C are all valid ISADA criteria (Identifiable, Substantial, Accessible).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Memorize ISADA as the segment-validity checklist; cost shows up in targeting evaluation, not segment definition.

**Q3. Answer: A, 50–70%.**
- **Why A is correct.** Doerr's *Measure What Matters* prescribes 50–70% attainment as the target zone for stretch OKRs.
- **Why the other options are wrong.**
  - B (100%): Indicates sandbagging, a failure mode per Doerr.
  - C (25–35%): Too low; indicates either execution failure or unrealistic targets.
  - D (95–100%): Same as B, sandbagging.
- **Exam-takeaway.** 0.6–0.8 is the healthy attainment range; consistent 1.0 is a red flag.

**Q4. Answer: A, An Initiative.**
- **Why A is correct.** "Ship 12 localized Wrapped variants by Nov 15" is a project / activity, it's an initiative. The key result would be an outcome metric like "≥35% of MAU open Wrapped within 7 days."
- **Why the other options are wrong.**
  - B (Key Result): KRs are outcomes/metrics, not activities.
  - C (Objective): Objectives are qualitative direction statements, not delivery promises.
  - D (KPI): KPIs are recurring measurement metrics, not one-time projects.
- **Exam-takeaway.** Initiatives = activities; KRs = outcomes. Confusing them ruins 70% of OKR rollouts.

**Q5. Answer: A, 50.**
- **Why A is correct.** GA4 caps Key Events at 50 per property (documented limit as of 2026).
- **Why the other options are wrong.** B (30), C (25) are smaller numbers students may confuse with the parameter limit (25 per event). D (Unlimited) is wrong, there is a hard cap.
- **Exam-takeaway.** Memorize GA4 numeric caps: 50 Key Events; 25 params/event; 50 custom dimensions (free).

**Q6. Answer: A, Free for both free GA4 and 360.**
- **Why A is correct.** As of 2026, the GA4 → BigQuery export is free at both tiers (you pay BigQuery storage/query costs, not the export itself).
- **Why the other options are wrong.**
  - B (Free for 360 only): Was true in legacy UA, not GA4.
  - C (Paid for both): Incorrect, Google removed the 360 export gate.
  - D (Not available 2026): The export is alive and well.
- **Exam-takeaway.** The export is free; the warehouse costs are downstream.

**Q7. Answer: A, `SELECT value.string_value FROM UNNEST(event_params) WHERE key = 'page_location'`.**
- **Why A is correct.** `event_params` is an `ARRAY<STRUCT>` in the GA4 BigQuery schema. You `UNNEST` it and filter by `key`.
- **Why the other options are wrong.**
  - B (`event_params.page_location`): Direct dotted access doesn't work on `ARRAY<STRUCT>`.
  - C (`JSON_EXTRACT`): The schema is structured, not JSON-blob.
  - D (`params['page_location']`): Not valid GA4 schema syntax.
- **Exam-takeaway.** `UNNEST(event_params)` then filter by `key`, this exact pattern appears on the GA4 cert.

**Q8. Answer: D, 400 conversions per conversion type / 30 days.**
- **Why D is correct.** GA4's documented DDA threshold; below this it silently falls back to last-click.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (100/month) is too low. B (no minimum) is wrong; there is a threshold. C (10K/month) is too high.
- **Exam-takeaway.** Below 400/30d → no DDA. Many smaller properties never qualify.

**Q9. Answer: D, Pivot-table-style ad-hoc analysis.**
- **Why D is correct.** Free-form Explorations are GA4's pivot-table builder for ad-hoc analysis.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (home screen), B (funnel distinct Exploration type), C (predefined report these are standard reports, not Explorations).
- **Exam-takeaway.** GA4 has five Exploration types; Free-form ≈ pivot table.

**Q10. Answer: C, 5–25%.**
- **Why C is correct.** Realistic measurement-loss recovery from server-side tagging, recovers technical losses (ad-blockers, cookie expiry) but not consent-based losses.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (~100%) is the marketing fantasy. B (50–75%) overstates the recovery. D (0%) understates, there is real signal recovered.
- **Exam-takeaway.** Server-side tagging recovers technical loss, NOT consent loss. 5–25% is the honest range.

**Q11. Answer: C, Managed hosting + power-ups for server-side GTM.**
- **Why C is correct.** Stape.io is a managed-hosting layer for GTM-SS with proprietary power-ups (cookie renewal, persistent client IDs, etc.).
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (reverse ETL that's Hightouch/Census), B (data warehouse), D (DMP declining category).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Stape.io = sGTM-as-a-service.

**Q12. Answer: D, Packaged, Composable, Self-hosted/OSS, Embedded-in-marketing-cloud.**
- **Why D is correct.** The four canonical CDP architectural patterns from Module 2.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (pricing tiers), B (generic IT taxonomy), C (B2B vs B2C splits), none describe CDP *architecture*.
- **Exam-takeaway.** Memorize all four patterns and one example vendor each.

**Q13. Answer: C, Reverse ETL (warehouse-native activation).**
- **Why C is correct.** Hightouch and Census are the category-defining reverse-ETL vendors.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (CDPs Segment, mParticle), B (CRMs Salesforce, HubSpot), D (DSPs, DV360, The Trade Desk).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Reverse ETL = warehouse → marketing destinations. Hightouch + Census are the leaders.

**Q14. Answer: C, Known explicit links (login, hashed email matches).**
- **Why C is correct.** Deterministic = explicit identity links (login event, hashed-email match). Probabilistic = ML/behavioral inference.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (behavioral ML probabilistic), B (IP only probabilistic and unreliable), D (random sampling, not identity resolution at all).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Deterministic = explicit; probabilistic = inferred. Regulators increasingly require consent for probabilistic.

**Q15. Answer: C, Show a permission prompt before using the IDFA for cross-app tracking.**
- **Why C is correct.** iOS 14.5 (April 2021) ATT requires explicit user permission for IDFA use across apps. Opt-in rates settled at 25–35%.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (auto-collect IDFA opposite of ATT), B (block cookies that's ITP, browser-side), D (encrypt all data, not ATT's mandate).
- **Exam-takeaway.** ATT = iOS app permission prompt; ITP = Safari cookie restrictions. They're related but distinct.

**Q16. Answer: A, Bottom-funnel, click-driven channels.**
- **Why A is correct.** Last-click structurally credits the channel closest to conversion, paid search, retargeting, branded affiliate.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** B is the inverse, last-click *under-credits* brand. C and D get no last-click credit by definition.
- **Exam-takeaway.** Last-click systematically over-credits bottom of funnel by 30–60%.

**Q17. Answer: D, Each channel's average marginal contribution across all possible orderings of the channel coalition.**
- **Why D is correct.** Shapley axioms (1953), fair distribution of coalition winnings averaged over all orderings.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (final touch last-click), B (regression coefficients different methodology), C (equal split, linear attribution).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Shapley = average marginal contribution. Factorial complexity → sampling approximations used in practice.

**Q18. Answer: B The "removal effect" drop in conversion probability when the channel is removed.**
- **Why B is correct.** Markov-chain attribution computes removal effect: `(P(conv|channel present) − P(conv|channel removed)) / P(conv|channel present)`.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (Hill saturation MMM terminology, not MTA), C (SHAP ML interpretability), D (Pareto front, Robyn MMM concept).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Markov MTA = removal effect computed on a state-transition graph.

**Q19. Answer: A, Apple's privacy-preserving iOS install attribution framework.**
- **Why A is correct.** SKAdNetwork is Apple's framework; AdAttributionKit is the 2024+ expansion.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** B (Google), C (Meta), D (open-source), all incorrect attributions.
- **Exam-takeaway.** SKAN = Apple, replacing IDFA-based attribution.

**Q20. Answer: C, MTA + MMM + Incrementality.**
- **Why C is correct.** The "measurement triangle", MTA for trackable click channels, MMM for untrackable + brand, incrementality to validate causality.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (ROI + ROAS + LTV, these are *metrics*, not methodologies). B (channel types), D (technical tagging layers).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Methods, not metrics, MTA + MMM + Incrementality.

**Q21. Answer: D, MMM is closer to truth for budget allocation; MTA may still be fine for tactical bid decisions.**
- **Why D is correct.** MTA over-credits trackable click channels; MMM captures aggregate causality including upper-funnel. Use each for what it's good at.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (MTA always correct false), B (discard both discards useful tactical signal), C (averaging, arithmetic mean of two different methodologies is statistical nonsense).
- **Exam-takeaway.** MTA for *within-channel* tactical; MMM for *across-channel* budget. Incrementality validates both.

**Q22. Answer: B, The decaying carry-over effect of past advertising on current sales.**
- **Why B is correct.** Ad-stock (Broadbent 1979) models the residual effect of advertising over time.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (spend → impression that's an audience/reach calculation), C (saturation Hill function, not ad-stock), D (CTR, surface metric).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Ad-stock = carry-over decay. Hill = saturation/diminishing returns. Different transformations.

**Q23. Answer: B, 1 week.**
- **Why B is correct.** With geometric decay λ=0.5, `half-life = log(0.5)/log(λ) = 1` week.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (0.5 weeks the value λ, not the half-life), C (2 weeks corresponds to λ ≈ 0.707), D (4 weeks, corresponds to λ ≈ 0.84).
- **Exam-takeaway.** λ = 0.5 → half-life 1 week. Practice the conversion both ways.

**Q24. Answer: C, `x^α / (x^α + K^α)`.**
- **Why C is correct.** The Hill saturation function. α controls shape; K is half-saturation.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (exponential decay different function), B (linear has no saturation), D (logarithmic, has saturation but the wrong functional form).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Memorize the Hill formula; know what α and K do.

**Q25. Answer: B, R (with Python port in development).**
- **Why B is correct.** Robyn is R-native; the Python port was in active development as of 2025–2026.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (Python only incorrect), C (Excel too limited for MMM), D (Java, wrong stack).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Robyn = R; Meridian = Python.

**Q26. Answer: B, Native Reach & Frequency inputs (not just spend).**
- **Why B is correct.** Meridian's defining differentiator: R&F-native ingestion, important for TV and CTV planning.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (closed-source Meridian is open-source), C (only Google Cloud runs anywhere with Python + TFP), D (single-channel, multi-channel).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Meridian's R&F support is its strongest reason to pick over Robyn.

**Q27. Answer: B, 2 years of weekly data.**
- **Why B is correct.** The accepted minimum; covers two seasonal cycles plus enough variation in spend.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (6 months monthly too few data points for ad-stock identification), C (10 years daily luxurious but rarely available; daily has its own issues), D (1 year hourly, wrong frequency, hourly is too noisy).
- **Exam-takeaway.** 2 years of weekly data is the threshold below which an MMM cannot identify ad-stock + saturation reliably.

**Q28. Answer: D, Constraining MMM coefficients using incrementality test results.**
- **Why D is correct.** Calibration formally constrains coefficient priors using geo-test or platform conversion-lift results.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (chart colors cosmetic), B (adding channels model spec, not calibration), C (running daily, frequency, not calibration).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Calibration = grounding the model in causal experiments (Wolfe & Wang, 2022, *JMR*).

**Q29. Answer: D, 50–80% (typical range).**
- **Why D is correct.** Most well-fit MMMs decompose 50–80% of revenue to baseline (brand equity, distribution, seasonality, price), the rest to paid marketing.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (5–15% far too low; would imply marketing drives almost everything), B (always 50% there's no rule), C (95%+, would imply marketing irrelevant).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Baseline outside 50–80% range is a red flag, investigate model spec.

**Q30. Answer: C, New entrants, supplier power, buyer power, substitutes, industry rivalry.**
- **Why C is correct.** Porter's Five Forces (Porter 1979, *HBR*).
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (PDCA quality cycle), B (digital ad metrics), D (SWOT different framework).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Memorize the five exactly: New entrants, Supplier power, Buyer power, Substitutes, Industry rivalry.

**Q31. Answer: A, JTBD, STP, OKR design.**
- **Why A is correct.** Spotify's Wrapped uses JTBD (relive a year, prove identity), STP (18–34 social-sharing segment), and OKR design (≥35% MAU open within 7 days).
- **Why the other options are wrong.** B (SWOT/PESTEL/BCG these are situational frameworks, not the operating ones for Wrapped), C (Ansoff/Porter/4Ps corporate/industry frames, not the campaign-level frames Wrapped uses), D (RFM/AARRR/AIDA, funnel/lifecycle frames, not the strategic ones).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Match frameworks to the *kind* of strategic problem: Wrapped was a JTBD + STP + measurable-OKR program.

**Q32. Answer: B, Product development.**
- **Why B is correct.** Ansoff matrix: existing market × new product = Product development.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (Market penetration same product/same market), C (Market development same product/new market), D (Diversification, new product/new market, riskiest).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Memorize Ansoff's 2×2 placement of all four quadrants.

**Q33. Answer: A, Low share, high growth.**
- **Why A is correct.** BCG matrix: Question Marks have low relative share in a high-growth market.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** B (high share, low growth = Cash Cow), C (high share, high growth = Star), D (low share, low growth = Dog).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Question Marks demand a decision: invest or divest.

**Q34. Answer: B, Push, Pull, Anxiety, Habit.**
- **Why B is correct.** Christensen's JTBD switching forces. Push = frustration with status quo; Pull = attraction; Anxiety = fear of new; Habit = inertia.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (AIDA funnel, 1898 awareness model), C (PDCA), D (digital marketing tactics).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Marketing job = amplify Push/Pull, reduce Anxiety/Habit.

**Q35. Answer: B, Conversions → Key Events.**
- **Why B is correct.** Google's 2024 rename of "Conversions" to "Key Events" in GA4.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (Goals → Conversions was the *UA* legacy rename 2014), C (Audiences → Segments not a real GA4 rename), D (Events → Hits, backward; UA had Hits, GA4 has Events).
- **Exam-takeaway.** "Key Events" is the new term in 2024+ GA4 documentation.

**Q36. Answer: A, Purchase probability, churn probability, predicted revenue.**
- **Why A is correct.** GA4's three built-in predictive metrics.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** B (behavioral probabilities not predictive metrics), C (paid-media KPIs not predictive), D (analytical frameworks, not GA4 features).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Three exact names: purchase probability, churn probability, predicted revenue.

**Q37. Answer: B, A Shopify-DTC pixel-and-survey hybrid attribution dashboard.**
- **Why B is correct.** Triple Whale's positioning, built for DTC Shopify stores, blending pixel data with on-checkout surveys.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (rigorous statistical MMM Triple Whale is not statistically rigorous in the MMM sense; Rockerbox or Meridian fit better), C (MMP that's AppsFlyer/Branch/Adjust), D (CDP, wrong category).
- **Exam-takeaway.** Triple Whale = DTC dashboard category, not statistical MMM.

**Q38. Answer: D "It depends DDA for trackable click channels, MMM for untrackable + brand, incrementality to validate."**
- **Why D is correct.** The strategist's canonical 2026 answer, each method is right for a different question.
- **Why the other options are wrong.** A (last-click over-credits bottom-funnel), B (current vendor's model vendor lock-in fallacy), C (linear, too simplistic and rarely matches truth).
- **Exam-takeaway.** "Which model is right?" is a category error, *combine* them.
