## Reading Passage 1 — Business Email

Read the email below and answer questions 1–4.

> **Subject: Follow-up on Project Proposal — Digital Marketing Campaign**
>
> Dear Mr. Rashid,
>
> I hope this email finds you well.
>
> I am writing to follow up on the project proposal I submitted last Tuesday regarding the digital marketing campaign for your new product line. As discussed in our meeting, the campaign would run from September through December and would target audiences across Pakistan and the UAE.
>
> I have made some revisions to the initial budget based on your feedback and have reduced the overall cost by approximately 12%. I would be grateful if you could review the updated proposal, which I have attached to this email.
>
> If you have any questions or would like to arrange a call to discuss the details, please do not hesitate to contact me. I am available Monday to Thursday between 10 a.m. and 5 p.m.
>
> I look forward to hearing from you.
>
> Kind regards,  
> Farhan Qureshi  
> Senior Marketing Manager  
> DigitalBridge Pakistan

## Answer Key

```
1.  C — "I am writing to follow up on the project proposal I submitted last Tuesday" — the purpose is to follow up.
2.  D — "I have reduced the overall cost by approximately 12%."
3.  B — "Attached to this email" means a document (the updated proposal) was included with the email.
4.  A — "I hope this email finds you well" is a standard formal polite opener. The other options are factual statements, not politeness markers.
5.  C — Second conditional for unreal present: "If she had a driving licence, she would drive to work."
6.  B — Third conditional: If + past perfect, would have + past participle.
7.  A — First conditional: If + present simple, will + base verb: "If the meeting finishes, we will go."
8.  C — Mixed conditional: past condition (had taken) + present result (would be) in London now.
9.  B — Present continuous shifts to past continuous in reported speech; "right now" shifts to "at that moment."
10. B — "Will" shifts to "would"; "tomorrow" shifts to "the next day."
11. B — Reported yes/no questions use "if/whether" + normal word order (no inversion, no auxiliary "do").
12. C — "I see your point; however, I'd like to suggest an alternative" is polite, professional disagreement.
13. C — "The ball is in your court" means it is your turn to make a decision or take action.
14. B — "Think outside the box" means to think creatively or unconventionally.
15. C — "Dear Sir or Madam" is the formal opening when you do not know the recipient's name.
16. B — "In the pipeline" means being planned or developed. "Three new products are in the pipeline" is correct usage.
17. C — "Revamp" means to improve or overhaul — the most natural word here. "Renovation" and "revival" are nouns, not verbs in this context. "Renovate" relates to buildings.
18. B — "Data" is treated as singular in modern business English: "The data suggests."
19. C — "Stated" is more formal than "said" and matches the formal register (CEO + merger). "Told" requires an object: "told reporters."
20. B — "Put off" means postponed (delayed to a later date, not cancelled).
21. A — "Run into" means to meet someone by chance. "I ran into my old professor."
22. B — "Take on" means to accept or assume responsibility for something.
23. C — "Who" is used for people: "The engineer who designed the system."
24. B — Non-defining relative clauses add extra information (not essential) and use commas: "My manager, who has worked here for 20 years, is retiring." The other sentences use defining clauses (no commas needed).
25. B — Inversion after "Not only" requires auxiliary verb before subject: "Not only did he fail..." (did + subject + base verb).
```
