**Total Questions:** 40

## Reading Passage

Read the passage carefully. You will answer 10 questions based on it (Questions 1–10).

> **The Language of Power: English in a Multipolar World**
>
> For much of the twentieth century, the spread of English across the globe was perceived — not entirely inaccurately — as an extension of Anglo-American cultural and economic dominance. Critics argued that the promotion of English as a global lingua franca inevitably marginalised indigenous languages and imposed a particular worldview on those who adopted it. This critique, often labelled linguistic imperialism, carries genuine force, particularly in post-colonial contexts where English arrived alongside political subjugation.
>
> Yet the twenty-first century has complicated this narrative considerably. English is no longer the exclusive property of the United Kingdom or the United States. It belongs, in a meaningful sense, to the more than one billion non-native speakers who use it daily to conduct business, pursue education, and navigate geopolitical relationships. A software engineer in Lahore negotiating a contract with a client in Tokyo, a researcher in Lagos collaborating with a colleague in Berlin — both are using English not as a concession to Western power, but as a genuinely neutral medium of exchange.
>
> The emergence of what linguists call "World Englishes" — regionally distinctive varieties such as Pakistani English, Indian English, Singaporean English, and Nigerian English — further disrupts the old hierarchies. These varieties are not deficient forms of a prestigious British or American standard. They are legitimate, rule-governed, and socially functional linguistic systems in their own right.
>
> The practical implications are significant. Learners of English no longer need aspire to a native-speaker accent or a narrowly defined cultural identity. What they require is intelligibility: the capacity to be clearly understood across a range of contexts and interlocutors. For the Pakistani professional entering the global workforce, this is genuinely empowering news. The goal is not to become someone else — it is to become the most articulate, confident version of yourself, in English.

## Answer Key with Explanations

```
1.  C — The passage argues that the relationship between English and power has become more complex, not simpler. Both the critique (imperialism) and the counter-argument (neutral medium) are presented.

2.  B — A lingua franca is a language used as a common means of communication between speakers of different languages. Context clues: "global lingua franca" and "neutral medium of exchange."

3.  C — The author presents both the critique of linguistic imperialism (paragraph 1) AND the counter-argument (paragraphs 2-4), concluding with nuanced practical advice. This is balanced and analytical.

4.  B — "It belongs, in a meaningful sense, to the more than one billion non-native speakers who use it daily." Non-native speakers now co-own and outnumber native speakers.

5.  C — "Regionally distinctive varieties such as Pakistani English, Indian English, Singaporean English, and Nigerian English." These are defined as legitimate regional varieties.

6.  B — "These varieties are not deficient forms of a prestigious British or American standard. They are legitimate, rule-governed, and socially functional linguistic systems." The sentence means regional varieties are valid, not inferior.

7.  C — "Learners of English no longer need aspire to a native-speaker accent... What they require is intelligibility." The goal is to be clear and confident, not to imitate a native speaker.

8.  D — "Marginalised" comes from "margin" (edge). In context: indigenous languages were "pushed to the edges" and made less important by the spread of English.

9.  B — Final sentence: "The goal is not to become someone else — it is to become the most articulate, confident version of yourself, in English."

10. C — The passage mentions Pakistani English, Indian English, Singaporean English, and Nigerian English. Chinese English is NOT mentioned.

11. B — Past perfect (had + pp) is used for the action that happened BEFORE another past action: "By the time the CEO arrived [past simple], the meeting had already started [past perfect]."

12. C — Subjunctive uses base form of verb for all subjects: "It is essential that he BE present." (Not "is", "was", or "will be".)

13. B — It-cleft for emphasis on the person: "It was Ahmed who solved the problem."

14. B — Inversion after negative adverbials: auxiliary before subject. "Rarely have I seen" (have + I), not "Rarely I have seen."

15. C — Non-defining relative clauses use commas and add non-essential information. "The director, whose report was published last week, is resigning." The "whose" clause is additional, not defining.

16. A — Mixed conditional: past condition (had accepted) + present result (would be living). Option D would be third conditional, not mixed.

17. C — "Avoid" takes a gerund: "She avoided answering the question."

18. C — Future perfect: will have + past participle: "She will have finished."

19. C — "Since" is used with a specific point in time: "since 2019." "For three years" would be correct for a duration; "since three years" is incorrect.

20. B — Reported speech: "cannot" → "could not"; "next week" → "the following week." She told us she could not attend the conference the following week.

21. B — "Established" is formal and precise academic vocabulary. "Showed" and "found" are acceptable but less formal. "Got" is too informal.

22. C — "Substantial" is the formal academic synonym. "Big" and "quite large" are informal. "Lots" is not an adjective.

23. B — "Move the goalposts" means to change the rules or expectations after an agreement has been reached, making it harder for the other party to succeed.

24. C — "Burn the midnight oil" means to work very hard, often late at night, especially under time pressure.

25. A — "Incompatibility" is the noun form needed here to function as the subject. "Incompatible" is an adjective; "incompatibly" is an adverb.

26. B — "Diplomatic" describes language that avoids directness to prevent offence. "Verbose" means using too many words; "articulate" means expressing clearly; "literal" means taking words at face value.

27. B — A "watershed moment" is a critical turning point after which things are fundamentally different. The term comes from geography (a watershed divides water flow between regions).

28. C — "Make progress" is the correct collocation. You make: progress, a decision, a mistake, a phone call. You do: homework, business, a favour.

29. B — "Organise" is the British English spelling. "Organize" is American English. The -ise/-ize distinction is one of the main British/American spelling differences.

30. C — "TLDR" means "Too long, didn't read" — a brief summary placed at the beginning or end of long text. Note: option B has a typo ("To" instead of "Too").

31. B — "Let's refocus — we need to return to the agenda item" is professional and constructive. It redirects without being rude.

32. B — This response refuses the current price politely, offers a conditional alternative, and keeps the negotiation moving forward constructively.

33. C — The introduction should paraphrase the question topic and state your position/thesis. Evidence goes in body paragraphs; the conclusion restates the position.

34. B — "Senior Cloud Engineer | AWS & Azure | 8 Years Enterprise Infrastructure" is specific, keyword-rich, and professional. The others are vague, unprofessional, or in poor English.

35. C — British convention: "Yours sincerely" when you know the name (Dear Mr. Smith); "Yours faithfully" when you do not know the name (Dear Sir/Madam).

36. B — This is confident, professional, self-introductory, and immediately invites the other person to speak. It is the gold standard for professional networking.

37. C — British understatement: "not quite what we were expecting" is a classic British way of saying it was significantly below expectations. The understatement makes it sound mild but the actual message is serious.

38. C — This response acknowledges the CEO's idea, signals you are about to offer a different view ("complementary perspective"), and grounds your disagreement in evidence — professionally and diplomatically.

39. B — Acknowledging audience confusion and rephrasing shows speaker intelligence, empathy, and communication skill. Option A ignores the problem; C is rude; D is defeatist.

40. C — This is a structured, quantified, contextualised response that demonstrates C1 fluency: specific role, years of experience, key achievement with data, and a forward-looking statement. Options A and D are beginner-level; B is vague and unhelpful.
```
