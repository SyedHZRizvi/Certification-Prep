## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B) وعلیکم السلام**
The only correct response to "السلام علیکم" is "وعلیکم السلام" (And upon you peace). This is the standard Islamic greeting-response pair. Any other response would seem odd.

**Q2 — C) کتنے کا ہے یہ؟**
"Kitne ka hai yeh?" = How much is this? This is the standard price inquiry phrase. "کتنا وقت ہے" = What time is it. "کتنی دور ہے" = How far is it.

**Q3 — B) کچھ کم کریں**
"Kuch kam karein" (Reduce it a bit) is the polite and standard bargaining phrase. It is firm but respectful — perfect for bazaar negotiation.

**Q4 — C) حساب کریں**
"Hisaab karein" = bring the bill / settle the account. "مینو دکھائیں" = show the menu. This is the standard end-of-meal phrase.

**Q5 — C) What shall I bring for you to drink?**
"Peene ke liye kya laayen?" — "peene" (for drinking) + "ke liye" (for the purpose of) + "kya laayen" (what shall I bring?).

**Q6 — D) سیدھے جائیں**
"Seedha jaayen" = go straight. "Baayen murein" = turn left, "Daayen murein" = turn right, "Waapas jaayen" = go back.

**Q7 — B) What is the problem / What ails you?**
"Kya takleef hai?" — takleef means trouble/suffering/pain. This is how Pakistani doctors open a consultation. Literally: "What pain/trouble is there?"

**Q8 — C) بخار ہے**
"Bukhaar hai" = I have a fever. "Pait mein dard hai" = stomach pain, "Sar dard hai" = headache, "Haath mein dard hai" = pain in the hand.

**Q9 — C) Don't worry**
"Ghabrayen nahin" = don't worry / don't panic. "Ghabrana" means to panic or be alarmed. This is a common reassuring phrase.

**Q10 — B) A standard and warm hospitality expression meaning "this is your home too"**
"Yeh aap ka bhi ghar hai" is one of the most common Pakistani hospitality phrases. It expresses that you are completely welcome and at home. It is not literal — it is warmth.

**Q11 — B) آپ نے بہت تکلیف کی، شکریہ**
"Aap ne bahut takleef ki, shukriya" (You've gone to so much trouble, thank you) is the culturally appropriate expression of gratitude for a host's effort. It acknowledges their hospitality.

**Q12 — B) Not at all / It's nothing (responding to thanks or apology)**
"Koi baat nahin" is the universal response to شکریہ (thank you) or معاف کریں (sorry). Literally "no matter" or "no issue."

**Q13 — C) Yes, please speak / go ahead (polite response)**
"Farmaiye" is a very polite imperative form meaning "please speak/command." Used on phones and in formal conversations to invite the other person to state their purpose.

**Q14 — C) God willing — I intend to come but acknowledge it is in God's hands**
"Insha'Allah" is deeply embedded in Pakistani speech. When used for future plans, it genuinely means the speaker intends to do something but acknowledges human plans are subject to God's will. It is sincere, not evasive.

**Q15 — C) This is the final price (no more negotiation)**
"Aakhri qeemat" = final price. In bazaar bargaining, this signals the shopkeeper's last offer. You can try one more counter, but this signals negotiation is nearly over.

**Q16 — B) مبارک ہو**
"Mubarak ho" = Congratulations. "Mubarak" means blessed/auspicious. For weddings specifically: "Shaadi mubarak" or "Shaadi ki bahut bahut mubarakbaad."

**Q17 — B) Please come again**
"Dobara tashreef laiye ga" is a very polite "please come again." "Tashreef laana" is a formal and elevated way to say "to come" — literally "bring your dignity/presence."

**Q18 — C) Accept the deal**
"Manzoor" = accepted/agreed. It finalizes a negotiation. The opposite would be "منظور نہیں" (manzoor nahin — not accepted).

**Q19 — B) Don't eat spicy chilies**
"Tez mirch na khayen" — "tez" = intense/sharp, "mirch" = chili/pepper, "na khayen" = don't eat. A common dietary advice in Pakistani medical contexts.

**Q20 — C) A bride at a wedding**
"Bahut khubsoorat lag rahi hain aap" — the feminine verb "lag rahi hain" makes this appropriate for a woman. At a wedding, complimenting a bride on her appearance is culturally expected and appreciated.

**Q21 — C) Create rapport and often justify a better price**
"Aap pehle gahak hain aaj" (You are my first customer today) is a classic Pakistani bazaar phrase. The implied logic is: the first sale of the day is auspicious, so the shopkeeper may offer a slightly better deal — but it is also used as a negotiating tactic.

**Q22 — B) Keep the rest as a tip**
"Baqi aap ke liye" = the remainder is for you. This is how Pakistanis give tips — rather than adding a tip line on a bill, they simply tell the server to keep the change.

**Q23 — C) Warm and appropriate — Pakistani culture uses family terms for elders even without blood relationship**
In Pakistani culture, calling older women "آنٹی" (Auntie) and older men "انکل" (Uncle) is standard and warm, even without family connection. It is a sign of respect and closeness, not presumption.

**Q24 — B) May Allah reward you / Thank you (Islamic expression of gratitude)**
"Jazak Allah" (full form: Jazak Allahu Khayran) means "May Allah reward you with good." It is a deeper and more spiritual expression of gratitude than simply "شکریہ." Common among practicing Muslims.

**Q25 — B) A standard, dramatic bargaining expression — not meant literally**
"Aap to mujhe loot rahe hain!" (You are robbing me!) is theatrical bargaining language used universally in South Asian markets. It is not a genuine accusation — it signals that your offer is very low from the shopkeeper's perspective, but negotiation can continue.

**Q26 — B) I recommend the biryani today — it's particularly good**
"Aaj biryani bahut achhi hai" = the biryani is very good today. This is a server's natural recommendation — highlighting the day's best dish. In Pakistani dhaba culture, the server often guides you to what is freshest.
