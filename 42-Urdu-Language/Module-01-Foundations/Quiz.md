## 🎯 Answers + Explanations

**Q1 — B) Right to left**
Urdu flows right to left. Only numbers maintain left-to-right direction even within Urdu text.

**Q2 — C) 39**
The standard Urdu alphabet contains 39 letters. Some sources count 36–40 depending on how variant letters and hamza are treated.

**Q3 — B) Nastaliq**
Nastaliq (نستعلیق) is the primary script for Urdu, developed in 15th-century Persia combining Naskh and Ta'liq traditions.

**Q4 — B) چ (Che)**
The letter چ represents the "ch" sound. ج (Jeem) is the "j" sound, ش (Sheen) is "sh," and خ (Khe) is the guttural "kh" sound.

**Q5 — C) 60%**
Approximately 60% of Urdu vocabulary derives from Persian and Arabic. The remaining 40% comes mainly from Sanskrit (through Hindustani), Turkic, and English.

**Q6 — A) شکریہ (Shukriya)**
Shukriya is the standard "thank you." For greater emphasis: "بہت شکریہ" (Bahut shukriya — Thank you very much).

**Q7 — B) وعلیکم السلام (Wa alaykum as-salam)**
The formal and expected response to the Islamic greeting "As-salamu alaykum" is "Wa alaykum as-salam" (And upon you peace).

**Q8 — C) آپ (Aap)**
آپ is the formal, honorific "you." Always safe to use. Using it with anyone is never considered rude.

**Q9 — C) Yes / a respectful acknowledgment**
جی is highly versatile: it means yes, signals you are listening, means "understood," and can soften any phrase. "شکریہ جی" means "Thank you (respectfully)."

**Q10 — C) سات (saat)**
۷ = سات (saat). ۶ = چھ (chhe), ۸ = آٹھ (aath), ۹ = نو (nau).

**Q11 — C) Mughal Empire**
Urdu developed in the 16th–19th-century Mughal Empire as a contact language between Persian-speaking rulers, Arabic-educated scholars, and local Indian populations.

**Q12 — C) Welcome**
"خوش آمدید" literally means "you came happily" in Persian origin, used as "welcome."

**Q13 — B) ٹ (Ṭe)**
ٹ is a retroflex consonant — the tongue curls back against the roof of the mouth. ت (Te) is the softer dental "t" where the tongue touches behind the teeth.

**Q14 — B) پندرہ (pandrah)**
۱۵ = پندرہ. ۵ = پانچ, ۱۱ = گیارہ, ۲۵ = پچیس.

**Q15 — C) Devanagari**
Hindi uses the Devanagari script. Spoken Hindi and Urdu share the same colloquial base (Hindustani), but the scripts and formal vocabularies differ significantly.

**Q16 — B) Makes the consonant aspirated (with a puff of air)**
Do Chashmi He (ھ) creates aspirated consonant clusters: بھ (bh), پھ (ph), تھ (th), کھ (kh), گھ (gh), ڈھ (ḍh).

**Q17 — B) A way to say goodbye**
"اللہ حافظ" means "May Allah protect you" and is the standard Pakistani Islamic farewell. "خدا حافظ" using the Persian word for God is the older equivalent.

**Q18 — C) A serious social slight/disrespectful**
Using تو with someone who expects آپ is a significant social error in Pakistani culture. تو implies either extreme intimacy or a dismissive attitude.

**Q19 — C) Right now / Just now**
"ابھی" means right now or just now. "آج" = today, "کل" = yesterday/tomorrow, "ہمیشہ" = always.

**Q20 — B) Market / Bazaar**
"بازار" (baazaar) is the market. This word is also borrowed directly into English as "bazaar."

**Q21 — D) Up to four (isolated, initial, medial, final)**
Urdu letters change form depending on position: isolated (alone), initial (start of word), medial (middle), and final (end).

**Q22 — B) Formal, literary, and older/courtly contexts**
"آداب" with the hand-to-forehead gesture is elegant and traditional. It is less common among younger Pakistanis in everyday conversation but marks cultural refinement.

**Q23 — B) Rupee (the Pakistani currency)**
"روپیہ" (rupiya) specifically means the rupee (Pakistani currency unit). "پیسہ" (paisa) means money generally or the smaller coin denomination.

**Q24 — A) بارہ (baarah)**
۱۲ = بارہ. ۱۱ = گیارہ (gyaarah), ۱۳ = تیرہ (tairah), ۱۴ = چودہ (chaudah).

**Q25 — C) Turkic phrase meaning "Language of the Camp"**
"Urdu" comes from the Turkic "Zabān-e-Urdū" — language of the (military) camp. The Turkic root "ordu" (camp/horde) also gave English the word "horde."

**Q26 — C) Usually omitted in everyday adult text; included in learner materials**
Short vowels (اعراب/harakat) are typically not written in standard Urdu text. Children's books, Quran texts, and learner materials include them to aid pronunciation.
