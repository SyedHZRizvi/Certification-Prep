## 🎬 A Story: Two Marketers Apply For The Same Job

It's Tuesday morning at a mid-stage Series B SaaS company. A hiring manager named Priya is staring at 247 applicants for a Growth Marketing Manager role. She has 90 minutes.

She skims the first batch of 50 resumes in 12 minutes. 47 of them list the same things, "ran campaigns on Google Ads," "managed a content calendar," "used HubSpot." All the verbs are the same. All the bullets are the same. Priya makes the same mental note 47 times: *can't tell if they actually did the work or just sat in the room while someone else did the work.*

Then she gets to applicant #48, let's call her Sarah. Sarah's resume has one paragraph and one link: `sarah-builds.com`. Priya clicks. In the next 4 minutes she sees:

- A 17-page SEO audit Sarah ran for an indie hot-sauce brand on Twitter, with screenshots from Ahrefs and a 90-day content roadmap. The hot-sauce founder's testimonial is embedded at the top.
- A Google + Meta campaign post-mortem: $200 spent, $612 generated, two failed creative variants shown alongside the winning one. The "what I'd do differently" section is honest.
- A Loom of a 7-step Make.com workflow that takes a webform submission, enriches it with Clearbit, scores it, and pings Slack with a recommended next action.
- A Google Sheet with a Markov-chain attribution model. Sarah is on a YouTube walkthrough explaining it.
- A working AI lead-qualification agent built with Claude, the demo bot is live, embedded right in the page.

Priya bookmarks Sarah. She doesn't bookmark applicants #49 through #112.

By Friday, Sarah has a phone screen scheduled. By the following Wednesday, an offer.

The other 246 applicants? Most of them had **more years of experience than Sarah**. Some had better-known employer names on their resume. None of them had a link Priya could click.

This is what this entire course is about: **the link that makes you the obvious choice.**

## 🧭 The Brutal Truth About Marketing Portfolios

Marketing has a portfolio problem that engineers don't have.

If you're a software engineer, "portfolio" means GitHub. The work is the artifact. A recruiter sees your commits, your repos, your tests, your README. The work *is* the proof.

If you're a marketer, what does "portfolio" mean? You can't link your Mailchimp campaigns. You can't link your Slack-channel arguments with the design team. You can't link the 14 internal docs you wrote about audience segments. **All your work lives behind logins you don't own.**

This creates a vicious cycle:

1. You join a company. You do great work for 2 years.
2. You apply for the next job. Your portfolio is... a resume.
3. The hiring manager has nothing to evaluate except your claims.
4. You don't get the job. Or you get it, but at the same level.
5. Two more years pass. Repeat.

The break-out marketers (Justin Welsh, Ross Simmonds, Amanda Natividad, Cody Schneider, Sara Stella Lattanzio) all did the same thing: they **built their own work publicly**. Not because they were extroverts. Because it was the only way to **own the artifact**.

This course is your version of that move.

## 🎯 The Core Question: Whose Mind Are You Trying To Enter?

Before picking projects, pick an audience. You have two paths:

### Path A: Hiring Manager (Job Track)

You want a salaried role. Marketing Manager, Growth Marketer, Performance Marketer, Content Lead, etc.

Your audience's question is: *"Can this person do the job from day one without me hand-holding?"*

What they need to see:

- ✅ Proof you can run a campaign end-to-end (Capstone 2)
- ✅ Proof you can think in systems, not just tactics (Capstones 3 & 4)
- ✅ Proof you can analyze data, not just produce it (Capstone 4)
- ✅ Proof you can use AI for leverage, not just experiment (Capstone 5)
- ✅ Proof you write/communicate well (Capstone 1, audit document)

### Path B: First Client (Freelance / Agency Track)

You want a paid project, retainer, or first client. Could be $500/mo, could be $5,000/mo.

Your audience's question is: *"Will this person make me more money than they cost?"*

What they need to see:

- ✅ Specific niche / industry signal (your audit / campaign was for THEIR type of business)
- ✅ Process you'll follow (Capstones map 1:1 to "what I'd do for you")
- ✅ Track record of shipping (Capstones exist, with dates)
- ✅ Communication style (your write-ups read like the way you'd email them)

### Path C: Indie / "Build In Public" (Personal Brand Track)

You want followers, an audience, possibly your own micro-product launched.

Your audience's question is: *"Should I follow this person and read their stuff?"*

What they need to see:

- ✅ Consistent voice across pieces
- ✅ Lessons-learned framing (vulnerability + honesty)
- ✅ Numbers and screenshots (proof, not platitudes)

**Most students need a blend.** Pick a primary path now, it changes the tone of every capstone.

## 🏆 The 5 Capstones, And Why These 5

There's no shortage of project ideas. Why these specific 5?

### Capstone 1: SEO + Content Audit

**Why this one:** Hiring managers see SEO/content as the highest-leverage marketing skill (because compounding). A 17-page audit is the cheapest possible signal that you understand a business deeply. It's also the easiest capstone to land your first paid client with, most small businesses don't have an audit, and you can sell one for $500–$2,000.

**Format hiring managers respect:** PDF document + linked spreadsheet of keyword opportunities + a 90-day roadmap.

### Capstone 2: Live Paid Campaign

**Why this one:** This is the ONE capstone that has actual money at stake. The reason it carries weight is the same reason a chef who's never run a real kitchen looks suspicious. You don't have to win, you have to *play the game with real chips*.

**Format hiring managers respect:** Public blog post or LinkedIn carousel, with screenshots, with the failure included.

### Capstone 3: Marketing Automation Workflow

**Why this one:** This is the modern equivalent of "I know HubSpot." Anyone can say "I know HubSpot." Fewer people can show a Loom walkthrough of a multi-step automation they built. AI-native marketing teams in 2026 expect candidates to show automation chops, Make.com, n8n, Zapier are the new "Microsoft Office."

**Format hiring managers respect:** Loom walkthrough + architecture diagram + JSON / blueprint export so the recruiter can import and tinker.

### Capstone 4: Attribution Model

**Why this one:** This is the "are you actually analytical?" filter. Most marketers say they're data-driven; very few have built an attribution model from scratch. Doing it once even with a small data set separates you immediately.

**Format hiring managers respect:** Public Google Sheet (or Colab notebook) + a 5-minute walkthrough video.

### Capstone 5: AI Marketing Agent

**Why this one:** In 2026, "I use ChatGPT" is table stakes, like "I use email" in 1998. The signal that matters is: *can you build with the API, not just chat with the UI?* A working agent (lead qualifier, content brief generator, competitor researcher) shows you've crossed that line.

**Format hiring managers respect:** Working hosted agent (even if scrappy) + GitHub repo + Loom demo.

## 📐 The Case Study Format That Always Works

Every one of your capstones gets written up the same way. This is the format hiring managers and clients have come to expect, it's the format Justin Welsh, Sara Stella Lattanzio, Cody Schneider, and every other modern marketing creator uses:

```
1. PROBLEM       , what business problem were you solving?
2. CONSTRAINTS   , what was your budget, timeline, what was off-limits?
3. APPROACH      , what did you actually do? (step by step, with screenshots)
4. RESULT        , numbers. The good. The bad. The whatever.
5. LESSONS       , what would you do differently? (vulnerability = trust)
```

🎯 **MEMORIZE THIS.** Every capstone in this course follows P-C-A-R-L. Tape it to your monitor.

**Why this format works:** It mirrors how a senior marketer naturally thinks about a project. When Priya the hiring manager reads "Problem → Constraints → Approach → Result → Lessons," she's reading a structure she'd use to evaluate her own team's work. The format is the credibility.

## 🚫 The 3 Portfolio Anti-Patterns

Avoid these. They show up in 80% of student portfolios and kill the credibility.

### Anti-Pattern 1: The "Tools List" Portfolio

A page that says "I know HubSpot, Salesforce, Marketo, Google Ads, Meta Ads, Klaviyo, Mailchimp, Ahrefs, SEMrush, Notion, Webflow, Make.com, n8n..."

**Why it fails:** Every applicant lists tools. Tools are not skills. Showing the tool is not the same as showing the work.

**Fix:** Each tool you list must point to a capstone where you used it for something real.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The "Internal Work" Portfolio

A page that says "I led growth at X company. Doubled MQLs. Built the content engine."

**Why it fails:** No one can verify it. No artifacts. No screenshots. No external link. Could be true, could be false, the hiring manager has to *trust you*. Trust isn't free.

**Fix:** Even if your job was internal, do a SIDE project capstone (publicly visible) that demonstrates the same skill. The capstone is the credibility, the internal role is the bonus context.

### Anti-Pattern 3: The "Polished But Empty" Portfolio

Beautiful Webflow site. Fancy fonts. Three placeholder case studies that say "Case Study 1, Coming Soon."

**Why it fails:** Everyone clicks the case studies. If they're empty, the whole site reads as "I built the storefront but never bought inventory."

**Fix:** Don't build the site before you build the projects. Build the projects first. The site is a wrapper for the work, not a substitute.

## 🏠 Where Should Your Portfolio Live?

Hosting decision matters more than you'd think, different platforms send different signals.

| Platform | Signal | Best For | Time To Set Up |
|----------|--------|----------|----------------|
| **Notion** | "Practical, ships fast" | Job-track applicants, fast iteration | 2 hours |
| **Read.cv** | "Modern, design-aware" | Indie / creator track | 1 hour |
| **GitHub Pages + Astro** | "I'm technical enough to ship code" | AI engineer / dev-tools marketing roles | 6–10 hours |
| **Webflow** | "Brand-quality, polished" | Senior or agency roles | 10–20 hours |
| **Squarespace** | "Generic" | Avoid for this purpose | , |
| **LinkedIn alone** | "Hasn't tried" | Avoid as the only surface | , |
| **Personal domain (any of above)** | "Owned this for a while" | Always good if budget allows | +1 hour |

### Recommended For This Course

**Start with Notion**, custom domain optional. Here's why:

1. It's free.
2. You can ship a working portfolio in 2 hours.
3. Notion's link-preview cards on LinkedIn look professional.
4. You can always migrate to Webflow later.

Most students who try to "do it right" with Webflow lose 2 weeks to design tweaks. Don't.

**Module 7 walks through this in detail.** For now, just pick where your work will live.

## 🗣️ The "Show Don't Tell" Rule

When a recruiter or prospect reads your portfolio, every sentence is either:

1. **Showing**, a screenshot, a number, a link, a Loom, a working demo
2. **Telling**, a claim about what you can do

The ratio matters. Aim for **5 shows per 1 tell**. If you're at 1 show per 5 tells, you have a resume, not a portfolio.

**Tell:** *"I'm experienced with paid social campaigns."*
**Show:** *"In this campaign for [client], we spent $200 across Meta and Google. Final ROAS (Return on Ad Spend): 3.1x. Top creative below. Three creatives that failed below that."*

## 🎤 How To Talk About These Projects In Interviews & Sales Calls

When the recruiter (or prospect) asks "Tell me about a project," you should be able to do this **in 90 seconds**, without notes, for each capstone:

```
[CONTEXT, 1 sentence]: "I ran a $200 paid campaign for an indie 
                         coaching business on Google + Meta."

[PROBLEM, 1 sentence]: "She had no paid funnel; everything was 
                         from organic LinkedIn, risky single channel."

[APPROACH, 3 sentences]:  
   "I picked Meta lookalikes from her email list and Google Search 
   on three high-intent keywords. I tested 3 creatives per platform. 
   Budget was split 60/40 toward Meta because of the lookalike asset."

[RESULT, 1 sentence with numbers]: 
   "We got 22 leads at $9 per lead and 4 paid clients in the first 14 days."

[LESSON, 1 sentence, honest]: 
   "Two of the four creatives bombed; if I redid it, I'd skip Carousel 
   on Meta and do all single-image with UGC."
```

Practice this for each of your 5 capstones until you can do it cold.

## ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---------------|---------|
| "I should wait until I have a 'real' client before building a portfolio" | A self-initiated capstone counts. Real client > self-initiated > nothing, but self-initiated >>> nothing. |
| "My portfolio needs to be on a custom domain" | Notion sub-domains work fine for landing the first job. |
| "I need to redact / hide details to look professional" | The opposite. Specificity = credibility. Vague case studies read as fake. |
| "If a campaign failed, I shouldn't include it" | Including failures (with what you learned) is the single highest-trust signal. Hiring managers can smell sanitized work. |
| "I should use stock photos and templates everywhere" | Stock = generic = forgettable. Use real screenshots even if ugly. |
| "I need 10+ projects to have a 'real' portfolio" | 5 deep beats 15 shallow. The 5 capstones in this course are by design. |

## 🎯 Exam Traps (Project Decisions That'll Trip You Up)

🚨 **Trap 1: Picking a niche before you have a body of work.**
A lot of students try to niche to "I'm the AI marketer for B2B (Business-to-Business) SaaS founders" before they've built anything. Result: their portfolio doesn't actually match the niche. **Build first, niche later**, by Capstone 3, you'll see which niche you naturally vibe with.

🚨 **Trap 2: Choosing a capstone subject you can't get screenshots from.**
You can't share Acme Corp's internal data. Pick subjects where you control the surface, your own micro-product, a friend's side business, a public indie maker who agreed, or *yourself*.

🚨 **Trap 3: Making the capstone too small to be impressive.**
A $50 ad campaign is not a campaign. A 3-page audit is not an audit. The rubrics in each Cheat-Sheet.md set the floor.

🚨 **Trap 4: Making the capstone too big to ship.**
A 100-page audit for a Fortune 500 is not impressive, it's evidence you can't scope work. Each capstone has a target page/scope count in the Cheat-Sheet.

🚨 **Trap 5: Confusing "polish" with "value."**
A Notion page with 5 great projects beats a Webflow site with 0 great projects. Always.

## 🎓 Key Terms To Know

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Case study** | A structured write-up of one project using P-C-A-R-L format |
| **P-C-A-R-L** | Problem → Constraints → Approach → Result → Lessons |
| **Capstone** | A larger, more comprehensive project that demonstrates mastery |
| **Portfolio piece** | A single capstone documented and hosted publicly |
| **Post-mortem** | A "what happened and what I learned" write-up of a finished project |
| **Build in public** | Sharing work-in-progress publicly (not just the polished end result) |
| **Show don't tell** | The principle that artifacts (screenshots, links, demos) beat claims |
| **Anti-pattern** | A common but wrong way to do something |
| **Tools list trap** | An empty portfolio that lists software instead of work |
| **Hosting surface** | Where your portfolio lives (Notion, Webflow, Read.cv, etc.) |

## Discussion, Socratic prompts

These prompts have no single right answer. Defend your reasoning from the frameworks in the reading, not from a one-line gut check. Use them as journal prompts, peer-discussion fuel, or interview practice.

1. **The "5 capstones" of this course are SEO audit, paid campaign, automation workflow, attribution model, and AI agent.** A student argues that for a B2B SaaS hiring market, two of these (paid campaign and attribution model) are wasted, they should be replaced with two more "AI agent" capstones to amplify the AI-native signal. Build the strongest argument for AND against this substitution. What does the answer depend on?
2. **Path A (Job Track), Path B (Client Track), and Path C (Indie Track) are presented as three audiences.** A senior marketer claims that for portfolio purposes, picking one path is a junior mistake, the same 5 capstones can serve all three audiences if framed correctly. Is she right, partly right, or wrong? Where does her argument break?
3. **The "Show Don't Tell" 5:1 ratio is presented as a hard rule, but a thoughtful counter-argument is that some hiring signals (judgment, taste, communication style) are inherently *tell* and don't compress into screenshots.** When would you violate the 5:1 ratio deliberately, and how would you defend that choice to a hiring manager?
4. **The reading argues "Notion is the default" hosting choice and "Webflow loses 2 weeks to design tweaks."** A designer-oriented marketer pushes back: for *brand* roles or *DTC (Direct-to-Consumer)* roles, a Notion site signals "doesn't care about craft." How do you reconcile this with the speed-of-shipping argument? Which path would you take if you were targeting a senior brand role at a DTC fashion company?
5. **The course explicitly recommends including failed campaigns in your portfolio (the "vulnerability = trust" principle).** A hiring manager you respect tells you the opposite at a networking event: *"Don't show me a campaign that lost money. I have 100 applicants who can show me wins."* How do you respond? Where is each of you right, and what does the answer depend on?
