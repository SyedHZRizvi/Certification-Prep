## 🎯 Model Answers + Grading Guidance

> Model answers describe what excellent looks like. C/D = the parts you still need to fix.

### Q1: Excellent looks like: **A**
Written permission via email or DM. This isn't legal paranoia — it's so you can confidently share screenshots without second-guessing.

### Q2: Excellent looks like: **A**
"Source: Ahrefs Site Explorer, captured 2026-05-22" under each screenshot. Junior auditors paste screenshots without sources. Senior auditors always cite.

### Q3: Excellent looks like: **A**
One page. Five findings. Ranked. Recruiters who scan only your exec summary should understand the entire audit in 60 seconds.

### Q4: Excellent looks like: **A**
The Top 10 technical issues with screenshots are non-negotiable. Without them, the technical section reads as generic advice.

### Q5: Excellent looks like: **A**
3+ competitor URLs in the Content Gap report. This is THE highest-value Ahrefs report for a portfolio audit. Without it, your audit looks like a Lighthouse report — not a strategic deliverable.

### Q6: Excellent looks like: **A**
8–12 articles. Each has: target keyword, search volume, KD, suggested word count, priority. Anything less = generic. Anything more = scope creep.

### Q7: Excellent looks like: **A**
Each article gets ONE sentence of "why this matters." Format: *"Closes the #2 missing keyword cluster; competitor [X] ranks #1 with a 2,200-word piece."*

### Q8: Excellent looks like: **A**
Top 5–10 existing pages, each with: what's working, what's missing, suggested improvements (specific, not "improve copy"). Senior marketers always do this layer — junior ones forget.

### Q9: Excellent looks like: **A**
The 5 trap findings: bounce rate as ranking factor, "more pages," "more backlinks" (without specifics), meta keywords, generic "speed." If any of these slipped in, fix before publishing.

### Q10: Excellent looks like: **A**
Every issue gets a "this is costing approximately X visits/month" or "this is making Google distrust the site" framing. Translates jargon into the language the business owner understands.

### Q11: Excellent looks like: **A**
15–20 pages. Less = looks shallow. More = looks unscoped. Both are red flags to a hiring manager.

### Q12: Excellent looks like: **A**
PDF + public link. PDF goes in your portfolio downloads. Public link lets people scan without committing to a download.

### Q13: Excellent looks like: **A**
Priority labels (P0/P1/P2) AND effort estimates (S/M/L). Without both, the recommendations are useless — clients don't know where to start.

### Q14: Excellent looks like: **A**
A linked Google Sheet with: keyword data, content gap data, page audit notes, technical issue list. Some senior marketers care more about this sheet than the PDF.

### Q15: Excellent looks like: **A**
Domain Rating + Total Keywords + Organic Traffic — for the subject AND 3+ competitors. This is the comparative frame that makes findings credible.

### Q16: Excellent looks like: **A**
Send + ask for testimonial. ~50% of indie founders will give one. A 1-line testimonial on your portfolio page is worth more than 5 pages of self-claim.

### Q17: Excellent looks like: **A**
Audit reads like YOU wrote it. Not like a 2018 template you found on HubSpot's blog. Voice is the differentiator between portfolios.

### Q18: Excellent looks like: **A**
Every recommendation rooted in data you can point to. *"This rec comes from the content gap report — competitors X and Y have 3 articles on this topic, you have zero."*

### Q19: Excellent looks like: **A**
Published. Public. Linkable. If it's sitting on your hard drive, it doesn't exist.

### Q20: Excellent looks like: **A**
LinkedIn / Twitter post with the link + a hook. Even modest engagement (5–20 likes) starts the algorithmic flywheel.

### Q21: Excellent looks like: **A**
Practiced out loud. Saying it 5 times beats reading it 50 times. This is what you'll use in interviews and sales calls.

### Q22: Excellent looks like: **A**
Honest, specific lessons. *"I spent too long on the technical section and not enough on content gap; if I redid it I'd flip the time allocation."* Vulnerability = trust.

### Q23: Excellent looks like: **A**
12–18 hours. Less = probably skipped steps. More = scope creep. The 14-day sprint plan is calibrated to this.

### Q24: Excellent looks like: **A**
Can defend every finding cold. The hiring manager interview question will be: *"Walk me through your highest-impact recommendation and why."* Be ready.

### Q25: Excellent looks like: **A**
GSC data is gold if you can get it. If you can't, explicitly note: *"Did not have GSC access for this audit; full clickstream data unavailable."*

### Q26: Excellent looks like: **A**
P-C-A-R-L structure, applied. This isn't pedantic — it's the structure that makes case studies scannable.

### Q27: Excellent looks like: **A**
Set + met. Self-imposed deadlines are the muscle this whole course builds. If you miss it by a lot, that's a process problem, not a content problem.

### Q28: Excellent looks like: **A**
You're proud of it. If you're not, you'll hesitate to share — and an unshared portfolio is worthless. The fix is usually: more screenshots, more specifics, less hedging.

## 📊 Self-Score Yourself

Count A's, B's, C's, D's:

- 22+ A's → 🏆 Ship it. This is recruiter-grade work.
- 16–21 A's + some B's → ✅ Solid. Address the C/D items, then publish.
- 10–15 A's, mix → ⚠️ Half-baked. Spend another 3–5 hours fixing weak sections.
- <10 A's → 🔁 The audit isn't ready. Re-read Module 2 Reading, identify the gaps, iterate.
