## 🎬 A Story: The First Yes

Picture someone call them Jordan who's just finished the portfolio. Five capstones, polished LinkedIn, Notion site live. Six weeks of work. But week 7 starts and Jordan's inbox is empty. No recruiters reached out. No clients DMed.

Jordan does what most students stop short of doing: they start sending cold messages.

Monday: 5 LinkedIn DMs to indie SaaS (Software as a Service) founders, each referencing the founder's product specifically + linking to one capstone that's relevant.

Tuesday: 1 reply. *"Hey Jordan, your SEO (Search Engine Optimization) audit format looks great. We don't need a full audit but we could use a content roadmap. What would you charge?"*

Wednesday: A 30-min call. Jordan walks through their case study for 5 minutes, then asks: *"Tell me about your business, where are you stuck on content?"*

Thursday: Proposal sent, $1,500 for a 30-day content roadmap deliverable.

Friday: Signed. First check.

The pitch didn't make Jordan a marketer. They were already a marketer. The pitch made them *findable* to a person who would pay them.

The other 4 cold messages didn't convert (yet). Two ghosted. One replied "thanks, not now." One replied 6 weeks later with a different project.

That's the math: send 5 → 1 conversation → 1 close in the first cycle. Send 20 over a month → expect 2-4 paid projects.

## 🎯 Two Versions Of "Pitch": Job vs Client

This module supports both tracks. Adapt based on your goal.

### Job-Track Version

Your "pitch" is a recruiter / hiring manager conversation. The "deck" is your portfolio site. The "cold messages" are tailored applications + LinkedIn DMs to hiring managers (not just recruiters).

### Client-Track Version

Your "pitch" is to indie founders, agency owners, or small businesses. The "deck" is a slide deck you screen-share. The "cold messages" are DMs and cold emails to specific prospects.

**Most of this module is written for client-track.** If you're on job-track, the same logic applies, substitute "hiring manager" for "prospect" and "interview" for "discovery call."

## 🏗️ Step 1: The 7-Slide Pitch Deck

This is the slide deck you'd screen-share on a Zoom discovery call. NOT something you send cold; this comes out in the call.

### Slide 1: Hook + Identity

```
"Hi, I'm [Name]. I'm a marketer who builds.

In the last 6 weeks I shipped:
- 5 portfolio pieces (next slide)
- An AI agent on GitHub
- A live $200 paid campaign

Here's how I might be able to help you."
```

Personal photo on this slide. Light. Your contact info bottom-right.

### Slide 2: The 5 Capstones Visual Grid

A 2x3 grid (or 5 tiles) with one image per capstone + 1-line description.

This is the "show don't tell" wall. You're not explaining; you're showing.

### Slide 3: The 1 Most-Relevant Capstone (Customized)

Pick the capstone most relevant to THIS prospect.

For an indie SaaS founder: probably the SEO audit
For a DTC (Direct-to-Consumer) brand: probably the paid campaign
For an agency: probably the automation workflow
For a B2B (Business-to-Business) SaaS: probably the AI agent

This is the only slide that changes per pitch. The other 6 stay the same.

```
[Capstone hero image]

For [Subject], I [did this in X weeks for $Y or as a portfolio piece].

The result:
- [Specific number]
- [Specific number]
- [Specific number]

What I'd do for you: [1-2 sentences tailored to their business]
```

### Slide 4: My Process (How I Work)

```
"Here's what working with me looks like:

Week 1: Discovery call + audit of your current state
Week 2: Strategy + creative production
Week 3: Launch / build
Week 4: Optimization + post-mortem

You'll get: [3 specific deliverables]
You'll do: [1-2 things you need from them, calls, asset reviews]
"
```

Set expectations. Make it look professional.

### Slide 5: What I Cost

```
"Three engagement tiers:

[Tier 1: ~$X, small scope, 2 weeks]
[Tier 2: ~$Y, medium scope, 1 month]
[Tier 3: ~$Z, full retainer, 3 months]

Most clients start at Tier 2."
```

This slide is where most marketers chicken out and leave it blank. **Don't.** Showing pricing upfront does 2 things: filters out tire-kickers, and signals confidence.

If you're job-track: replace this slide with "What I'm looking for in a role" (3 bullets).

### Slide 6: Why Me (Differentiation)

```
"3 things I think I do differently:

1. [Specific differentiator with proof]
2. [Specific differentiator with proof]
3. [Specific differentiator with proof]"
```

Examples of differentiators:

- *"I work AI-native, I build with the API (Application Programming Interface), not just chat with ChatGPT"*
- *"I always publish a post-mortem, you see what worked AND what didn't"*
- *"I work on small budgets, my biggest case study used $200, not $20k"*

### Slide 7: Next Steps + CTA (Call to Action)

```
"If this fits, here's what we'd do:

1. 30-min discovery call (this week)
2. Custom proposal in your inbox in 48 hours
3. Decision by [date]
4. Start by [date]

Or if it's not a fit, no hard feelings, happy to refer you to someone else."
```

The "no hard feelings" offer is paradoxically effective. Reduces pressure, increases close rate.

## 🏗️ Step 2: The Proposal Template

After a discovery call (Slide 7), you send a proposal. Have a template ready.

```
# Proposal: [Project Name]
## For: [Client Name]
## From: [You]
## Date: [Date]

---

## What You Want
[1-2 sentences in their words from the discovery call]

## What I'll Do

### Phase 1: [Name] (Week 1)
- [Deliverable]
- [Deliverable]

### Phase 2: [Name] (Week 2)
- [Deliverable]

### Phase 3: [Name] (Week 3-4)
- [Deliverable]

## What You'll Have At The End
[1 paragraph: specific outcomes + artifacts]

## Timeline
[Start date] → [End date] (X weeks)

## Investment
$[Amount], structured as:
- $[X] upon signing
- $[Y] at midpoint
- $[Z] upon delivery

## What I Need From You
- [Specific time / asset / access]
- [Specific time / asset / access]

## Next Step
Reply "Approved" to this email and I'll send the contract + 
initial invoice. We can start [date].

Questions? Reply directly.
```

Keep proposals to 1 page. Most agencies send 12-page proposals; they get read for 30 seconds, then ignored. A clean 1-page proposal gets signed.

## 🏗️ Step 3: The Cold Outreach Script Kit

You'll send 5 cold messages this week. Templates that work:

### LinkedIn DM Template (Indie Founder)

```
Hi [Name],

I noticed you shipped [specific thing they did recently], really 
[specific reaction, e.g., "the way you handled the X feature is clever"].

I'm a marketer who's been building a portfolio of case studies this 
year. One of them is [name + 1-line description, e.g., "an SEO audit + 
content roadmap I did for an indie SaaS founder"].

Curious, is content / SEO / paid (pick the relevant one) something 
you're thinking about for [their product]? If not, no worries; 
happy to be a connection regardless.

Either way, I'd love to follow your build.

[Your Name]
```

DON'T attach the portfolio link in the FIRST message. Wait for them to ask.

### Cold Email Template

```
Subject: Quick note on [their product]

Hi [Name],

[Specific compliment about a recent thing they did, researched, not generic]

I'm a marketer who recently built a portfolio of case studies, 
including [1-line specific example]. 

If you're thinking about [specific area: SEO / paid acquisition / 
automation / attribution / AI tooling] for [their company], 
I'd love to share what I've learned, no pitch, just useful context.

15-min call this week or next?

[Your Name]
[Portfolio link] (optional in the signature)
```

### What Makes Cold Outreach Work (Real Rules)

1. **Specificity**, Mention a SPECIFIC thing they did. Generic = ignored.
2. **No pitch in message 1**, Just connect. Pitch only if they reply.
3. **One ask, one CTA**, Don't list 5 things you can do.
4. **Show value before asking**, Reference a capstone they'd find useful.
5. **Respect their time**, Keep it ~80 words.

### What Kills Cold Outreach

- Generic openers ("I love what your company is doing")
- Massive paragraph of credentials
- Multiple links in the first message
- "I help [vague audience] [vague outcome]"
- Asking for a call before establishing why they should care

## 🏗️ Step 4: The First-Call Agenda (30 min)

When someone replies and books a call, here's what you do.

```
0:00, Intro (3 min)
  - "Thanks for hopping on. I have a quick agenda, does this work?"
  - Then: "I'll spend 5 min on me, 20 min on you, 5 min on next steps."

3:00, Your story (5 min)
  - Share screen, show pitch deck slides 1-3
  - Walk through the most-relevant capstone for THEIR situation
  - Don't go past slide 3. Pause.

8:00, Their situation (15 min, the most important part)
  - "Tell me about your business in 2 minutes."
  - "Where are you stuck on [marketing area]?"
  - "What have you tried?"
  - "What's the goal in the next 3-6 months?"
  - Take notes visibly. Repeat back. Use their words.

23:00, Recap (3 min)
  - "Based on what you said, I think the project that fits is [X]."
  - "Here's what that would look like: [2-3 sentences]"
  - "Cost is around $Y. Timeline is Z."

26:00, Next step (3 min)
  - "If you're interested, I'll send a 1-page proposal in 48 hours."
  - "If you'd rather think on it, totally fine. Want me to follow up in a week?"
  - "What questions do you have?"

29:00, Close
  - Thank them. Confirm next step. End ON TIME.
```

The biggest mistake first-time freelancers make: turning the call into a presentation. **The call is them talking, not you.** 5 min on you, 20 min on them.

## 🏗️ Step 5: The 5 Cold Messages (This Week)

This is the section most students avoid. **Send the 5 messages.** Not next week. This week.

### Picking The 5 Prospects

The 5 should be:

- Real businesses (not "I'll find some")
- People you can verify exist (Twitter / LinkedIn / company sites)
- Specific (you can point to something they did)
- Reachable (DM open, email findable)

**Where to find them:**

| Prospect Type | Where To Find |
|--------------|---------------|
| Indie SaaS founders | Twitter ("indie SaaS" / Indie Hackers Discord) |
| Indie e-commerce | Etsy bestsellers, Shopify Trends, Twitter "small business" |
| Coaches / Course creators | LinkedIn search, Substack Top Lists |
| Agency owners | LinkedIn ("Agency Owner" filter) |
| Local services | Google Maps for your city + filter by 4.5+ stars |
| B2B SaaS hiring managers | LinkedIn job postings + look up the marketing lead |

### The 5 Specific Messages

For each of the 5, write a specific message. Don't copy-paste, each one is custom.

```
Prospect 1: [Name + Link]
Hook: [Specific recent thing they did]
Capstone reference: [Which of your 5]
Send via: LinkedIn DM / Email / Twitter DM
Sent on: [Date]
Reply: [Yes / No / Maybe later]

Prospect 2: ...
```

Track in a simple sheet. Even if 4/5 ghost (likely), you've planted the seed.

## 🏗️ Step 6: The Follow-Up Cadence

Most students send one cold message and quit. Don't.

```
Day 0: Initial message
Day 4: Soft follow-up ("Wanted to make sure this didn't get buried")
Day 10: Value follow-up (share a 1-liner insight, no ask)
Day 21: Final check-in ("If now's not the time, no problem, happy 
                       to circle back in a quarter")
After Day 21: Move them to "monthly newsletter" list
```

**Why this works:** Cold-outreach response rates are 5-15% on the FIRST message. They jump to 15-25% by message 4. Most people quit after 1.

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes That Kill The Pitch Stage

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Sent the link in cold message 1 | Save it for message 2, after they reply |
| Pitched yourself instead of asking about them | 5 min on you, 20 on them |
| No pricing in the deck | Show pricing. Filter early. |
| Generic openers ("I love your work") | Be SPECIFIC about what they did |
| Sent 1 message and gave up | Follow-up cadence is mandatory |
| Stopped at "this week I'll send 5" without sending them | The whole module is sending. Send. |
| Made the deck 30 slides | 7 slides. Done. |
| Proposal is 8 pages | 1 page proposals get signed |
| Wrote follow-ups in panicked tone | Calm. Specific. No begging. |

## 🚨 The Emotional Trap

The single biggest blocker in this module is fear. Specifically:

- *"What if they say no?"*, Most will. That's the math.
- *"What if they think I'm spammy?"*, Specific + valuable messages are not spam.
- *"What if I'm not ready?"*, You've done 5 capstones. You're ready.
- *"What if I undercharge?"*, Better to land 1 project at $1k than 0 at $5k.
- *"What if they ask a question I can't answer?"* Say "I don't know, let me get back to you" confident humility.

The students who finish this course ALL had these fears. The students who finish AND get paid sent the messages despite the fears.

## Discussion, Socratic prompts

These prompts probe the trade-offs in pitching strategy, what to optimize for, who to target, and how aggressive to be in the first message.

1. **The reading says "Don't attach the portfolio link in the FIRST message. Wait for them to ask."** A pragmatic counter: in 2026, indie founders get 50+ cold DMs a week; if you don't include the link, they'll never bother to ask. When does the no-link-in-message-1 rule increase reply rates, and when does it kill them? What's the empirical test you'd run on your own 5-message batch?
2. **The "5 min on you, 20 min on them" rule for discovery calls is presented as universal best practice (per *The Mom Test*).** A successful sales rep counters: for first-meeting cold-call dynamics, prospects often *want* you to drive, they don't know what to ask. Is the 5/20/5 split right, or is "let the prospect lead" a wisdom-of-the-old-world artifact? When would you flip it to "20 min on you, 10 min on them"?
3. **The 3-tier pricing recommendation ("most clients start at Tier 2") is intentional sales engineering.** A junior who'd never priced before pushes back: showing 3 tiers feels manipulative, like the obvious "decoy effect." Is 3-tier pricing intellectually honest, or is it a sales trick dressed up as customer-friendliness? Defend or reject. What's the alternative (single price, hourly only) and what do you lose?
4. **The reading recommends Day 0 / 4 / 10 / 21 follow-up cadence and says quitting after 1 message is the biggest failure mode.** A different school of thought (from B2B founder Brian Balfour and others) is that follow-ups *decrease* close rate because they signal desperation, better to make the first message so compelling no follow-up is needed. Who's right, and under what conditions? Where's the line between "professional persistence" and "needy desperation"?
5. **The "real test" is named at the end: a hiring manager DMs you next week, or a first prospect replies and asks for a call.** A counter-perspective: the real test isn't the first inbound, it's the *5th client engagement*. Anyone can luck into a one-off; building a repeatable practice is the real win. Should the course conclude with the first close, or should there be a Module 9 that covers retention, repeat work, and pricing power growth? What's missing from the current ending?
